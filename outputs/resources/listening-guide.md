# Listening Guide: Newsroom Robots Podcast

A curated selection of episodes organized into 6 topic modules for IJBA students. Each module contains 3-4 episodes with a brief description of why each matters. Episodes are available at newsroomrobots.com.

---

## MODULE 1: AI Foundations -- What Works, What Does Not

*Start here. These episodes ground the conversation in practical reality: what AI actually delivers in newsrooms, where it fails, and how experienced practitioners think about its limits.*

### Episode 1: Martin Schori -- The AI Hub Powering Innovation at Sweden's Aftonbladet
**Date:** September 12, 2024 | **Length:** ~8,400 words

**Why it matters:** The most honest assessment of AI's editorial limitations in the corpus. Schori, who leads AI at Scandinavia's largest newspaper, reports that the promise of AI automating boring editorial tasks was "exaggerated." Headline generation failed. Transcription proved to be the only clear productivity win. But the election chatbot (150,000 questions, zero hallucinations) shows what works when done right. Essential listening for calibrating expectations.

### Episode 2: Zach Seward -- How a Five-Person AI Team Is Powering Innovation at The New York Times
**Date:** May 30, 2025 | **Length:** ~9,900 words

**Why it matters:** Seward reframes AI's value as "creating structure out of unstructured data" rather than generating prose. The NYT's use of AI semantic search to analyze 500 hours of leaked recordings demonstrates the highest-impact application. His adoption insight -- "access to tools is really not the thing that unlocks people; it's examples that resonate with them" -- challenges assumptions about how to spread AI literacy.

### Episode 3: Ole Reissmann -- Revolutionizing a Legacy Newsroom with AI at Germany's Der Spiegel
**Date:** June 19, 2024 | **Length:** ~8,900 words

**Why it matters:** A masterclass in strategic AI leadership at a legacy brand. Reissmann trained 870+ employees, built a secure internal AI platform, and ran 10 concurrent projects -- while arguing "maybe the most obvious use cases of generative AI are not the ones we should go after." The gender bias audit using AI is one of the corpus's most striking examples.

## MODULE 2: Investigative AI -- Accountability Journalism at Scale

*How AI transforms investigative journalism from a luxury of large newsrooms to a capability available at any scale.*

### Episode 4: Rune Ytreberg & Lars Adrian Giske -- Building AI Tools for Investigative Journalism in Local News
**Date:** February 18, 2025 | **Length:** ~8,100 words

**Why it matters:** The most compelling case study in the entire corpus. A 25-person Norwegian newsroom built AI tools that monitor 130 municipalities, analyze 5 million fishing transactions, and compress daily document review from 60 minutes to 5. Two summer interns produced five cover stories in their first week. The fisheries investigation led them to a police raid site ten minutes after officers. Proof that AI-powered accountability journalism works at local scale.

### Episode 5: Cheryl Phillips -- How AI Is Uncovering Hidden Stories in Local Government
**Date:** May 30, 2025 | **Length:** ~10,500 words

**Why it matters:** Phillips builds shared AI infrastructure at Stanford serving 40+ newsrooms. Agenda Watch scrapes municipal agendas across hundreds of agencies. DataTalk lets reporters query campaign finance data in plain English. The ICE contracts investigation -- finding sanctuary cities signing ICE deals -- shows cross-jurisdictional pattern detection in action. Essential for students interested in data journalism.

### Episode 6: Garance Burke -- A Journalist's Guide to Investigating Artificial Intelligence
**Date:** July 8, 2024 | **Length:** ~7,000 words

**Why it matters:** Burke inverts the frame: instead of using AI for journalism, she investigates AI as a journalist. Her AP work on child welfare algorithms prompted a DOJ investigation. Chatbot testing found AI giving voters incorrect election information. Her framework -- who built it, how it works, who benefits, who is harmed -- provides a practical methodology for investigating any AI system.

## MODULE 3: Trust, Ethics, and Audience Relationships

*The ethical infrastructure of AI in journalism: transparency, disclosure, bias, and the trust relationship with audiences.*

### Episode 7: Natali Helberger -- Audience-Centric Approaches to AI Ethics in Media
**Date:** July 24, 2024 | **Length:** ~7,400 words

**Why it matters:** The most research-grounded ethics discussion in the corpus. Helberger's quantitative findings -- 60% of Dutch respondents feel misled by undisclosed AI use, trust in news and social media approaching parity for AI -- ground the transparency debate in data. Her critique that "transparency without agency is meaningless" challenges the label-everything approach.

### Episode 8: Mattia Peretti -- Balancing AI Innovation with Journalistic Integrity
**Date:** May 1, 2024 | **Length:** ~7,400 words

**Why it matters:** Peretti, who leads JournalismAI at the LSE, articulates the consensus ethical framework: principles over prescriptive guidelines, the "why before how" orientation, and the parallel development triangle (literacy, experimentation, guidelines must develop together). His warning that strict guidelines communicate distrust of your team is the single most-cited ethical insight.

### Episode 9: Alessandro Alviani & Fabian Heckenberger -- How Germany's Suddeutsche Zeitung is Building AI Products That Audiences Can Trust
**Date:** January 10, 2026 | **Length:** ~9,200 words

**Why it matters:** The most operationally detailed ethics episode. SZ developed a consistent AI design language, expert-in-the-loop verification, live monitoring with sensitive keyword alerts, and a transparency blog. It shows what ethics looks like as infrastructure rather than as a document on a shelf. Alviani's "there is no done column" captures the reality of ongoing AI governance.

### Episode 9b: Vilas Dhar -- Why the Future of Journalism Is Still Human
**Date:** October 8, 2025 | **Length:** ~9,000 words

**Why it matters:** Dhar, president of the McGovern Foundation, provides the strongest philosophical grounding for human-centered AI ethics in the corpus. His three-part framework -- responsible data provenance, clear bright-line boundaries, and operational transparency -- bridges abstract principles and daily practice. His argument that AI "cannot understand vulnerability, pain, empathy, care, responsibility, or love" defends the irreplaceable human core from a funder's perspective rather than a practitioner's. His insistence that the journalism market is too small for Big Tech to serve -- "innovation must come from within journalism itself" -- reframes the industry's agency.

## MODULE 4: Organizational Strategy and Leadership

*How newsrooms structure teams, manage change, and make strategic decisions about AI deployment.*

### Episode 10: Kasper Lindskow -- Building a Scalable AI Infrastructure at Denmark's JP/Politikens Media Group
**Date:** March 18, 2025 | **Length:** ~12,900 words

**Why it matters:** The longest and most strategically dense episode. Lindskow addresses the central tension: "Even if the technology develops exponentially, rolling it out and adapting it is change management -- a very linear process." His research on recommender systems (identical metrics, different editorial effects) and his framework for balancing centralized AI infrastructure with local editorial hubs are essential for understanding organizational AI strategy.

### Episode 11: Fabian Heckenberger, Naja Nielsen & Gard Steiro -- The Hard Truths About AI Every Newsroom Leader Can't Ignore (Nordic AI in Media Summit)
**Date:** July 9, 2025 | **Length:** ~7,700 words (recorded live at Nordic AI in Media Summit)

**Why it matters:** Three senior newsroom leaders from VG, Schibsted, and Ippen debate the hardest questions: whether the article format is dying, whether human-in-the-loop is sustainable at scale, and whether existing business models can survive AI. Steiro's "my most important task as a leader is not to point direction but to foster a discussion" and Nielsen's "people are not in doubt that we are competent; they are in doubt about whether we want to do them well" are landmark statements. The live summit format captures real-time disagreement between leaders more candidly than studio interviews.

### Episode 12: Jim Friedlich, David Chivers & Matt Boggie -- How the Lenfest AI Collaborative Placed AI Engineers in 10 Newsrooms
**Date:** December 19, 2025 | **Length:** ~8,500 words

**Why it matters:** The most detailed account of collaborative AI infrastructure. The Lenfest model -- placing first-ever engineers in under-resourced newsrooms, with fellows reporting to editors not funders -- addresses the structural problem that local newsrooms cannot hire AI talent independently. The "person-and-a-half effect" of shared learning is a model for the industry.

### Episode 12b: Ludwig Siegele -- Inside The Economist's AI Playbook
**Date:** September 23, 2025 | **Length:** ~8,300 words

**Why it matters:** Siegele runs The Economist's AI lab as an internal startup of 8-10 people combining editorial and commercial staff. His account of the prototype-to-production gap -- "moving from AI prototype to production is much harder than building the prototype" -- is the most concrete articulation of the industry's central operational challenge. The Gutenberg-scribe analogy ("scribes had to reinvent themselves; the apprentice of Gutenberg was a scribe who became a printer") reframes the transformation narrative. His candid assessment that the personal investment to integrate AI into established workflows is "largely underestimated" explains why adoption stalls.

## MODULE 5: The Business of AI in Journalism

*Revenue models, platform dynamics, and the economic sustainability question.*

### Episode 13: Jeff Jarvis (Part Two) -- Rethinking the Journalism Business Model in the Age of AI
**Date:** February 1, 2024 | **Length:** ~5,600 words

**Why it matters:** Jarvis frames the existential economic challenge: "If we still think that we're valuable only because we make this thing called content, we are doomed." His distinction between training (fair use) and inference (licensable) provides a framework for the copyright debate. Provocative and foundational.

### Episode 14: Tav Klitgaard -- How Zetland Turned a Newsroom Problem into a Global AI Business
**Date:** December 15, 2025 | **Length:** ~7,600 words

**Why it matters:** The only complete business model success story in the corpus. Zetland built Good Tape from an internal transcription need, grew it to $3M revenue, and divested it. The insight that journalism's data-protection standards became a selling point ("If it's good enough for journalists, it's definitely good enough for me") suggests a path from newsroom tool to commercial product.

### Episode 15: Sara Beykpour -- The Next Chapter in News Aggregation
**Date:** August 14, 2025 | **Length:** ~9,900 words

**Why it matters:** Beykpour built Particle, an AI news aggregator designed around "agreed-upon facts from all sides." Her multi-source requirement (three articles, two publishers minimum) and LLM-as-judge "reality check" step represent a concrete technical approach to trust at scale. Her question -- "When an AI is ultimately going to do more reading than the humans, how do we monetize the AI's eyeball?" -- crystallizes the platform threat. The Tolbit content marketplace represents one of the first models for compensating publishers when AI uses their content for inference.

## MODULE 6: Education, Literacy, and the Future

*How journalists learn AI, how journalism schools should teach it, and what comes next.*

### Episode 16: Djordje Padejski -- Why AI Literacy Belongs at the Core of Journalism Education
**Date:** September 8, 2025 | **Length:** ~8,300 words

**Why it matters:** The most directly relevant episode for journalism students. Padejski at Stanford teaches AI as "a socio-technical phenomenon" requiring critical engagement. His approach -- teaching fundamentals over ephemeral tools, embedding AI across the curriculum rather than isolating it in one course, and the investigative proposal pedagogy -- provides a model for how you are being taught.

### Episode 17: Florent Daudens -- How Open-Source AI Puts Newsrooms Back in the Driver's Seat
**Date:** July 15, 2025 | **Length:** ~11,100 words

**Why it matters:** Daudens, who returned to Hugging Face after leading AI literacy at Radio Canada, quantifies the adoption divide (80/20) and advocates for open-source AI as a democratic alternative to concentrated platform power. His argument that shared prompt libraries outperform individual ChatGPT use, and that "prompting is the new literacy," directly addresses what journalism students need to learn.

### Episode 18: Francesco Marconi & Scott Austin -- 2025 Year in Review: What Actually Changed in AI and Media
**Date:** January 1, 2026 | **Length:** ~12,200 words

**Why it matters:** The capstone episode. Marconi and Austin synthesize two years of AI in journalism: the shift from shock (2023) to experimentation (2024) to "operational reckoning" (2025). Marconi's framing of newsrooms as "data gathering companies," his warning about MCP as "the USB-C for AI context," and his observation that "many agents are just GPT wrappers" provide the forward-looking framework for the next phase.

| Week | Module | Episodes | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Module 1 | Episodes 1-3 | Ground truth: what works and what does not |
| 3-4 | Module 2 | Episodes 4-6 | Investigation: AI for accountability |
| 5-6 | Module 3 | Episodes 7-9, 9b | Ethics: trust, transparency, bias |
| 7-8 | Module 4 | Episodes 10-12, 12b | Strategy: teams, leadership, collaboration |
| 9-10 | Module 5 | Episodes 13-15 | Economics: business models and platforms |
| 11-12 | Module 6 | Episodes 16-18 | Future: education, literacy, what comes next |

**Listening tip:** Each episode runs 30-50 minutes. Students should take notes on: (1) one surprising claim, (2) one point of agreement with previous episodes, and (3) one point of disagreement or tension.
