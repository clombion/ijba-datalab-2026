# Discussion Prompts: AI in Journalism

30 discussion questions organized by theme, designed for classroom use with IJBA data-driven journalism students. Each question includes a brief framing note and suggested format.

---

## THEME 1: THE AUTOMATION PARADOX -- What AI Replaces and What Remains

### Q1. The Headline Test
Martin Schori at Aftonbladet reported that AI-generated headlines consistently failed to match human editors. Yet Der Spiegel successfully automates SEO headlines for 150 articles daily. **Where is the line between tasks AI can automate and tasks that require editorial craft?** Map five newsroom tasks on a spectrum from "fully automatable" to "irreducibly human."
*Format: Small group exercise + class debrief. 15 min.*

### Q2. Experience as Multiplier
Tordecilla found that veteran reporters got more value from his AI audit tool because they could detect anomalies (overpriced government laptops) that junior reporters missed. **Does AI amplify existing expertise rather than replacing it? What does this mean for journalism students entering the field?**
*Format: Pair discussion. 10 min.*

### Q3. The Time Reinvestment Question
SZ's Alviani asks: "How do you reinvest the time savings?" iTromsoe's 55-minute daily time saving could go toward deeper reporting or could be absorbed by budget cuts. **If your newsroom saves 10 hours per week through AI, what should that time be used for? Who decides?**
*Format: Role-play exercise -- assign students roles as editor, reporter, finance director, AI lead. 20 min.*

### Q4. Displacement vs. Augmentation
Alessandro Alviani provided firsthand testimony of job losses at Microsoft News. But iTromsoe hired developers and the NYT created a new team. Dhar at the McGovern Foundation argues AI "cannot understand vulnerability, pain, empathy, care, responsibility, or love" -- yet CNN laid off hundreds while hiring for AI roles. **Is "augmentation not replacement" an accurate description of what is happening, or is it a comforting narrative that obscures real displacement?**
*Format: Structured debate. Two teams. 20 min.*

## THEME 2: TRUST, TRANSPARENCY, AND THE AUDIENCE

### Q5. The Disclosure Dilemma
Aftonbladet's Schori says audiences "don't really care" if content is AI-generated as long as it is accurate. Helberger's research says 60% feel misled by undisclosed AI use. **When should AI use be disclosed to audiences? Design a disclosure policy for a hypothetical newsroom.**
*Format: Small group policy-writing exercise. 20 min.*

### Q6. Trust as Competitive Advantage
Zacharison at the BBC says trust is the organization's strategic moat. Delfi achieved 21% digital subscriber penetration partly through high trust. **Can trust really function as a business strategy, or is it too intangible to monetize?**
*Format: Full-class discussion. 15 min.*

### Q7. The Book List Incident
Seven of ten AI-hallucinated books from Chicago Public Media went viral on Bluesky and Colbert's show. CEO Bell responded with radical transparency. **Is immediate public admission the right strategy for AI failures? Are there situations where a different response would be more appropriate?**
*Format: Case analysis. Students write a 200-word crisis response plan. 15 min.*

### Q8. Generational Divide
Youth told Schibsted researchers: "Your product makes us feel like shit." Meanwhile, older paying subscribers expect traditional factual accuracy. **How can a single news organization serve fundamentally different audience expectations? Should it even try?**
*Format: Pair discussion, then class synthesis. 15 min.*

## THEME 3: ETHICS IN PRACTICE

### Q9. Principles vs. Rules
Peretti warns that "too strict guidelines communicate to your team that you don't trust them." Zetland discovered an audio producer using AI voice cloning without authorization. **Are principles-based policies strong enough, or do newsrooms need specific rules? Draft three principles and three rules for AI use.**
*Format: Individual writing + group comparison. 15 min.*

### Q10. The Bias Mirror
Der Spiegel used AI to discover its own gender representation imbalance. Chua at Semafor built tools to detect what is missing from stories about marginalized communities. **Should newsrooms use AI to audit their own biases? What happens when the audit reveals uncomfortable truths?**
*Format: Full-class discussion. 15 min.*

### Q11. Human in the Loop -- or On the Loop?
VG's Steiro told the Nordic AI Summit: "If we still insist on humans in the loop for everything, we won't take the advantages of AI." He advocates multi-agent error-checking instead. SZ requires article authors to verify AI summaries. Beykpour at Particle uses an LLM-as-judge "reality check" step. **How much human oversight is enough? Draw a line between "necessary oversight" and "performance theater."**
*Format: Spectrum exercise -- students position themselves physically along a line from "always human in the loop" to "machine autonomy acceptable." 10 min.*

### Q12. The Environmental Blind Spot
Daudens at Hugging Face cites research showing each ChatGPT image uses the energy of a full iPhone charge. Reissmann questions whether user-facing AI at scale is "a good use of energy." Dhar urges newsrooms to "transform our social practices so that we can leverage the ways we use these tools to minimize the queries we need, but get much better outcomes." No newsroom in the corpus has operationalized environmental impact into its decisions. **Should environmental cost be a factor in AI deployment decisions? How would you measure it?**
*Format: Small group brainstorm. 15 min.*

## THEME 4: TECHNICAL DECISIONS WITH EDITORIAL CONSEQUENCES

### Q13. Why RAG Works
Every production system in the corpus uses retrieval-augmented generation -- grounding AI outputs in curated sources. Aftonbladet's election chatbot achieved zero hallucinations with nearly 100 sources. **Why is RAG the consensus approach? What are its limitations?** (Consider: What happens when the curated sources themselves are incomplete or biased?)
*Format: Technical walkthrough + discussion. 15 min.*

### Q14. Open Source vs. Proprietary
Daudens, now at Hugging Face, advocates for open-source AI as "fighting the concentration of power" -- a mission he continued from Radio Canada to the platform itself. Delfi mixes open-source and commercial models. Der Spiegel chose Azure for European data residency. Siegele at The Economist describes running multiple LLMs simultaneously rather than committing to one provider. **What are the editorial implications of choosing open-source vs. proprietary AI? When does the choice matter?**
*Format: Pro/con table exercise. 10 min.*

### Q15. The Hallucination Problem
Siegele found The Economist's style-guide checking was only 80% accurate -- unacceptable for publication. Schori reported headline generation consistently failing human standards. **If hallucination is an inherent feature rather than a bug to be fixed, what does this mean for using AI in journalism?**
*Format: Full-class Socratic discussion. 15 min.*

### Q16. Data Infrastructure First
Krustok at Delfi invested eight years in data foundations before AI became possible. Parrikh at Times of India built "banking-grade" data pipelines. **Why do practitioners insist that data infrastructure must precede AI deployment? What happens when newsrooms skip this step?**
*Format: Brief lecture + Q&A. 10 min.*

---

## THEME 5: ORGANIZING FOR AI

### Q17. Cross-Functional Teams
Alviani at Ippen placed editors as "co-creators" in a 10-person cross-functional team. Seward at the NYT requires journalism backgrounds for all AI team hires. Giske at iTromsoe has developers attending morning editorial meetings. **Why does editorial proximity matter for AI teams? Design the ideal 5-person AI team for a mid-sized newsroom.**
*Format: Small group design exercise. 15 min.*

### Q18. The Prioritization Battle
Zacharison says: "Kill this idea in its sleep." Schori describes constant tension between subscriber features, generative AI experiments, and new tools. **With limited resources, how should a newsroom decide which AI projects to pursue? Create a decision matrix.**
*Format: Small group exercise using impact-effort framework. 15 min.*

### Q19. Measuring What Matters
Heckenberger proposed three ROI baskets: subscriber retention, efficiency gains, and experimentation. Zacharison admits "negative ROI at this stage." **How should newsrooms measure the value of AI when the most important gains (better journalism, broader coverage) resist quantification?**
*Format: Pair discussion. 10 min.*

## THEME 6: POWER AND PLATFORMS

### Q20. The Scraping War
Siegele reports The Economist receives more visits from AI scrapers than human readers, and content appears in ChatGPT despite active blocking. Yet he argues "if these model makers need fresh content, they have to let us live" -- a symbiotic dependency. Franz describes Google's Gemini solving Google's own CAPTCHAs. Beykpour's Tolbit marketplace represents one attempt to replace scraping with compensation. **If technical defenses against scraping have failed, what options do publishers have? Is legislation the only remedy?**
*Format: Policy debate. 15 min.*

### Q21. The Right to Read
Jeff Jarvis asks: "Does the machine have the same right to read and learn and use information as a journalist?" Every journalist reads others' work without paying. **Is there a meaningful distinction between human learning from published work and machine learning from published work?**
*Format: Structured philosophical debate. Two teams. 20 min.*

### Q22. The Have-Nots
The Lenfest Collaborative exists because most local newsrooms cannot hire AI talent independently. Phillips's Big Local News serves 40+ organizations that lack resources for solo AI development. Dhar at the McGovern Foundation argues the journalism market is "not big enough to command its own market presence" -- no company will build bespoke AI for journalism "unless it comes from within journalism itself." **Is AI widening the gap between well-resourced and under-resourced newsrooms? What collaborative models could address this?**
*Format: Solution-design workshop. 20 min.*

---

## THEME 7: REIMAGINING THE NEWS PRODUCT

### Q23. The Death of the Article
Steiro told the Nordic AI Summit: "I started in journalism 25 years ago and I still write my stories the exactly the same way. So the article will die, should die. Storytelling won't die." Ippen offers reader format choice (text, bullet points, audio, easy language). Beykpour's Particle extracts agreed-upon facts from multiple sources into multi-perspective summaries. **If the article format is dying, what replaces it? Redesign a news story as a multi-format product.**
*Format: Creative design exercise. Students reimagine one current news story in 4 formats. 25 min.*

### Q24. Personalization vs. Editorialization
Lindskow's research shows two recommender systems with identical engagement metrics can produce radically different editorial effects -- one promoting tabloid content, one maintaining diversity. **Can algorithmic personalization coexist with editorial values? Where should editors override algorithms?**
*Format: Full-class discussion. 15 min.*

### Q25. Serving Non-Users
Stenbom tested products on 15-year-olds rather than existing subscribers. 30% of Nordic audiences actively avoid news. **Should newsrooms build products for people who currently reject their product? How do you test with audiences you do not have?**
*Format: Brainstorm session. 10 min.*

## THEME 8: JOURNALISM EDUCATION AND LITERACY

### Q26. The 80/20 Divide
Daudens, drawing on experience at both Radio Canada and Hugging Face, estimates 80% of newsroom staff are disengaged from AI while 20% are deeply engaged. Brown warns over 60% have never tried ChatGPT. Siegele adds that the personal workflow investment is "largely underestimated" -- journalists with ten-year habits resist integrating "this alien thing." **What would it take to move people from the 80% to the 20%? Design a three-month AI literacy program for a 50-person newsroom.**
*Format: Small group program design. 20 min.*

### Q27. Teaching AI to Journalists
Padejski teaches AI as "a socio-technical phenomenon." Phillips delays AI tool introduction until three-quarters through her curriculum. Seward says examples drive adoption more than training. **How should journalism schools teach AI? Write a syllabus outline for a one-semester course.**
*Format: Individual exercise + peer review. 20 min.*

### Q28. Vibe Coding and the Democratization of Tool-Building
Tordecilla built a government audit tool with custom GPTs in minutes. Franz describes journalists doing "vibe coding" -- building full-stack products without formal programming knowledge. **Does the ability to build tools without coding change what it means to be a journalist? Should journalism students learn to build AI tools?**
*Format: Pair discussion. 10 min.*

---

## THEME 9: LOOKING AHEAD

### Q29. The Agent-Mediated Future
Franz warns: "Your next reader is an agent." Marconi describes MCP as "the USB-C for AI context." If AI agents become primary consumers of news, publishers need structured data and API-accessible content. **How should newsrooms prepare for a future where their primary audience is not human? What changes today?**
*Format: Scenario planning exercise. 20 min.*

### Q30. Your AI Strategy
You are the new head of AI at a 100-person regional newsroom with a modest budget, a 15-year article archive, and a team of skeptical but curious journalists. Drawing on everything discussed in this course, **write a one-page AI strategy for your first year.** Prioritize three initiatives, explain your rationale, and identify the biggest risk.
*Format: Individual capstone exercise. 30 min. Can serve as a take-home assignment.*
