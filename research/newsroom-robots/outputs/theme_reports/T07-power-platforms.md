# Power, Money, and the Platform: Big Tech and the News Industry

## Overview

Theme T07 captures the political economy of AI in journalism -- the structural power asymmetries between technology companies and news publishers, the battles over training data and content licensing, the collapse of search-driven traffic models, and the collaborative responses emerging across the industry. This theme reveals a sector grappling with an adversary that is simultaneously its supplier, competitor, and potential business partner, producing a strategic ambivalence that runs through the entire two-year corpus.

## Prevalence

T07 surfaces as a primary or secondary theme in approximately 30 of the 42 episodes, making it one of the most pervasive concerns in the corpus. It appears as a primary theme in landmark episodes featuring Jeff Jarvis (Jan 2024), Ezra Eeman (Mar 2024), Mattia Peretti (May 2024), Garance Burke (Jul 2024), Anna Eshoo (Aug 2024), Ludwig Siegele (Sep 2025), Markus Franz (Nov 2025), the Lenfest collaborative (Dec 2025), and the year-in-review episode (Jan 2026). The codebook assigns 71 codes across four sub-themes: training data wars (25 codes), platform dependency and disintermediation (14 codes), power concentration and structural inequality (17 codes), and collaboration and collective action (15 codes).

## Key Findings

**1. The scraping war is already lost.** Multiple guests acknowledge that technical measures to block AI crawlers have failed. Ludwig Siegele reports that Economist content appears in ChatGPT despite active blocking, and Markus Franz describes Google's Gemini solving Google's own CAPTCHAs to access publisher sites. The industry's technical defenses are structurally inadequate against companies whose core competence is information retrieval.

**2. The licensing paradox deepens strategic confusion.** Publishers face a contradictory incentive structure: licensing content to AI companies provides short-term revenue but may accelerate the disintermediation that threatens long-term survival. Ezra Eeman articulated this paradox vividly: contributing quality journalism to improve AI models risks "digging your own grave." Jeff Jarvis proposed distinguishing training (fair use) from inference (licensable), but this framework remains untested legally.

**3. Traffic collapse from AI search is measurable and accelerating.** The Lenfest episode quantifies a 20-30% decline in Google-referred traffic from AI Overviews. Sara Beykpour frames the existential question: "When an AI is ultimately going to do more reading than the humans, how do we monetize the AI's eyeball?" The search-to-article revenue model, already fragile, faces structural obsolescence.

**4. News is a small market that Big Tech will not serve.** Vilas Dhar and Ritvvij Parrikh independently converge on a sobering reality: journalism's market size is insufficient to motivate bespoke AI development. Dhar states plainly that "there's not going to be some set of companies that are going to say we're going to innovate massive foundational changes in AI for journalism unless it comes from within journalism itself." This structural neglect forces the industry to build its own solutions.

**5. Collaborative responses are emerging but remain fragile.** The corpus documents meaningful collective action: the Norwegian national LLM initiative, Spiegel and Springer's shared press archive, the Lenfest Collaborative embedding AI engineers in ten newsrooms, the EBU coordination for European broadcasters, and Big Local News sharing infrastructure with 40+ organizations. Markus Franz frames the imperative: "The enemy is not publisher A or B. The enemy are the technology companies."

**6. The AI haves-and-have-nots divide is widening.** FT Strategies' sprint reveals a structural gap between well-resourced publishers who can negotiate enterprise AI deals and smaller organizations who cannot. Estonian publisher Ivar Krustok notes that small-market publishers cannot negotiate the same terms as large English-language organizations. This asymmetry maps onto existing global disparities, with the Global South facing both structural barriers and leapfrog potential.

**7. Content unit economics are approaching zero.** Francesco Marconi's year-in-review diagnosis is stark: as AI can extract facts from any published article and repackage them instantly, the marginal value of commodity content collapses. The Canadian publishers' lawsuit against OpenAI demonstrated that AI platforms can simply exclude publishers without meaningful loss, proving that news content is functionally replaceable in the AI ecosystem.

## Evolution Over Time

The discourse shows a clear arc from combative to pragmatic. Early 2024 episodes (Jeff Jarvis, Jan 2024) center on the NYT v. OpenAI lawsuit and fair-use doctrine, with litigation framed as the primary response. By mid-2024, Mattia Peretti acknowledges the tech-publisher relationship as "very, very tricky" while warning against naivety about tech companies' profit motives. By late 2025, the conversation shifts toward building internal capabilities (Lenfest, iTromsoe) and exploring partnership models like Google NotebookLM (Siegele) and Tolbit content marketplace (Beykpour). The year-in-review episode codifies this as the "embrace-and-defend dichotomy" -- simultaneously fighting for fair compensation and building on AI platforms.

## Consensus vs. Debate

There is near-universal agreement that AI companies should compensate publishers for content use. The debate centers on mechanism and strategy. Jeff Jarvis provocatively argues that machines should have the same "right to read" as human journalists, drawing criticism from those who see this as capitulation. The Economist's Siegele advocates partnerships over licensing, while Eshoo insists legislation is necessary. A quieter debate concerns whether open-source AI represents genuine power decentralization (Florent Daudens) or merely shifts dependency from one set of actors to another. The question of whether to collaborate with tech companies or resist them remains the single most divisive strategic question in the corpus.

## Concrete Examples

- **Spiegel + Springer Press Archive**: Joint digitization of all German journalism since the Federal Republic's founding, creating a shared competitive asset.
- **Lenfest AI Collaborative**: Microsoft and OpenAI-funded but editorially independent program placing AI engineers in ten under-resourced newsrooms, with fellows reporting to editors, not funders.
- **iTromsoe Fisheries Database**: A 25-person local newsroom using AI to analyze 5 million fishing transactions, producing investigative scoops that competed with publications twice its size.
- **Tolbit Content Marketplace**: First-of-kind exchange transaction compensating publishers when AI platforms use their content for inference, piloted with Time magazine.
- **Economist + Google NotebookLM**: A partnership providing content to NotebookLM in exchange for co-learning and partial control over content presentation -- Siegele calls it "give and take."

## Actionable Insights

- **Build data infrastructure as your competitive moat.** Proprietary archives, structured local data, and curated editorial corpora are the assets AI companies cannot replicate. Invest in making this data AI-accessible through vector databases and RAG architectures before negotiating any external partnerships.
- **Collaborate on technology, compete on content.** Join or create publisher consortia for shared AI infrastructure, model training, and engineering resources. The individual publisher is structurally outmatched; collective bargaining is the only viable counter to platform power.
- **Monitor AI-mediated traffic and plan for continued decline.** Track referral sources from ChatGPT, Perplexity, and AI Overviews. Develop non-traffic-dependent revenue models (memberships, events, data products) that are resilient to further disintermediation.
- **Negotiate from a position of informed strength.** Even organizations that buy rather than build AI must understand the technology deeply enough to evaluate vendor claims, negotiate terms, and avoid lock-in. Kasper Lindskow's advice: "You need to be able to ask that vendor the right questions and make the right demands."
- **Treat journalism innovation as internal R&D, not vendor procurement.** The market will not build bespoke AI for journalism. Invest in small, cross-functional teams that combine editorial expertise with engineering capability to build tools from the inside out.
