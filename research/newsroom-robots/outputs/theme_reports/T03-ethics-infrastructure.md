# The Ethics Infrastructure: From Principles on Paper to Practice in the Newsroom

## Overview

This theme tracks how newsrooms build, implement, and struggle with ethical frameworks for AI -- from high-level principles to granular procurement checklists, from bias mitigation to the persistent gap between stated values and actual behavior. It matters because ethics in AI journalism is not a solved problem declared at a conference podium; it is a daily negotiation conducted under deadline pressure, resource constraints, and technological uncertainty. The corpus reveals an industry that has converged on what the principles should be but remains far from operationalizing them consistently.

## Key Findings

1. **The industry has converged on principles over prescriptive guidelines.** Peretti's formulation is the clearest: "The risk with putting too strict guidelines is to communicate to your team that you don't trust them to know how to do the right thing." Klitgaard at Zetland framed it around trust ("you cannot trick people") rather than tools ("you cannot use ChatGPT"). Nearly every organization that discussed policy design rejected prescriptive rules in favor of durable principles that survive tool changes. Roy articulated the consensus framework: transparency, accuracy/oversight, privacy/data, and purpose-driven use.

2. **The guidelines-to-practice gap is the field's defining failure.** Helberger's survey found that nearly 20% of newsrooms adopt non-use as their AI ethics strategy -- avoidance masquerading as principle. Stenbom exposed that no publisher currently has a structured accountability framework for ChatGPT-produced content. Zetland's voice-cloning incident -- where an audio producer violated guidelines without leadership's knowledge -- illustrates how policies fail silently. Dhar warned that "restrictive policies drive experimentation outside organizational controls," causing data leakage.

3. **Human oversight is evolving from simple in-the-loop to a spectrum.** SZ introduced the "expert-in-the-loop" concept: article authors reviewing AI summaries, not generic human reviewers. Franz described a continuum from coordinator to consultant to observer. Steiro argued that human-in-the-loop for everything makes AI's advantages impossible and advocated multi-agent error-checking as a structural alternative. This evolution is the sharpest ethical fault line in the corpus -- the point where efficiency gains directly collide with accountability principles.

4. **Responsible procurement is the neglected ethical frontier.** Helberger found responsible procurement to be the least popular ethical approach among newsrooms surveyed. Yet it may be the most consequential: vendors dump liability on end users through terms of service, add unnecessary AI features at premium prices (Krustok/Delfi), and obscure their training data practices. Lindskow argued that "even if you buy, you should still make sure you really understand the technology well enough to ask the vendor the right questions." The procurement knowledge gap leaves small newsrooms especially vulnerable.

5. **Bias reveals itself in AI's mirror -- and journalism's historical failures.** Reissmann's gender report project elegantly inverted the bias narrative by using AI to reveal Spiegel's own coverage imbalance -- "now it's actually an LLM that's helping us discover our biases. I think that's quite beautiful." Padejski showed how culturally embedded biases differ between models (DeepSeek from Chinese society, ChatGPT from American). Chua built tools that identify not just factual errors but what is missing from stories about trans and immigrant communities. Jarvis argued that "all [AI] is playing back to us is our cliches and our biases and our misapprehensions about the world." The implication is that AI does not introduce new bias so much as make existing editorial biases visible and scalable.

6. **The "why before how" orientation has become the dominant ethical heuristic.** Peretti: "I wish there was more time spent on a step before thinking, what are we trying to do?" Reissmann: "Maybe the most obvious use cases of generative AI are not the ones we should go after." Roy: "It's really important to know the why we're using AI before even getting to the how." Gautam: "Not every inefficiency warrants an AI solution." This consensus -- asking whether AI is the right tool before asking how to use it -- functions as the practical ethical checkpoint that survives across organizational contexts.

7. **Environmental sustainability is the missing dimension.** Helberger noted its absence from her newsroom surveys despite its prominence in public discourse. Daudens cited Sasha Luccioni's research showing each ChatGPT image uses the energy of a full iPhone charge. Dhar raised AI's water and energy footprint. Reissmann questioned whether user-facing AI products at scale represent a "good use of energy right now." Yet no organization in the corpus has operationalized environmental impact into its AI decision framework.

## Evolution Over Time

**Early 2024:** Ethics were discussed as design principles embedded in project planning. The FT Strategies sprint included a dedicated responsible AI session. Alviani at Ippen represented the most mature early example: guardrails established before development begins, illustration-only image policy, accountability placed on humans rather than algorithms.

**Mid 2024:** The field shifted from principles to measurement. Helberger's empirical research quantified the guidelines-to-practice gap. Burke advocated journalistic basics (who/what/where/when/why) as an ethical framework for AI coverage. Peretti introduced the triangle model: literacy, experimentation, and guidelines must develop in parallel, or none succeeds.

**Late 2024 - Early 2025:** Ethics became more nuanced. Stenbom introduced the FAST framework (Fairness, Accountability, Sustainability, Transparency). Dhar offered a three-part operational framework: responsible data, clear boundaries, transparency. The Axel Springer mandate ("you only have to explain if you are not using AI") inverted the default, sparking debate about whether this is progressive or reckless.

**Late 2025 - Jan 2026:** Ethics operationalized into products and workflows. SZ's expert-in-the-loop, live monitoring with sensitive keyword alerts, and consistent AI design language represented ethics embedded in systems rather than stated in documents. Chicago Public Media's book list incident demonstrated that ethics policies often emerge from failures rather than foresight. Padejski's course at Stanford taught AI as a "socio-technical phenomenon" requiring critical engagement, not just tool use.

## Consensus vs. Debate

**Consensus:** Principles over prescriptive rules. Human accountability for AI outputs, not algorithm blame. The "why before how" orientation. AI-generated content without human review should not be published. Bias is a real concern that AI can both amplify and reveal.

**Debate:** Whether non-use is a legitimate ethical strategy or avoidance. How far the human-on-the-loop model can extend before accountability erodes. Whether the Axel Springer "explain if not using" mandate represents dangerous normalization. Whether environmental cost should be a factor in AI deployment decisions.

**Unresolved:** How to operationalize responsible procurement at scale, especially for small newsrooms. How to maintain accountability when AI systems are opaque even to their builders (Burke: "even the engineers can't explain what's happening under the hood"). Whether copyright and training data ethics should be a newsroom concern or an industry-level fight. How to prevent the ethics-practice gap from widening as AI tools become more powerful and more embedded.

## Concrete Examples

- **Ippen Digital/SZ (Germany):** Guardrails before development, illustration-only image policy, expert-in-the-loop for summaries, live monitoring with sensitive keyword alerts, consistent AI design language across products.
- **Der Spiegel (Germany):** Used AI to audit its own gender representation bias across 100,000 articles -- turning the bias tool against institutional blind spots rather than just external content.
- **Zetland (Denmark):** Voice-cloning guideline violation discovered after the fact, leading to clearer principles. Refused to clone journalist voices for audience-facing translation to preserve the journalist-audience bond.
- **Chicago Public Media (USA):** AI-generated book list incident led to immediate policy creation. "Guardrails against content generation" became the organization's defining ethical stance.
- **Semafor (USA):** Chua built tools addressing representational harm: trans misinformation/misframing bot identifies factual errors and what is missing from stories, applicable to immigration and other underserved topics.
- **Dhar/Patrick J. McGovern Foundation:** LISA framework (Listen, Involve, Share, Assess) as a stakeholder-centered ethical design process. Bright-line rules about what AI will never be used for (synthetic quotes, unattributed sources).

## Actionable Insights

- **Write principles that survive tool changes.** Frame AI policy around journalistic values ("you cannot trick the audience") rather than specific tools ("you cannot use ChatGPT"). Klitgaard's approach ensures policies remain relevant as technology evolves.
- **Develop a procurement checklist before adopting any vendor.** Helberger's finding that responsible procurement is the least popular but potentially most consequential ethical practice should be a wake-up call. Ask vendors: What data trained this model? Who is liable for errors? What happens to our data?
- **Use AI to audit your own biases, not just external content.** Spiegel's gender report and Chua's misframing bot demonstrate that AI can reveal editorial blind spots that decades of human review missed.
- **Expect the ethics-practice gap and design for it.** Zetland's voice-cloning incident shows that even well-intentioned organizations will have policy violations. Build monitoring systems that catch violations quickly rather than assuming compliance.
- **Treat environmental cost as an ethical variable.** No organization in the corpus has done this operationally, creating an opportunity for early movers to differentiate. At minimum, factor energy consumption into build-vs-buy decisions for AI tools at audience scale.
