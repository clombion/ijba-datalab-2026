# Organizing for AI: Leadership, Strategy, and Institutional Transformation

## Overview

This theme examines how news organizations structure, lead, and manage AI integration at the institutional level -- the leadership signals, team configurations, strategic frameworks, prioritization disciplines, change management approaches, and ROI measurement challenges that determine whether AI transforms an organization or remains a collection of disconnected experiments. It matters because the technical and editorial possibilities of AI are meaningless without organizational capacity to adopt, scale, and sustain them. Across the corpus, a consistent finding emerges: AI is not primarily a technology problem but a leadership, culture, and organizational design problem.

## Prevalence

T06 appears as a primary or secondary theme in approximately 38 of 42 episodes. It dominates episodes featuring senior leaders and strategists: Steiro at VG/Schibsted (Jul 2025), Zacharison at BBC/Swedish Radio (Nov 2025), Siegele at The Economist (Sep 2025), Reissmann at Der Spiegel (Jun 2024), Schori at Aftonbladet (Sep 2024), Lindskow at JP/Politikens (Mar 2025), Parrikh at Times of India (Oct 2024), Eeman at NPO (Mar 2024), Gautam at CNN (Dec 2024), Stenbom at Schibsted (Nov 2024), Chua at Semafor (Jun 2025), Bell at Chicago Public Media (Jan 2026), and both Alviani episodes at Ippen/SZ. The FT Strategies sprint episodes (Jan 2024) and the Nordic Summit (Jul 2025) are fundamentally organizational strategy discussions. Only narrow-scope episodes (Gill on image generation, Tordecilla on custom GPTs) treat it tangentially.

## Key Findings

**1. "Why before how" is the consensus strategic principle.** Peretti at JournalismAI declared: "I wish that sometimes there was more time spent on a step before thinking, okay, what are we trying to do?" Roy articulated it explicitly: "It's really important to be able to know the why we're using AI before even getting to the how." Reissmann at Spiegel chose foundational infrastructure (vector database) over flashy consumer-facing products because it served the mission. Gautam warned against "shiny object syndrome." The finding is consistent: organizations that start with the editorial problem outperform those that start with the technology.

**2. Cross-functional team structures outperform siloed AI units.** Alviani at Ippen described editors as "co-creators, not just users" within a 10-person cross-functional team. Seward at NYT staffed a five-person team where every member has journalism experience. Lindskow at JP/Politikens balances centralized AI infrastructure with local editorial hubs. Giske at iTromsoe embedded developers in the newsroom, attending morning editorial meetings. The Economist's AI lab learned from its 2007 Project Red Stripe failure -- an isolated innovation unit that produced nothing. The pattern: AI teams must be close enough to editorial to understand real problems, but distinct enough to maintain technical focus.

**3. Leadership signaling is the single most powerful adoption catalyst.** Klitgaard at Zetland described the CEO's "stop everything, allocate all resources" moment. Giske's editor-in-chief designated an AI-supported project as the top priority for six months. Zacharison found that the BBC editor's support was essential for institutional momentum. Steiro at VG argued a leader's most important task "is not to point direction, but to foster a discussion within the organization." Conversely, Daudens noted that when leadership fails to create space for experimentation, 80% of staff remain disengaged. The finding: executive sponsorship is a precondition, not a bonus.

**4. Change management is linear even when technology is exponential.** Lindskow articulated this directly: "Even if the technology develops exponentially, rolling it out and adapting it -- that's change management. That's a very linear process." Gautam described CNN's nearly year-long onboarding process for a new platform. Siegele found that 10-year work habits resist even obviously superior tools. The industry-wide pattern of shock (2023) to experimentation (2024) to operational reckoning (2025) reflects this linearity. Organizations that respect the human pace of adaptation -- rather than demanding immediate transformation -- achieve more durable change.

**5. Prioritization discipline separates successful organizations from struggling ones.** Zacharison at the BBC advocated ruthless triage: "Just because it's an idea doesn't mean it's a good idea. Kill this idea in its sleep." Schori at Aftonbladet described the "prioritization battle" between subscriber features, generative AI experiments, and new tools. Heckenberger at SZ proposed a three-basket ROI measurement (subscriber retention, efficiency gains, experimentation). Reissmann managed approximately 10 concurrent projects with three annual innovation cycles. The consistent finding: the scarcest resource is not technology or budget but organizational attention.

**6. Historical analogies frame the stakes but also risk complacency.** Jarvis invoked radio and television as precedents for protectionist industry responses. Siegele used the Gutenberg-scribe analogy. Bell at Chicago Public Media drew the explicit lesson: "The journalism industry had mixed results on adapting to the internet's disruption and missed a lot of opportunity." Stenbom cited Schibsted's institutional "muscle memory" for risk-taking dating to the 1800s. The analogies serve as motivational warnings, but several guests cautioned against false equivalences -- AI's impact on newsrooms may not follow prior disruption patterns.

**7. The innovation lab model is evolving toward integration.** Schori at Aftonbladet created the AI hub because the central product team deprioritized newsroom AI ideas -- the hub had to be editorially led. Siegele at The Economist built an 8-10 person lab separate from but not isolated from the newsroom, learning from past isolation failures. Stenbom at Schibsted described InLab's dedicated teams with short mandates. Franz at Ippen separated innovation and editorial AI teams. Pilhofer at Chicago Public Media rejected a separate AI roadmap entirely: "We have a product roadmap of which AI is a part." The trend is toward embedding AI capability within editorial operations rather than housing it in standalone units.

**8. Measuring AI ROI remains the industry's unsolved problem.** Heckenberger proposed three baskets: subscriber retention, efficiency gains, and a dedicated experimentation budget. Schori acknowledged the difficulty of defining KPIs for experimental AI projects. Reissmann questioned whether deploying user-facing AI at scale is even a good use of energy. Zacharison noted "negative ROI at this stage" is the reality for most AI investments. The honest finding: almost no newsroom can demonstrate positive financial returns from AI, and the metrics that matter (editorial quality, audience habit formation, institutional learning) resist quantification.

## Evolution Over Time

The organizational conversation evolved markedly across the two-year period. In early 2024, the FT Strategies sprint represented the state of the art: structured design thinking applied to AI ideation, with publishers still asking whether and how to begin. By mid-2024, concrete organizational models emerged (Ippen's editor-centric approach, Spiegel's Head of AI role, Aftonbladet's AI hub). Through 2025, the discussion matured toward systemic questions: Lindskow on balancing centralized infrastructure with local autonomy, the Nordic Summit leaders debating scenario planning versus roadmaps, Seward describing a deliberately slower approach favoring long-term ambition. By late 2025 and early 2026, the discourse shifted to sustainability and institutional reckoning: Marconi framing newsrooms as data companies, Chua calling to "burn it to the studs," and Franz warning that the publishing industry's blindness to agentic disruption mirrors past failures. The question evolved from "how do we organize for AI?" to "how do we organize for continuous transformation?"

## Consensus vs. Debate

**Consensus exists on:** The necessity of cross-functional teams; the why-before-how principle; leadership sponsorship as a precondition; the importance of experimentation culture; the persistence of change management challenges regardless of technology speed; and the inadequacy of existing ROI measurement approaches.

**Debate persists on:** Centralized versus decentralized AI organization (JP/Politikens advocates a central unit; Stenbom advocates local hubs with short mandates). Build versus buy for core editorial functions (Lindskow: build core, buy generic; Pilhofer: buy when vendors already solve the problem). Whether AI should have a separate strategic track or be fully integrated into the product roadmap. Whether incremental optimization or radical reinvention is the appropriate strategic posture (Chua: "burn it to the studs"; Siegele: "current efforts are still very incremental"). Whether newsrooms should aspire to be technology companies or resist that identity.

## Concrete Examples

**Aftonbladet's AI hub:** Created because central product/tech teams deprioritized newsroom AI ideas. Editorially led, staffed by majority editorial people, reporting directly to the editor-in-chief. Physical proximity to the newsroom enabled identification of real pain points. Managed the prioritization battle by convincing management to greenlight the election chatbot through clear editorial value propositions.

**The Economist's AI lab:** An 8-10 person internal startup with a technical lead, design lead, product lead, and developers. Deliberately separate but not isolated, learning from the 2007 Project Red Stripe failure. The editor-in-chief (Zanny Minton Beddoes) is "even more gung-ho" than the AI lead. Built experimentation infrastructure (80-entry internal competition) rather than dictating top-down adoption.

**Lenfest AI Collaborative:** Placed AI fellows as first-ever engineers in 10 newsrooms. Fellows report to newsrooms, not funders, ensuring platform independence. The cohort's peer learning created a "person-and-a-half effect" where shared knowledge multiplied individual capacity. The model addresses the structural problem that under-resourced newsrooms cannot hire AI talent independently.

**NYT's five-person team:** Three strategic pillars (investigations, internal workflows, reader experiences). Built general-purpose tools (Echo) that let desks self-serve rather than managing bespoke projects. Deliberately slower on CMS integration than competitors, prioritizing long-term architectural ambition. Reached half of 2,000 journalists through approximately 40 desk-specific presentations.

## Actionable Insights

1. **Start with editorial problems, not AI capabilities.** Conduct structured discovery sessions (Alviani's product discovery interviews, Zacharison's 1,224-idea survey) to identify genuine pain points before evaluating technology solutions. Every AI initiative should answer the question: what editorial or audience problem does this solve?

2. **Design cross-functional teams with editorial proximity.** Place developers in the newsroom, not a separate floor. Ensure AI team members have journalism experience or are deeply immersed in editorial culture. Make editors co-creators of AI tools, not passive recipients.

3. **Build prioritization discipline as an organizational muscle.** Use frameworks like impact-effort matrices, time-boxed experimentation with clear kill criteria, and explicit ROI baskets. Accept that most experiments will fail, but require each to teach something transferable.

4. **Signal leadership commitment through visible action.** Allocate resources publicly, participate in training personally, discuss failures openly, and protect experimentation time from production pressure. The CEO or editor-in-chief's behavior sets the organizational default.

5. **Plan for linear change management within exponential technology cycles.** Budget 12-18 months for meaningful organizational adoption. Build continuous learning infrastructure (biweekly sessions, Slack channels, peer ambassadors) rather than one-time training events. Accept that habits change slowly and design tools that minimize behavioral change required.
