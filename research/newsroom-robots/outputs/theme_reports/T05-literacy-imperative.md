# The Literacy Imperative: Upskilling the Newsroom for an AI Era

## Overview

This theme addresses how journalists and newsroom staff learn about, adopt, resist, or engage with AI. It encompasses formal training programs, peer learning communities, experimentation culture, no-code empowerment, adoption barriers, and the widening gap between AI-engaged and AI-disengaged staff. The theme matters because technology alone changes nothing -- organizational learning determines whether AI tools sit unused or transform workflows. Across the corpus, the literacy imperative emerges not as a nice-to-have but as a survival condition: newsrooms that fail to build AI competence risk repeating the industry's catastrophic failure to adapt to the internet.

## Prevalence

T05 is one of the most pervasive themes in the corpus, appearing as primary or secondary in approximately 38 of 42 episodes. It surfaces as a primary theme in episodes featuring training practitioners (Daudens at Radio Canada, Padejski at Stanford, Phillips at Stanford/Big Local News), organizational AI leaders (Schori at Aftonbladet, Reissmann at Der Spiegel, Lindskow at JP/Politikens, Zacharison at BBC/Swedish Radio), product-focused guests (Tordecilla, Seward at NYT), and collaborative models (Lenfest, Alviani at SZ). Only the most policy-oriented or business-model-focused episodes (Beykpour on Particle, Eshoo on policy) treat it tangentially. Guests span continents and organizational sizes, from 25-person iTromsoe to the 2,000-journalist NYT newsroom.

## Key Findings

**1. The two-tier adoption divide is the industry's defining literacy challenge.** Daudens quantified it at 80/20: roughly 80% of newsroom staff are disengaged from AI, while 20% are deeply engaged. Roy reported that 40% of surveyed journalists had never tried ChatGPT; Marconi found 38% of early-career journalists had never used it and 50% were against it. Neil Brown warned that over 60% of newsroom staff have never tried ChatGPT, "risking a repeat of the print-to-digital failure." The divide is not merely about skill -- it reflects time pressure, cultural resistance, fear, and inadequate organizational support.

**2. Access to tools does not drive adoption; relatable examples do.** Seward at the NYT articulated this clearly: "Access to tools is really not the thing that unlocks people... It's examples that resonate with them." SZ's science department editor built a custom assistant that generated 30+ colleague adoption requests in two hours through peer demonstration. Aftonbladet's Prompt Queen masterclass succeeded because it was practical and proximity-based. The consistent finding is that journalists adopt AI when they see colleagues solving real problems, not when they receive top-down directives.

**3. Structured training programs vary widely but share common elements.** Radio Canada developed a three-tier program (expertise group, three-hour training sessions, prompt clinics). Spiegel trained 870+ employees with consistently oversubscribed sessions. Swedish Radio surveyed 1,400 staff and collected 1,224 AI ideas. Schibsted's Shift AI Academy offers role-specific learning tracks. SZ achieved nearly 50% newsroom adoption via Langdog. The common threads: hands-on experimentation over lectures, iterative short sessions over one-off workshops, and linking training directly to editorial pain points.

**4. Peer learning outperforms top-down evangelism.** Krustok at Delfi pivoted from top-down generic training to peer-driven idea sharing after finding the initial approach insufficient. Giske and Ytreberg paired individual journalists with developers in mini-workshops. The Lenfest fellows formed a self-organized biweekly working group. Daudens advocated shared prompt libraries over individual ChatGPT use, which he called "artisanal and not very effective." Proximity -- sitting in the newsroom, attending morning meetings, overhearing editorial frustrations -- consistently drives adoption more effectively than presentations.

**5. No-code tools are transforming who can build.** Tordecilla built a Philippine government audit tool with custom GPTs in minutes -- work that previously required "thousands of hours of ML training." Giske's Kotemon enables journalists to build RAG applications without coding. Franz described vibe coding as enabling "non-specialists to build full-stack products." Phillips's DART Matrix provides story recipes for rural newsrooms with zero data experts. The democratization of tool-building is shifting journalists from consumers to creators of AI applications.

**6. Journalism education faces a structural AI literacy gap.** Padejski at Stanford teaches AI as a "socio-technical phenomenon" requiring critical engagement, not just tool use. He structures courses around fundamentals rather than ephemeral tools: "I cannot just teach them how to use NotebookLM today, but there's going to be a different NotebookLM in five, ten years." Phillips delays AI tool introduction until three-quarters through her curriculum, building coding fundamentals first. Marconi identified a "journalism school penalty effect" where graduates emerge AI-averse. The consensus: education must embed AI across the entire curriculum, not isolate it in a single course.

**7. Production pressure is the greatest barrier to experimentation.** Siegele at The Economist noted that "the barrier to entry... is largely underestimated. Especially journalists, once you've worked for ten years in a certain way." Workflow integration friction, time scarcity, option overload, and the fear of looking foolish combine to paralyze adoption. Zacharison found that even with CMS-integrated tools, the Aftonbladet summary feature required constant reminders. The implication is clear: literacy programs must reduce friction to near-zero and embed AI tools directly into existing workflows.

**8. Experimentation culture requires organizational permission and psychological safety.** Stenbom at Schibsted advocated forums where employees can openly discuss AI risks and concerns rather than suppressing skepticism. Klitgaard at Zetland framed early practical exposure as the antidote to fear. Zacharison noted the "innovation label" grants permission for experiments that would face more scrutiny in regular operations. Dhar warned that overly restrictive policies push experimentation outside organizational controls, creating data leakage risks. The finding: permission to fail is not a platitude but a structural requirement.

## Evolution Over Time

In early 2024, literacy discussions centered on the novelty of generative AI and the need for basic awareness (FT Strategies sprint, Alviani's internal academy launched the day before ChatGPT). By mid-2024, structured programs proliferated (Radio Canada, Spiegel, Aftonbladet), and the adoption divide became visible. Through 2025, the conversation matured: Seward identified examples over access as the adoption key; Lindskow distinguished between building for AI front-runners versus average journalists; Phillips and Padejski grounded literacy in educational pedagogy. By late 2025 and into 2026, the discussion shifted toward systemic adoption infrastructure -- Langdog as a platform, the Lenfest collaborative as a model, and the recognition that change management "is a very linear process" (Lindskow) even when technology evolves exponentially. The question evolved from "how do we train people?" to "how do we build organizations that learn continuously?"

## Consensus vs. Debate

**Consensus exists on:** The urgency of AI literacy as a survival condition; the inadequacy of one-off training sessions; the superiority of hands-on experimentation over lectures; the two-tier divide as a real and growing problem; and the need for workflow-integrated tools rather than separate platforms.

**Debate persists on:** Whether all journalists need prompting skills (Lindskow: "we shouldn't make all journalists prompt engineers"; Daudens: prompting as "new literacy"). Whether training should precede tool deployment or follow it (Phillips: fundamentals first; Stenbom: early exposure removes fear). Whether AI literacy means understanding the technology or simply using it effectively. Whether journalism schools should ban, restrict, or fully embrace AI in coursework.

## Concrete Examples

**Radio Canada's three-tier program:** Daudens built an expertise group for strategy, three-hour training sessions reaching hundreds of journalists (shifting attitudes from "garbage tsunami" to "opportunity tsunami"), and prompt clinics producing working tools from daily editorial pain points.

**Aftonbladet's Prompt Queen:** Schori's company-wide masterclass trained every employee. The AI buffet of 25-30 simple prompt-wrapper tools lowered the threshold. A journalist with no programming background built the Budreader tool independently.

**NYT's example-driven adoption:** Seward's five-person team reached half of 2,000 journalists through approximately 40 desk-specific presentations. Published investigations using AI (Election Integrity Network analysis of 500 hours of leaked recordings) served as the most powerful adoption catalyst -- journalists adopted tools when they saw investigations succeed.

**SZ's cross-departmental contagion:** One science editor's custom assistant generated 30+ colleague requests in two hours, demonstrating that bottom-up peer-to-peer demonstration scales faster than top-down training mandates.

## Actionable Insights

1. **Invest in relatable examples, not abstract training.** Document and share concrete cases of AI solving real editorial problems within your organization. Published investigations, time-savings data, and peer demonstrations drive adoption more effectively than workshops.

2. **Build AI into existing workflows, not alongside them.** Journalists will not switch to separate platforms. Integrate tools into the CMS, Google Docs, Slack, or whatever journalists already use daily. The two-click principle (Siegele's Google Docs extension) should be the design target.

3. **Create tiered literacy paths, not universal mandates.** Not everyone needs to be a prompt engineer. Distinguish between tool users (need button-and-dropdown UX), power users (comfortable with chat interfaces), and builders (create custom tools). Design training and tools for each tier.

4. **Formalize peer learning structures.** Slack channels, biweekly show-and-tell sessions, AI wins-and-fails sharing, embedded fellows, and prompt libraries create organic adoption networks that outlast any training program.

5. **Embed AI across journalism education curricula.** Do not isolate AI in a single course. Teach fundamentals (how models work, what they cannot do) and build critical evaluation skills before introducing specific tools. Assess students on their ability to evaluate AI output, not generate it.
