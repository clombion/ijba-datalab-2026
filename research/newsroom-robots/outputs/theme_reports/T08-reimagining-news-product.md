# Reimagining the News Product: Personalization, Formats, and the Experience Layer

## Overview

Theme T08 examines how AI enables fundamentally new ways of packaging, delivering, and experiencing news -- from personalization engines and conversational interfaces to format multiplication and visual AI. The theme traces a strategic shift in the industry's self-conception: from organizations that produce content to organizations that design experiences. Across the corpus, the argument builds that the competitive frontier is not what news organizations know but how they deliver what they know, and that AI's most transformative potential lies not in generating content but in transforming the relationship between information and audience.

## Prevalence

T08 is coded as a primary or secondary theme in approximately 28 of 42 episodes. It appears as a primary theme in episodes featuring Ezra Eeman (Mar 2024), Der Spiegel's Ole Reissmann (Jun 2024), Adrian Gill (May 2024), Aftonbladet's Martin Schori (Sep 2024), Times of India's Ritvvij Parrikh (Oct 2024), Agnes Stenbom (Nov 2024), JP/Politikens' Kasper Lindskow (Mar 2025), Sara Beykpour/Particle (Aug 2025), Gina Chua/Semafor (Jun 2025), Markus Franz/Ippen (Nov 2025), and the SZ episode (Jan 2026). The codebook contains 61 codes across four sub-themes: personalization and recommendation (23 codes), conversational and voice interfaces (7 codes), format innovation and content multiplication (21 codes), and AI-generated visuals (10 codes).

## Key Findings

**1. Personalization is the single most desired AI application among publishers.** FT Strategies' design sprint in early 2024 found that content reformatting and personalization were the most popular use case categories, comprising roughly 70% of publisher ideas. By late 2025, multiple organizations have deployed functional systems: Ekstra Bladet's front-page recommender, the Times of India's Signals system (85% CTR increase), Aftonbladet's AI summaries (40% expansion rate), and Ippen Digital's reader format choice feature.

**2. The article format is declared dying -- but storytelling persists.** Multiple leaders make the provocative claim that the traditional article is obsolete. Gard Steiro states flatly: "The article will die, should die. Storytelling won't die." Fabian Heckenberger concurs: "News articles are maybe already dead." The replacement is not a single format but a multiplicity: summaries, bullet points, audio versions, video recaps, easy-language adaptations, chatbot interactions, and push notifications -- all generated from the same underlying information.

**3. Experience ownership may matter more than content ownership.** Nikita Roy articulates the emerging thesis: "The future of media and journalism isn't just about owning the content, it's about owning the experience. It's about the inference, the interaction, the integrations that you bring into your experience that might end up being more valuable than even page views were." This represents a strategic pivot from content-as-product to experience-as-product, with AI enabling the transformation layer.

**4. Conversational interfaces show promise but face monetization barriers.** Aftonbladet's EU election chatbot received 150,000+ questions and achieved a tenfold login conversion rate. Swedish Radio's conversational news service saw 80% of users pressing pre-suggested questions rather than typing their own. However, Delfi's Ivar Krustok reports that audience-facing AI chat "is not profitable" because it replaces page views without matching ad revenue. The format works editorially but breaks existing business models.

**5. The editorial-algorithm tension remains unresolved.** Kasper Lindskow's research at JP/Politikens reveals that two recommender systems with identical engagement metrics can produce radically different effects on news flow -- one promoting tabloid content, the other maintaining editorial diversity. This finding challenges the assumption that personalization can be evaluated purely on engagement. Editorial constraints on recommendation algorithms are essential but difficult to calibrate.

**6. Format innovation targets underserved audiences.** Several product innovations explicitly serve previously unreachable audiences: SZ's easy-language articles make journalism accessible to immigrants and readers with cognitive disabilities; Ippen's WhatsApp channels deliver hyper-local news to communities without traditional news habits; Schibsted's News is Music experiment reached young people who never read articles; and Aftonbladet's text-to-speech serves immigrant communities in their languages.

**7. AI-generated visuals are constrained by editorial policy.** There is strong consensus that AI image generation must be limited to illustration styles, never photorealism. Ippen, SZ, and multiple other organizations enforce this as a red line. Adrian Gill articulates the creative professional's position: "It's important for my work not to let AI be visible. You can leverage AI, but at the end of the day, your own vision has to be front and foremost."

## Evolution Over Time

The trajectory moves from aspiration to deployment. Early 2024 episodes discuss personalization and format innovation as theoretical possibilities. By mid-2024, Aftonbladet's election chatbot and Ippen's reader format choice feature demonstrate working products. Through 2025, the conversation shifts to measuring impact (Lindskow's recommender research), addressing monetization challenges (Krustok's unprofitable chat), and confronting the agent-mediated future (Franz's "your next reader is an agent"). The late 2025 and early 2026 episodes reflect a maturing understanding: product innovation must be tied to business models and editorial values simultaneously.

## Consensus vs. Debate

Broad consensus exists that news organizations must move beyond the article format and offer audiences flexible, personalized experiences. The debate centers on how far to cede editorial control. Helberger's finding that transparency without user agency is "meaningless" pushes toward more audience autonomy; editorial leaders like Lindskow and Stenbom worry about echo chambers and the distortion of public discourse. The question of whether AI should curate what audiences see (algorithmic recommendation) or let audiences choose their own experience (format selection, conversational querying) divides practitioners. A secondary debate concerns whether product innovation should focus on existing loyal subscribers or target news avoiders and non-users -- with Stenbom's InLab explicitly testing products on 15-year-olds rather than existing paying readers.

## Concrete Examples

- **Aftonbladet EU Election Chatbot**: RAG-based chatbot drawing from nearly 100 sources, achieving zero detected hallucinations, 150,000+ questions, and tenfold login conversion. Moved from human-in-the-loop to human-on-the-loop oversight.
- **Times of India Signals**: Self-learning recommender system that increased CTR by 85%, recirculates 50% of page views from articles older than two days, and operates with minimal data points per story.
- **Ippen/SZ Easy Language Service**: AI-powered slider between standard German and simplified language, making journalism accessible to non-native speakers and readers with cognitive disabilities.
- **Schibsted News is Music**: Articles transformed into AI-generated rap songs for young audiences. Stenbom: "I remember word by word every article that we did last year because it was music."
- **BBC My Club Daily**: Synthetic voice podcast delivering personalized news summaries, representing the convergence of voice interface and format multiplication.
- **Particle**: AI-powered news aggregation with multi-source summaries, bias meter, "opposite sides" feature, and in-story Q&A -- a ground-up reimagination of the news product form.

## Actionable Insights

- **Invest in format multiplication before personalization algorithms.** The most widely successful innovations let readers choose their format (bullet points, audio, simple language) without requiring sophisticated recommendation engines. This is lower risk and delivers immediate audience value.
- **Constrain recommendation systems editorially, not just technically.** Engagement-optimized algorithms distort news flow. Build editorial guardrails (topic caps, diversity metrics, sensitive content exclusions) into recommender systems from the start, not as an afterthought.
- **Design for the agent-mediated future now.** If AI agents become the primary consumers of news content, publishers need structured data, metadata-rich archives, and API-accessible content. The shift from optimizing for search ranking to optimizing for AI inference is already underway.
- **Test new products on non-users, not loyal subscribers.** Stenbom's lesson is counterintuitive but critical: existing power users will always prefer the status quo. Products designed to reach news avoiders must be tested on the audiences they aim to serve.
- **Solve the monetization gap before scaling conversational products.** Election chatbots and Q&A interfaces drive engagement and logins but do not yet generate sustainable revenue. Develop business models (subscription funnels, premium tiers, sponsored interactions) before committing to audience-facing AI at scale.
