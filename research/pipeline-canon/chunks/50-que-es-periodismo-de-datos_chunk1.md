# What is data journalism?

This journalistic discipline draws on many others: investigative, in-depth, precision, computer-assisted and analytical. It works **with large volumes of data**, makes maximum use of **interactive** **visualization** and incorporates the **programmer** into the journalistic team.


**sandra crucianelli**


Many colleagues ask me what data journalism (DJ) is. They find no logic in the name, since journalists have always used data to produce journalistic content. In reality, it should properly be called "database journalism" (DBJ); but through use and convention, the shorter name prevails.


- Is it investigative journalism (IJ)?
Certainly, techniques specific to investigative journalism are used.


- Is it in-depth journalism (IDJ)?
Also, depending on the topic being analyzed.


- Is it precision journalism (PJ)?
Sometimes, yes — it is necessary to resort to the social research methods defined in the 1960s by Philip Meyer for their application in the field of journalism.

- Is it computer-assisted journalism (CAJ)?
Almost always, because we have to analyze data and we use spreadsheets as well as the procedures specific to this discipline. In fact, I think CAJ is now limited to when we handle small volumes of data and, for larger cases in terms of volume of information, it has ceased to be CAJ and has become data journalism.

To start from scratch and make everything clear, the definitions are as follows:


- Investigative journalism: three necessary conditions are required for a story to be considered investigative: it must be a matter of social relevance, to which it is added that someone intends to keep it secret and, finally, additional work from the journalist is required, outside the everyday practices of news coverage. These are the cases in which matters related to irregularities in the administration of public money are investigated, such as corruption cases, when situations of inefficiency or abuse on the part of privatized public service companies are revealed, and everything that causes harm to society (for example, an ecological crime case).


- In-depth journalism: it is the same as IJ, except that in this case no one is trying to keep secret what the journalist is investigating. Thus, for example, a study on the variables of poverty with data from national censuses.


- Precision journalism: formulated as a discipline — as I already noted — by Professor Philip Meyer, it involves the use of social research methods applied to journalism such as qualitative research (case analysis, focus groups, discourse analysis, etc.) and quantitative research. The latter includes descriptive research (when all cases are considered: for example, a census among legislators) and what is called inferential research, that is, when working with a universe and, given the difficulty of addressing each member of that universe, a representative sample is designed, which is then put under study. This is the case with opinion polls or electoral polls with predictive purposes.


**- Analytical journalism:** analyzes a complex reality, with the aim of achieving public understanding. It combines some aspects of investigative journalism and explanatory information. Its mission is to offer evidence based on interpretations of reality, explaining them in a comprehensible way. It contextualizes a background topic through the description of historical details and statistical data. The result is a complete explanation, designed to shape the public's perception of the phenomenon. It aspires to gather disparate data and make connections that are not immediately evident, and is often effective in analyzing the relationships between facts rather than the facts themselves. It uses the so-called geographic information systems (GIS) as a pillar of visualization.


**- Computer-assisted journalism:**
known as CAR (_Computer Assisted Reporting_) by its English acronym, it is defined as "any process that uses computer assistance during data collection and processing." It has based its practice on the use of Excel spreadsheets for the analysis of information contained in tables.


**The products of data journalism**
There are at least four different products, generally complementary, that arise from this practice:
**1.-** Data-driven articles
**2.-** Interactive visualizations
**3.-** Open datasets
**4.-** News apps


- Data-driven articles: generally, they are short articles built from large volumes of data such as those that might be contained in databases or datasets designed from one or several databases (http://www.lanacion.com.ar/1484852-los-millones-de-la-ape-como-se-repartio-en-2011-la-caja-que-era-de-moyano).


- Interactive visualizations: in general, they are complements to data-driven articles, but they can be content in themselves without any need for a reference article: in some cases, a title, subtitle and an explanation to the audience on how to read the data from the visualization are sufficient (http://www.lanacion.com.ar/1546303-los-bienes-de-los-funcionarios-en-la-primera-news-application-de-la-nacion).


- Open datasets (known as data sets): media organizations do not necessarily devote themselves to producing data-based journalistic work. They can also, like _La Nación_ of Argentina, dedicate a large part of their efforts to opening up public data. In countries where there are no public information access laws, these don't work as they should; on government websites some data is available but other data is not, hidden under various layers of information or in formats for displaying the data on the web that are not accessible (seeing data on screen is not the same as accessing it). For data to be accessible it must be shown in a reusable format. For example, an official document uploaded to Flickr. In these circumstances, media outlets and organizations can devote themselves to the search, extraction and opening of public data, as _La Nación_ does through its data catalog. That is, since there are data that governments or organizations do not allow access to — for whatever reason — media outlets can venture into that territory, and will surely make great contributions. Data can be managed or administered from simple and free platforms like Google Drive (http://data.lanacion.com.ar/dashboards/5068/inflacion-y-precios/).


- News apps: known in English as _news apps_, they are the fruit of the joint work of journalists and computer programmers. Often, the volume of data is so large that it is impossible to find a story unless an application is designed that allows us to group and analyze variables, for example, by geographic location, by date, by company name, etc. (http://www.gastopublicobahiense.org/ - http://www.nacion.com/CustomerFiles/nacioncom/Generales/Subsitios/Investigacion/2012/propiedadessubvaloradas/props_diputados/index.html - http://www.texastribune.org/library/data/government-employee-salaries/ -http://projects.propublica.org/docdollars/).


**No open data culture**
There is no open data culture in the majority of Spanish-speaking countries. However, what governments fail to do in terms of open data, the community, civil society organizations or media outlets will do.

The prognosis is not new. An example came to light in 2010, when the _Texas Tribune_ requested from the state government the detail of the salaries of the 674,000 public employees, with the intention of designing an application that would allow citizens to consult the salaries paid with public funds, including elementary school teachers and public university professors (http://www.texastribune.org/library/data/government-employee-salaries/).
For data journalism one can do without many things, since there are free digital tools that all journalists can use to overcome almost any obstacle, but what we cannot do without is data. It is a necessary condition without which we cannot even begin to formulate any journalistic hypothesis.
In many cases, the format in which data is displayed on websites is not reusable. Truly, journalists can resolve these obstacles by learning specific techniques. But what about citizens? Don't taxpayers have the right to better access to data directly related with public money?
Information that is delayed, disorganized, still outdated or in inadequate formats can certainly be read on screen,


but visualizing data is not the same as accessing it. It is the classic example of an image converted to a PDF in which the detail of a table can be read, such as a budget execution. One would have to resort to an optical character recognition (OCR) technique to extract the data; although these programs don't always work efficiently and there is no other option but manual data entry, which enormously complicates the task of every reporter, especially if the content of the table we want to process is extensive.
In the film _Eyes Wide Shut_, directed in 1999 by Stanley Kubrick shortly before his death, the plot revolves around two specific feelings: uncertainty and dissatisfaction. To give an example, in Argentina, without the thriller components surrounding that dark story, 103 years later, citizens and journalists find ourselves with _Data Wide Shut_. Faced with it, we experience uncertainty and no one can feel satisfied with a scenario in which one must have specific skills, when not pay for additional resources, to access legitimately public data.


**How to build a dataset**
The data has already been retrieved from the web or downloaded to the PC. Perhaps the journalist had to resort to manual data entry, since this is frequent when documents, upon request, are delivered in paper format and it is not possible to extract the information and subsequently restructure it in tables, considering that optical character recognition programs do not always work efficiently.
In data journalism, we consider a dataset to be a collection of grouped data elements that allow for easy recording. In them, the data are organized into variables, generally measured over time and in downloadable formats.

**Visualization for beginners**
Data journalism handles a large volume of data. Without an appropriate visualization accompanying the analysis of that data, it would be impossible to simply visualize the variables under study. Chronicles should be extensive and could be guilty of being boring if they are filled with numbers that people do not easily understand when not adequately presented. Therefore, short chronicles are suggested, with forceful conclusions and an


important, dominant visualization in the article, when telling a story.
Interactive visualization experts are capable of designing, from a large amount of data, a graphic that allows the reader to decide what they want to visualize and how. Data visualization explains in a comprehensible way the relationships between a large amount of information generated by different means.

**Intelligent interactive graphics**
Data visualization is experiencing spectacular development in our time, which directly impacts journalism; not only in digital media, print journalism and television are also using these tools to visually present their stories in a more interesting way.
A few years ago, to be a journalist, searching for data, approaching sources and knowing how to write was sufficient. In our time, that has changed, because journalists began to perceive that, if we don't handle these resources properly, we can even lose the opportunity to find scoops. This requires the reporter to develop special skills.
A large part of the information is found in databases or hidden on the web. In the late 1970s, when I began my career as a reporter, the scenario was one of few visible data, so journalists of my generation had to adapt to working in a terrain more dominated by oral sources than documentary ones.


That has changed in the 21st century, in which the overabundance of information can even plunge us into chaos. Before, the reporter would write and the infographics team would do their work based on the data the journalist provided. That relationship has changed drastically in recent years.

**Geographic information systems**
They are defined as data integration systems, capable of displaying geographically located information. Census data, for example, tend to be boring; although, managed through GIS programs, they can offer a clear view of the variables under study.


Users of these systems can create interactive maps, scrutinize spatial information and edit data and maps, so as to offer better representations of reality.
In simple terms, a GIS is _software_ that allows a better visual representation of numerical data, as these are distributed geographically and spatially.
The Institute of Analytical Journalism (http://www.analyticjournalism.com/) has been working for years on the incorporation of these tools into the field of communications. The site contains a large number of valuable tools that will be very helpful for journalism teachers and reporters interested in the topic.

te" — corresponding to the port operating in Bahía Blanca — was entered in the box for "the exact phrase," and the KMZ format was selected, which is the one that returns Google Earth views. The other suitable format for selection is KML.
KMZ and KML formats are used to represent geographic data. A KML file specifies a location, an image

- a polygon. It can contain a title, a basic description of the location, its coordinates (latitude and longitude), as well as other data. As for the KMZ format, it is simply a compressed KML file. Specifically, KMZ uses the ZIP compression format.


Hybrid applications: 'mashups'
There is a varied range of Google Maps and Google Earth applications. For example, Eosnap (http://www.eosnap.com/) is a portal dedicated to observing the Earth from space. Each day, the site generates satellite images that are of undoubted interest in the field of communications.
Many of these resources are hybrid (_mashup_ - remix), that is, sites that use content from other applications to create new functions. _Mashups_ allow the combination of data that exists in different sources, such as YouTube, Flickr, Google Maps and Google Earth, among many others.
The scenario in which _mashups_ are developed is incredibly versatile and new resources appear every day. At this point, any reporter might feel some annoyance at the fact


that most of these tools are available in English. Certainly, the reporter who does not master that language — for reading it, at least — will have difficulties keeping up with the use of these technologies. However, hybrid applications in Spanish are being developed, which have enormous potential. Among these _mashups_ the following stand out:


- Tagzania (http://tagzania.com/): allows tagging places, which means users form a collaborative database of enormous potential. Any journalist could be surprised when they enter the name of the city where they live, as they might find more results than expected, as happened in my case. A similar resource is Flof (http://flof.com.ar/bin/home/).


- WikiLoc (http://es.wikiloc.com/wikiloc/home.do): for marking routes, paths, hiking or cycling routes. It combines the use of GPS when points of interest are created.


- Woices (http://woices.com/): for finding or recording geolocated audio.


- The Simile Project (http://simile-widgets.org/): it is a required reference within the field of data visualization. Semantic Interoperability of Metadata and Information in Unlike Environments (Simile) is a research project aimed at maximizing interaction between different digital collections. A large


part of the information contained in digital libraries is invisible to online search engines. Therefore, this resource groups an interesting variety of tools that try to resolve that problem. Simile is a resource developed by The World Wide Web Consortium (www.w3c.org), with the support of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (USA), among other organizations participating in the initiative.
The tools developed in Simile use semantic web technologies to improve the sharing of web resources, which helps to unlock the content of digital libraries around the world.
Its best-known resource is Piggy Bank (http://simile.mit.edu/wiki/Piggy_Bank), a Firefox extension that allows introducing the browser to the semantic web, facilitating the processing of web pages, in such a way that information not visible from the web can be retrieved. Once installed, two icons will appear, which must be added to the toolbar.
But it is not the only one. Exhibit (http://www.simile-widgets.org/exhibit/), for example, makes possible the creation of presentations of items through a carousel, which can be personalized through the selection of various parameters.
Another marvel of this project is Time Plot (http://www.simile-widgets.org/timeplot/), a system that allows graphing statistics in an original way, giving the option to insert a time-


line through the incorporation of specific events.
For its part, Citeline (http://citeline.mit.edu/) facilitates the editing of interactive bibliographies.


**Necessary conditions**
The answer to what the minimum necessary conditions are for processing data from a newsroom and arriving at conclusions useful to citizens is complex. We live in a world in which almost everything is expressed in numbers. Therefore, handling large masses of data requires certain skills on the part of those who seek to find relevant news behind them.
The mission is not simple: a tangible meaning must be found from the abstract, people must be told how that great tangle of numbers affects their lives. Only in this way can the necessary impact be achieved to live in better-informed societies.
Data journalism requires permanent training and knowledge of subjects generally far from the preferences of the conventional journalist. Well-oiled sources are not sufficient: one must understand how the Public Administration works, know how to interpret laws, decrees and ordinances. If a journalist does not understand how the institutions of their country function, they will find it difficult to embark on the path successfully.

  - Command of the English language: most of the tools frequently used in data journalism have no

counterpart in Spanish. Currently, an important gap is forming between those who access these resources due to their bilingual condition and those who do not.

  - Skills developed in web searching: using Google in the traditional way is not enough. One must learn to get more out of advanced searches, by format, by domain and by date; but also, in the absence of a national open data initiative, monitor the possible official sites where there is data.

## Data journalism requires permanent training


- Know the basic principles of the Excel program or similar, capable of processing numerical information in spreadsheets.

  - Have basic mathematics and statistics knowledge, both descriptive and inferential: a journalist who is "innumerate" will not have sufficient capacity to face the process known as "the interview with the numbers." This is of particular interest when it comes to analysis of public budgets, taxes and other issues such as crime and environmental contamination.
The journalist is facing an unknown scenario. We have been trained for a world in which data scarcity prevails, not for a world in which there is an overabundance of


information. Data comes to us from multiple fronts and in large quantities. There is still no formal instruction on data journalism at universities, except in exceptional cases. Therefore, future data journalists will have to invest — basically in time — to take charge of their own training in this discipline.
Data journalism involves hard work and, often, costly effort. One must access dozens, if not hundreds or thousands of spreadsheets, texts, graphics. Sometimes programmers must be hired to design applications capable of capturing data contained in web pages. Effort will always be a strong presence in this field. Sometimes the routine is disappointing, because you won't always find what you're looking for. You'll have to read heavy files, mountains of paper, make calls that no one will return and knock on doors that probably won't open. The data journalist must always consider the possibility that, on occasion, there are no stories behind the data. Although, of course, that is not the most common outcome.
Now that you know the worst about data journalism, if you wish to press on, then you are prepared to face the challenge; and you will surely succeed, because in this blessed profession what shines is not luck, good connections or even brilliant minds: in this endeavor only the persevering shine.
