**Resistance to change**


Discussion of change in journalism education needs to take account of two linke
debates. First, how far should programmes concentrate on preparing students for employ
ment and practical skills, rather than a more ‘academic’ approach strong on theory, crit
cal or otherwise (see, for example, Deuze, 2006; Folkerts, 2014; Harcup, 2011; Hirs
2010; Zelizer, 2004)? Second, a lack of innovation – a focus of criticism (Folkerts, 2014
21), attributed partly to journalism education being a ‘handmaiden to industry, not i
critic or visionary guide’ (Dennis, 1983: 3), and thus linked to the first. It is not hard t
see how innovation might be more prevalent where the mission resembles a developmen
laboratory for journalism (Deuze, 2006).
Resistance to change is attributed also to the prevalence of shared norms and socia
ization to the sector – a key goal of journalism education (Mensing, 2010). With th
rise of online publishing, Singer (2003) noted that ‘one consistent reaction has been t
seek to protect the journalism school’s franchise (and that of the media industry) b
emphasizing constants rather than change’ (p. 150). Journalism educators keep to wh
they know, rather than introduce the unconventional or experimental (Stephens, 2000
They perhaps lack relevant skills or experience needed to innovate and teach speciali
areas – and the relatively high cost (per student) of small courses represents anothe
obstacle, along with any organization’s tendency to change only slowly unless force
(Picard, 2014). Others identified the ‘inertia and glacial pace of change in academ
(Ghiglione, 2001: 16) and the conflicting pressures of employers’ and academic prior
ties (Reese, 2001: 6).
One can argue that much of journalism has been slow to engage with technologic
change (Domingo et al., 2015) – so programmes operating in ‘follower’ mode hav
been accordingly slow. If those in ‘innovator’ mode have responded more quickl
perhaps their relatively low number has had little impact. But it is hard to disagre
with Folkerts (2014) that ‘journalism education has, to a great degree, ignored th
larger contours of the digital age’ (p. 63). Nor is education itself immune, affected a
it is by network effects, shifts in the student–teacher relationship and so on (Johnso
et al., 2014) and the increasing marketization of higher education (HE) (e.
Molesworth et al., 2011).
In addition, the development of data journalism education may be impacted mor
profoundly and/or immediately than other areas of journalism education because of i
interaction with other fields – notably statistics, computing, data science and visualiz
tion. For ‘mainstream’ journalism education, these may be an optional extra; for da
journalism, they relate to its core functions.

**Relevance and currency**


he credibility of a professionally oriented programme depends also on its relevance and
urrency, although cutting-edge practice almost inevitably outstrips the curriculum
Breen, 1996; Simon, 1969). Relevance and currency pose challenges in the learning and
aching of a newer area such as data journalism more acutely than in the case of longer
stablished aspects of journalism, such as reporting or interviewing. The involvement of
ractising data journalists in developing the curriculum and in teaching helps to address
his, but any individual is likely to be constrained by the extent of their own experience
nd what they know of others’ practice. In a rapidly evolving, divergent and interdiscilinary field, this may not be sufficient.
For any subject, the curriculum depends primarily on the intended learning outcomes;
arning/teaching activities and assessment tasks/criteria stem from these (Biggs, 2003:
6–29). While the learning outcomes can be developed in consultation with practitionrs, researchers and colleagues, a challenge arises here from the relatively short history
f data journalism, and its continuing evolution – notwithstanding its important roots in
omputer-assisted reporting (Hewett, 2013). There is little by way of an established
anon’ or agreed body of knowledge, theory and practices on which to base a curricuum. It is a work in progress, with continuing fluidity, differing approaches and a relavely small corpus (compared to other aspects of journalism) of ‘classic’ examples of the
ind that journalism educators tend to draw upon for case studies and other learning
ctivities.
The data journalism module has addressed this challenge by taking an approach akin
continued professional development, highlighting current practice. One assessment
equires students to blog about their own progress as data journalists and encourages
hem to regard data journalism as, in effect, a beat for their reporting. This builds on
xperiential learning and social constructivist approaches in journalism education (see,
or example, Rhodes, 2008; Schwalbe, 2009). Students publish posts on their own sites
nd/or on their collaboratively run website. Typically, they include ‘how to’ posts; critial analysis of pieces of data journalism; interviews with practitioners; their own data
urnalism practice – including experimentation – and reflections on this; and the evaluion of relevant new tools or techniques.
As well as underlining current practice in data journalism, this approach has a range
f potential pedagogical benefits (based on research evidence from a range of discilines), linked with independent, active and collaborative learning (Hewett et al., 2014)
y encouraging critical reflection, it aims to support professional development (Sheridan
urns, 2004). The structure and activities help students to collaborate and operate effecvely in the environment of modern networked journalism. They foster, too, an open
pproach and independent learning (important for future career development) that
nvolve contact with practitioners and peers – reflecting a key element in the practice and
rowth of data journalism, identified by Howard (2014). This also tends to enhance the
rofile of the students in the field of data journalism and among potential employers.

journalism, and those in the United Kingdom are less able to draw on experience i
computer-assisted reporting than those in the United States (Hewett, 2013). Journalism
schools typically supplement their core staff by involving practising journalists as visi
ing lecturers, which helps to address questions of currency. But the pool of individua
with current experience as data journalists is small, and an employer’s demands on the
sought-after skills and experience may make it difficult to accommodate teachin
Calling on data journalists (some with relevant teaching experience) and a pioneerin

he analysis of changes over time and pattern-detection. It introduces aspects of computional thinking (Wing, 2006), and many students have used FoI effectively in their

Such points raise consideration of the overall integrity and coherence of the proramme – both its scope and, for example, where ethical issues related to data journalism

n relation to the law, and even public administration (e.g. in relation to the data-related
ctivities of public bodies). This illustrates some of the difficulties familiar to many eduators of parcelling up practice into discrete modules – contrary, in many ways, to the
emands of real-world activity and understanding a discipline holistically (Trowler,
998: 90–91).
Developing the MAIJ as a digital journalism MA with data as a core element, rather
han as a narrower data journalism programme, perhaps ensured its feasibility, but may
ave limited its scope and ambition. Greater emphasis on data would need some existing
ements to be omitted from the curriculum (Wenger and Owens, 2012: 10), and further
pecialization. More on statistical analysis, visualization/design and programming are
rime candidates. This might be consistent with the field maturing, along with the expections (and experience) of students and employers. However, the picture appears mixed;
ome employers want depth; some prefer breadth (see, for example, Stabe, 2015). The
cope here for greater collaboration with other departments in the university has yet to be
ealized; the initial timescale overtook discussions on how to coordinate modules.


**ocialization revisited**


he question of socialization permeates many of the issues identified above. It is simulneously a key part of (many) journalism programmes (Becker, Fruit and Caudil, 1987:
9) and identified as an obstacle to innovation and the reform (Domingo et al., 2015;
Mensing, 2010). But what if socialization were geared less to long-established practices
f journalism and more to newer, still developing norms and practices of data journalism
and other ‘new’ forms)? Such boundaries between ‘old’ and ‘new’ may not always be
vident, but data journalism has less of the traditions and ‘“best practices” of the previus generation’ (Mensing, 2010: 515) that tend to constrain fresh approaches.
What might socialization of this kind look like in practice: students are taught by curently practising data journalists; internships of relevant data work alongside established
rofessionals, ideally with interdisciplinary teams (e.g. web developers and designers);
nd students learning explicitly about new and changing practices, ideas and approaches.
hey would hear and learn from others working in data journalism (not only those formally teaching them) and in related fields (e.g. computing and open data). Mathematical,
atistical and technological literacy – and, indeed, high levels of competence – would be


essential, almost assumed. So would, for example, finding stories in data; understandin
its structure and context; gathering, cleaning and analysing it; and presenting data sto
ries. Important, too, might be working collaboratively with other specialists, and explo
ing new tools, techniques and theoretical approaches.
Some of this is evident in the MAIJ and its data journalism module. Students lear
from practising data journalists, for example, including core techniques. Placements ar
encouraged where they can develop these. They are assessed partly on discovering wh
is going on in the field of data, and – explicitly – on their own experimentation an
reflections. They are encouraged to look beyond the formal curriculum and to networ
and attend events such as Hacks/Hackers and the Mozilla Festival. The building of usefu
networks is also part of the MAIJ module on strategic social media.


**The market**


The influence of the market-oriented approach in HE is evident in the stakeholder analy
sis. As Knight (2002) identified, ‘academic staff increasingly have to attend to compet
tion and image management’ (p. 10). This study suggests one might add studen
satisfaction and recruitment. Attracting students and their tuition fees (or other extern
funding) was key to developing the MAIJ. But it was interest from employers and a
anticipated fall in demand for other sister programmes, rather than clear interest from
(potential) students, that were key drivers, in terms of the ‘market’.
Consider the perspective of potential students. Many may simply not know of da
journalism, as one later recalled (Scott, 2015) and others have told me. It is, after all,
rare beast in student media and local newspapers (and radio), which predominate in ou
postgraduate journalism applicants’ work experience. Even in national news organiz
tions, where some have interned, data journalism is largely confined to specific enclo
sures, and students are unlikely to encounter it ‘close up’ unless they seek it out.
This may change, with one regional newspaper group running a data unit (McAth
2013) and indications that student journalism may be involving more data journalism
(see, for example, Frost, 2012; Halliday, 2013). But if to most would-be journalism stu
dents it remains an obscure specialism, as well as to parents and university career advi
ers, will data journalism education be constrained by a lack of appeal? Furthermore, wh
do potential and actual students – whom market-led universities need to attract an
‘satisfy’ – make of studying it? How do they view data analysis and statistics, particu
larly if they are ‘arts graduates who dropped maths and the sciences as early on in the
education as possible’ (Burn-Murdoch, 2013: 155)? Some advanced techniques ma
deter students, suggested Denham (1996: 57), and US journalism faculty feared ‘aliena
ing prospective students’ with data analysis (Yarnall et al., 2008: 159).


**Conclusion**


This study highlights some of the complexities involved in developing a programme th
offers a dedicated data journalism module. These stem partly from the additional, ne
demands on staff, other resources and services – and sometimes students – that
involves. Integrating data journalism smoothly with existing modules/teaching ha


roved a challenge, as have the establishment and maintenance of links with fields unfamiliar not only to much of journalism (e.g. statistics and computing) but also to its stuents and educators.
While the specific circumstances of the programme examined in this article have to
e taken into account in considering any wider applicability of conclusions, it leads me
offer some questions that may be relevant to other journalism educators.
First and fundamentally, what is the place of the data journalism education being
ffered to students? Is it one course or option within a wider programme (probably more
eneralist/introductory) or the main focus (more specialist), for example? How far does
assume previous relevant learning or experience, or involve few if any prerequisites?
What about the balance (and levels) of theory, practice, experimentation and the use of
pecific tools and techniques?
Second, and again part of Deuze’s (2006) contextual factors, consider the ‘supporting
nfrastructure’ as well as the curriculum content – points such as computing resources,
he learning environment, group size, links with colleagues in other disciplines, and how
he data journalism teaching will interface with other modules or courses. Teaching and
dministrative workloads must be addressed, too, and at least some of the extensively
ocumented resistance to change in journalism education may have to be tackled (see
terature review above).
Third, how will data journalism education operate in the context (in many countries) of
n increasingly market-oriented HE sector? The costs of establishing and running a data
urnalism programme may be at odds with the expectations placed on academics to
tract and ‘satisfy’ students. Despite promising job prospects, data journalism risks being
jected as unappealing by potential students with an arts or humanities background, prevent among this established ‘market’, and to whom it may simply be unfamiliar. Engaging
uccessful alumni (as well as current students and perhaps employers) may have an
mportant role to play here in addressing the concerns of prospective students.
Fourth, how are data journalists involved, and perhaps other practitioners? Are they
he main teachers, specialist instructors and/or guest speakers? How else will students
nteract with and learn from practitioners, whether on placement, at events with networkng opportunities, freelancing or through social media? Do these include specialists in
elated areas, such as statistics, information design, computing, open data and freedom
f information? Will these or other approaches help to ensure the currency of what stuents learn, in a diverse field that is developing rapidly?
This study suggests that socialization with practitioners is not incompatible with innoation in journalism education. What may be involved is more selective socialization
hat is focused (in this case) specifically on data journalism. A key argument against
ocialization in journalism education is that it reinforces past patterns of practice (thus
esisting innovation) and marginalizes critical reflection and experimentation. But data
urnalism, as a more recently established field with norms and practices that are still
volving, is arguably more fluid and welcoming to such approaches. This may also be a
eflection of the more open and networked nature of data journalism compared to CAR
Coddington, 2015).
Such complexities arguably exacerbate some of the challenges in innovating effectively
n journalism education (see review section above). Data journalism can be seen partly as


an extension of the ‘traditional’ areas that journalism programmes have long taught: di
covering relevant facts, verifying (and disputing) claims, reporting and analysis, for exam
ple, and builds on CAR. But many aspects bring journalism education into fields wit
which it is much less familiar. With apologies to Star Trek, one could say that this is jou
nalism but not as we know it – or not as many journalism educators have known it.
As this appears to be the first study of data journalism education that is focused on
single programme, conclusions are necessarily tentative and limited in wider applicabi
ity. However, this article raises suitable questions for further investigation. Wh
approaches are deployed, and constraints encountered, elsewhere in data and other inno
vations in journalism education, for example? An international comparative study o
different programmes (including curriculum content) could be valuable. We need t
know more about students’ views and experiences, and the effects of their status increa
ingly as ‘consumers’ or ‘customers’. Are pedagogical innovations required to enab
journalism education to embrace data (and other areas) more fully and maintain cu
rency? Finally, could a clearer emphasis on (critically) reflective practice offer a wa
forward here, while also taking us beyond the perceived split between ‘vocational’ an
‘academic’ journalism education?
