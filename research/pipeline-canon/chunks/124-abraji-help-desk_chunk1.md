# Elections Guide for Investigative Reporters — Introduction

Elections around the world face increasingly common threats, including disinformation campaigns, foreign interference, and voter suppression. The Economist Intelligence Unit found that in 2020, 116 of 167 alleged democracies saw their indices worsen.

Journalists face a complex landscape where authoritarian leaders consolidate power through corrupt alliances, algorithms amplify disinformation, and democratic governments use health crises as a pretext to suppress fundamental freedoms. Traditional election coverage has proved insufficient.

## Guide Structure

1. **Investigative tools**: digital resources for tracking conversations and disinformation
2. **Preparation**: understanding electoral rules and data protection
3. **Investigating candidates**: records, hidden assets, and contacts
4. **Political messages and disinformation**: analysis of platforms such as Telegram and Twitter
5. **Moldova case study**: practical methodology for investigations

## General Recommended Strategies

- Consult citizen electoral monitors and local databases
- Report presuming democratic functioning, even in autocracies
- Collect evidence by crowdsourcing via apps such as WhatsApp
- Use simple Boolean searches on Google
- Set realistic expectations for voters

## The Moldova Case

The "Kremlinovici" investigation by RISE Moldova: began with a leak from an anonymous source, proceeded with interviews verified against databases, institutional collaboration, and open-source tools for tracking. It identified Russian interference, legal violations, and documented how campaign speeches were prepared externally.

# Chapter 1: New Electoral Investigation Tools

## Established Techniques

**Geocode in TweetDeck**: Copy incident coordinates from Google Maps to track social media posts in specific areas, facilitating investigations into electoral violence.

**Boolean Search**: Google search operators for locating specific data.

## Multipurpose Tools

- **Aleph**: OCCRP database of leaked documents for tracking electoral crimes
- **CrowdTangle**: Monitor disinformation campaigns on Facebook and Instagram

## WeVerify Twitter SNA

Maps electoral conversations on Twitter, identifying influencers and beneficiaries of political narratives. Features:
- Scatter charts of disinformation amplifiers
- Narrative propagation timelines
- Most retweeted and most active users
- Connection maps between users and hashtags
- List of the 25 most shared sites

Limitation: less effective in countries where WhatsApp, Facebook, or Telegram dominate. Up to 15,000 tweets simultaneously.

## UA/Pub Tool: Tracking Anonymous Networks

Website owners use the same Google AdSense (Pub-) and Analytics (UA-) IDs across multiple sites. Process:
1. Find tags in the source code (Ctrl+F for "UA-" or "Pub-")
2. Search on DNSlytics.com, SpyOnWeb.com, BuiltWith.com
3. Validate via Whoisology.com and DomainBigData.com
4. Expand search on the Wayback Machine

Exploits the "personal greed" of actors who sacrifice anonymity to maintain advertising revenue, revealing connections between apparently unrelated sites.

# Chapter 3: Investigating Candidates

Journalists frequently assume they know public figures who run for office, neglecting in-depth investigations.

## Tools for Exploring Online History

- **TweetBeaver**: Downloads thousands of tweets in CSV/Excel + follower analysis
- **WhatsMyName**: Searches usernames across ~600 online services
- **Wayback Machine**: Checks altered or deleted historical statements
- **Who Posted What?**: Bypasses Facebook limitations, searches by date/subject
- **Creepy**: Geolocation of Twitter/Flickr/Instagram posts on Google Maps
- **Advanced Google "AROUND(17)"**: Finds associations between candidates and other actors
- **Facial recognition**: Pimeyes, Yandex, TinEye to find old photos

## Finding Contact Information

- **Name2Email**: Determines emails based on names and company patterns
- **Hunter**: Extracts emails from organization websites (25 free searches/month)
- **Truecaller/Sync.ME**: Phone numbers (outside EU/GDPR)
- **Pipl Pro**: People search (reduced access for GIJN members)
- **LittleSis**: Maps connections between political activists (North America)

## Checking Red Flags and Hidden Assets

- **OpenSanctions**: 140,000 people with access to public funds + 24,000 sanctioned individuals
- **Aleph (OCCRP)**: 150 million entities
- **OpenCorporates**: 100 million+ corporate entities
- **OpenSecrets**: Campaign financing and lobbyists (USA)
- **Whoxy**: Original website registrations for shell companies

## Methodology of Juliana Dal Piva (Brazilian investigative journalist)

1. **Start from scratch**: investigate family, former spouses, whereabouts of children
2. **Court records search**: note names of parties and lawyers
3. **Personalized biography**: family, financial connections, "who are they really?"
4. **"Spider web" map**: circles for spouses, children, allies, properties, companies
5. **Wealth analysis**: assets accumulated before or during political office?
6. **Identification of allies and enemies**: "Who are their enemies who used to be allies?"
7. **Asset disclosure**: electoral portals, undeclared assets, overvaluation
8. **Spouses' social media**: "gold mine" for undeclared assets
9. **Whistleblowers**: secure encrypted channels
10. **Field observation**: follow candidates, pay attention to low-ranking staff
11. **Note tactic**: handwritten notes slipped under doors mentioning a search for "opinions"
12. **Mass outreach**: 30–40 messages; replies may come months later
