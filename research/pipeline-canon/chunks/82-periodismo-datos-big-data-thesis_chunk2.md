**V.1.1. Journalistic values and technological tools**


The binomial of journalism and technology is a constant throughout all the interviews.
When David Ruiz, developer of the data unit at _Eldiario.es_, stated that


data journalism is based on the use of technological tools to do journalism, he did so
asserting that "journalism always has to be there," to ensure the


veracity of the data and "to go further when necessary." Ruiz linked these technical
capabilities to traditional journalistic values and emphasized that "DJ is about searching, delving into


information to add content that is objective." For Javier Galán of the CEPID, DJ
is journalism that makes use of computer tools to handle data, which is


intimately related to the accessibility of technological tools for the
journalist. According to Hugo Garrido, coordinator of El Mundo Data, DJ has been


"popularized" thanks to the technological democratization of recent times, that is,
thanks to "access to certain technological tools that facilitated access to large


quantities of data and the ability to visualize them." This is a vision of DJ that, moreover, takes
the collaborative work of the hacker community very much into account, as Frank Belyeu emphasized:


"Data journalism is about making use of all that, all the tools that are being created
all the time by the hacker community, and combining the journalistic perspective with


technical knowledge to produce a more rigorous, more precise, better journalistic product."


**V.2. The profile of the data journalist**


When Spanish data journalists spoke about the profile of the data journalist,
they again emphasized an interest in the journalistic profession alongside technical skills that a


traditional journalist does not typically have. Frank Belyeu considered that the background of a
data journalist can be anything: "having a journalistic motivation and being


interested in technology," while Hugo Garrido noted that "ideally they would have
programming knowledge." This data journalist from _El Mundo_ even went so far as to


specify a series of "basic" skills that should form part of the data journalist's profile:
"1- Handling technology, 2- not being afraid of numbers, 3- having


basic knowledge of statistics, and 4- being comfortable with mathematics." For David
Ruiz, the journalist-technician interrelationship is the essence of a data journalist, in which the


ability to work with technical tools from the internet is fundamental:


"The most important thing in the profile of a data journalist is being accustomed to working
with online tools, moving around information and knowing how to handle it in an agile and


flexible way—in other words, being prepared to accept any type of information and knowing
how to convert it into something interesting for the public, for readers."


The basic tools of a data journalist were described by Paula Guisado as a way of
differentiating this profile from that of the traditional journalist: "a data journalist is a journalist


capable of using tools that are uncommon in traditional journalism (Excel, data, statistics)
that allow them to approach information in a different way." It is thus that for this journalist at _El Mundo_


the profile of the data journalist is defined in relation to what she considers "traditional journalism,"
something very recurrent throughout the interviews with all the interviewees in the sample. In this


regard, Adrián Blanco drew an analogy between the way the traditional journalist handles sources
and the way the data journalist relates to them through the analysis of databases:


"A data journalist differs from the traditional one in many ways: the latter uses the
phone to call sources and write. But this year [2015] and increasingly, a mixed


profile is being sought: a journalist who can program. More focused on a journalist-programmer
      - data analyst, a more complete profile than the usual one."


The use of databases and the ability to analyze them in order to generate stories of journalistic
interest is one of the fundamental aspects of the data journalist's profile according to all


the interviewees. In the case of Belén Picazo, she compared the ability to analyze databases
to investigative journalism, emphasizing the technical capacity in programming and design


that the data journalist must possess: "DJ is IJ, because from a database
you extract news, things that were not known before. The data journalist has to have notions


of journalism (essential) and not merely basic ones of web development—meaning programming
languages and design." These basic skills were reiterated by Jesús Escudero


when he stated that "a data journalist must have at least two of the characteristics we mentioned
before: programming or visualization. Being able to graph information appropriately,


even if not very complex ones." In this way, in addition to the technical capacities for working
with spreadsheets and databases and knowing some programming language, the data journalist's


profile also encompasses basic knowledge of design and the use of data
visualization tools. From the CEPID, Javier Galán summarized it very concisely:


"I am a data journalist: I consider myself capable of analyzing databases and visualizing them."


As can be observed from what all the Spanish data journalists said, the
mixed profile of a data journalist requires IJ skills, data processing and analysis through


spreadsheets such as Excel or more complex databases, basic knowledge
of statistics, web development and design, the ability to visualize data using


online tools, and familiarity with at least one programming language. However, a single
journalist fulfilling each and every one of the technical skills described in this data journalist's


profile is by no means common. In fact, the Spanish data journalists
interviewed only meet some of these technical characteristics and usually work in


teams with other journalists whose skills complement their own, thereby achieving
through collaborative work what the ideal data journalist's profile could accomplish alone.


In this regard, in the daily work of the data journalists interviewed, the most important technical
skill is knowing how to work with data that can be structured and analyzed:


"What differentiates us data journalists from the rest of the journalists in the newsroom is
that we have trained in working with data and do so on a regular basis. In this


regard, any journalist from any section who decided to train in data work
and carried it out in their area would be a data journalist" (Paula Guisado).


For Adrián Blanco, at _Elconfidencial.com_ there are three data journalists: "we are three
journalists with a grounding in programming and databases," while Paula Guisado considers


herself a data journalist because she is "a journalist who works with data (…) I do the job of
searching for and analyzing data and thinking about how to organize the information in a chart when


necessary." Thus, despite the unlikelihood of a single journalist encompassing
all the characteristics of the ideal data journalist profile, certain minimum competencies


must be mastered in order to handle data—structuring and analysis—and to be able to visualize it. For
Belén Picazo there are many types of data journalists:


"A data journalist is a journalist who must have qualities beyond journalism. I know just
enough programming and have basic notions of design: a large part of


the work I do is design. There are many types of data journalists: those who analyze,
those who process, those who visualize, etc."


In this way we see how in Spanish DJ there is a division of labor across at least three
areas: data processing (programming and spreadsheet management), data analysis


(use of spreadsheets and mathematical notions for interpretation) and data visualization
(design). This division of labor tells us that, after meeting certain minimum requirements,


the data journalist's profile tends to cover technical characteristics that
specialize them in one of the three areas mentioned, allowing them to work as part of a team with other


technical professions or data journalists specialized in the other areas mentioned.


**V.3. The need for multidisciplinary teams in DJ**


In Spanish DJ, there is a technical impossibility for a single journalist to fully cover
all the fundamental aspects of the news production process. That is,


no single person encompasses all aspects of the ideal mixed profile of a data journalist. This has
led this discipline to organize itself into data units or data journalism teams, in which


each journalist handles one part of the process.
This fact and the organization of multidisciplinary teams was mentioned by Jesús


Escudero:


"It is very difficult in DJ for one person to fulfill the entire profile: it is usually a
multidisciplinary team with multiple profiles. At a minimum, three people are needed to


form a data unit: a journalist, a programmer and a designer. The journalist
has the journalistic nose and knows what information may be of public interest and how to


obtain it; the programmer is important for determining whether that information is structured
on the web through data scraping, and then in a second step also for


visualizing it; and a designer is also needed to visualize that information, because the journalist
is very tied to text." (Jesús Escudero)


The organization of work according to the journalistic production process has been a reality
in all the media outlets and collectives interviewed, where collaborative work is


fundamental. In larger outlets, such as _El Mundo_, this collaboration focuses more on
members of the team itself who then collaborate with the other sections. For Paula


Guisado, "at _El Mundo_ we are fortunate to have a data unit made up of
three data journalists and a developer… it consists of the head, who has a mixed profile


as journalist and computer specialist, two data journalists and a programmer." In the case of
_Eldiario.es_, being a smaller outlet, the collaboration among those who do DJ is more


cross-cutting in relation to the rest of the newsroom. According to Belén Picazo, "at _Eldiario.es_
there are two of us, although then there are more people who collaborate and help from the different


sections. There are people dedicated to visualization, others to analyzing data, others to finding
news within that data, to writing texts." In the case of _Elconfidencial.com_, larger


than _Eldiario.es_ and smaller than _El Mundo_, the data unit is in constant
collaboration with the members of Elconfidencial.lab—of which they are part—and the different


sections of the outlet. According to Adrián Blanco, each data journalist on his team has
assigned roles according to their specialization: "we have similar profiles, but each one is


specialized in one thing and then there is a programmer too. That is, there are four of us in the
day-to-day running of the data unit. We have a fairly organized workflow with assigned roles."


As can be observed, the collaborations at _El Mundo_, _Eldiario.es_ and _Elconfidencial.com_
tend to be organized according to each data journalist's strengths, with the role of


the developer or programmer within the team standing out.


Since the CEPID does not belong to any larger, more vertical media structure, the
different profiles on the team perfectly complement each of the news production processes


in DJ, with three journalists, two programmers and one designer.
Javier Galán explains that "the journalists conceptualize, rely on the programmers, and


the graphic aspect is handled by the designer." At first glance it is clear how these entrepreneurs
were able to represent, at least at the structural level, the ideal of a DJ team. Having at


least one specialist in each of the news production processes is something that
the coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo Garrido, found lacking in his team: "we had a


full team until the end of June, then a workforce regulation measure was applied and some of
the team members were let go, precisely those in the most differentiated profiles." The most


differentiated profile Garrido referred to is the designer or infographer, responsible for
data visualization and a key profile for better communicating the information created by


data journalists. This profile, along with that of the journalist-programmer, is one of the
key pieces when it comes to advancing DJ in Spain according to Frank Belyeu. The editor of one


of the most complete DJ teams in the sample stated clearly that by 2015, among the
problems for the proper development of DJ in Spain was the fact that newsrooms had not managed


to bring in the mixed profiles of graphic designers and programmers "who dedicate themselves
just as journalists do to producing information." By this he was making a clear reference to the


incorporation of these mixed profiles into the day-to-day work of newsrooms.


**V.3.1. Structure and dynamics of DJ teams**


All the teams portrayed during the interviews work independently,
in a more or less cross-cutting manner, on current affairs topics and without pressure from the editorial


line in the case of media outlets. However, in the latter case the flexibility of the
cross-cutting work they do can be affected by the degree of verticality of the organization they belong


to, which tends to be directly proportional to the organization's size. In this
regard, although data journalism topics in their outlets arise from the interest of both the data unit


and the sections, all these DJ teams support the sections of the newsroom on a daily basis
when required to do so, which usually involves visualizations, data structuring and analysis. In the case


of digital native outlets, there is reciprocity of support according to their size. At _Eldiario.es_ the DJ team
tends to turn to the sections when they are overwhelmed by their projects, as David


Ruiz noted:


"Day to day, a data journalism project comes up, something minor: a
visualization, a map. An attempt is made to produce it within the _Eldiario.es_ workflow. But when


we want to produce something with a bit more substance, we have to call on the rest of the people
to get a little more work capacity."


Belén Picazo, referring to the DJ team as _el Lab_ and its collaboration with the rest of the
sections of the outlet, stated that ideas "do not arise only from El Lab, we are not just one


department: people from all sections come together to research, to create things—
we are quite well supported by all the sections that make up _Eldiario.es_." For its part,


the data unit of _Elconfidencial.com_ supports the other sections on a daily basis depending on
the type of structured information provided by the sections. According to Jesús Escudero, the data unit


"can take charge of data collection or only of visualization when
the section already has the information structured: it depends on the project or the story." In turn,


as Adrián Blanco states, this unit relies on Elconfidencial.lab when
producing certain types of content without losing its independence as a department: "we are


independent from the newsroom, but it uses us when it needs database analysis.
It is a two-way relationship: graphics and databases. With the lab we develop much more


complex products."


A characteristic shared by all the data units of the Spanish media outlets in the sample is
that, alongside collaboration with the different sections, there is an independent agenda that


maintains the unit's own production. In addition, all of them are itinerant within the structure
of their organization. Even El Mundo Data collaborates with the different sections according to


their requirements, although due to the vertical structure of this large outlet,
it does not receive support from them as _Eldiario.es_ does. On the other hand, since _El_


_Mundo_ has no innovation lab, it has no one within the outlet to turn to for support in producing
more complex content, as _Elconfidencial.com_ does. The


vertical structure of _El Mundo_, unlike the structure of the other two digital
outlets, gives greater weight to the one-way flow from sections to the data unit,


meaning that the team's cross-cutting scope, even in the choice of topics, can be subject
to approval by section heads:


"We propose topics, you gravitate toward the ones you like most and keep current events in mind. If I
want to work on something for two months, I have to find an anniversary or something to use


as a hook to develop that information… sometimes you are just given the topic" (Hugo Garrido)


However, and precisely because of its large size and track record, _El Mundo_ has participated since
late 2015 in the European network of in-depth journalism collaborations _European_


_Investigative Collaborations_ (EIC), to which it invariably assigns one of the data journalists from El Mundo Data.
This tends to affect the day-to-day production of DJ pieces, but benefits medium- and long-term


products that are set independently on the agenda.


For its part, since the CEPID was—at the time of the interview—an independent
investigative journalism and data organization, it has no verticality problems whatsoever in its


structure. They are few in number and work together, in a fully cross-cutting manner, are paid by project,
and are immune to what its editor Frank Belyeu called coercion on media by "economic


power: 'economic interests of banks, of advertising'." However, DJ
pieces are not their main source of income: "the main form of income is


training" (Javier Galán).


**V.4. The future of Spanish DJ within the structure and dynamics of the media**


The coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo Garrido, highlighted that the future of data journalism
in Spain is uncertain and pointed to the scant examples of existing DJ teams.


Specifically, at his outlet "the situation is somewhat uncertain due to company dynamics
and the shareholder structure of the institution." In fact, from the founding of El Mundo Data until


the time of the interview, _El Mundo_ had already had three different directors due to this
balance of forces between shareholders and the company. According to Garrido, each director came with


their own agenda and projects to be taken into consideration, changing the working relationships
and inertia of the entire staff and, above all, of the data unit: "not all of them were aware of


the benefits and optimal functioning of El Mundo Data." This situation of DJ in
large organizations with vertical structures, as identified by Garrido, is consistent with what


Frank Belyeu said about how little data journalism is practiced in
newsrooms: "it is still hard for certain levels within newspapers, within the traditional structures, to


believe in it." In this regard, Adrián Blanco stated that Spanish DJ "has a lot of room for
improvement and expansion," in reference to the few Spanish outlets with data units.


This uncertain future of DJ in Spain in relation to the current situation within the structure and
dynamics of the media is not only a reality in large legacy institutions, but also in digital native


outlets. In the case of _Elconfidencial.com_,
Jesús Escudero stated: "data journalism in Spain is in an incipient state:


we are the first data unit in Spain and have only been around two years, so
we can say that we are not fully developed"—a quote that must be understood in the


context of the moment when the interviews were conducted. For her part,
from _Eldiario.es_ Belén Picazo stated that in Spain "right now [2015] we are doing in DJ


what we can, but I believe it will be of greater quality and of course, larger outlets
will dedicate their economic efforts to covering it."


All the interviewees stated that in order to improve the state of Spanish data journalism,
the majority of outlets would have to commit to it. All highlighted the


DJ work done outside the media, such as that of activist organizations CIVIO and OpenKratio,
the entrepreneurial collective Outliers Collective, and


specifically funded projects such as _TheMigrantsfiles.com_. In this regard, Javier Galán
stated that the data journalism group in Spain is a small group in which contact among its members is


constant, and that "with some delay relative to the small groups, media outlets have started
to create data units." However, as commented by the coordinator of El Mundo Data and former member of CIVIO,


Hugo Garrido, in 2016 _El Mundo_ laid off one of the most differentiated profiles on the
DJ team and the digital native _El Español_ closed its data unit, so the commitment to


creating data journalism units in media outlets has remained uncertain.


Another problem highlighted by Frank Belyeu regarding data journalism within the structure and
dynamics of traditional media is that it cannot fall into the trap of taking on daily tasks,


serving every purpose, like a one-person-band journalist: "you cannot ask the data journalist
to produce a daily news item. That could be a problem into which DJ falls." Jesús Escudero stated that the main


difficulties in practicing data journalism is time: "at minimum a quick report takes us two days." For
his part, David Ruiz stressed that day to day he produces minor DJ projects: "a


visualization, a map," but when "you want to produce something more complex, you have to call on
the rest of the newsroom." That is, there are difficulties in incorporating DJ pieces into the


daily routine of a media outlet, even if that outlet is a digital native.


For Adrián Blanco, one of these difficulties related to the working criteria
imposed in newsrooms arises in the choice of topics: "often the newspapers themselves do not


commit to certain data journalism topics that I consider more important than other day-to-day topics."
According to all the interviewees, most of these difficulties arise from


the immediacy demanded by the media outlet when dealing with current affairs topics. Blanco
links these difficulties to two specific factors: 1—


the few data units that exist in Spain, and 2—their size relative to the rest of the
organization they belong to: "In Spain there is data journalism at


_Elconfidencial.com_, _Eldiario.es_ and _El Mundo_, but these are very small units relative to
the whole organization, a very small percentage." All of this leads both to the imposition of topics


by the sections and to the investment of time in journalistic pieces that are not part of the data unit,
leaving very little time for the production of DJ pieces.


Of all the organizations in the sample, the only one that was not affected by this type of
problem was the CEPID, since this collective's way of working is project-based and it is not


accountable to sections that form part of a larger structure.


**V.5. Spanish DJ is in an incipient state**


Most of the interviewees link the difficulties for the practice of data journalism
in Spain to its incipient state and compare it with that of other parts of the world,


especially the Anglo-Saxon world. The editor of the CEPID, Frank Belyeu, highlighted the
existence of quality DJ in the USA that can be funded philanthropically without


any need to belong to any traditional media structure:


"There are clear examples of DJ being funded by disinterested philanthropic organizations,
such as the Pulitzer-winning _ProPublica_. I am always inspired by them: they have


entirely philanthropic funding and produce the highest-quality investigative journalism
in the whole of the USA. There are examples, although not yet in Spain. Maybe


because there is no outlet like _ProPublica_, but it has happened in the USA."


Throughout the interviews with the CEPID members, it became clear that the _ProPublica_
model has indeed been their inspiration, though without the philanthropic economic support that


has allowed _ProPublica_ to continue its work to the present day. Taking into
consideration that Javier Galán stated that the main source of income for the CEPID was


training, and that regarding DJ pieces they have "worked sometimes for zero euros and at other
times for large sums," the lack of economic viability of data journalism projects at the CEPID is


another reason for the incipient state of this discipline, according to these data journalists. In this regard,
the developer at _Eldiario.es_, David Ruiz, stated that "you have to find the economic impact from


practicing DJ." On the subject of the possibility of freelance journalism
in Spain, his colleague Belén Picazo asked herself the


following question: "how much can you really get paid for a DJ piece like the one I have
spent an entire month working on?" Picazo shares with the journalist from El Mundo Data, Paula


Guisado, her vision of a better DJ in Spain through the inclusion of this discipline in
newsrooms. According to Guisado, the incipient state of Spanish DJ will improve in the future:


"Data journalism in Spain is in an incipient state, in which it is already beginning to
consolidate in some outlets, others are starting to get involved, and some


have not yet taken the initiative. Nevertheless, I believe that data journalism has arrived on the
Spanish media landscape to stay."


The economic factor of Spanish media outlets works against the state of DJ and its future,
as many data journalists with mixed profiles have had to emigrate in order to


practice this discipline properly. On this subject, Picazo stated the following: "I would like
to believe that the future of DJ in Spain is good, although the best journalists who have ever


dedicated themselves to this have ultimately had to leave because here they have either lost
their jobs or are not allowed to do what they want."


Another factor working against the state of data journalism is its degree of innovation,
since the most cutting-edge DJ pieces in Spain are seen by their own creators as


insufficiently innovative work when compared to work produced in other parts of the world. According to Jesús
Escudero, "data journalism in Spain is in an incipient state" and although "we are still


moving forward doing things that are innovative in Spain, in other [foreign] outlets
they were done two or three years ago." This data journalist from


_Elconfidencial.com_ is referring to the Prometómetro, a database produced by his data unit
at _Elconfidencial.com_ that records all the economically quantifiable electoral promises of the


political parties. Escudero highlighted that this data report
"is based on what _The Guardian_ did in its Australian edition two years ago. So


we are still growing and we are incipient." This comparison with other countries is a
common thread in almost all the interviews. Frank Belyeu stated that there is still enormous ground


to cover in Spanish DJ: "right now it is at least five years behind the United
States." In this case, Belyeu advocated for a differentiation of Spanish DJ from that of


the USA: "unfortunately in Spain we are not going to innovate, we are going to follow the lead of
the United States. They are way ahead and we will follow their steps. It would be great if


we managed to differentiate ourselves, to do something innovative—that would be brilliant."


At this point the editor of the CEPID linked project funding to the degree of
innovation in data journalism. After stating that in Spain "we are used to everything being free,"


he highlighted the cultural difference between Spain and other parts of the world when he stated that "if
crowdfunding works in other parts of the world, then it can work here too." Despite this, Belyeu


showed a somewhat optimistic nuance when he stated that in 2015 we were at a high point with the creation
in mainstream media of data journalism sections: "this is the year, everything is starting now." This is consistent


with the assertions made in 2016 by the coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo Garrido,
when he spoke of the incipient state of Spanish DJ and the stalling of its first impulse:


"in digital media there was more or less a boom and things are not going very well now."


Throughout most of the interviews, it became clear that the comparison with DJ in the
Anglo-Saxon world has at least two constants: 1—project funding, and 2—the lack of journalistic


innovation. Both were used to indicate that Spanish DJ is in an incipient state.


**V.6. Culture of transparency: the problem of accessing public information in Spain**


The open data–DJ relationship has been one of the most commonly cited factors by the
journalists in the sample as a direct cause of the lack of innovation and lower journalistic


quality in the news production process of Spanish DJ. After stating that open data is much more
developed in other countries, such as Chile, Jesús


Escudero highlighted journalistic pieces resulting from "the analysis of more than 300,000
public records" in the USA and the UK, comparing them with pieces produced in Spain.


He then stated the following: "I do not think we have reached the point in Spanish DJ where
major investigations have been published. There is still a long way to go—we are


very far behind Anglo-Saxon outlets like _The Washington Post_ and _The Guardian_."


Similarly, most of the interviewees linked the thematic gaps in Spanish DJ
to the lack of public information. Paula Guisado, of El Mundo Data, stated that "access


to data is often a problem when approaching a topic," while the director of the CEPID, Frank Belyeu,
stated that in Spain "data journalism has gaps due to the lack of data: I do not think the gaps


are on its own part. Spain is very far behind on this." In this way Spanish DJ was again
positioned behind that practiced in other parts of the world. According to the data journalist at _Eldiario.es_,


Belén Picazo, "Yes, there are gaps in DJ, but not in its treatment but in the supply of information. There
are things that cannot be investigated because there is no database or source that publishes that


information."


In this regard the data journalists highlighted several "thematic gaps" due to a
problem of access to public data—for example in the case of public education in relation to


the Catholic Church, the Church-State relationship in Spain, military spending or the use of public funds
by Spanish military institutions, as well as access to the income and asset declaration data of members of Congress.


On the subject of information on State expenditure with the Church, Frank Belyeu asked:
"to what extent is the State secular?" For Belén Picazo, the gaps in the Church-State


relationship can be observed when investigating state-subsidized private schools:


"When we did the piece on state-subsidized schools at the MediaLab we investigated using
the information provided by the Ministry of Education, which is basically nil (…) the


Church issue, I think, is not complex—it is the following: the Church does not provide information, so
it is not the same to access a state-subsidized school as to access the Church directly."


The editor of the CEPID, Frank Belyeu, expected some improvement with the Transparency Law,
"but there are gaps: for example, the military. They hide behind security and therefore have


reasons to deny an information request. This undermines investigation."


Another difficulty in accessing public information according to several of the interviewees
is the portals where it is published. David Ruiz stated that "there is data that is not


where it should be." Furthermore, most data are not structured in a uniform database
that makes it easy to work with them. The coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo


Garrido, mentioned this when explaining one of the most complex and difficult-to-produce
DJ pieces done by his data unit:


"In terms of working with a massive volume of data, a project we started in March and continued:
the declaration of assets, income and activities of members of parliament. I was


interested in it because I come from CIVIO and have a more activist streak. We also went through
a very turbulent period when transparency was on everyone's lips, and we ran into


the transparency problem in Spain: a scanned PDF… we had to
enter the information one by one into a database. It is a systematization task that
Congress is not doing. And then you realize that nobody oversees any of it."


Belén Picazo highlighted this technical difficulty as a sign of opacity or lack of transparency
on the part of the government when she stated that in Spain there is "no kind of open government


or data that are truly important." Throughout her interview, this journalist
also made reference to the congressional asset declaration as an example of institutional opacity:


"The asset declarations of members of Congress are shameful: you cannot run an
OCR because they do not follow the same standards—some declare one thing and not another,


some declare everything and others declare nothing. DJ is done from a source in which
they are offering you PDFs that have nothing in common with each other: they are not open


and they claim to be."


According to these data journalists, the systematization of unstructured data is a task
that takes a great deal of time and, since it is public data, it is not something the journalist


should have to do: the data should be fully open. This is consistent with what the
data journalist at _Elconfidencial.com_, Adrián Blanco, said when he stated that


journalistic investigation takes precedence over leaks in DJ: "ideally, public databases are
used more and more original databases are created from public information than from leaks." However,


and due to institutional opacity, this is not the case in Spanish data journalism. Frank Belyeu stated that
"there is far more leak journalism than DJ in Spain" and that in this way "journalism loses quality:


leaked information is always going to be self-serving." His colleague at the CEPID, Javier Galán,
holds a similar view when he states that "the issues that have had the greatest impact in


the media have been fed by leaks," although "the vast majority of DJ articles are based on
journalistic investigation or the observation of what data public authorities publish." In Spain, as


Picazo stated explicitly, the Spanish government's open data portal "publishes obvious things that
in the end are simply making the data journalist's work more difficult," so leaks have carried


more weight in Spanish DJ than investigations based on public data, owing to the lack of
supply of public information.


**V.6.1. Non-compliance with and inadequacy of the Transparency Law**


Based on the responses of almost all the interviewees, the Transparency Law and the
government's transparency portal do not help the practice of data journalism as they should, and


in some cases even hinder journalistic investigation in general. For the
developer on the DJ team at _Eldiario.es_, David Ruiz, the transparency portal is


inadequate and would need to undergo a process of greater data opening: "if they tell us that
that is everything and that is as far as they will go, it will obviously be inadequate." From a


technical standpoint, Ruiz stated that "the portal itself does focus on information that it is not logical for it to
contain" and that it has a serious conceptual flaw: "it should be a key to understanding the


rest of the public information that exists, not simply try to embrace all information."


For the journalist from El Mundo Data, Paula Guisado, the Transparency Law is a starting point
with a long way yet to go: "the transparency portal needs more political will." Like Guisado, many


journalists in the sample linked the inadequacy of the law and the portal to political will.
The editor of the CEPID, Frank Belyeu, stated that the law "is a first step that is very, very far


from what a Transparency Law should eventually become. I think it is simply an electoral act: the
government's transparency is totally inadequate." For his part, the coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo Garrido,


stated that the transparency portal "encapsulates how this government understood
transparency and accountability: for show. The law is manifestly in need of improvement."


This perception of inadequacy of the law and the portal is also linked in most
cases to the lack of data openness, the poor functioning of the portal, and failures in


responses to public information requests. Many examples from the interviewees
point in this direction, with some even considering that rather than helping, the law and the portal


hinder their work as data journalists. Belén Picazo commented the following on the matter:


"I think the transparency portal makes the work more difficult. On the first day we said:
'look, there is really no information here that we had not already seen'—that is truly


relevant to citizens. The information provided consists of very institutional things. They give
glimpses of data, of information, but you cannot investigate further, produce stories: to


continue you need to request more and in the end, it is almost always a refusal."


From all the interviews it becomes evident how data journalists perceive the
main problems with practicing DJ in Spain as being related to the delay in responding to


public information requests and their denial by the administration,
even among those journalists who consider the Transparency Law adequate. In this regard,


Javier Galán stated that "the Spanish Transparency Law is adequate in its mechanisms and deadlines,
but in practice it is not being applied correctly: the deadlines are not met—I have verified this."


For his part, Hugo Garrido stated that "sometimes you depend on an information request and
we have to play with the legislative deadlines: maybe you do not have access to the information


you requested for up to a month or two months." This means that even in cases where the law
can serve the production of a DJ piece in Spain, the culture of transparency within the public


administration prevents compliance with the law. Galán stated the following regarding the speed with
which some requests are denied: "I have the feeling that, in response to some


requests, everything is based on passing the buck in under a month." For his part,
Jesús Escudero stressed that the civil servants responsible for providing public information try


to use excuses of inadmissibility orders or limits: "the limits should be the exception and
not the rule. But in the public administration it is the rule: deny information just in case


that information might cause problems."


Every time the administration takes longer than the established time to respond to public
information requests, the Transparency Law is violated, and all the Spanish data journalists


interviewed have been through this situation on multiple occasions. However, this
non-compliance with the law becomes fully effective in cases where the administration


does respond to public information requests, since according to most of the
interviewees, the denial of these requests through the portal is not the exception but the rule,


which reveals its deficiency and reaffirms its inadequacy. According to Belén Picazo, "the current
Transparency Law is totally inadequate. The formats in which information is published are also


incorrect, and not everything that should be published is published. You can request information, but
it takes an extremely long time." In a very similar vein, the data journalist at _Elconfidencial.com_,


Adrián Blanco, stated the following:


"The Transparency Law is clearly inadequate, quite deficient. They have a month to
respond to you and silence counts as a response—that is, they are under no obligation to reply, and


many times the information does not arrive. In most cases they deny the information."


Jesús Escudero reiterated that this problem occurs because "there needs to be a culture
of publishing data in Spain" and "it is not so much a problem of lack of legislation but of a culture of


transparency—of the public administration realizing that all public information belongs to
citizens and they must make it accessible."


**V.6.2. The Organic Law on Data Protection as an obstacle to requesting public information**


Another recurring problem throughout all the interviews regarding the denial of
public information requests is the use of the Organic Law on Data Protection


(LOPD): the majority of denied requests are on grounds of data protection,
with the LOPD cited as the reason for withholding information. In this regard, Adrián


Blanco stated that the administration always hides behind the data protection law, which
"is outrageous in Spain. It is quite bad, it is like a barrier that needs to be resolved, because


you lose information that this law prevents you from obtaining." For Paula Guisado, the data protection
law "is often an excuse invoked to avoid providing information that should be public. It is certainly


important to take data protection into account, but it is abused as a shield against the obligation
to share inconvenient information." For his part, Hugo Garrido appealed to the legal hierarchy of


the Transparency Law and the LOPD to assert that "the LOPD takes precedence over the Transparency
Law. Given how the law was drafted, it could not be otherwise. It is a framework law for certain areas."


To explain the use of the LOPD to deny public information access requests, Escudero linked it to
the inadequacy of the Transparency Law and the fear of investigative journalism on the part


of the administration:


"I think it is good that there is a Transparency Law for the first time, but it has
drawbacks: you cannot access emails, information. Private data is heavily taken into


account when denying information and, in the case of investigative journalism,
I still see a great deal of fear on the part of the administration when it comes to disclosing that


public data."


The data journalist at _Eldiario.es_, Belén Picazo, noted that information about senior civil servants
should not be denied to journalists, since "there is certain information that, if it can provide more


context or information, should always be made available, as long as the privacy of individuals
is respected." The CEPID also had numerous information requests denied when they involved data


on public officials. Its editor, Frank Belyeu, stated that this should not happen and that all public
officials should be subject to full public scrutiny: "everything that can handle public money must be


transparent." His colleague Javier Galán gave a similar response regarding the denials of his public
information requests involving officials: "I would like to know the schedule, meetings and


remuneration of the civil servant, and the public servant should be fine with that. Their private life is
of no interest to me, as long as it has no connection to their position." The data journalist at _Elconfidencial.com_,


Adrián Blanco, noted that the predominance of the LOPD over the Transparency Law
is not something exclusive to public information requests involving public officials,


but is a reality that arises in almost any topic:


"I requested information about secondary school students' grades and they hid behind the fact that
they could not give me the names in order to deny me all the information. These are absurd situations


where, just in case, they deny your public information request on the basis of the Organic Law on
Data Protection. I think it is quite a serious problem."


His colleague Jesús Escudero reiterated the non-compliance with the Transparency Law
by civil servants by citing a document signed by the Transparency Council and the Spanish Data Protection Agency


establishing the following criterion: "for senior officials, personal data must be subject to the Transparency
Law. A separate question is whether the law is actually complied with or not." However, he added the nuance that


"another question is whether this private data should extend to all public servants: a
doctor, a teacher, etc.," although after reaffirming that in Spain this should be the case at


least for deputy directors general and higher positions, he highlighted examples from other
countries where this does in fact extend to all civil servants:


"There are examples, such as in Chile, where the transparency portal publishes everything that
a public servant earns, from the head of government down to the teacher in a


rural school. In detail each month and annually. And there you do not see that fear, that protection of
privacy: it is understood that what is public takes precedence over what is private."


Frank Belyeu highlighted the absurdity of the situation in Spain when one considers that
personal data protection should serve, above all, when there are giants like Google or


Facebook holding all that information "who use your data to sell it: the law must be
implemented and strengthened against that type of applications or programs that can know


everything about you."


**V.6.3. The Transparency Council, its sanctioning capacity, and the lack of independent arbitration bodies at the regional level**


Another problem for the proper compliance with the Transparency Law in Spain
according to the interviewees is the fact that the Transparency Council has no sanctioning capacity.


After stating that in this body "resolutions are executive, but you are not required to comply with them,"
Jesús Escudero noted that one is dependent on the goodwill of the administration if the Council rules against it:


"There are already several administrative legal proceedings open against a ruling of the Transparency
Council: for example, the Transparency Council urged RTVE to publish its Eurovisión


invoices for this year, and RTVE has taken the Transparency Council to court
(…) we have reached the paradox of a public company having taken another


public institution to court to avoid publishing public information—namely, the taxpayer money
that was used to fund the Eurovision festival."


According to some journalists in the sample, the inadequacy of the law and its non-compliance
becomes even more acute and complex at the regional level. The data journalist at _Eldiario.es_, Belén


Picazo, encountered particularly long delays when requesting information from
the Community of Madrid: "with the Community of Madrid you submit a request and they can


take months to reply, and then that information is not in the format you need, so you have to make
another request. The bureaucratic timelines and deadlines are excruciatingly slow, endless." Hugo


Garrido went deeper into the causes of the problem of the inadequacy of the Transparency Law
at the regional level. After noting that the law does not apply directly to the Autonomous Communities, he


commented that in their internal regulations "they incorporate in some way the spirit of transparency
and apply it as they see fit," so there is no independent body to resolve administrative


appeals when a public information request is denied:


"What I find in my day-to-day work, in this case with the [regional] assemblies and legislative
chambers, is that since each of them has its own regulations, an internal body resolves the appeals,


and if you want to appeal through administrative channels it is they themselves who resolve it—
so everything stays in-house."


**V.6.6. The Transparency Law as a minimum-standards law**


The coordinator of El Mundo Data, Hugo Garrido, explained that all the situations outlined
above arise because "in Spain there is a minimum-standards Transparency Law (…) there is a


very significant problem: when the law was drafted, in anchoring it to the constitution, it was
not included in the block of fundamental rights."


For his part, Frank Belyeu highlighted the inadequacy of the Transparency Law by comparing the
initial intentions before it was enacted with its final result:


"Regarding the Transparency Law, the preliminary draft was going to be a good one, like the United
Kingdom's, and now it has ended up as something that is practically meaningless: when any


information request can be denied by a simple administrative procedure, it means that
the law is not going to bear fruit."


Garrido underscored how deplorable this situation is when one also takes into account that, within the
EU, "Spain arrived among the last" to pass a Transparency Law, meaning it


"would have been good to incorporate all the prior experience." Recurring, as is common across all
the interviews, to the comparison with the Anglo-Saxon world, he stated that in contrast to


the Spanish case "the United Kingdom is also a country that came to it late—2005, 2006—but
has produced one of the most forward-looking laws." To explain these inconsistencies


surrounding the Transparency Law, the coordinator of El Mundo Data stated that they are connected
to the political will of the government that enacted it: "three years after the law was passed,


the implementing regulation has not been approved, and therefore what you have are unmistakable
signals of what the genuine intention is—whether this exercise will be effective or


whether the exercise of that right will be full."


**V.7. The view of international data journalists and experts: definition of data journalism and the profile of the data journalist**


For American data journalist Steve Doig, DJ differs from traditional journalism
essentially in its technical dimension: it is still journalism practiced with skepticism and


informational balance so that each side can tell its story, even if it uses
computer tools to obtain and analyze data sets:


"It is not different from regular journalism, except that you are using data as a source, in the
same way that journalists have always used documents and people as sources of


information (…) the additional skills are that the data journalist can use
computer tools to acquire data, usually from government sources, but


not always. It is the ability to use computer skills to find patterns in the
data—what is usually called interviewing the data."


According to this conception, in DJ data sets are obtained mostly from
public sources and are used to find patterns that reveal newsworthy topics that


we could not otherwise see. Steve Doig does not differentiate DJ from PP or from
CAR, and encompasses the latter two within DJ. According to this view, he began to practice


as a data journalist in the early 1980s when "the USA already had a long tradition
of data-driven investigations dating back to the 1960s," in the era of Philip Meyer.


Doig emphasized that DJ began to emerge in Europe approximately 15 years ago,
around the time _El País_ started inviting him and other colleagues to run summer courses for journalists.


This is a conception very similar to that held by the professor of journalism José Luis Dader,
who stated that precision journalism and DJ are the same thing: "there is no difference whatsoever."


For this academic it is a matter of fashion—at one time one label is used and at another a different one:
"PP or data journalism is any type of journalistic information that uses and systematically


analyzes numerical and alphanumerical data to generate content of journalistic interest." While Steve Doig
emphasized that the basic profile of the data journalist is to use computer tools such as Excel to find patterns in


data, Dader went further in what he perceives as PP and DJ by connecting this
pattern-finding that Doig mentions to the social scientist's search for trends:


"What distinguishes PP is a more sociological perspective, close to academics in the
social sciences when observing current affairs. Many times the lens of a traditional


journalist is marked by the search for the exceptional, the unusual, and the
narration of events that are also relevant to the economy or politics. The precision or data


journalist goes, rather, in search of the opposite: what is the real trend of something that is
not well understood. Their story lies in discovering structures… such as what the


statistical structures of poverty are, whether it is a trend in a country and whether that
situation is associated with certain variables."


It is thus that for this academic the profile of the data journalist is not characterized by a more
technological profile than that of the traditional journalist, but by a more sociological vision of


journalism. Both Doig and Dader not only agree that precision journalism and data journalism are
the same thing, but also do not consider all PP or DJ to be part of IJ. In this regard, Doig stated that


today some DJ stories are the standard of everyday journalism and involve no investigation whatsoever:


"As in the USA politicians running for office have to file all kinds of information about who
is donating to the campaign, it is now routine for reporters covering elections to wait


until the next campaign finance filing deadline to take that data and analyze it—that is not
even investigative journalism."


For his part, Dader, while agreeing with Doig that these stories are not IJ pieces, dismissed them
as "very superficial" to be considered data or precision journalism—regardless of the fact


that they were produced with technological advances unimaginable in the heyday of PP:


"Some very spectacular data visualizations are presented, and they also demonstrate the
potential of new technologies for presenting dynamic and interactive tables impossible


to produce when the press was on paper… but they have been made simply with a small table
published and circulated by some public institution where nothing more has been done: there has been


a technological display of what to do with some numbers that were already in an Excel spreadsheet."


For this professor they are merely "stories called data stories in the media." The Spanish academic
justifies this position by considering that these stories lack a perspective closer to the social sciences.


According to Dader, there is precision or data journalism when the following condition is met:


"It is the journalist who imagines what kind of data is needed to answer certain questions,
is capable of finding data that can answer those questions, and can even relate


different databases to create new levels of comparison that were not present in the
original data."


To explain that DJ stories can exist that are not investigative journalism,
Dader stated that it is possible to do data journalism that does not enter into conflict with


sources, simply by analyzing some data systematically and creating information that
would not otherwise be discovered—that is, with the journalist as creator of information rather than


merely reproducing or visualizing it, regardless of the technological tools
used.


Throughout the entire interview, Steve Doig expressed a vision of DJ as a tool
for doing journalism better—a way of seeing this discipline similar to that expressed by


Finnish data journalist Temo Teebest. This journalist from YLE stated that "DJ is a tool and
investigative journalism has always dealt with data. What happens is that DJ is a fashionable term,


understandably." Unlike Doig, Teebest seems to equate data journalism with IJ: in his conception
there appear to be no differences in the nature of the data in investigative journalism from years past


and that which forms part of current DJ—something very similar to what Dader expressed. However,
Teebest seems to contradict this conception when he reveals that many of the


major stories he considers data stories are not investigative ones, going so far as to state that
"both investigation and the creative use of data are very important in DJ:


both can produce great stories, some more in the realm of entertainment than investigation."


The conception held by several of the interviewees that IJ has always used data and that DJ
is a tool stems from statements such as that made by the professor and researcher of data journalism and


transparency at the University of Jyväskylä, Heikki Kuutti, that "data has no use on its own" and "you
have to know how to use it." It is thus that for Kuutti, DJ is above all journalism and involves the


"collection and structuring of large quantities of information for analysis," from which valid
conclusions can be drawn. Although he does not consider PP and DJ to be the same thing, he agrees with Dader in not


considering many stories labeled "data stories" in the media to be data journalism:


"I am greatly depressed by the current attitude of some journalists toward the use of data—
they have no idea of the purpose of collecting it and creating stories with it. They only do it because


it is possible, the same type of work that anyone could do. I always tell them that analyzing the
results is only the beginning of journalistic work, but they generally publish the results, the initial phase,


as a story in itself. They should be doing something more."


In this regard, what separates DJ from other branches of journalism is the
methodological perspective that Dader linked to an approach drawn from the social sciences. However,


in addition to conceiving of DJ as part of IJ, Kuutti emphasized two fundamental aspects of
DJ that are directly related to a change in the nature of data:


"It is actually an old idea… When I visited the USA 20 years ago, there were some
computer tapes—not this kind of big data—but if they had not collected all this


information in electronic formats it would have been impossible to discover their stories, so
technically it has changed, but the idea remains the same. Access to public information has also


changed a great deal, since official information is stored in electronic formats."


The Finnish researcher explicitly acknowledges that the large quantities of information
have changed and it is not the same type of "big data." Furthermore, by highlighting that


public information has also evolved, he suggests that the speed and variety of data
have as well: it is something implicit in the way IJ is practiced today and not in the way it was done


20 years ago in the USA, when CAR was in full swing.


**V.7.1. Quantification of newsworthy facts and reinforcement of journalistic values in DJ**


All the international data journalists and experts interviewed highlighted the
importance for DJ of the quantification of newsworthy facts, which is in turn


subject to journalistic values. In this regard, after emphasizing that, above all,
DJ is journalism, the professor and researcher of data journalism at the University of


Jyväskylä, Turo Uskali, described DJ as journalism based on large quantities of data
as follows:
It deals with data and involves a great deal of mathematics and statistics. Data is only a
tool for journalism, it is nothing on its own, so you have to use it.

It is about handling large quantities of information, gathering it and structuring it for
analysis in order to reach some kind of conclusion."

His departmental colleague, Heikki Kuutti, defined DJ while also describing the profile of
these professionals. He emphasized that, despite the technical profile, DJ is journalism: "Sometimes a
data journalist can also program, so they can write code, sometimes they are
known for their visualizations, but the most important thing is that they be good as a journalist.
Let's not forget that part."

According to all the international experts and data journalists, objectivity is one of the most
reinforced values in DJ. In all of this they agree with what all Spanish data journalists said,
whether through the oft-mentioned reproducibility of information, verification, or _fact-checking_. Doig
expressed it very concisely when he said that DJ as a tool allows the journalist to "go beyond
mere anecdote" and "put evidence" in their stories: "DJ has added value and credibility
to journalism." The director of Access Info Europe, Helen Darbishire, clarified from the
perspective of transparency activism that, although this objectivity pursued by DJ
was also pursued by investigative journalism in the twentieth century "and even in the late
nineteenth century," with the rise of _fake news_ and other phenomena typical of the big data era, the
fact-checking linked to the practice of DJ has become more necessary today
than ever:

"Many times people have prejudices and data journalism will verify the
sources… if we have better information, the idea is to make decisions with better
knowledge and thus improve them, as well as the debates. That is what is happening in Europe with
issues like migration and Catalonia right now: there is so much information and so much false data that it is
essential to have journalists doing _fact checking._ I saw many journalists
publishing false information: we need them verifying everything."

**V.7.2. Relationship between profiles, processes, and teams in DJ**

Just as the Spanish data journalists in the sample, the foreign data journalists
and experts agreed on the need for a minimum level of technical expertise
to be a data journalist. The transparency expert, Helen Darbishire, highlighted the
use of new technologies in DJ to improve journalistic quality and the reinforcement of
values such as objectivity and truthfulness of information, among others. She reiterated that what
most distinguishes the data journalist from the traditional one is technical knowledge, emphasizing, as
many other interviewees did, that knowing how to use Excel and some visualization is
fundamental: "Verify and communicate. Knowing how to use Excel means knowing how to verify better.
Knowing how to use data visualizations helps you explain, but the instincts are the same
as any good journalist's."

Similarly, all interviewees know that the existence of a single person who
covers all the technical aspects of the news production process in DJ—the definition
of data journalism and the profile of the data journalist—is not at all common. In this regard,
Finnish academic Turo Uskali stated that "fully skilled data journalists
are like unicorns: very, very rare… it is very rare to find a person who is
capable of doing everything. That is why the most common way to proceed is to form a team." His
departmental colleague at the University of Jyväskylä, Heikki Kuutti, said something similar
when speaking about the profile of the data journalist and the need for teams in DJ:

"You cannot do DJ without some kind of technical expertise… traditional journalists
have worked with words, they don't understand numbers and when they find something with numbers
it is difficult: they tend not to be mathematical people, but they are good at expressing themselves, so
they need an advanced technician who knows what can and cannot be done with
computers and to give them instructions on what they want to write about the data."

When speaking about his personal experience as a data journalist, Steve Doig highlighted his
ability to analyze data and find stories, in contrast to his capacity to
communicate them interactively and effectively, thus underlining the need for DJ teams:

"A fundamental aspect is that my particular niche in DJ is data analysis to
find stories. I never get involved in the interactive presentation and communication
part of them. There are some unicorns who can do everything, but I am definitely not one
of them: I only analyze the data, what they do after that is magic to me.
Most of those magicians also would not be as good at analyzing data and
finding stories."

For his part, the Finnish data journalist Temo Teebest went beyond highlighting his
technical expertise and emphasized that he considers himself a data journalist "because it is an easy and
understandable term these days," although "there is no such thing as a data journalist profile: it is a team
effort." It is precisely this conception of collaborative work over the profile of the
data journalist that brings him closer to the most frequently cited fundamental aspects by his
Spanish colleagues:

"There is not much difference between the data journalist and the traditional one. As we work in
a team—programmers, designers, and specialized journalists—the difference would lie in
the fact that we cooperate with other sections and teams… DJ has changed the
lone-wolf journalists. It has become quite well known that collaborative work is
better than keeping the data to oneself."

Similar to what was expressed by Spanish data journalists, Teebest explained that collaboration
is both internal and external: internal within the structure of his outlet—when collaborating with
other sections—and external outside of YLE, a fact corroborated by the Finnish academics
who have studied this phenomenon most. In this regard, Heikki Kuutti stated the following:

"In DJ you work in teams, as at _YLE_ and _Helsingin Sanomat_: they have people with
different expertise… the profile of the data journalist depends on how much they are used in the
office. I have two examples who work with everyone, so anyone can
ask them about this: data journalists can be used in the sports section,
culture, economics, or any section, there are no limits."

While Kuutti stated that the profile of the data journalist at the two largest outlets in
Finland is shaped by their collaboration with other sections—internal collaboration—his
departmental colleague at the University of Jyväskylä, Turo Uskali, highlighted the
collaborative work between the data units of the two largest media outlets in Finland, YLE and
Helsingin Sanomat. This example of external collaboration is particularly striking because it
takes place between the two outlets that compete most with each other in the Nordic country:

_"YLE_ and _Helsingin Sanomat_ have shared secrets in a Facebook group. They are two
competitors, the only organizations with data units, and they share ideas and
comments especially if they spot errors or flaws in what the other organization is
doing… Twice a year each one has a kind of visit to the other's newsroom and
they discuss what they have been doing, what type of projects they are developing, and what
they have learned, sharing these practices openly."

This conception of DJ in which collaborative work is a fundamental aspect was
identified by Dader as _Data Base Journalism_, including it within the aspects that
form part of his conception of PP. For this Spanish academic, when the journalists of
the 1980s and 1990s spoke of _Data Base Journalism_ "they were simply talking about _big data_,
only the expression had not yet been invented: the volume of data that some of those
journalists handled was also in the millions." According to Dader, the only difference is that
now data is more accessible, since collaboration was already commonplace in the
American newsrooms of those decades:

"Collaboration existed from the beginning: among experts (multidisciplinarity) and through
collaboration of different methodologies. Both were already present in that era. In
1989 a unit was being created at _The New York Times_ where a sociologist,
a computer scientist, a statistician, and a journalist all worked: all in service to the entire newspaper.
Back then it was built from the top because there was a great deal of financial capacity, now it has
been built from the bottom due to the situation of the media."

However, Dader's conception of _big data_ again does not take into account
the nature of today's data, despite having emphasized that with new
technologies it is more accessible. The comparison this academic draws between the
collaborative work of current DJ and the creation in 1989 of multidisciplinary teams in
some US newsrooms also fails to account for the exo-institutional collaboration
of today's teams: as Teebest said, in DJ the figure of the lone-wolf journalist is increasingly rare.

**V.7.2.1. The Panama Papers as an ideal example: the evolution of the culture of transparency in journalistic practices**

An ideal example of the need for collaboration in DJ due to the nature of
today's data was mentioned by all the experts and foreign data journalists
in the sample: the _Panama Papers_. In that regard, Finnish
academic Turo Uskali highlighted the exo-institutional character of this collaboration and
its implications for journalistic practices:

"The Panama Papers, which of course have been the largest leak that has ever
occurred, have been the most powerful demonstration of the power of datasets, and
they have also reinforced the need for cooperation between different newsrooms. Not even
the newsroom with the most resources can, on its own, make news out of this data:
they need to share and cooperate with other colleagues, and there were between 300 and 400 journalists
cooperating on the Panama Papers: in a way, I think it is an example of a culture
of transparency in journalism."

This does not mean that DJ is confined solely to the processing of large
quantities of datasets that can only be handled through large-scale collaboration.
According to Steve Doig, large-scale data journalism pieces and smaller ones
share similar ways of working, so the size of the data is not a determining factor:

"The Panama Papers are another example of an enormous amount of unprocessed data
that requires very special techniques to be able to organize and parse
across several journalists and outputs. In those cases it is not trivial, but a great many
other stories have been done with Excel on one computer. Excel can work very well
with more than a million records and normally you will have to deal with just a couple of
thousands: it is not a matter of quantity or scale."

Although DJ pieces can be much smaller, the most important point stressed by
data journalists and experts is the cultural change that these practices imply in
the values of journalism. According to Finnish academic Heikki Kuutti, while the nature of
data in the big data era has much to do with the possibility of large-scale investigations such as
the Panama Papers, it is the cultural practices regarding these technologies and access to information that
make them possible:

"It is very good to have this kind of environment—_big data_—because it has a great deal to do with
technology, but perhaps more to do with access to information. If technology on its own
is not enough, you have to have good access. There are technical advances and good
practices in the use of these technologies."

Thus, beyond the large quantities of data that Dader associated with the _Database Journalism_ of PP
and the collaborative work of _The New York Times_ in the 1980s and 1990s of
the twentieth century, the new culture of transparency that forms part of the DJ studied by Uskali and
Kuutti is not only the result of the democratization of technological tools, but also
has to do with a new journalistic culture. For Turo Uskali, DJ is changing the
values of transparency in journalistic practices, something directly linked to
transparency activist groups operating within a logic characteristic of the free _software_
culture:

"DJ is changing the values of transparency in journalistic practices, because it is
something new when you share the sources you have collected: you do not usually share
knowledge of how to conduct interviews, but in some ways datasets
represent different types of possibilities. However, there is a gap between the ideal of
everything being transparent and reality: sometimes some datasets are published and there is not total transparency yet, but there is a kind of force that is
thinking and opening up to provide new opportunities: the idea behind the data culture
that comes with the free _software_ culture."

For the Finnish expert, the open data movement that has so greatly influenced the
development of data journalism has an "ethos" that forms part of the free _software_ culture.
For the transparency expert, Helen Darbishire, it is the open data movement
together with the development of new technologies that has made possible a DJ as we
conceive of it today, through the impetus of investigative journalism:

"Now with new technologies governments are managing much more data, so
journalists can do new things with data, as CIVIO does. New
technologies have provided many more possibilities for processing and understanding data in
easier ways, which is very important: if we have better information, the idea is
to make decisions based on better knowledge and thus improve them, as well as the debates…
In the development of IP we have had the possibility of having access to information
available proactively or reactively from governments. Here the open data movement has
pushed things along a great deal."

This cultural transformation in journalistic practices is still fully underway; it is not yet
an established fact in most large or small newsrooms, however much
they may have data journalism teams. In this regard, when Uskali referred to
the state of DJ in Finland, he emphasized that this discipline, as a process, is coexisting with
other practices that are not characteristic of the culture of transparency linked to DJ and its
tendency toward collaborative work. Once again, this Finnish academic used the ideal
example of DJ represented by the Panama Papers to speak of "old policies" in outlets with
data units both in Finland and the US:

"What is interesting is that, for example, in Finland only _YLE_ has been part of the
Panama Papers, and not _Helsingin Sanomat_. It may be that there are still old policies at
_Helsingin Sanomat_ that do not want the outlet to join teams at a national level.
YLE may be more liberal in this respect, more open to cooperation, but this is
just my interpretation: neither did _The New York Times_ collaborate with the Panama Papers
because they have a track record of policies that obliges them to do everything by themselves, even
when they have experience in the field, as they collaborated with WikiLeaks."

This kind of cultural clash between the mindset of media boards and that of
data journalists, who see cooperation as a fundamental aspect of their professional practice,
has been a constant in this expert's research. It is something he has found
not only in Finland, but in the Nordic countries, Great Britain, and the US. One of the
most notable examples of this—in his ongoing research—is precisely about one of the
most open outlets to cooperation in Finland, _YLE_:

"There is still this openness of qualities, ideas, and practicality among all Finnish data journalists and in other places too: they are free to share ideas, but even
in YLE when they wanted to share the code on _GitHub_,[7] management told them: 'don't do
that,' so they have not been able to be as open and transparent as management
would have wanted to be with respect to the competition and other organizations."

**V.7.3. Culture of transparency in DJ:** _**open data**_ **and open data philosophy**

Almost all the experts and foreign data journalists in the sample agreed
that, without _open data_ and the open data philosophy associated with this movement, there can be
no data journalism. José Luis Dader was the only interviewee to express disagreement
with this assertion, by linking open data and its philosophy to the availability and
opening of data by a body subject to investigation:

"Without the _open data_ philosophy it is difficult for DJ to exist, the greatest possible access to
data is needed, but sometimes there can be data that institutions do not make available and yet
journalists can recover that data or obtain it from other sources to demonstrate
practically the same thing."

7 _GitHub_ is a collaborative software development platform for hosting projects using the _Git_ version control system. Several DJ projects use it to share information.

The academic used this argument to reiterate that DJ and precision journalism are
the same thing and that, in reality, "the problem has to do with sociological
imagination," that is, "through what indirect route could the journalist turn to obtain the
data they need to answer their questions." However, the American data journalist Steve Doig,
who also considers DJ and PP to be the same thing, was
very clear in saying that without _open data_ and the open data philosophy there can be
no data journalism:

"Without the publication of the data used and the open data philosophy there is no DJ. One
of the most important things about doing this type of journalism is making our data available
to others so they can see it. After all, we must be transparent
about the method we use and how we obtained that data."

Thus, in the open data philosophy and _open data_, Doig gives more importance to the
culture of transparency associated with the data journalist than to the openness of some
body subject to investigation, the same openness that Dader linked to the scientific method
and the possibility of any other researcher reproducing the results
of a data investigation or story. On the other hand, all the Finns in the sample do not
perceive in absolute terms the relationship between DJ and _open data_ and the open data
philosophy. In this regard, data journalist Temo Teebest understands "that scholars
want an absolute definition, that they prefer to define it that way, but the answer is not something
'binary' or 'black' and 'white'." This Finn stated that "the answer lies in the shades of grey:
the more _open data_, the more DJ and IP." Similarly, academic Turo Uskali emphasized
that the relationship between data journalism and _open data_ and the open data philosophy "is not
the kind of thing seen in black and white," since there are more shades of grey in it:

"I think it depends on the level of cultures and, of course, there is a great spectrum within them.
We could do a work mapping based on different indicators: how open a
society is, whether they have a minimum of access to information laws, and how many investigative
and data journalists there are in that country. You can see a large gap between nations when
you look at what level they are at, and how easy it is to do DJ pieces. I think that is the main
difference between Finland and Spain in many processes: we are on the same planet, but
in general, in Spain you have to work much harder to obtain information than in
Finland, but it is still possible to do DJ in all countries, even in North Korea!"

For his part, academic Heikki Kuutti referred to the relationship between _open data_ and data
journalism as part of the big data context, where technological democratization is
fundamental: "It is good to have this kind of environment—_big data_—since we would not have DJ
as we know it, of great size and quantity of data." However, like his
compatriots, Kuutti noted that this relationship "is not so black and white, but rather grey,"
since it is a process in full development where technology and its cultural use go
hand in hand:

"I can say it is something rare, developing all the time and expanding. Technology
is advancing and you don't know the possibilities of what you are doing in a few
years. In the beginning there were no personal computers, 20 years ago you could have them and now
you have all these software applications, but there are many ethical aspects about what you obtain
and what you should publish."

As can be observed, all testimonies show how technological democratization,
data openness, investigative journalism, and access to information laws
are directly related to DJ. Moreover, they are factors that all the
interviewees have taken into account when assessing the state of DJ in their respective
countries and the different regions in which they have worked as data journalists or
researchers of this phenomenon.

**V.7.4. The state of DJ in the US, Finland, the Nordic countries, and Europe**

Data journalist Steve Doig stated that DJ in the US is in very good shape and well entrenched.
This statement stems from a conception that considers PP, CAR, and DJ to be the same thing. For
this reason, in his assessment he emphasized that data journalism in the US is very strong,
among other things, due to its long history:

"There is hardly a newsroom now that does not do what we say we do, some
degree of DJ. It has been gaining momentum for the last 30 years, because it has a long
history here… There is no longer any argument about whether data journalism should be part
of newsrooms. As a professor, I receive individual calls from editors from around the country
asking for someone who can do it, a student: someone young, inexpensive but very
good at DJ. Someone is always being hired in a neighboring state."

For his part, Kuutti referred to the state of DJ in those areas where he has studied it:
the US, the Nordic countries, and in particular, his own country: Finland. In his case, when indicating where
there is better data journalism, he emphasized the quantity and quality of investigative journalism
in each country or region, thus highlighting DJ in the US, Great Britain, and the
Nordic countries:

"The US, of course, is number one. They have many journalists and systems as good
as the IRE and NICAR[8]… Sweden is ahead of us, perhaps Denmark too.
In general, there are the Nordic countries, the US, and Great Britain… in fact there are only two
data units in Finland: at YLE and Helsingin Sanomat, with few people, but they are
very good… all of this has to do with IP: the best data journalists are
investigative journalists. They find the reasons for certain behaviors and

8 IRE is the acronym for _Investigative Reporters and Editors_, a non-profit organization founded in 1975
so that investigative journalists from around the world could help each other by sharing ideas, information-gathering techniques, and sources. NICAR is the acronym for the _National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting_, a program of IRE created in 1989 to promote CAR, which has become a global reference for DJ.

explanations for situations that can be explained with computers and then, after that,
they conduct an investigation based on what they find there."

Similarly, Uskali highlighted IP in the Nordic countries by asserting that large
leaks and investigative journalism go hand in hand in DJ: "in fact, those
working on the Panama Papers are investigative journalists. It is a perfect example
of how investigative journalists and data journalists collaborate: more than 16
met in Helsinki last spring." From the perspective that in the future all
journalists, in general, should have the necessary skills to be data journalists,
he also stated that "in many countries we are at an early stage." He said this
after making the following assertion about the state of DJ in Finland and the Nordic countries:

"We only have two data units, with just 10 or 12 people working in each one,
when the newsrooms of those outlets have at least about 200 journalists…
There are some things at local newspapers, especially the larger ones, but they don't have data units and don't call themselves data journalists… in general the Norwegians are doing
very well, they have been among the best from the beginning in the Nordic countries. They have good
assets and have also been able to win some prizes internationally. I think that is
a good measure: the prize that the Global Editors Network gives annually."

When linking the recent technological democratization with the creation of
major DJ pieces, Steve Doig also used data journalism awards
as an indicator of the state of this discipline around the world. Like Kuutti, Doig
highlighted the role of organizations such as the IRE in the improvement and dissemination of DJ
worldwide:

"DJ is definitely everywhere in the world. One of the best ways to see this is through
the _Data Journalism Awards_ competition… many of the most
impressive ideas do not come from large news organizations, but from very
small newsrooms of five people, and pieces done in online workshops. Realizing that
the tools are not that difficult to use once you train a little has to do with
this… at the last IRE conference in New Orleans we had 1,800 journalists,
many of whom came to take part in workshops on how to use Excel, Access, and
write programs in Python. That is how this expands."

Thus, in the categorization of the state of DJ around the world, the topic of
training and incentives emerged as something fundamental. In this regard, and referring to
Finnish data journalism, Turo Uskali mentioned hacktivist Antti Poikola of the _Open
Knowledge Foundation_ as part of the genesis of the first projects, along with the journalism school
of Jyväskylä, where Uskali teaches DJ classes:

"Poikola is immersed in the open data culture and had this creativity competition
with _open data_ to make applications based on datasets… he was the first to
give a DJ course in Finland at one of those minor schools, not at university level, and
it started in 2013… now I think we are the only journalism school that has been able to
maintain regular DJ courses since then. Perhaps a couple of years will pass before
other courses are organized. In Helsinki there are none and I think we are the best
established place to learn data journalism and the first to have a textbook in
Finnish based on our research, also in Finnish."

For her part, Helen Darbishire stated that in Spain DJ "is advancing, but we do not
have much: it is a new field in Spain. There are other countries like the United Kingdom where
there is much more." According to Darbishire, the reason for this is that most outlets and
journalists do not have time or resources to do data journalism, although "happily
we have journalists who do a great deal with their free time, with an impressive
dedication." In her experience, DJ is nourished by _open data_ and investigative
journalism, from which it can be inferred that, without transparency activism and the dedication
of IP, DJ is very difficult to practice. A different view from that of academic José Luis
Dader, who stated that DJ in Spain is in a truly poor state when
compared to pieces made in the 1990s of the twentieth century. In this regard, Dader
explained—without using terms such as PP or DJ—that "there were journalists who, without labeling it
and by pure instinct, were producing reports or analyses of social trends that were much
richer and in the tradition of quality precision journalism."

Both experts agree that the state of DJ is not good, although Dader does so by
comparing today's DJ with a PP practiced several decades ago by journalists who did not
bother to assign these labels to any journalistic work. In Darbishire's view,
Spanish data journalism is something new and its connection to the culture of
transparency is very similar to the one this culture previously had with IP. In her
conception she links these types of journalism with the development of science and the need
for more transparency on the part of society:

"It is interesting because there are journalists who have done investigative journalism before
transparency laws. Throughout the entire twentieth century and a few people in the
nineteenth. Very much in line with the development of science and the conception that knowledge
is real… Civil society has grown, for example, CIVIO—we are on the board of trustees—
was born five years ago, Access Info eleven years ago. We have tried to encourage other NGOs to
exist and work on that, and happily we have many more NGOs working to promote and
defend democracy now than eleven years ago. It is important."

Darbishire's perspective gives more importance to Spanish civil society, which she has
seen grow in democratic values such as transparency to the point that a
non-profit foundation born in late 2011, CIVIO, is a Spanish and international example of the
practice of DJ and activism for transparency. In this regard, the Spanish Transparency Law
is a milestone for DJ, since "the political class knows at least
what we are talking about," that is, we are talking about transparency: "before the law was passed
they did not know what we were talking about. Now it is understood that it implies transparency and the
discourse has matured enormously."

**V.7.5. Culture of transparency in public administration and the right of access to information: DJ, transparency laws, and bureaucratic culture**

From an activist perspective, Helen Darbishire stated that "transparency is the result of having
the right to information and the right of access to information is about what is
happening, what governments are doing." This has two sides: the obligation of
governments to publish proactively and the citizens' right to request information and
receive a clear response or an explanation for denied requests. From this
perspective she stated that, both in Spain and in Europe, when we have a good
quantity of information from public bodies, it will be possible to speak of
transparent administrations:

"But in most cases we are not fully there, so we have advanced
a great deal in the last 15 or 20 years regarding these rights, but we are still
working on it… much of the information needed to follow and participate in decision-making
is still not available and that is where we are."

In this regard she said that a transparent administration is one that provides good information
to the public, so a Transparency Law is an essential tool for defending
human rights in democracy. Based on her experience, the lack of public information and of
political will to generate and deliver it on the part of the administration are a problem
in Spain, so in order to enforce the law it is essential that there be a Transparency Council
with binding rulings, sanctioning capacity, and inspection duties. To explain this she made a
comparison between similar public information requests made
to the EU and to the Spanish State regarding lobbies. In the case of the request to the EU
she stated the following:

"We have a case involving EU lobbies where we are trying to obtain
a legal assessment document from the European Commission's lawyers on the future of the
Transparency Law and lobby registry. We received last week a record of
the inspection visit that was made to the European Commission's offices, to verify
which documents they hold with respect to our request, which is remarkable.
We do not have the documents, because they are fighting to avoid publishing them, but
they had been inspecting the documents and we even have the names of the people
present during the inspection: it is fascinating."

However, when faced with similar requests in Spain, Darbishire has received communications from
ministries that implicitly deny the existence of the requested documentation, and there is
no way to inspect such an assertion on the part of an independent body that watches over
compliance with the Transparency Law:

"In Spain we have a problem with lobby information during the drafting of the
law regulating self-consumption of energy in Spain, because the ministry says—though not
explicitly—that there were no more documents submitted during a period of two years and
we are certain that lobbyists submitted documents. The Transparency Council
cannot demand or go to inspect or request a list of the documents: we are
blocked."

This is a case that coincides with the experience of the Spanish data journalists in the
sample regarding the inadequacy of the Transparency Law. Darbishire also acknowledged
that in this and many other cases "it is not all about laws, it is about culture," so that in the strategy
of her NGO is the cultural transformation of Spanish society: "we are in the process of
changing the culture." It is a perspective that enriches what has been said up to now by the Finnish
academics regarding the new culture of transparency among Finnish data journalists,
who tend to collaborate and share information in a manner similar to the Spanish data journalists
in the sample. As Darbishire stated, there is a culture of transparency
linked to "a reality that is natural in bureaucracy in all countries: information
is power and bureaucracy—even when it is doing nothing wrong—has resistance to
change. Even if you eliminate corruption, it has difficulty showing how it has done its
work." This culture of transparency—or rather of opacity—in public
administration clashes head-on with the new culture of transparency acquired by data journalists
around the world. However, there are differences and levels of clashes depending on each country and
region.

**V.7.5.1. Culture of transparency and access to public information in Finland**

The Finns in the sample not only consider their Transparency Law to be sufficient,
but also find that, in practice, the culture of transparency in Finnish administration facilitates
access to public information in ways not possible in other countries. Turo Uskali gave more
importance in his country to the culture of transparency linked to the practice of requesting
public information than to the adequacy of the law on access to public information:

"I think the law is sufficient, very liberal and offers great opportunities to citizens
and specialized journalists. We have had access to public information laws for
about 250 years, so the law is not that important: it is theory and that is fine,
it is in excellent shape. But there is also the practical side, whether it is easy or not to obtain
the information. However, we also have several problems."

His departmental colleague, Heikki Kuutti, praised these cultural practices in the
local public administration of the city of Jyväskylä, where the proactive publication by
municipalities and local governments is quite transparent and does not require either an
electronic signature or nationality to access data. Moreover, unlike the experience
of Spanish data journalists with our public administration, they make these
publications in formats that facilitate their reading and analysis, such as Excel
or CSV spreadsheets:

"We have an Open Access portal here in the city of Jyväskylä that
publishes all information, all the data that goes to other companies: everything they use.
All types of data in Excel formats with hundreds of thousands of individuals… information
about how the city is using this money and everything they are buying from any
company and for what purpose. It is very transparent and that is a very convenient way of
finding any kind of information based on where the money flows, that is the
main point… in Finland all this type of data is open, you yourself can
consult it on the internet."

For his part, data journalist Temo Teebes praised his country's access to information law,
although he criticized the outsourcing of public services to private companies
as a new obstacle to public information access that the
various administrations used to expose:

"The access to information law in Finland is very good. There is a big difference from
other parts of the world. However, public bodies are outsourcing their
functions to the private sector and in that regard the law is insufficient, since anything that
involves taxpayers' money should be transparent."

It can be observed how both at the national and municipal level the proactive publication of
information is in very good health in Finland. It can also be seen how,
like the Spanish data journalists in the sample, in Finland data journalism
shares the view that everything involving public money must be
transparent. However, the adequacy of the public information access law and the
culture of transparency in public administration in Finland are suffering
setbacks due to the concealment of public information that is also considered
the commercial secret of private companies. Regarding this, Heikki Kuutti considered that both the
authorities and mixed companies also have interests and this is a major problem:

"Right now some public activities have moved to business activities so
the authorities are acting with a money-saving mindset and denying all
information channels, because they form part of their commercial interests… There is
a good example in Helsinki with the renovation of the metro by a company in which
the city is also a part-owner: the other owners have conducted an investigation and have
found many problems in rebuilding the metro, but the company does not want to give that
information to anyone. They want to keep it for themselves even when the city itself wants it
open: the company's board denies this because, according to the law, it is responsible
for the money earned by the company."

This is a problem that derives from the business culture of the private sector, which has been
managing public affairs, but it can be seen how, within the limits of the bureaucratic culture,
the culture of transparency in Finland is very well developed, which aids the
better performance of data journalism. As Turo Uskali asserts, conflicts between
privacy laws and the right of access to information that Spanish data journalists
denounced are not a problem in Finland: "I do not think it is a major issue. After
all, the authorities know that journalists will respect privacy and, from what I have
studied, there has been no major problem so far."

**V.7.5.2. LOPD, electronic DNI, and the culture of transparency in Spanish public administration: comparison with other realities**

Very different has been the experience recounted by the Spanish data journalists in the
sample due to the _Ley Orgánica 15/1999, de 13 de diciembre, de Protección de Datos de
Carácter Personal_ (LOPD), which can be traced as the origin of many of the problems of
non-compliance with Spain's current Transparency Law. Dader spoke about this topic in
Spanish journalism, emphasizing that the _Ley de Régimen Jurídico de las Administraciones
Públicas y del Procedimiento Administrativo Común_—Law 30/1992 of 26 November
1992—which regulated the right of access to public information, "did not mark a milestone in
new precision journalism stories in Spain, because there was already a law on
administrative procedure from the Franco era that was very similar, with no major changes." In
fact, according to this academic the 1992 law added many more "exceptions that allowed
the civil servant to avoid providing documentation" than those contained in the previous law from the
dictatorship era—Law of 20 July 1957 on the legal regime of the State administration—although
what was truly damaging from the point of view of
transparency and the practice of journalism was the LOPD of 1999:

"This law was a distortion of the European data protection directive: a law
much more restrictive than what the European law says was produced. A terribly
closed and restrictive interpretation was made that placed the right to privacy
above the right to public information. From that point on, a fear was generated among civil servants about releasing data… Right now the current Transparency Law
is still hamstrung by the LOPD."

The LOPD is a factor that prevents compliance with the current Transparency Law and that has
helped to create a bureaucratic culture removed from a culture of transparency more
akin to that practiced by data journalism, which confirms what Helen
Darbishire said regarding the North-South divide in transparency culture in Europe: "there are the
Nordic countries like Sweden, with their laws of 1766, and Finland—which was in turn part of
Sweden at that time—… both in Finland and in Norway there is a culture of transparency
more integrated into the bureaucratic culture." These statements coincide with what was
expressed by Finnish experts and data journalists, who do not usually have major problems when
requesting public information. If we also take into account what Kuutti said, asserting that
"anyone in the world, even you, can make an information request
in English and you will get a response within a few days," the ease of requesting information appears to be
one of the greatest differences between those "two Europes" mentioned by Darbishire:

"From Access Info we have requested information in the vast majority of
European countries and the ease of obtaining information depends on where we are talking about.
We have an example from last year where we requested information on a Friday based
on a photo we had seen of the Finnish Prime Minister on 18 March 2016:
we requested the following day and on Monday we received a PDF version of what he was showing on Thursday."

In her exposition on the different degrees of transparency and access to information in the
EU, Darbishire mentioned that one of the key indicators for measuring cultural transformation
is the number of people who request information through transparency portals. In the Spanish
case she pointed to a very significant brake on this cultural transformation,
also highlighted by Dader when he spoke of the culture of transparency in
Spanish public administration: the electronic DNI (national identity document). In this regard, projects by _Access
Info_ such as _AsktheEU.org_, which consists of using their platform to help individuals
make public information requests to the EU, are impossible to replicate at the Spanish level.
Regarding this, Darbishire explained the following when comparing the Spanish situation with that of
Mexico:

"We have had to close Tu Derecho a Saber because it cannot be done, it is impossible given the
information access requirement: in Spain an identity requirement is imposed through a
digital certificate. In Mexico they have a platform where you can request information from
anywhere in the country, here you cannot, because you need a digital certificate. It is a shame, because
this has been made easier in the EU and allows for this type of platform that, in many countries, is
encouraging requesters. With _AsktheEU.org_ we have helped people make
information requests."

Like Darbishire, academic José Luis Dader linked the implementation of this
requirement to the political will to make public information requests effective: "the
electronic DNI or digital signature is one of the main problems. In practice it greatly restricts
the possibility of many people being able to make queries. In the administration they have
tried to establish technological barriers that would prevent widespread use of the transparency portal."

After asserting that a Transparency Law is an essential tool for being able to
defend human rights and advance democracies, Darbishire emphasized that in
Spain it is far from sufficient, even more so when compared with the situation in Northern Europe. In this
regard, Darbishire considers that political will from those in positions of
representation and from the people who form part of the civil service of institutions is
key to cultural change:

"The Scandinavian laws are older and weaker, but they work better because of the culture
and political will… a person in a ministry who understands and wants to drive
transparency can change the entire entity… we have seen cases in the EU. When
the head of transparency at the Commission has changed, it has changed the requests de facto:
it can depend on one person and their attitude, who has created a culture of transparency
within an administration."

**V.7.5.3. Culture of transparency and access to public information in the United States**

For his part, Steve Doig stated that the US has very strong transparency laws and
requesting information is not usually a problem. However, as a federal state, each
state has its own constitution and access to information laws vary:

"In Florida, for example, transparency and official records are in the state
constitution and it is very difficult for a state agency to deny any public information,
with exceptions for very specific things. In other states they have tighter relationships
with transparency: there is little room for bureaucrats and politicians to try not to
give public information. Sometimes the same types of data I have requested and been given
in a state like Florida, they try not to give me in another state like Arizona and
I have to go to court to obtain them: in the end I get them, but there are different levels of
access."

In this regard he reiterated that transparency is such a powerful force in the US that it
operates in a way that is not seen in other parts of the world, not even in Europe. According to his
experience, European journalists are going through the same process that he and his American
colleagues went through years earlier in the US: insisting on reports, going to court, and not leaving
at the moment when they are told they cannot have the requested information: "you have to
push, that is what we did for decades here in the US. We created a transparency
that is now a reality."

Regarding privacy protection laws and the transparency of public officials,
he emphasized that civil servants should not expect any kind of privacy regarding the amount of
money they receive, the people they deal with in their offices, and those who request or
demand things of them: "it is a requirement for political figures and this comes from the Nixon era, when the
candidate routinely showed their tax returns." This example regarding
tax returns is very important in the case of the US, since in that country
"it is one of the most private things for ordinary people." Despite this bureaucratic culture
conducive to transparency, Doig has witnessed cases in which they have tried to deny him data
for containing personal medical information:

"There are examples involving medical reports: it is against the law for doctors and
hospitals to reveal data about their patients unless the patient agrees…
I am looking for a dataset about every person who has been in state hospitals in the past year, without their addresses or personal names… but the agency that
has this data denies it to me saying that by filtering and cross-referencing it with others I could
obtain personal information from a particular medical report: just because it is theoretically possible does not mean I am going to do that, so it is wrong that they deny me the information. That is why I am
claiming that data in the courts and eventually I will have it."

Both in the case of the US and in that of Finland, the majority of denials of
public information occur due to ignorance of the law, which means they are usually obtained
once one goes to court, since denying them would be a violation of the law. According to Kuutti, sometimes the
administration does not know whether they have to hand over the data or not: "many of the denials
come with the explanation that they do not know whether this data is public or not. They have to
deny it and then provide it, because we always go to the administrative court and obtain the
ruling that decides whether the information is public or not. We have hundreds of such cases in
Finland." Thus in Finland there is a binding administrative body that
obliges public entities to provide information they have denied and, what is more important: every
denial of information must be accompanied by an explanation of why it is being
done.

**V.7.5.4. Failures of the Transparency Law: its legal standing, the inability of the Transparency Council to impose sanctions, and the double administrative silence**

According to Helen Darbishire, many of the denials in Spain are not explained, as they
shelter behind the double administrative silence of the law that treats a request as denied once
a period passes without a response. In this regard she advocated for changes to the law to facilitate
access to information for all journalists and citizens, "which would facilitate good
journalism and, of course, DJ, because journalists do not have time to conduct investigations,
nor the patience and dedication that Jesús Escudero has, who is working and
litigating."

Another major problem that Access Info Europe sees in the functioning of Spain's Transparency Law
has to do with this lack of time to litigate for requested information. It is the fact that rulings of the Transparency Council are not
binding. Moreover, this body has no sanctioning capacity, so "you have to
go to court to claim certain information," but this costs a great deal of time and
money that most journalists do not have. In this context she mentioned strategic litigation as a
fundamental tool for cultural change in Spain:

"In the OGP[9] case it is not logical that in 2015 we were denied reports submitted by the
ministries to the Moncloa on the application and implementation of the Open
Government Action Plan… We won a favorable opinion from the Transparency Council and the
government chose to litigate against the Council. We won at first instance, the government
appealed to the second instance, the Audiencia Nacional, and now—2017—it is a final ruling
of the Audiencia Nacional: the government should give me the information and it still has not
done so. Time has passed and the information is less relevant, but we have won
an interpretation of the law that limits the application of article 18." (Helen Darbishire)

This is a case that recalls the RTVE litigation against the ruling of the Transparency Council,
mentioned by one of the Spanish data journalists interviewed: Jesús
Escudero. With the OGP case, Darbishire has shown us failures in the application of the
culture of transparency by the Spanish government in appealing Council rulings;
non-compliance with the law in failing to deliver the information after going to court; and
inadequacy of the law in article 18:

9 OGP stands for Open Government Partnership.

"It is a very broad exceptions article that says that in order to apply the exceptions they must
provide justification. The interpretation is very useful since it has cited the Strasbourg
Human Rights Court ruling. So this is an example of a litigation that has
won three things: obtaining the information, correctly interpreting the law, and making
reference in Spain for the first time to the fundamental human rights nature of
this right." (Helen Darbishire)

It is at this point that what Access Info Europe considers one of the most serious
problems with the law resurfaces: its legal standing. According to Darbishire, when the law was being
drafted, campaigns were conducted to try to convince the Spanish government that the law constitutes
a fundamental right recognized by the European Court of Human Rights since
2009: "We did not achieve that, which is why we do not have an organic law, but a regular one that conflicts with many other laws."

Darbishire insists that on top of the law's legal standing there is the complication that
we have in Spain the option of different levels of government with different options for
complying with the regulation, which adds the non-compliance of many regional and
local bodies, which end up subordinating themselves to the state law that "is not the best: it has a score
of 79 out of 150 points in our international ranking and we are in 80th place out of 115
globally." However, she also mentioned that this complexity of the Spanish case with its
legislation "different in different parts, ministries, regions, etc.," yields results in those places where
there is some political will, such as Madrid or Barcelona, where, for example, no
electronic registration is required to request information:

"We have different levels that more or less do not come from the law, from the legal framework, but from
political will, and with that the possibility and ease of requesting and receiving information
is very different depending on the level and the entity."

**V.8. Analytical summary of results**

This section is an analysis that summarizes the findings of the in-depth interviews
conducted in relation to the research objectives and questions. This is a necessary step
for the correct presentation of ideas in the conclusions, which must be as
specific as possible in relation to our object of study. In this regard we will divide this
section into five ideas that bring together the most important findings of this part of the
research.

**V.8.1. Characterization of data journalism through _big data_ as a differentiating element from other forms of journalism**

Data journalists and experts do not explicitly admit that _big data_ is
a sociocultural phenomenon that differentiates DJ from other earlier forms of journalism
in which databases were used and large datasets were analyzed. However,
if we take into account the analysis of natural units of meaning, we observe that they do
recognize in DJ elements characteristic of _big data_, such as:

1. The democratization of tools for working with data thanks to free _software_
and hacker activists or _hacktivists_.

2. The enormous importance of the existence of open data and the demand for greater
transparency, both institutional and on the part of the journalist or team producing the
data stories.

3. Collaborative work in: 1—data journalism teams, 2—between the DJ team and other sections of their journalistic organization, and 3—between different
journalistic organizations.

4. Teamwork with groups of multidisciplinary professionals, with the news story
as a boundary object between the different profiles that form part of the team.

5. The tendency to make the data and results of DJ stories available to the public and to colleagues for reuse. This is a characteristic of the
_open data_ and _free software_ movements.

6. The visualization of data to better understand the information gathered and
at the same time make the results more "digestible" for any user.

All of these are characteristics that form part of _big data_ as a sociocultural phenomenon,
which includes the _open data_ and _free software_ movements and collaborative work in the
practices of the data journalists in the sample. These are practices that foreign Finnish experts
have also detected in data journalism in Finland, the United Kingdom, and
the United States, so this is not a phenomenon exclusive to Spain and can be generalized
internationally, at least within the sphere of Western countries.

Nevertheless, although some experts insist that data journalism, precision journalism,
and CAR are the same phenomenon and that DJ is a fashionable label that
indicates only a change of name, all the interviewees agreed in highlighting the
technological advances that have benefited data journalism. In fact, digitalization
and the possibility of cross-referencing data—thanks to technological advances—were
considered by several interviewees as differentiating features or characteristics unique to DJ compared to other
forms of journalism. Not without reason, for the data journalists and experts interviewed, DJ is a
more objective journalism with fewer biases, meaning that some of the mythologies of _big
data_ as a sociocultural phenomenon form part of the conception of data journalism: the
objectivity and truthfulness of data.

Regarding cultural phenomena, journalists and experts acknowledge that greater
access to data thanks to the open data philosophy or _open data_ is another characteristic
element of data journalism, which establishes another difference with respect to
earlier eras in which transparency laws as we know them today did not exist. Within this open data culture there is also the possibility of reproducing the results or information used by DJ, although we might think that precision journalism already
aspired to this through its approach to the scientific method. However,
in the practices of DJ we can see an evolution of the culture of transparency
in journalistic practices, since data journalists themselves tend to show the
process by which they reached their information and to make raw data available to
other researchers. The search _per se_ of precision journalism for practices derived from the social sciences does not exist.

Specifically, these practices form part of a revaluation of the profession through
journalistic precision and the reinforcement of classical journalism values through the use
of new computer tools that did not exist in the times of PP and CAR and that,
today, have spread thanks to the democratization of technology through the
hacker activist community. Although these transparency practices carried out by
journalists are similar to those of the scientific method to which precision journalism aspired,
in reality they are more rooted in a vision similar to that of the free _software_
community—reuse by other actors—and which Himanen (2001) called the hacker work ethic.

One of the most striking results is that the majority of interviewees recognize the
importance of transparency and other elements characteristic of the _big data_ era in
journalism, such as verification or _fact-checking_ linked to objectivity and verification of
data. In this regard, these two elements that form part of the classical values of
traditional journalism are re-signified as part of the mythologies of _big data_,
becoming established as differentiating elements of DJ with respect to precision
journalism and CAR.

From a technological and sociocultural point of view, data journalism represents a
substantial change with respect to other forms of journalism that used databases
and the analysis of large datasets, which can be summarized in the following points:

1. The use of techniques to generate information and handle large quantities of data as
was never previously possible, with tools for accessing that data and being able to work with it
(cross-referencing, filtering, analyzing, etc.).

2. The framing of a conflict between the investigating entity and the investigated body through
the open data movement and the active and reactive publication of data by
administrations.

3. The reinforcement of journalistic depth and investigation through rigor,
truthfulness, and verification of information with _fact-checking_ techniques that did not exist
when PP and CAR emerged.

4. The re-signification of traditional journalistic values such as objectivity and
truthfulness of data within the mythologies associated with _big data_, which lead to DJ
being conceived as a discipline that reinforces journalistic depth and investigation.

Another differentiating element of DJ revealed by the results of the in-depth interviews
comes directly from the impossibility of finding a data journalist who combines all the skills
characteristic of the ideal profile, whereas in PP and
CAR there are names of journalists associated with these practices who could work alone,
such as Philip Meyer, David Burnham, or Elliot Jaspin. Thanks to this impossibility, new cultural
actors have been incorporated into the news production process, which has
led to the organization of teams in which the collaborative work practiced by
professions characteristic of the _big data_ era—such as programmers, web developers, and
digital product designers—is fundamental. This collaborative work—implicit in the
free _software_ culture—where the news story is a boundary object between journalists,
programmers, and designers, is another fundamental aspect of DJ that distinguishes it from the
practices of PP and CAR.

**V.8.2. Transparency Law and the emergence of DJ in Spain**

The _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_ was
published in the BOE on 10 December 2013. Its staged entry into force meant that until a year later, from 10 December 2014 onwards, no information requests could be made
nor could data placed at citizens' disposal by the public administration be consulted. This fact has an evident parallel with the emergence of DJ in some Spanish outlets
from late 2013, its further development throughout
2014, and its extension to other outlets between 2015 and 2017. These are data that coincide with the
view of all interviewees based in Spain regarding the situation of transparency
in this country, namely that despite its shortcomings it has improved since the law was enacted
and even more since it came into force a year later. All of this from the perspective of a
journalism that clearly appears to be committed to greater institutional transparency in
Spain.

Improvements in transparency have occurred because the law provides journalists, activists, and citizens in general with mechanisms for access to public
information that did not exist before, which in turn has served as a platform for demanding greater transparency. One of the
points where this entire improvement process has been most visible was after the
law came into force, when some regional and
municipal mechanisms were created to access public information that attempted to compensate for some of its
shortcomings. This was a point highlighted by transparency expert Helen Darbishire and
some of the data journalists interviewed. However, the fact that these regional and municipal mechanisms were highlighted is a clear indicator that something is going wrong
with the law at the level of the Spanish State and its portal. In this regard, many of the Spanish data journalists pointed to the inadequacy of the law and its non-compliance, thereby supporting
what Helen Darbishire said in her interview.

**V.8.3. Inadequacy of the Transparency Law**

The inadequacy of the law and the portal was confirmed with a near-unanimous position in the
interviews with Spanish data journalists and experts. The law's lack of sanctioning capacity and
the absence of a lobbying registry evidenced this inadequacy in the interview results. Furthermore, given the repeated identification by the journalists in the sample of
the opacity of the Spanish government and the poor functioning of its transparency portal, the
law's mechanisms for requesting public information that should compensate for these failures
encounter two widely identified problems:

1. The delay in responding to information requests when a decision is made to
respond.

2. The many refusals to respond to public information requests.

The latter is due to the fact that the law and the portal go hand in hand: with an inadequate Transparency Law one cannot expect a portal better than the law from which it was born, unless the civil servants responsible for information requests and
the publication of public data had a culture of transparency that would lead them to
make up for this inadequacy of the law: in the Spanish case this is also not the situation.

An interesting finding from the interviews with foreign experts was that, unlike
the Spanish case, Finland appears to have a culture of transparency in
public administration that would compensate for possible inadequacies of its law, which dates from 1952. For
this reason in Finland, with a public information access law sixty years older
than Spain's Transparency Law, there seems to be a more suitable environment for the
development of data journalism. The same can be said of the United States according to the
experience of the data journalist interviewed, where the law is broadly complied with
and the culture of transparency in public administration in states such as Arkansas and
Florida allows for the proper development of data journalism. Moreover, at the national level, the
separation between the privacy of a public official and information of public interest
is so well established in the United States that for several decades there has been a
tradition of publishing economic and social data about political figures.

According to all the Spanish data journalists interviewed, the academic expert in
precision journalism, and the transparency expert, there is a shortage of a culture of transparency
in Spanish public administration, to which is added the lack of sanctioning capacity
of the Transparency Council, the poor functioning of the portal, the requirement of an electronic
DNI to be able to make public information requests, and the lack of political will
on the part of the then Partido Popular government that enacted the _Ley 19/2013 de
Transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_.

**V.8.4. Culture of transparency in Spain and non-compliance with _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia y buen gobierno_**

Among the results of the interviews with Spanish data journalists and experts, even the
most conciliatory statement regarding the Transparency Law—in this case, the one
made by the CEPID data journalist, Javier Galán—formed part of the general opinion
among all interviewees based in Spain that Spanish public administration lacked sufficient culture of transparency. In this regard, Galán
noted that although the law was appropriate in terms of deadlines, these were not being met. All
opinions about the functioning of the law and the portal point to a lack of culture of
transparency in public administration, exemplified in the following points:

1. The functioning of the portal: it publishes obvious things.

2. The double administrative silence that allows a request to be treated as denied if not
answered, and the large number of requests denied due to lack of response.

3. The delay in responding to public information requests, which in itself is evidence
of non-compliance with the law.

4. That the privacy of a public official takes precedence over the Transparency Law in
many responses to public information requests, all of this despite
interpretive criterion CI/004/2015 between the Spanish Data Protection Agency
and the Transparency Council regarding the personal data of a person
holding public office (CI/004/2015, 2015).

Moreover, the constant comparison with transparency cases related to major
DJ pieces in the US, the UK, and the Anglophone world in general shows that the data journalists
interviewed have a perception of what _open data_ and _open data governing_ should be that goes beyond what is practiced in Spanish institutions. This perception
of what information is public and what is not reveals a culture of transparency very
different in the data journalists interviewed from the one exercised in public
administration through its portal, evidenced in the responses to public information requests made by the journalists in the sample. The fact that interviewees stressed that Spain's portal should be like that of Chile, which shows the salary and assets of
absolutely all civil servants, is another example of this.

The perception that the Transparency Law is a minimal-compliance law for show, which
seeks to appear to comply with European regulations but at the same time allows
the government to ensure it is not effective, seems to be validated solely by the law's legal standing. Since the LOPD is an organic law and the Transparency Law is not, those responsible for responding to public information requests have a very broad basis on which to ground
the denial of requested information. Those in senior positions in institutions are aware of this and
it seems to validate the thesis put forward by the Spanish data journalists and experts
interviewed: the law was an electoral gesture.

The inadequacy of the law is also evidenced by the absence of mechanisms to enforce it,
where the Transparency Council's lack of sanctioning capacity is a crucial
point. Faced with the lack of what Escudero and Darbishire called a "culture of transparency," the law would need to be amended to oblige civil servants to comply with the
deadlines established therein, while also obliging and not merely "urging" public institutions
to publish their data: none of this is possible without a law with sanctioning powers. Hence Escudero highlighted the contentious-administrative proceeding that RTVE
filed against the Transparency Council ruling that urged it to publish the breakdown of
Eurovisión 2015 expenses, while Darbishire highlighted the OGP case in which the
Spanish government appealed Transparency Council rulings and the information request had to be taken all the way to the Supreme Court.

In this regard, strategic litigation as a fundamental tool in transparency activism and
the practice of DJ in Spain is yet another indication of the inadequacy of the Transparency Law and its mechanisms for self-enforcement, since the majority of data journalists do not have the resources—either financial or temporal—to engage in litigation
against the State to obtain public information. For this reason, without sanctioning powers in the law and the current negative administrative silence that governs it, there can be no effective data openness. This directly affects the practice of DJ and data journalists and
experts confirm it. It is therefore not coincidental that DJ in Spain continues in an
incipient state despite the boom it experienced throughout 2015.

**V.8.5. Imbalance between the LOPD and the Transparency Law**

Another point to bear in mind is the imbalance between the personal data protection of a
civil servant and the Transparency Law. Despite the fact that, in the case of senior public officials, both the Transparency Council and the Spanish Data Protection Agency agreed that personal information is subordinated to the Transparency Law (CI/004/2015, 2015), the interviewees provided various examples of denials of information requests
sheltering behind the LOPD.

We can conclude that since the emergence of DJ in Spain there has been a boom in this
discipline directly related to the entry into force of the Transparency Law and the
functioning of the transparency portal. However, the inadequacy of the law through negative administrative silence and its inability to impose sanctions have stalled the growth
of this emerging discipline, keeping it in an incipient state and dependent on
leaks of information that should be public. To this is added the non-compliance with the
law regarding the subordination of senior public officials' privacy to the Transparency Law, meaning we can state that the incongruences between this law and the LOPD that both the Transparency Council and the Spanish Data Protection Agency
attempted to resolve remain in practice due to a lack of culture of transparency
in public administration.

All the Spanish journalists in the sample highlighted their journalistic values through
a code of ethics that, among other things, should be taken into account when
providing them with public information containing data on senior civil servants. This means that
beyond the administration's excuses for denying information based on the
LOPD, these data journalists distinguish themselves even further from traditional journalists by
reaffirming that unlike other outlets that published personal information from some
leak, they always take people's privacy into account and would only publish data
that is relevant to citizens.

The fact that the DJ pieces with the greatest impact on public opinion come from
leaks—Panama Papers, Falciani List, Caso Cifuentes y Casado, among others—rather than from public information is a clear symptom of a lack of culture of transparency in
Spain's public administration: most journalists said that leaks intensify when information of public interest that should be public is not published.
Moreover, relying on _crowdsourcing_ is another of the solutions they have turned to when faced with the lack of
a culture of transparency in public administration, the inadequacy of the law, and its
non-compliance.

**VI.2.2.1. Qualitative analysis of reportages**


Of the 5 reportages published by _El País_ during the analysis period, the pieces of

investigative journalism produced by Elena G. Sevillano in July 2015 are those that

most clearly show the consequences of the inadequacies of the Transparency Law. In an

article titled "Casi el 30% de peticiones al portal de transparencia se quedan sin respuesta"

(Sevillano, 2015a), now investigative reporter at _El País_ and then writer on

environmental and health topics, she criticized the failures to respond to public

information requests on the national portal. Another reportage by this journalist exposed how the

Ministry of Health concealed conflicts of interest of the committee that drafted the

National Strategic Plan for Addressing Hepatitis C, while in another she flagged the

parliamentary clashes over this issue. Although through this journalist we see how

_El País_ addressed topics similar to the other outlets, which by then already had data

journalism teams, the rest of the reportages with nominal authorship and multiple sources

were used as political weapons in the Catalan elections of September and the

general elections of December, not deviating an iota from the outlet's customary editorial line.


The reportage of September 21 titled "La ley de transparencia de Mas mantiene opacas

las donaciones" (Güell & Vidal, 2015) sought to counter the resonance of Artur

Mas's declarations about the Catalan transparency law as "one of the most complete and

demanding in the world." The other nominal-authorship reportage from _El País_ with multiple

sources was published on December 16 under the title "Los partidos se vuelcan en el voto

de las mujeres, que suman el 62% de los indecisos" (Junquera & Marcos, 2015), in which

transparency is a tangential topic for contextualizing the gender policies developed

by each of the parties in their programmatic content. In this regard, N. Junquera and

J. Marcos equated in a couple of lines the transparency proposals of the five major

parties heading to elections on December 20, before developing a very

complete article on programmatic differences in gender policies.


Of the nominal-authorship reportages with multiple sources from _El Mundo_, only one

shares characteristics of political weaponization according to the outlet's editorial line, as

shown by the article of December 17 by Antonio Moreno (2015) titled

"Colau aumenta la subvención al memorial a los caídos en 1714," which attempts to link the

political formation of the mayor of Barcelona with Catalan independentists through

a subsidy to an association that, among its functions, sought to "preserve the memory of
the Catalan patriots who fell" three centuries ago. To do so, Moreno contextualized the

subsidies in a climate of political uncertainty around Podemos and its confluences

ahead of the December 20 elections, citing sources from various political parties

to link the subsidy to a policy pursued by Catalan independentist parties and

giving weight to the statements made against it by Ciudadanos representatives.


The rest of the _El Mundo_ reportages clearly reflect an intention to place in

public opinion problems surrounding transparency without needing to follow the

editorial line of the outlet. Such was the case of the reportage of October 1, 2015 titled "¿Se le

puede regalar un velero de 50.000 euros a Don Juan Carlos?" (Terrasa, 2015), which

breaks down the transparency law and criticizes practices of the Spanish monarchy that would run counter

to the spirit of this law. The same occurred with the articles "El nuevo negocio de la

transparencia" (De la Torre, 2015), "Transparencia con pegas" (Treceño, 2015), and "El auditor

avisa desde 2013 del descontrol en los gastos de Sáez" (Salvador, 2015), published on

October 1, October 11, and December 18, 2015 respectively: these are reportages that

contextualize very well the problems of lack of transparency in various public

bodies regardless of whether their content affects the outlet's editorial line.


In the reportages with nominal authorship and multiple sources from _Elconfidencial.com_, the

intention of positioning transparency as a fundamental topic on the media

agenda is evident. Articles such as those by Ana Gómez (2015a, 2015b) titled "Lo que

nunca saldrá a la luz pese a la Ley de Transparencia" and "Los nuevos Ejecutivos de Madrid

intentan ponerse las pilas con la transparencia," of July 20 and August 4, 2015

respectively, are pieces that through their multiple sources give resonance to

declarations from CIVIO, Access Info Europe, Transparencia Internacional, and the

Transparency Council. All of this in contexts where political and institutional spokespeople such as

the mayor of Madrid, Manuela Carmena (Ahora Madrid), or the president of the regional government of

the Community of Madrid, Cristina Cifuentes (PP), are central to the reportages.


In the first article by Ana Gómez, the journalist criticizes the rejection of information

requests to public entities while promoting resolutions by the Transparency

Council, which urges them to respond. In the second reportage, Gómez extols the

stated intention or political will in the speeches of the regional and local governments of

Madrid while amplifying the declarations of Helen Darbishire and positions the

Transparencia Internacional ranking, all in the case of the two governmental entities

cited which are also political adversaries.


This positioning of voices favorable to transparency in contexts of multiple sources

was also seen in the reportage by data journalist Daniele Grasso (2015), titled "RTVE

elude al Consejo de Transparencia y oculta las facturas de Eurovisión," which presents

step by step how the public entity has managed to circumvent the Transparency Law and

not respond to a public information request, also evidencing the inadequacy of

the law for not complying with resolutions handed down by the Transparency Council instructing it to

publish the requested data. Similarly, the reportage by Carlos Sánchez titled

"Rebelión interna en el INE: un sindicato pone en solfa las prácticas del presidente" gives

voice to an INE union confronting the opacity of this public institution.


As part of positioning transparency values with the general public, a

reportage from _Elconfidencial.com_ signed by Kike Marín (2015) under the title "La FIFA acaba

con la impunidad de Villar, algo que no se ha hecho nunca en España" prompted the

tracking of cases of opacity and corruption by the president of the Spanish Football

Federation, Ángel María Vilar, to criticize Spain's inaction in the face of this type of

opaque behavior:


"…is being investigated for the alleged purchase of votes in the

awarding of the World Cups to Russia 2018 and Qatar 2022. The Bilbao lawyer has an

open file for refusing to testify in this entire process before prosecutor Michael Garcia.

The procedure is now in the hands of the dispute resolution chamber of the

supreme body of European football." (Marín, 2015)


In the _Eldiario.es_ reportages with nominal authorship and multiple sources, institutional

opacity in the government and the monarchy is criticized. Although these criticisms continue to support the

editorial line of the outlet, voice was given to sources that served as informational contrast in an

environment of oversaturation of news with interested sources—very evident in the case of

the news items from this native digital, as already seen in previous sections. Thus

_Eldiario.es_ gave voice to sources such as the then-president of the Transparency Council,

Esther Arizmendi, the RTVE Castilla-La Mancha workers, and the UN Sustainable Development

Goals (SDGs) in 3 of its reportages.


In another, _Eldiario.es_ criticizes the non-publication of public expenses for the coronation of

Felipe VI, while in an article jointly signed by Jaron Rowan and Pedro Jiménez

(2015b), the cultural programmatic content of the 5 main parties was broken down

ahead of the December 20 general elections to show how consistent they are

with their programs in using transparency as a political banner. This reportage is a clear

example of how all political parties incorporated transparency as part of their

discourse, as well as of the interest of outlets with data journalists in positioning it as a

fundamental topic in public opinion. In this regard, _Eldiario.es_ only identified the electoral program of Podemos as consistent with the cultural and transparency policies that the

purple party claimed to defend before public opinion:


"The word transparency bursts in with force, the exchange of files continues to be confused with

piracy, and the cultural industries seem inevitable, despite the precariousness… In

general terms, PODEMOS presents a conciliatory program… Transparency,

good practices, and accountability are central elements of

this party's cultural program." (Rowan & Jiménez, 2015b)


Although this reportage does not go against the outlet's editorial line in praising Podemos,

it actually meets parameters shared by reportages from another outlet with a

different editorial line: _Elconfidencial.com_.


In this regard, of the two reportages from _Elconfidencial.com_ with own authorship—both

signed by the native digital's data unit, Elconfidencial.lab—one used as its sole source

the electoral programs of political parties, published on December 10 under the

title "Así son las propuestas en transparencia para el 20D: nuestro voto, a Podemos"

(Elconfidencial.lab, 2015a). The article, published on the day the transparency law entered into force, highlighted how its values have entered Spanish political life forcefully by being reflected in the electoral programs for the coming legislature.


Among other things, Elconfidencial.lab tallied the common proposals of all political

parties and highlighted three that mentioned reforming the law and improving the portal, the creation

of a lobbying register, and the adoption of a sanctions regime. In this regard, according to this

data unit, Podemos went beyond the other formations by proposing to reform the commercial register website,

create a database of contracts and bids, and publish the donors of political parties who contribute more than 5,000 euros per year:


"If in quantitative terms Ciudadanos and PSOE lead the field (81 and 80 mentions of the concept

'transparency' in their programs, respectively, compared to 37 for Podemos, 33 for the PP and 26

for Unidad Popular), does the number of times the political buzzword of the moment appears coincide with the

quality of its proposals? Let us see… The party led by Pablo Iglesias has

presented the most ambitious program on transparency." (Elconfidencial.lab,

2015a)


Another article by Elconfidencial.lab that used a single source was published on

September 2 under the title "Descubre cuántos viajes (oficiales) al extranjero ha realizado el

Gobierno" (Elconfidencial.lab, 2015b). In it, a database created by

the data unit of _Elconfidencial.com_ was used, built from data provided by several ministries

in response to multiple public information access requests sent to all Spanish

ministries through the Transparency Portal, requesting the list of official trips abroad by each minister. This has been a perfect example of a data journalism piece that meets all the fundamental aspects developed in the theoretical framework of this study and, for purposes of this content analysis, is another example of the effort of these data journalists to position values related to

transparency as a fundamental topic in public opinion.


The two reportages without direct source reproduction and with own authorship from _Eldiario.es_

were each based on single sources derived from the transparency portal, both on dates

close to the entry into force of the transparency law. One article, published on December 8

under the title "Cantabria no está preparada para aplicar la Ley de Transparencia a

dos días de su entrada en vigor" (_Eldiario.es_, 2015b), criticized the lack of preparation of this

autonomous community to independently set up the regional transparency portal. Another reportage of December 11 under the title "RTVCM destina un 11% menos a

sueldos de directivos desde septiembre" was based on information published on the regional

transparency portal of Castilla-La Mancha to carry out a comparative study between the

salaries of RTVE Castilla-La Mancha managers before the new regional government that

started its term in May.

**VI.2.3.1. Editorials**


As for editorials, they are the most important articles of the opinion genre and, in the

case of traditional outlets, are usually labeled as such in their publications. However,

native digital outlets do not usually publish editorials or label them as such, which

is because their opinion articles tend to appear in their blog sections or assimilated external blogs. In these cases, publications signed by the outlets' directors

are usually considered editorials: in the case of _Elconfidencial.com_ this is Nacho Cardero and

in _Eldiario.es_, Ignacio Escolar.


The outlet with the most editorials in the sample was _El Mundo_, with 3 publications of this type, which

represent 42.85% of its total opinion articles. It is followed by _Elconfidencial.com_

and _Eldiario.es_, with 1 editorial each and representing 10% and 40% of their total

articles respectively. _El País_ had no editorial, which is a very

important fact to take into account in the analysis and sets it apart from the other outlets in the sample.


Of the 3 _El Mundo_ editorials, one was published on June 26 under the title "La regulación

del 'lobby' es una saludable iniciativa de transparencia institucional" (_El Mundo_, 2015a),

while the other two were published in December under the headlines "La endogamia en los

partidos frustra la regeneración" (_El Mundo_, 2015b), on December 6, and "Un voto para

una España unida, plural y regenerada" (_El Mundo_, 2015c), on December 19. The June article

marks the outlet's editorial line with respect to political formations by asserting

that "The initiative to regulate lobbying included in the investiture pact for the Community

of Madrid between the PP and Ciudadanos is a good example of the healthy effects for

democratic regeneration being achieved with its agreements by the formation of Albert

Rivera." In the December 6 editorial, the lack of openness of political parties

to society is blamed for "the scarcity of transparency" as well as "the emergence of cases of

corruption and nepotism," issuing a call to halt the professionalization of

politics. On December 19, _El Mundo_ devotes its editorial to the future Government so that "the

fight against corruption and the regeneration of democratic life" is its priority,

thereby encouraging the fulfillment of "citizens' right to transparency" as part of this

regeneration.


In all its editorials, we see how _El Mundo_ not only uses transparency to support its

editorial line, but also how it attempts to influence traditional parties to halt the

momentum of emerging political forces such as Podemos and its confluences, which it does not

mention in any of its editorials. However, it is striking how it does cite

Ciudadanos as part of this democratic regeneration, urging the PP and PSOE to follow its

example. In this regard, it is clear how in the outlet's editorial line, transparency and good

governance are a tool for influencing politics.


As for _Elconfidencial.com_, we see how in its editorial of November 23, under the title

"Iñaki Urdangarin no lee a Séneca (o por qué Zarzuela abjura de un pacto con la Fiscalía),"

Nacho Cardero (2015) does justice to the outlet's editorial line by praising the

monarchical institution through Felipe VI, whom he identifies as the architect of the then recently

approved Transparency Law. However, it is this vision of Felipe VI as a key element

of democratic regeneration in Spain that the director of _Elconfidencial.com_ uses to

attempt to influence the Nóos case, highlighting the possible consequences of not allowing

Urdangarin to be properly prosecuted, among which is the undermining of

Felipe VI's image as a symbol of democratic regeneration. In this case, the editorial from

_Elconfidencial.com_ has sought to influence politics through the transparency and good

governance that is part of the democratic regeneration linked to the new representative

of the monarchical institution.


In the case of _Eldiario.es_, the editorial of September 18 by Ignacio Escolar (2015) under the

title "Las cuentas de _Eldiario.es_ en su tercer aniversario," although it is a

corporate communication, if we take into consideration the lack of information about the accounts of Spanish outlets shared with their readers and society in general, it can be classified as an exercise in

transparency. Although it is not surprising that the outlet with the most publications in the total

articles across all genres on transparency and good governance should try to use the topic to

promote itself, it is nonetheless notable that there are not more editorials seeking to support the

editorial line of the outlet through transparency. In fact, the other three opinion

articles from this outlet treat transparency as a secondary topic in subjects of energy policy

(2 articles) and media policy (1 article).


**VI.2.3.2. Opinion articles and notable examples**


In the _Eldiario.es_ articles from July under the headlines "La persecución del

Gobierno a la energía solar es ya obsesiva" (Morales de Labra, 2015a) and "El control político

de los medios de comunicación" (Sanclemente, 2015), published on July 12 and 16

respectively, transparency is used to criticize institutional opacity toward

public government policies. In the first article, Jorge Morales focuses on the

energy policies pursued by the government that negatively affected

energy self-sufficiency, criticizing the opacity of electricity bills and

governmental inaction in the face of such lack of transparency. The second article criticizes governmental

opacity regarding subsidies to private media outlets and the purchase of institutional

advertising in these outlets to influence their editorial line, calling for the use of the new political landscape that "offers a magnificent opportunity to

eradicate the practices of obscurantism and media manipulation."


In the other opinion article from _Eldiario.es_, published on August 29 under the title "¿Quién

iba a decir que un simple panel solar iba a destapar las vergüenzas del sector eléctrico?,"

Jorge Morales (2015b) again attacks public energy policies, this time centering the article on the political integration of domestic electricity self-consumption, for which

"objectivity, transparency and future vision" would be needed.


In the case of _Elconfidencial.com_, the majority of opinion articles reinforce the

editorial line of the outlet, although there are more diverse voices and even those of transparency

activists. An example of transparency activism published in this outlet without

deviating from the editorial line is represented by Javier Martín, from the Fundación Compromiso y

Transparencia. Of his two articles, the one published on July 15 under the title "José

Folgado, el político que sonrojó a los empresarios" (Martín, 2015a), made an ode to the politician

at the helm of Red Eléctrica España as an example of institutional transparency, asserting

that "a politician who came to the presidency of a company with public capital (Red Eléctrica)

by virtue of party membership has revealed himself as the paradigm of good governance in

our country." The other article, published on September 19 under the title "A propósito de la

financiación de las fundaciones políticas" (Martín, 2015b), uses the report by the Fundación

Compromiso y Transparencia on the funding of foundations linked to political parties

to denounce the opacity of all political formations. The author's political tendency, very consistent with the editorial line of the outlet, was seen not only in this equating of

all political formations as opaque, but in the fact that he highlighted the case of

corruption surrounding Convergencia Democrática and the measures taken by Ciudadanos in

the context of an electoral campaign for the Catalan elections.


Another example of a publication that praises transparency and good governance without deviating from

the editorial line of _Elconfidencial.com_ was the article titled "Carmena ya no quiere a

Podemos," in which Fran Carrillo (2015) presented Ahora Madrid as an example of

transparency of the new politics while lambasting Podemos as "populist" and using other

epithets. The article sought to siphon votes from the Madrid formation linked to

Podemos in the December general elections, presenting a supposed distancing

between both political platforms. However, articles such as the one published on

September 24 under the title "Encuentros en la tercera fase con los concejales de plataformas

ciudadanas" (Adsuara, 2015), do not take such a marked political stance in

Spanish politics. In this example, any citizen platform that has reached the

institutions of political power is praised and urged to go beyond the transparency linked to

the fight against corruption:


"We must overcome the concept of transparency, which has unfortunately become fashionable,

very closely linked to and referenced in the fight against corruption, and speak instead of

open data, which has a far broader scope. Once we overcome the epidemic of corruption we have

suffered, the question will no longer be whether a politician or public

administrator is honest (since we should start from the assumption that they are and not presuppose

the contrary), but whether they are competent. If Public Administrations open their data,

the media and citizens will be able to find out what they do with our money and

judge whether they use it well or badly; whether they invest it properly, or whether they squander it."

(Adsuara, 2015)


In this way, the opinion articles of _Elconfidencial.com_ evidence a tendency

detected earlier in news and reportages: despite the editorial line of the outlet, transparency and good governance form such an important part of the native digital's content

that it can even be reflected in articles that might seem to go against the

editorial line of the outlet and in some cases even modify it. In this regard, articles of

evident partisan political charge such as "Fantasmas y enchufados en Andalucía" by Javier

Caraballo (2015), contrast with others such as the one published by the activist platform Xnet

(2015) under the title "Protejamos a los denunciantes y alertadores," in which an

analogy was made with the Snowden case to assert that in Spain "the people who denounce corruption are persecuted rather than protected," later advocating the following:


"The protection of whistleblowers/alerters is a struggle to defend the collective work

of transparency and denunciation and not a way of creating a society of informers

as authoritarian governments have often done." (Xnet, 2015)


Two more evident examples of this contrasting opinion in _Elconfidencial.com_ arise in the

context of the December 20 electoral campaign. The first is the article titled "El

Ibex ante el 20-D: temor a un Gobierno débil y terror a un cambio generacional," published on

December 14, 2015 with the byline of José Antonio Navas (2015). The article is a clear

political apology against Podemos, since according to the author the purple formation represents the

"economic regression in a recovering Spain." The transparency discussed

here is corporate and is used as a weapon against the Santander group, mentioned as

an example not to follow in business practices: the Spanish business community should give way to

new generations, just as the political leadership should with Ciudadanos so as to

curb the momentum of Podemos. It is an article that contrasts sharply with one by Elena Herrero

Beaumont (2015) titled "La transparencia del nuevo poder," published on December 12

of 2015. That article, which forms part of the El Rincón de la Transparencia section of

_Elconfidencial.com_, is an apology for new conceptions of power represented by the

city councils of Madrid and Barcelona: "the new forms of power, linked to

transparency and participation, augur a priori positive scenarios for society as a

whole."


Despite this pluralism in the opinion articles of _Elconfidencial.com_, those in

keeping with its editorial line are still in the majority, as with the last article to be cited

from this outlet. In it, published on December 17 under the title "Enchufismo, marca

España," Javier Caraballo (2015) used critiques of the opacity of Spanish political and institutional

life to make an analysis of its causes in which nepotism, in the case of

Andalusia, is clearly linked to the PSOE of Susana Díaz.


Of the 4 opinion articles from _El Mundo_ that are not editorials, 1 was "Letters to the editor," [12]

published on December 11, in which one of the 3 citizens who wrote to this

traditional outlet, David Pérez Tallón, expressed the following about the citizen demand

for the transparency championed by political parties in their electoral campaigns that

year:


"It is curious that during campaigns they proclaim, as an electoral appeal, that they will not raise

taxes or will even lower them, when anyone who looks a little deeper will realize that what

In this way, through editorials on transparency and this letter to the editor, _El_

_Mundo_ has succeeded in incorporating into its editorial line citizens' demands for transparency

and possible responses to its failures in Spanish institutional life, in the context of

a possible political change ahead of the general elections of December 20, 2015. All of

this implies not only the attempt to influence politics through transparency and good

governance as part of democratic regeneration, but also demonstrates how in its

opinion articles, _El Mundo_ has attempted to influence the very public policies on

transparency and good governance affecting the development of data journalism, which is seen more clearly

in the article of August 6 titled "'Lobbies' y transparencia: una combinación difícil"

(Entrena, 2015b).


In this publication, Rafel Entrena (2015a) celebrates the intent of Decree 171/2015, of July 28,

of the Departament de Justícia of the Catalan Generalitat, as "a step forward in the

transparency of the public sector and the prevention of possible situations of conflicts of

interest and corruption." However, the columnist develops a sharp critique throughout

his publication regarding the effectiveness of the decree, since "if what is intended is to give

transparency to the legitimate and legal actions of entities," this will not be achieved with "merely

providing for the formalization of the application registration on the applicant's own initiative with no more

incentive" than publicly declaring that one is an interest group. In this regard, for Entrena

the logical course would be to incentivize registration in the lobbying register with measures such as "facilitating

their participation in regulatory initiatives or preferential access to senior officials or

parliamentary groups."


In another article from _El Mundo_, Elisa De La Nuez addressed the conflicts of interest of the

experts appointed by the Ministry of Health to draft the National Plan against

Hepatitis C by publishing on August 21 "Honestidad intelectual y conflicto de intereses" (De

La Nuez, 2015). In this publication, the opacity with which the experts

were appointed and the ministry's failure to investigate are criticized, before highlighting the possibility of requesting

public information by any citizen and the role of the Transparency Council in this

case:


"Fortunately, the Transparency and Good Governance Council has resolved in

favor of a citizen's request that the declarations of interest
of experts: in this area, as in so many others, transparency is essential given that


it is the only tool available to us to reveal the possible private interests


that are hidden — sometimes unconsciously — behind statements or declarations


in a public debate." (De La Nuez, 2015)

Finally, one of the opinion articles authored by the outlet itself, without being an editorial, from


November 5 and titled "Proposals for Democratic Regeneration" ( _El Mundo_, 2015d),


contains proposals that the outlet puts forward to the future government to consider for reforming


Spanish democracy. In this regard, point 14, "Transparency as a Citizen's Right,"


highlights transparency as a fundamental pillar of democratic regeneration,


underscoring all the points that editorials and opinion articles had already


touched upon as part of this fundamental change in transparency:

"Parliament will regulate access to public information as a new


fundamental right. It will also draft a new law delimiting the relationship of 'lobbies' with


Parliament and the rest of the state institutions. All amounts paid by


any Administration under the heading of subsidy, aid, grant, or similar must


appear in the Transparency Portal in alphabetical order, whether for individuals or companies,


including the concept and amount. THE TRANSPARENCY LAW, passed in 2013 by


Parliament, has two important limitations. The first is that access to


information is not a citizen's right but a beneficial concession by the Administration.


The second is that it establishes broad exceptions to circumvent the duty to inform." ( _El_


_Mundo_, 2015d)

That said, the article proposes a law that recognizes that all Spaniards have the right


to request all data from the public administration, to improve the gaps in the transparency


portal, and to include in it "the identity of all companies, foundations, and individuals who


receive public subsidies." It is worth noting that the article was updated on the eve of


the elections of December 20, 2015, and was also cited by the editorial of December 19


already mentioned. It is in this way that through its opinion articles, _El Mundo_


also attempted to influence politics through the transparency and good governance that


forms part of democratic regeneration, as well as the public transparency policies


that affect the development of data journalism.

In _El País_, no such marked intentionality as in _El Mundo_ was found to


position transparency as a fundamental topic, although it was used as a political weapon


to reinforce its editorial line. In this regard, on August 21, Ángel S.


Harguindey (2015), in his column "El Averno," criticized the PP's refusal to allow the


president of the Transparency Council, Esther Arizmendi, to appear before Congress


to explain its planned budget expenditures. He did so by asserting that "the joke lies in the fact that


the aforementioned Council was created to 'safeguard the right of


access to public information and ensure good governance,'" comparing the PP's intention to


Groucho Marx.

Something similar occurred with the article published two days before the Catalan elections, on September


25, 2015, under the title "La alargada sombra del 3%" [The Long Shadow of 3%]. In this publication, Pere


Ríos (2015) attempts to counter Artur Mas's use of the Catalan transparency law


as a political banner in the Catalan elections. To do so, he highlights


that the only reason the then-president of the Generalitat took this action was


because of the numerous cases of political corruption affecting Convergència.

What further marked the intentionality of _El País_ — to position the political voices


aligned with its editorial line rather than transparency and good governance as a


fundamental topic — was the article of August 29 titled "Ilusiones y retos" [Illusions and Challenges] (Causapié, 2015). The


author, then spokesperson for the socialist group on the Madrid City Council, Purificación


Causapié, used transparency as a political banner in the models of city proposed by


the PSOE ahead of the December 20 elections:

"We will promote concrete proposals: from the launch of an anti-fraud office


to an ethical code that conveys to Madrid society the commitment of each and every


public representative to transparency and good governance." (Causapié,


2015)

Despite this tendency of _El País_, a couple of publications were found that do not focus on


the outlet's editorial line while using transparency as a political weapon. This was


the case with an article published on June 22, 2015, under the title "La transparencia:


propuestas y realidades" [Transparency: Proposals and Realities], authored by Rafael Entrena (2015a), who also published


in _El Mundo_. This article criticized the implementation failures that the new Transparency


Law would have when it came into force in December of that year. In attempting to explain the


causes of these failures, it highlighted the need to go "beyond the circumstantial character of antidote


against corruption," assessing the real difficulties of its activation:

"The reality is that the principles of transparency have been, to a large extent, developed


by university professors, disseminated by NGOs, adopted by political parties,


in many cases, more as a reaction to the climate of disaffection than out of genuine


conviction… What has been lacking, in my view, is a calm debate about the scope of the measures to


be adopted, and a pedagogical effort regarding the true background of the proposals." (Entrena,


2015b)

In this way, the lack of a culture of transparency in Spain was reflected in possible


failures in the implementation of the new Transparency Law, making it an article that does not go against


the outlet's editorial line given that the primary responsibility for


its implementation falls on the governing party at that time, the PP. Another of the


articles without the characteristics of being a political weapon was published on December 14


under the title "Ahora no pocos políticos comen del menú del ministerio" [Now Quite a Few Politicians Eat from the Ministry Menu], in which


José Luis Barbería (2015) simply expresses the opinion that the culture of transparency


is changing in Spain, which is reflected in the political programs of all the


parties that stood in the December 20 elections.

Finally, it is worth highlighting how _El País_ gave voice to the then president of the Transparency


Council, Esther Arizmendi, who published an article on September 27 [13]


under the title "Preguntar ya no es de mala educación" [Asking Questions Is No Longer Bad Manners] (Arizmendi, 2015). On the

"Undoubtedly, a tremendous advance, because there are things that, with public knowledge, would


never happen, and because the axiom 'the greater the transparency, the less corruption' holds


inexorably true. And, in the midst of this change, there is a fundamental driver: citizens.


Without them, nothing would make sense. The Transparency Law and the right to know requires


and demands their participation." (Arizmendi, 2015)

**VI.2.4. Interviews**


Interviews were the journalistic genre with the fewest publications in the entire


sample: with only 4 publications, they represent less than 2% of the total.

_Elconfidencial.com_ published no interviews, while _El Mundo_ and _El País_ published


only one each. _Eldiario.es_ was the outlet with the most interviews published, though


these numbered only 2.

The interviews of _Eldiario.es_ were conducted in the lead-up to the elections of December 20,


2015. On the one hand, on November 22, an interview by Adolf Beltran


(2015) was published with Manuel Alcaraz, professor of Constitutional Law and director of the Transparency


Department of the Consell of Alicante, under the title "Nos hemos encontrado una


comunidad enferma y le estamos sirviendo de UCI" [We Have Found a Sick Community and We Are Serving as Its ICU]. This publication praised the


compliance with the transparency law and the activation of the Valencia portal after just


5 months following the approval of the national law. On the other hand, on December 15, a


collective interview conducted by the subscribers of _Eldiario.es_ (2015d) was published with CSIC scientist and


environmental specialist Fernando Valladares, under the title "Fernando Valladares:


Para ciertas cosas del cambio climático podría ser tarde" [For Certain Things Regarding Climate Change It May Already Be Too Late]. This interview highlighted the


institutional opacity of the Ministry of Environment and how its functions would improve if


the Transparency Law were complied with. This latter publication by _Eldiario.es_ followed the format


of questions and answers, in which questions were posed by various users on the


_Eldiario.es_ website. For this reason, it is the only interview in the sample without a named author.

In the case of _El Mundo_, on September 2, an interview by Miguel


Cabrera (2015) was published with the then new rector of the University of Almería, the professor of


statistics Carmelo Rodríguez Torreblanca, under the title "Levantaremos las alfombras si hay


irregularidades del equipo anterior" [We Will Lift the Carpets If There Are Irregularities from the Previous Team]. Even before beginning the interview questions,


Cabrera noted that the professor "was elected almost by acclamation," which


"may give an idea of the need for renewal at the institution," making the


interview markedly favorable toward the interviewee at the expense of the


previous administration of the University: "marked during the previous mandate by the political affiliation


of its leaders and a management that was at least not very transparent, which led to allegations of


nepotism and hidden privatizations."

In the interview, _El Mundo_ uses a particular case at a public university to


highlight the values of transparency it had emphasized in its opinion articles without going against


its editorial line. It is another example of how the outlet attempts to make


transparency and good governance a model to be followed throughout Spanish society.

Finally, it is necessary to highlight that on the day the transparency law came into force,


December 10, _El País_ published its only interview, conducted by


Joaquín Ferrandis (2015) with Manuel Alcaraz, bringing the total to two interviews with this Compromís councillor


in outlets with different editorial lines. In this case, the interview emphasized


the obscure points and difficult aspects of implementation of the provincial transparency law


passed by the PP before losing the elections in Valencia:

"We are responding to one of the great needs of the Valencian Community and of the


Generalitat: rebuilding the credibility of politics after the PP corruption cases


of recent years. Transparency and participation have now become a fundamental


priority… We plan to draft four regulations to clarify the obscure points


of the law, such as the creation of transparency councils or participation councils


that the law does not explain how to elect. It is a law that is very difficult to apply


in some areas. Expecting all municipalities to be able to comply with it is not sensible


because they do not have the information technology systems to do so." (Ferrandis, 2015)

In this sense, through Alcaraz the insufficiency of the transparency law was exposed along with


some of the reasons for its non-compliance, while at the same time giving resonance to


political statements that do not usually form part of _El País_'s editorial line. However,


the connection of the Compromís transparency councillor in a regional government


of the socialist party explains why these political statements found space


in the outlet. Moreover, all the resonance was at the expense of one of the political parties


most opposed to the outlet's editorial line up to that point: the PP. Nevertheless, it


remains an article that exposes the shortcomings of the law and some of the causes of its


non-compliance.


**VI.3. Results by Topic**


As explained in greater depth in the content analysis methodology,


each unit of analysis was read in its entirety to extract its main topics and


classify them into thematic categories. In this regard, from the perspective of Framing theory, this


section sought the implications that this framing has on the placement of the news


event by focusing on transparency and good governance in a certain way and setting an agenda of


attributes, such as the sufficiency or insufficiency of the transparency law, compliance or


non-compliance with the law, lobbying regulation, sanctioning capacity, etc. These attributes


were chosen based on the research questions and according to the main ideas


related to transparency and good governance in each unit of analysis (Chapter II). In


this sense, each unit of analysis was treated based on the way the discourse on


transparency and good governance was constructed, as well as based on the


salience of certain features that stand out in the choice of particular words used to construct


this discourse and build the story.


**VI.3.1. Thematic Analysis**


Of the 231 publications, 112 showed in some way the insufficiency of the transparency


law, making this the most widely covered topic in the entire sample. The topic that followed in


quantity was Compliance with the Law, with 51 published articles. In descending order,


the remaining topics were: Non-compliance with the Law (38), December 20 Elections (20),


Sufficiency of the Law (8), and Other (2). This last topic is divided into two publications: one


about the figure of Felipe VI and one about _Eldiario.es_'s finances.

From the perspective of topic publication over time (Graph 33),


it can be seen how, with the exception of month 4, Insufficiency of the Law is the topic that predominates across


all selected periods. Bearing in mind that the last period has the most


publications due to the December 20 elections and the anniversary of the Transparency Law,


it is notable that the second period with the most publications on


Insufficiency of the Law is month 1.

Publications such as "Castellón presenta ante Alcaraz el primer modelo de transparencia" [Castellón Presents the First Model of Transparency to Alcaraz] from


July 16 in _El Mundo_, "Podemos impulsa una Ley de Cuentas Abiertas para prevenir la


corrupción" [Podemos Pushes an Open Accounts Law to Prevent Corruption] from July 15 in _Eldiario.es_, and "Victoria Anderica: Voy a crear una ordenanza


que obligue a dar la máxima información posible" [Victoria Anderica: I Will Create a By-Law That Requires Providing the Maximum Amount of Information Possible] from July 26 in _Eldiario.es_, are examples


of how the political formations that came to power through the


regional and local elections a month earlier exploited the law's shortcomings to


promote themselves politically through new transparency measures. These


measures are themselves evidence of the insufficiency of the national law and of how attempts have been made to


offset it through regional laws and municipal by-laws. These attempts will


be reflected later in the sub-topic Political Will.

The topic Compliance with the Law is the one that concentrates the greatest number of publications in


month 4, and this is related to the continuation of new political formations that made


a point of new transparency measures, which were added to the attempts by the then governing


party (PP) and institutions led by its affiliates to


position themselves as defenders of transparency.

In _Eldiario.es_, articles such as "Sanidad se compromete a que ningún paciente grave esté más


de un mes en lista de espera quirúrgica" [Health Ministry Commits to No Seriously Ill Patient Waiting More Than a Month for Surgery], from September 24, were published alongside


"Las víctimas del metro y Raimon condecorados con la alta distinción de la Generalitat" [Metro Victims and Raimon Decorated with the Generalitat's Highest Honor] from


October 10 and "La transparencia entra en vigor" [Transparency Enters into Force] from October 14, positioning


Encarna Guillén (PP), Monica Oltra (Compromís), and Manuel


Alcaraz (Compromís) respectively. These examples demonstrate how transparency permeated


the discourse of regional and local political spokespersons of all stripes due to its use


in political programs, with clear positive results for


new political formations in local and regional strongholds of the governing party.

**VI.3.1.2.1. Insufficiency of the Law**


In the sub-topic Insufficiency of the Law, it is striking that more than 50% of the


publications form part of the topic Compliance with the Law. Analyzing it more closely,


we observe that all 11 publications are news articles, of which only one does not reproduce


the interests of a single self-interested voice. Thus, articles such as "Presidencia aumenta


presupuesto del CNI por amenaza del terrorismo yihadista" [Presidency Increases CNI Budget Due to Jihadist Terrorism Threat], published by _El Mundo_ on August 20,


2015, reproduced the interests of the PP and the national government by positioning the words


of the then Under-Secretary of the Presidency, Federico Ramos, regarding the increase of


budgets for the National Intelligence Centre:

"Ramos has also referred to the Government's commitment to transparency and, in this


regard, has cited the Transparency and Good Governance Law, which entered into force last


December 10. As of July, the Transparency Portal has received more than three


and a half million queries and 2,534 requests for access to various information. The


Ministry of the Presidency was required to handle 265 requests, of which 241 have already been


answered, he reported. To improve its operation, from September onwards a


new, simpler access method will be enabled using the applicant's own


national identity card." (EFE, 2015d)

As this example shows, these statements were positioned to prop up the supposed compliance with the transparency law by a politician affiliated with the governing party, despite the fact that the same information clearly reveals the law's insufficiency due to the requirement of an electronic national identity card — a measure that is moreover presented as a positive one. In a very similar way, the majority of the publications forming part of the topic Compliance with the Law and the sub-topic Insufficiency of the Law were presented,


positioning the then president of the Castellón Provincial Council, Javier Moliner (PP), the


president of the Alicante Provincial Council, César Sánchez (PP), the president of the Association


of Basque Municipalities (Eudel), Imanol Landa (PNV), and the transparency deputy, Luis


Martínez (PP).

The only exception in this sub-topic as part of the topic Compliance with the Law was an


article titled "Las nóminas de los diputados de la Asamblea de Madrid: desde los 1.000


euros hasta más de 5.000" [The Salaries of Madrid Assembly Deputies: From 1,000 Euros to More Than 5,000], in which the EFE agency reported on the incorporation into the


Madrid Assembly's website of a transparency section. In this news item, the


insufficiency of the national law was made evident given that it was a municipal measure not then required


by the transparency law and which sought to compensate for the law's shortcomings and


instances of non-compliance.

The 8 publications of the sub-topic Insufficiency of the Law that form part of the topic December 20


Elections showed the insufficiency of the law as part of the political programs of the


political parties, either through the reproduction of a couple of self-interested sources (PSOE,


Podemos, and Geroa Bai) or through the analysis of electoral programs in relation to


the state of transparency in Spain during that electoral period. The two publications


about Non-compliance with the Law as a topic are a clear example of how the insufficiency


of the law and its non-compliance are intimately related. In the article titled "La


transparencia: propuestas y realidades" [Transparency: Proposals and Realities], Rafael Entrena published in _El País_ how the


electoral campaign showed transparency practices by politicians and candidates not covered


by the law:

"Candidates, especially those with party colleagues affected by


corruption scandals, seemed immersed, in a kind of catharsis, in a sort of
competition of "I am more transparent than you." In recent weeks we have heard


proposals of all kinds and scope: from the submission of asset declarations by the


spouses of political officials, to the detailed publication of the agendas of


public officeholders, the creation of municipal anti-fraud offices, or the accountability of


representation expenses." (Entrena, 2015b).
