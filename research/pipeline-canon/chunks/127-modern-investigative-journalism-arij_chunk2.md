b. Protect the innocent


Not infrequently, the safety of individuals may be compromised by reporters who
seek or use information irresponsibly. A striking recent example was the unintended
release of hundreds of thousands of US government documents held by Wikileaks,
which made available the names of US agents in war zones. Though no evidence
exists that any of these people were consequently killed, the risk existed.


Less obviously, reporters may compromise people who are not yet, or may never be,
their sources – for example, by contacting them at their place of work, where anxious
bosses may monitor their communications. Later in the term, when students
present a list of potential human sources for their projects, you can usefully ask how
they intend to contact those sources.


**C. Conflicts of interest and independence**
**must be expected**


A conflict of interest arises when a journalist has material or affective bonds with one
or more actors in a given story, which may affect his or her judgment about the story.

Working journalists, or students doing internships, may be very concerned by this
issue, because they often work in a related field (like public relations) on the side.


It is very common for investigative reporters to use the revenue from another job to
support their investigative work. In fact, this can be called a contemporary business
model. Cash flow is often an issue for freelance investigators, who are paid when a
story is filed. Many supplement their incomes by teaching or commercial writing.
Sebastian Younger, the author of the investigative narrative, _The Perfect Storm_
(1997), supported his research by working as a tree surgeon; we know another
investigative reporter who earns revenue as a flight attendant, and uses the
job-related travel opportunities to visit sources.


However, a smart reporter will foreswear writing as a journalist about a former client,
for at least three years after the relationship ends. Further, the reporter must
disclose his or her past relation to the client in the event that the client figures in a
story.
Let us be frank with our students: Journalism creates numerous opportunities for
enrichment, and many publishers and journalists take advantage of them. For a
contemporary investigator, this is a mistake. It can put an end to his or her career as
a journalist, or lead to even greater risks. As the great French-Norwegian
investigating magistrate Eva Joly said, “If you want to do this work, you have to be
clean.” _10_

_Case 1: The reporter is invited to lunch by a source. The source takes the_

Answer: The reporter should not be in this situation in the first place. He

who will buy lunch.


This question of how to handle more or less small favors is a very common ethical
dilemma for reporters in the real world. It has numerous variants.


For example, a reporter will be invited to a corporate event where gifts are
handed out. Sometimes the gifts are of small value, like tee-shirts or baseball
caps. Sometimes they include housewares, or watches, or even cars.


Or, the reporter may be invited on a voyage to an exotic and charming
location, all expenses paid, in order to listen to experts or officials linked to the
sponsor.


The profession’s ethical best practices here are not complicated. At a minimum, the
reporter should disclose to the public the price of all the favours provided. If that is
too embarrassing, the reporter should refuse. The reporter should also expect that
if he or she says anything critical about the sponsor, it is very unlikely that the
reporter will be granted favours in the future.


The instructor can note that this was not considered an issue worthy of notice in the
US until Watergate changed the rules of journalism. Only then did the Society of
Professional Journalists warn its members that gifts could compromise their
integrity, and that no gift of value should ever be accepted.  In general, the only time _11_
a gift can be accepted is when refusing it would offend the source, and even then, the
reporter must consider whether or not the source’s offense is merely feigned.

accepting such employment, the reporter should have discussed it with his

This is not a fictional case. It occurred in France, where medical reporter and editor
Jean-Yves Nau of Le Monde, the country’s leading newspaper, was discovered to
have accepted a part-time job from the director of the National Centre for Blood
Transfusion, who was later convicted of selling blood products contaminated by the

However, he did not reveal that he had been employed by the director of the Centre

An investigative reporter can expect that if he or she works on a prominent affair
involving powerful business or political interests, adversaries or targets of the
investigation will closely examine the reporter’s past. If conflicts of interest emerge,
they will be exploited.


To reduce those risks, the reporter must practice transparency. That is, he or she
must expose to other actors in a given story, and to its audience, facts that may
influence both the way that a story is told and how it may be interpreted. A material
relation to one or another actor in the story is an obvious example. So are family ties.
Students should keep in mind that by attempting to keep such facts secret, they
augment their value as news when they are finally revealed.

Our concern here is that, like O’Keefe, students are often drawn to the undercover
genre for the wrong reasons. Undercover work appears to them as an adventure. It
looks much more exciting than searching for documents, or spending hours
transcribing interviews. It also looks simpler and easier: One goes into a certain
situation, squeezes it for interesting material, then comes out and tells what one has
seen. The public loves such stories, which appeal to fantasies of entering hidden
worlds and punishing falsehoods.


However, to be legitimate undercover work must meet a double test: Is the story of
great public importance, and is there no other way of doing it?  An example of a story
that meets both criteria is ARIJ’s investigation into abuse of handicapped children in
private care centerscare centers. We will discuss the story later in this session.


Students should be aware of other ethical risks:


First, by going undercover, one violates the rights of sources. Normally, they
are entitled to know what a reporter is doing, and to reply to any questions or
issues the reporter might raise. The only exception is when sources can be
expected to immediately reply to a journalist’s questions with violence, or to
destroy evidence of illegal or unethical activity. In any case, an undercover
reporter cannot openly question what is going on, because that will expose his
or her real identity. The reporter must therefore contact sources, when
possible, after the undercover research is over, to solicit comment. It is
sometimes the case that the reporter misinterpreted what he or she saw, as
O’Keefe did when he falsely accused a man.


Second, there is a tendency for undercover reporters to tell only the part of the
story that justifies their clandestine entry. That is, they show only the worst of
what they have seen, and leave out the rest. This was a key factor in the
infamous Food Lion case, involving an undercover investigation by ABC News
in the US. The network charged that a supermarket chain sold spoiled, unsafe
food to consumers. The company sued the network, claiming that it had
created an untrue portrait of the firm’s operations. The company won. _17_


Third, the undercover reporter has limited access to information – far more
limited, in reality, than a reporter who works openly. It is very hard for an
undercover reporter to verify certain facts, because that raises the risk of
exposure.


Finally, the undercover reporter forfeits the right of journalists to be treated
with respect. A journalist who does not identify him or herself as such is a spy
in the eyes of sources. Sources who do not like journalists will most often
simply tell them to go away. Sources confronted by a spy may beat, torture or
kill the intruder. That is exactly what happened to Tim Lopes, a great
Brazilian reporter who frequently worked undercover. On his last night alive,
Lopes sought to covertly film the drug traffic in a slum neighbourhood. His
equipment was noticed, and he was kidnapped off the street and tortured to
death.  If it could happen to a reporter as tough and experienced as Lopes, it _18_
could happen to anyone.

If your students insist on doing an undercover project, require that they present a
plan which includes, at a minimum, the following elements:


Why the story can be done no other way;


why it is of great public importance;


how they will exit from the site of the reportage;


who is standing by to assist if something goes wrong, and how the reporter will
safely contact them.


Projects like penetrating a criminal organisation should be firmly rejected on the
grounds that the student needs first to develop his or her reporting and security
skills. If students cannot answer these questions convincingly, they are acting
irresponsibly toward themselves and their colleagues, who may be obliged to grieve
for them.


The instructor can evoke the above issues through class discussion of the story by
Hanène Zbiss, “Quranic kindergartens in Tunisia: Building a Wahhabite elite.” This
report, which won the 2014 Samir Kassir Prize for freedom of expression, involved
numerous ethical issues that can be evoked and discussed in class.


The decision to work undercover was taken by the reporter after it became
clear that many Quranic kindergartens in Tunisia were operating illegally,
and that the only way to view their operations was to go in undercover. In
other words, the story was not about going undercover; going undercover was
the only way to get a key part of the story.


The undercover work was supported by extensive documentary research
concerning the companies that owned the schools, and by interviews with
government officials and parents.


The BBC agreed to be a partner on condition that certain ethical practices
were respected. That included responsibility for the reporter. Thus the
reporter was trained in use of a camera, to avoid accidents that might expose
her. She was also required to prepare an exit plan in case her identity was
exposed.


The reporter was personally shocked by the practices she observed in the
school. However, the most important story was not the exclusively religious
nature of instruction. Instead, it was in the utilization of children to forward a
political project, namely the creation of an elite that would dominate Tunisian
society. This is where she focused her strongest efforts, thus building the
story beyond the question of whether religious schooling is appropriate for
children.


At the end of the investigation, the reporter solicited comment from owners of
the schools. They refused. She had nonetheless fulfilled her obligations.

By profession, journalists are opportunists. They grab what they can quickly take,
and don’t waste time longing for what seems out of reach. This attitude serves well
in news reporting, where the reporter is always running after the action. But in
investigative reporting, we usually need sources that other reporters don’t care or
know how to access, in order to verify a story. This chapter deals with how to achieve
that exploit.


A. What are confidential sources?


We speak of open sources, but not closed sources, because “closed” sources may not
always stay closed. We prefer to speak of sources that are more or less confidential.
They may include people who have reasons not to speak with us right away, or
documents that are difficult to access. While news reporters will often give up on
these sources, investigators follow procedures that open them up.


Confidential sources include:


Characters whose interests in a story are strictly private;


Official sources (including business leaders) who wish to conceal something
from the public, regulators or their competitors;


Private documents that are shared by a self-selected group of business or
government partners, or among a tightly-defined circle of friends.


B. Why are human sources reluctant to speak?


People with little or no interest in talking to journalists often include the culprits in
a story. They may be cynical, corrupt, or even hardened criminals. Yet this is a
minority case, in any region or country where we have worked or spoken with
professionals. Most people prefer to do their business honestly.


There are excellent and perfectly legitimate reasons for certain individuals or
organisations to act discretely. The most obvious example is that many business
opportunities, such as launching a new product or acquiring a property, can be
compromised by premature public exposure.


The point is that a reporter ought not to assume, in the absence of indications to the
contrary, that a source who does not want to share information is acting from evil
motives or an evil personality.


You can make this point very easily by asking for a student volunteer. Ask the
volunteer: _Have you ever done anything that you are ashamed of?_
If the student is honest, he or she will say yes.


1 1 4


Then ask the others: _Have you?_


Then ask them: _Do you want me to publish this information on the evening news? Or_
_on Facebook?_


You can tell your students: _The fact that someone does not wish to speak with a_
_reporter is in no way proof that they are evil._ Often, reluctant sources will be
individuals who made a mistake that they regret. If the mistake has consequences
for others, it should be brought to light. But this can be done with understanding
that everyone makes mistakes.


Perhaps they were implicated against their wills in a dreadful situation, and are
frightened of the consequences. That includes employees of a government or
business who have witnessed events that disgust or offend them, but cannot speak
openly without risking loss of their livelihoods, or even their lives.
How, then, do we find such sources?


One place to search for confidential sources is the story’s timeline. Each event in the
timeline concerns various participants and witnesses. If they have not been
previously cited in news stories, there is a good chance that they have something to
say, as well as good reasons for not saying it. How, then, do we persuade these
people to say what they have seen and know?


C. Building the confidence of reluctant human sources


1. When sources are afraid to speak


Many sources seek out reporters to hear their side of the story after they have
already been attacked in the media. More often, sources will seek someone
trustworthy to hear their stories. Most people have a natural urge to explain, and a
natural desire to be understood. However, obtaining information from these sources
is nonetheless rarely fast or simple. Success often depends on meeting certain
conditions:


The reporter must demonstrate that he or she is fair and honest; this is
achieved through the reputation that one builds as one moves through the
source village. In any village, everyone knows everyone else. They will talk
about the reporter, and about what the reporter asked them. A reporter who
appears aggressive, embarrassed, or interested only in secrets will not last
long among them.


1 1 5


Milad Al-Jabbouri-Alhayat illustrates these principles in her story, “Children
Indulging in Iraqi Violence to the Level of Suicide.” The story focuses on children who
participated in terrorism, and is constructed nearly entirely from interviews with
them. The story began when the reporter noticed video clips of children carrying
weapons in YouTube posts; she hypothesized that these glimpses indicated a general
phenomenon. She found child terrorists in prisons by reviewing news clips of court
cases. The reporter describes the process of getting the children to speak while they
were still in prison:


_“The hardest thing in the investigation is to reach the kids. No one had covered_
_the story from the perspective of the kids – they had covered the issue and how_
_the kids worked, but no one looked at it from their perspective._


_I applied for a permit to enter the prison to interview the kids. The warden_
_welcomed the idea. He believed the kids are victims. He wanted someone to_
_cover this._


_The next problem was the kids’ ability to talk about the ones who recruited_
_them. I sought the help of a researcher who studied jails in Irak. He trained me_
_to engage with them and get them to talk – he coached me. He thought the story_
_was important._


_The best way was to ask questions about their daily lives, favorite songs,_
_singers, movie stars, personal issues away from soldiering. I had to build_
_personal relations so I could gain their trust before going into the sensitive_
_areas.”_


The reporter must demonstrate that he or she can and will protect anyone
who speaks with them. Often, the best way to do this is by ensuring that the
sources will remain anonymous if they so request. However, students should
not offer anonymity to all of their sources. On the contrary, they should
negotiate with sources to try and get their statements and identities on the
record. The instructor’s experience will be very important here, because _1_
understanding the fears and anxieties of sources requires judgment, and
judgment is based in experience. Students may wish to embark on a long
discussion of principle concerning this point; you may wish to ask them to
research the issue, and see what other reporters have said.  This can be the _2_
basis of a class assignment.

2. Demonstrating that the reporter is a worthy witness


A reporter who already knows most or all of the facts that can be obtained from open
sources will always be of interest to sources, including confidential sources, because
the reporter is visibly capable of understanding what the source has to say.


Mark Schapiro, a leading environmental reporter, has developed this principle in an
original way: He takes a collegial approach to investigating complex subjects, in
which the reporter assumes that sources are acting in good faith, and continually
builds expertise in their domain.


Schapiro began his landmark investigation into the market for carbon emissions by
gathering open source documents, “from UN assessments to scholarly journals and
reports by the EU, the World Bank and by the British, German, and French
governments, and by NGOs”.


From this reading he collected the names of actors in the story:


_While reading, you start laying a plan for interviewing. I highlight key people who are_
_either authors or quoted in stories who indicate some level of familiarity with the topic,_
_and write down a list with their names and apparent areas of expertise…. The_
_challenge is to find characters and settings, in a story where what is being_
_investigated is a system._

Often, students or beginning reporters adopt a confrontational stance to reluctant
sources. Usually, this is something they learned by watching movies in which a lone
reporter takes on terrible enemies and defeats them. We urge you to emphasise to
your students that aggression is not a substitute for research; most often, it is a sign
of incompetence, or of fear. Dogs do not show their teeth because they are smart or
brave. Before confrontation occurs, a reporter must acquire expert knowledge of the
subject, the sources and their activities. Otherwise, the reporter is merely bluffing,
and if the bluff is exposed, the consequences will range from becoming a joke in the
source village, to facing physical danger.


3. Class exercises


You may ask your students: _How might we try to convince reluctant sources to talk to_
_us?_ Possible answers, and exercises to illuminate them, include:


In general, people find it easier to confirm information than to volunteer
information. We can use our hypothesis for this purpose, especially if the
reluctant source is someone we believe to be honest. We can say, for example:
“This is what I think happened. Is that accurate? Please tell me if I am
wrong.”


In discussion, ask your students to frame such questions, using their
hypothesis and timeline. For example, a reporter might ask a university
housing official: “My data shows that there is a shortage of housing in this
city. Is that why landlords can charge students high rents?”


You may ask the students to present one of the documents they found
through their open source research. Now, ask them what they think the
document means for their story – does it confirm, modify, or disprove it?
What further questions does it suggest? Ask them to frame a question based
on that source.

D. Accessing confidential documents


Confidential documents are not meant for publication. They are created for economic
or legal reasons and distributed only among the partners involved in a certain action
or transaction. We distinguish between:


Personal documents: notes, diaries, pictures, audio or video. Typically, they
are created as a memento of an event or thought that matters to an
individual.


Process documents: emails, letters, memos, minutes, internal reports,
logbooks, instructions, plans, proposals, budgets, inspections, audits,
evaluations, surveys, receipts, bank statements, tax statements, requests,
complaints, offers, orders, contracts, tenders, data bases, etc. They are
created to smooth financial, commercial, social or juridical procedures.


Exactly because they are hard to get, these private documents will give a story an
authenticity and credibility that are hard to gain from public sources. In many such
documents – for example, in business transactions or judicial inquiries – there are
standards, laws or procedures to ensure that certain standards of accuracy are met.


You can tell your students: _In any investigation, we discover the existence of certain_
_documents as we proceed. But this is much easier if we know in advance some of the_
_documents that we are likely to find_ .


1. Building a “paper trail”


A “paper trail”, in reporters’ slang, is a chain of documentation that
establishes causality or responsibility for a given event. There are two simple
ways to imagine a paper trail that includes documents of interest to us.


a. Using the timeline to map a paper trail


We can deduce the existence of many documents that might have appeared at a
moment in our timeline. For example, a meeting will be followed by a letter or
memorandum that sums up participants’ conclusions, or a business deal will be
sealed by a contract.


Many projects start with plans, followed by tender offers, tenders and contracts, then
status reports when things go into motion, followed by evaluations. In politics, the
sequence may be: campaign promise, legislative and executive actions, and
consequences (reports, prosecutions, etc.). Each such event generates a document –
a program, a law, a lawsuit, a judgment. The full sequence of these documents is a
paper trail.

_Who approved it?_

I. Introduction


Very few journalism students have ever conducted an interview that lasted longer
than one hour, and the same can be said of many professional reporters.
Investigators must be able to conduct long conversations, often with sources who are
afraid or unwilling to speak. This chapter prepares students for the work (and
pleasure) of interviewing, and in particular, conducting interviews in the Arab region.
You may tell your students that this is at once the most demanding, and the most
satisfying task they will undertake as reporters. They will go deeply into their
sources’ lives, memories and emotions, and find the meaning in what they have seen,
done and heard.


You should inform your students that you expect their interviews to be as complete
as possible. That is, what sources say should be noted integrally. You may tell them
that many skilled reporters learn shorthand in order to keep up with what sources
say. They may also use recording equipment, especially when they are engaging
sources who may later deny what they have said. (Most portable telephones now
include a voice recording function.) However, recording generally requires two hours
of transcription for each hour of sound.


II. Basic techniques of professional interviewing


A. Identifying the source to interview


The first question for an interviewer is: Whom do I wish to interview?


In most investigations, the first sources to speak are those who feel
victimised.


The second most talkative group, once they see our interest in the story, will
usually be expert witnesses who can provide perspective on events.


In general, those responsible for a bad situation are the last who wish to
speak about it. However, they may make an official statement that enables us
to verify some facts.


You may point out to your students that this sequence is very different from
the one often followed by news reporters: News reporters usually aim at the
highest ranking or most prestigious individuals involved in their stories.
These individuals are often responsible for the situations that an investigator
examines.


In contrast, the ideal source in most investigations is someone in the middle
ranks of an organisation, at operational or planning levels. These people have
access to significant documents, but have very little influence on how policies
are formed or implemented. They may thus consider themselves victims,
obliged to carry out policies or actions in which they do not believe.


1 2 4


In chapter 11, we asked our students to identify friendly sources in their stories. Ask
one of the groups to propose one of these sources for discussion. They should
identify the source and his or her role in the story.


Now ask the students: “ _Why would we talk to this person? What should be our goal?”_


A general goal is to access the source’s unique qualities. These include:
insight, observations, experiences, judgments, or documents. When we have
collected them, these unique qualities become our assets.


Thus ask the students: “What specific assets do you hope to collect from this
person? What do you think they know, or possess in the way of documents,
that you would like to obtain?”


This is a good moment to ask the students: Should we approach this person as an
innocent (see Story-Based Inquiry, Chapter 4) or an expert?


Ask students to say why a given role may be more or less effective here. You can tell
them at the end: “There is no ideal role in any interview. Just be conscious of the role
you play, and why you chose it.”


B. Backgrounding the interviewee


Before we interview someone, we should know, at a minimum, what role they
probably played in our story. Are they victims, witnesses, or perpetrators?
Which other actors or events may they have observed?


We should also know any facts about them that we can collect from the
Internet – a CV, interviews, news, etc. If the person is an expert in a given
domain, or has experience that illuminates their role in our story, we should
take note of it. (For example, if there is a corrupt prosecutor in our story, we
want to know if he or she has ever been accused of corruption before.)


As an exercise, the students can be asked to background a given individual,
before or after the class session. Possible subjects include a prominent
business executive, artist, or politician. The professor may prepare his or her
own background on the figure in question, in order to benchmark the
students’ work.

Quickly review their notes to see if anything needs clarifying


Ask: “Is there anything I did not ask, but you would like to tell?”


Ask: “Do you feel that you had a chance to tell everything you wanted?”


Ask whom the source believes they should speak with next;


Ask for documents in possession of the interviewee. This especially includes

F. Assignment: Identify and interview a source


It is important for students to use these skills often. We suggest that as the class
draws to an end, you inform them that they must now identify a human source
whom they would like to interview, then prepare and execute the interview. The
source need not be a Deep Throat. Students might just as well interview a janitor
who cleans the classroom building. Whoever they  interview, however, they must
first seek background information. For example, if they interview the janitor, they
should observe the condition of the buildings, the equipment at the janitor’s
disposal, and if possible the maintenance budget for the school.

Ideally, the source will be someone connected to a story that the students are
planning. In that case, ask students to show where the source fits in their source
village map. Suggest that the source should be the easiest to contact in the village.
The interest of this exercise does not reside in the exploit of getting access to
someone prominent or difficult to see; it is in sharpening preparation and listening
skills.


We have experimented with requiring students to record these interviews and
prepare a full and faithful transcript, or simply to furnish their notes. The great
advantage of a transcript is that you will see student mistakes very clearly. In
particular, you will notice when they are not paying attention to the source, because
a transcript will give you questions as well as answers. The most characteristic error
of student interviews is not listening when a source tries to signal that they are
asking the wrong questions. The source will grow more and more frustrated as the
student plows ahead, determined to obtain answers only to the questions he or she
has prepared.


The disadvantages of a transcript, however, are first, in some cultures sources dread
being recorded; that transcription involves much more work for the student; and
finally, that not all students possess professional recording equipment. The
instructor will know if recording is culturally viable in the region. The equipment
problem may be solved by asking students to use the recording feature on their
portable phones, which nearly all of them possess. The audio files from the phone
can be transferred to a computer for transcription.


However, the transcription work will not go away whatever device they use (computer
applications designed to recognise voices and transcribe them work poorly in our
experience). In general, two hours of transcription will be required for every hour of
recording. If you ask students for a transcription, please warn them about the time
required, and also request that they provide no more than one hour of transcription.

Whatever approach you take, please assign this task to students for completion as
soon as possible after your class sessions on interviewing.

Annex: Interviewing in Arab cultures


Interview techniques are culture-specific in many ways. Americans love to be
recorded; the French hate it. We asked experienced journalists and sociologists who
have worked in the Arab world to share their insights about interviewing in the
region. We have divided their answers into two parts: Interviewing the poor, and
interviewing the powerful. In both cases, common themes emerge: Showing respect
to the sources, and obtaining clear answers. However, the means to these goals
differ. Possible uses for the material include background to guide class discussion,
or advance reading for students.


1. Interviewing the poor


a. A common problem: Fear of journalists


“People here think that if they display problems, they will have problems. They don’t
trust local media. If they see it on BBC, they think it has value. But because of the
constant lies, media being controlled by authorities, they don’t trust local media,
even independent media.


“Arab people are suspicious. They hate journalists. It’s a stereotype. They think
journalists will take the information, publish it and expose you. We don’t have trust
between journalists and the people. They are afraid of journalists. Especially people
in poverty areas – the majority, not the elite people or educated people.


“You are meeting vulnerable people whom no one listens to.”


b. Getting access to the sources


“You need to know who has influence, and send the message by a courtesy visit why
you are there and what you’re doing. Sometimes we read the questions to let them
know what we’re doing.


“The dress code is important. You will always look like a stranger. If it’s a
conservative place and I’m a woman, I have to take that into consideration. If I have
to go to certain places I put on a hijab. I meet them on their ground, their level.
Choose neutral colors, dark blue, black. Extra large, to not show the figure, and
sports pants, anything that covers the hip. Avoid any word in English. It’s different
from meeting someone who works in a company or a bank. Then, you have to seem
intelligent, your question must be smart, you must wear nice clothes. For people
from poverty areas, you shouldn’t dress like a rich woman. Their wage is under 200
JOD/month, it’s not fair to speak to them with my language, my jewelry and my
mobile. It’s not fair. You have to be like them. I simplify my language, to not use
English or difficult words in Arabic.


“If you go to a Bedouin tent and start doing interviews, there are 15 minutes of
silence, you say ‘God Bless you,’ they make coffee and pour a cup, then tea. It’s
extremely rude if they don’t offer you something. You’re a guest first, and then you
have a purpose. It applies to peasants to an extent. You can’t rush it.”


“Whatever you’re offered, you have to accept it. Food in this region is a symbol of
going out of your way, demonstrating your selflessness, accommodating them. If you
go to urban poor and they get you coffee, it is a huge expense for them. If they ask
you to stay for lunch you have to stay and eat as they eat and be very happy. You
cannot say you don’t like the food – it’s rude. This is a strong word in Arabic. To
make fun of someone’s accent, appearance, clothes.”

c. Opening the conversation


“Then you do an introduction. You say, ‘I’m here doing this. Do you have the time?
Can I take notes? If you tell me something I must not write, I put down the notepad.’
I demonstrate that at the beginning. You’re a seeker of truth, not a surgeon doing an
operation.


“If I see a mother with children I talk about my daughter. To open a dialogue or
channel of communication.


“I don’t like to use religion. But sometimes people use that in dealing with me. I did
a story about people who change their religion. Wearing a hijab affected both sides.
Sometimes Chirstians were afraid – that wearing the hijab meant I look at him badly;
I told them my idea, how I want to deal with this story; that I believe in freedom. Then
they started to trust me and show what they feel. The people who converted from
Christianity to Islam, they thought I was with them. I told them, ‘I will take your
suffering and the suffering of others as of equal value. If you accept that, fine.’ I like
to be clear with people. Otherwise they may feel betrayed later.


“I keep checking why I’m there. It’s important to remind myself. Why am I
investigating these people? I have to keep mentioning this to myself and to them.”


d. Asking the right questions


“A list of questions is disempowering for these sources. It places you in a superior
class.”


“You can disagree. People raise voices. You can take an extreme position and argue
strongly. You cannot point fingers, ridicule people or act in a condescending manner.
You can’t be blunt. Arab culture does not like bluntness.


“They can’t be asked direct questions. If we are talking about violence, or rape, I can’t
ask directly – it’s sensitive, and people will not answer. We use the third person
formula – ‘if you know somebody exposed to violence.’ The answers will be in third
person, if there is a stigma. If you ask battered women what kind of a man hits his
wife, they’ll say, ‘Maybe she did something wrong, God says it’s his right.’ Women
who don’t get hit say, ‘He’s an asshole.’ They become indirect when it’s something
they know about directly. It’s a protective sense.

2. Interviewing Arab powers: A conversation with Rana Sabbagh, executive director
of ARIJ


a. Making contact


“Whatever the level of society I try to approach people through personal contacts. ‘Do
you know someone who works in the bank?’ My friend may contact the manager, or
put us in contact. You have to know who’s running their offices. If that doesn’t work,
who’s their friend, who can call them and say, ‘I recommend you talk.’


“Or you drive the officials nuts and call back three times a day. Or you wait for them
outside places you know they will be going – parliament, the entrance to the
ministry. You may wait on the pavement eight hours. We did that with Arafat. The
more you care about it, the more you get.”


b. The importance of self-respect


‘Many times journalists give up from the first ‘no’. Sometimes they say, ‘We tried to
call them, they didn’t answer.’ Send a fax. You insist that the journalist go to the
ministry or the courts and finds an access to official information. Sometimes the red
lines are imaginary. The journalists want to be lap dogs. Or they’re afraid to ask. You
have to break all these perceptions. And respect yourself.


“Journalists should not look at officials as though they are untouchable – they are
public servants, there to serve us, not vice versa. Journalists have to be well
prepared, knowing exactly why they are interviewing and what they want, and have
enough ammunition to show the official they are expert.


“In the West journalists go in and do interviews knowing what they’re going for. Only
a few in the Arab world do this. They waste three fourths of the interview talking
about niceties, then they ask the vital questions, and the official says, ‘Your time is
over.’ They’re not used to ambushing officials. I did, I got what I wanted. You have to
be thick skinned. They’d say, ‘You’re invading my privacy.’ I’d say ‘It’s my right to
have information.’ It requires patience and believing in what you’re doing.


“It always helps when you go in and shake hands and ask if your source feels good
today. You send him a copy of the interview when it’s published. You engage them to
show you’re not a bad person.


“They’ll talk to me, because when they give me data I won’t use it in a wrong way. I
use it in context, without bias. I will tape my interviews and ask for facts and
documents. If they don’t want to talk more I will ask who has the data. We agree on
the basis, off record, background. Sometimes if it’s off record, I will ask for parts to
be on the record. If they ask to see the report in advance I say no, but I will read them
their quotes.”

c. Why Arab officials dislike journalists


“Most officials do not trust journalists because of their lack of professionalism.
Officials request written questions and answers because they worry about being
misquoted. They end up resorting to public statements and press releases to make
sure that journalists do not get them wrong.

“Because most journalists look down on themselves and are not empowered, they
approach officials with an inferiority complex, and beg them to get the data, and go
out of their way in praising them and flattering them. They don’t like to confront, and

an enemy.

“You have to be polite – you call a Minister or ambassador ‘Your excellency’, the king
is ‘Your Majesty’. They care very much about their titles.


“From the beginning you should be in touch with the target – maybe they will change

will say, ‘You told me this, but this evidence says it isn’t true. Give me answers.’

give me all your information about my topic.’ Then when I know I have all the
evidence I can be more direct and stronger.


“We were working on corruption in a local municipality. This journalist was attacking
the mayor of the council – trashed them. I said, ‘You have to get his opinion on every
word or I won’t publish it.’ He said, ‘Let’s publish the story, then his reply the next
day.’ No. The mayor had refused to meet with him, saying, ‘You are against me.’ So I
talked to the mayor: ‘You have to respond to these accusations.’ I met him with this
journalist. We asked him about problems at the municipal garage, illegal
appointment of employees. The mayor was elusive, wanted to appear like he had no
relation to the problems.”

d. Insist on clear answers


“People here do not answer questions directly. They think in circles. They want to
beat around the bush – Arabic language is flowery, very broad and colorful, poetic.
That allows them to talk in circles. You can say, ‘I don’t understand that, can you be
more specific, what about this point. Can we formulate the response in a way people
can understand?’ Or say: ‘Sorry, I have to ask again, I want to be sure that I got my
facts right and understand you correctly.’


“You have to be creative, and take the open door. The ex-Minister of Higher
Education had all the details in a corruption case involving the president of a
university – we wanted him on tape. I went with the reporter to the university where
the ex-Minister was teaching. I said, ‘Let’s look at the documents and discuss the
whole issue.’ The first step was to convince him to chat. The second step was, ‘Why
not appear on camera?’ He refused. He said, ‘Only my voice.’ So the camera was on
his hands while he spoke. He gave us very important information by recording his
voice.”

Following Watergate, investigative reporters took the lead in developing new research
methods for the entire profession. Perhaps the most important was the introduction
of social science research methods, using computer technology to compile and
analyse various kinds of data. Philip Meyer, who developed this technique, called it
“precision journalism.”  By the mid-1990s, after the personal computer and Internet _1_
had made access to large amounts of data cheap and rapid, the technique had been
renamed “computer-assisted reporting.”  It is now called “data journalism”. _2_ _3_


_1_


_2_ _3_


The underlying principle remains the same: creating statistically valid analyses of
events and phenomena that could otherwise not be accurately understood. In the _4_
past decade, data journalism has evolved at a stunningly rapid pace. The subject _5_
can certainly provide more than enough material for a separate course. In this
session we are going to consider one of the possibilities – and a crucial one – offered
to our students by data journalism.


Any time we count something – the money that is spent on a project, the salaries that
are paid to public officials, the number of buses that are equipped for handicapped
passengers, and so on – we are obviously analysing data. But from another
perspective, all investigative work involves using and analysing data of some kind.
When we interview someone, or read a document, and keep the content for later
analyses, we are also collecting data.


In other words, data journalism is not necessarily limited to statistical analysis. All
kinds of information can be treated as data. We can therefore harness the power of
a computer in compiling and analysing it – to search for patterns, to create order,
and to manage large quantities of material.


In this class, we will show students that by investigating, they create data. We will
then show them how to master that data, not only for the purposes of telling a story,
but in order to create assets and resources for their future work.


If your students are typical, they will look very uncomfortable when you evoke the
subject of data. So do most journalists. Few reporters feel competent to perform
statistical analyses. Even the word “data” scares them.

II. Building a masterfile (30 minutes)


Ask students to open a new word processing file on whatever computer is at
hand. If you wish, you may open the file on a computer that is connected to a
projector, so that the class can follow your actions on the screen.


_You can tell students: When we create a timeline, we create a chronological structure_
_for our story. We will now make that same structure the backbone of our database. We_
_start with the hypothesis and the timeline._


Type a hypothesis on top of the new document. We recommend that you use
a student hypothesis from the previous session. If all the students are working
on computers, they may use their own hypotheses.


Below the hypothesis, students should insert their prospective timelines.


_You may explain: This is the beginning of a chronological structure. We will now insert_
_information into this structure._


If they know the date at which a given event occurred, they enter that data
into the file. The date may include the year, month, day, or even the hour
when the event occurred. In other words, it should be as precise as possible.
Any date format can be used, but it must be consistent. That will enable the
reporter to search for a given date effectively.


Next, they insert in this new space a full description of a source that gives
information about that particular event. This technique enables them to keep
their information and its source together. You can assure them that even
experienced investigators sometimes forget how they learned a certain fact.
But if they cannot account for how they learned it, they cannot use the fact in
their story. This is the problem we are now solving, well before we compose the
final story.

Isn’t this a lot of extra work?

confident about your students’ work, too.

A. The Value created by investigative reporting


1. The public value of exposing injustice


a. Creating an informed citizenry


The classic argument for independent journalism in a democracy was set out by
James Madison, a principal author of the US Constitution. Madison wrote to a
friend: “Knowledge will forever govern Ignorance: And a people who mean to be their
own governors, must arm themselves with the power which knowledge gives.”  As _1_
Charles Lewis, a great contemporary figure in investigative reporting,  puts it: “To _2_
what extent can a democracy… exist without an informed citizenry?”  The answer, _3_
of course, is that without valid information about what leaders are doing (as opposed
to what they say they are doing), there can be no real democracy.

The instructor can remind students of the coalition model of investigative reporting:
Whether or not there is official action and cooperation in response to an investigative

stakeholders become de facto allies for the reporter’s desired reform.

for corrupt practices to continue in the face of public exposure. The risks for
wrongdoers rise; their enemies perceive their vulnerabilities; their friends are
ashamed of their company. The wrongdoers may or may not care, especially if they
are powerful enough to silence their enemies. But in Tunisia, in Egypt, and in the
Ukraine, to speak only of examples from recent years, the cost of maintaining power
through corruption and injustice steadily rose, until it became unsustainable. Even
small streams can cut deep holes in mountain rock.

society cares about the issues that matter to investigative reporters, and not
everyone has the time to care, let alone to do something about those issues.

2. The private economic value of investigative stories


Investigative reporting can also create economic value for individuals or enterprises,
as well as for society. This principle is deeply embedded in the history of journalism.


a. Investigative news and business advantage


If the value created by investigative reporting is to be general, then a report must in
theory be available to everyone at the same time. In the Internet age, this is indeed
feasible. But in practice, only a minority of the public will be aware of a given report.
There is thus an advantage for those who follow the news, and especially
investigative reporting, on subjects of interest to them. That advantage resides in the
possession of reliable information on which actions and decisions may be based. In
other words, investigative reporting can reduce the risks inherent in any course of
action undertaken by an entrepreneur or an organisation.


The first regular news publications in the English language were 17th-century
newsletters, sold by subscription, which offered information concerning events in
Europe that might affect businesses in England. Subscribers paid high prices for
news that could impact their personal fortunes.  They could hardly rely on official _6_
announcements for that information. Even if their rulers told them the truth, by the
time everyone else knew it there could be no chance to gain commercial advantage.
To this day, newsletters, newspapers or magazines targeted at particular industries
command a premium price in the news marketplace.


Jenny Luesby, a trainer who helped to create the _Business Daily_ of Nairobi, has well
described the cost of not having such information for businesses in the developing
world:


_In most emerging markets, there is hardly any public and palatably packaged_
_information about which markets are growing and which are not. Reliable_
_reporting of prices is scant for everything from raw materials to final goods,_
_from wage rates to rental fees._
_Managers and entrepreneurs in developing countries therefore lack vital_
_information. For them, it is extremely difficult to find out with any precision_

_– which activities are currently making the best profits,_

_– which businesses are growing fast,_

_– which market segments are suffering from over-supply,_

3. Media independence as a business asset


a. The classic model: “good journalism is good business”


The classic model of how investigative journalism creates economic value for the
profession was defined by James Breiner, a leading researcher of business models
for news media:


_When I was editor of Business First of Columbus (Ohio, USA), our paper did an_
_investigative story about a bank’s behind-the-scenes manipulations to get the_
_state to take over a failed office project. The stories scuttled the deal and_
_caused the bank, our biggest advertiser, to cancel its contract._


_The reaction of Publisher Carole Williams to the cancelled contract set an_
_example for me and gave me words to live by. The lost revenue would hurt us,_
_she said, but would not result in cutbacks at the paper. We had other_
_advertisers. Investigative stories strengthened our credibility and made_
_advertisers want to be associated with us, she said. In other words, good_
_journalism was good business. In turn, profitability safeguarded our editorial_
_independence._ _[12]_


This argument is not only American, as some students may object. It has been
confirmed by studies of the Kiev Post in Ukraine  and Nation Media Group in Kenya. _13_
In general, every media market supports at least one source of reliable information
for each significant stakeholder group in the market. The reason is that without this
information, it is very difficult for various actors to conduct their affairs. Thus, while
specific stakeholders may become angry with a particular investigative report, others
will recognise the necessity for such work, on condition that it is conducted fairly and
professionally.

b. If good journalism is good business, and investigative reporting is good
journalism, why do people say it’s bad for business?


Many publishers and editors argue – often on the basis of little, if any experience –
that investigative reporting is too slow, expensive and risky for their media to
undertake. This argument is made in every country we know of. Your students may
well have heard it; they may be afraid that investigation will take more time and
other resources than they can possibly afford. You can address that fear, and it may
be helpful to know what is behind it.


The argument that investigation is always slow, uncertain and costly first emerged
in the post-Watergate period, when the great majority of investigative reporters,
editors and publishers hardly knew what they were doing. In the process of learning
their craft, they increased their costs and risks in numerous ways. These failings are
still obvious whenever investigative reporting begins to be practiced in a region
where it was previously unknown:


Inefficient processes lead to vast amounts of wasted time. In particular,
reporters compile massive amounts of information about institutions or
individuals without first defining a hypothesis that would enable them to
focus on viable stories.


Poor information management creates legal risks. Reporters in a hurry
often make grave mistakes, because they have not understood what sources
are telling them; this happened to Woodward and Bernstein during the

c. The price of corruption in journalism


Anecdotal but exemplary evidence suggests that regardless of a media’s reputation
or investigative excellence, corruption elsewhere in the format will impact its brand,
effectively wasting the value of investment in investigations. An example:

d. The operational conditions for creating value from investigative reporting


Investigative reporting creates lesser or no value, for society as for journalists, if it is
not properly managed, promoted and distributed. Our case studies suggest the
following principles:


Branding: Investigative reporting adds most and best to the fortunes of a
media enterprise when it is perceived as setting a standard of independence
for the entire format. In other words, it does little good, from a business
standpoint, to publish an occasional investigation if the rest of the format is
corrupt or incompetent.


Professionalism: There must be close management of investigations, to
ensure that they are well conceived and executed, that budgets are respected
(or adapted to the growing scale of a major story), and that return on
investment is defined for key projects. At an enterprise level, return on
investment may be measured through increased audience or advertising
revenues, new skills and capabilities, brand recognition or reputation, new
information or data assets, etc. (We will deal with return on investment for
the individual reporter later in the term, when students are asked to define
projects.)


Promotion: Investigations are not simply “thrown over the wall”. They are
announced in advance, in particular to allies who will support them. Print
runs are increased for exclusive stories. (In the case of the Nation Media
Group, print runs for major investigations are 20 % higher than usual.)


Defense: Hostile response from targets of the investigation is anticipated and
prepared for. Contacts are made with supporting forces in the society, so that
adversaries will find themselves surrounded by watchful monitors. Lawyers
review material before it is published. Management at the media are fully
briefed on the story and can support it.


Students will certainly object that these conditions are met by hardly any
media in their region, if by any at all. That may indeed be true.


But that situation is neither inevitable, inescapable nor eternal. Kenya and the
Ukraine are hardly easier environments for journalists than the Arab states.
Nonetheless, the Nation Media Group and KP Media proved that independent
journalism could thrive in those countries, precisely because readers and viewers
care that their sources of news are independent and credible. They also proved that
investigative capability, and the big, long-running stories it creates, are key to
winning and keeping their public’s attention. Finally, they proved that when other
businesses and stakeholders perceive that a media is fighting for their interests by
opposing corruption and injustice, they will support it with advertising and
circulation investments. The task and glory of your students will be to achieve
similar success in their region.


We will discuss the value of investigative journalism for your students in the next
session.

A. The Mobilisation and Coalition Models


In their book _The journalism of outrage: Investigative reporting and agenda building in_
_America,_ David L. Protess et al. closely examine how investigative journalism
achieves results – in terms of prosecution, or legislation, or other action that leads to
reform. Their work has never been surpassed. They propose two models that
journalists can use to drive reform:


1.


Mobilisation. The idea is that public outrage at the revelation of scandal
forces political and social leaders to take action. Thus journalists incite the
public to anger, and the public’s anger is heard. Of course, this is the
legend of Watergate. The instructor can remind the class that the legend
is not entirely accurate: Though public outrage at the unfolding scandal
was great, it would not have sufficed unless it also supported journalists,
judges and political leaders who opposed their President.


Yet we cannot simply dismiss the mobilisation model. Prior to Watergate,
the environmentalist Rachel Carson and consumer activist Ralph Nader _1_ _2_
demonstrated the power of journalists engaged in a cause to mobilise
public opinion. Neither was a professional journalist, but both of them
used investigative reporting to persuade the public that their issues
mattered, and both had lasting effects. Among those effects was to
demonstrate to the news industry that environmental and consumer
affairs reporting could be the object of investigative projects.

The mobilisation model began well before the emergence of muckraking
journalism. In the late 19th century, campaigns by newspapers in the US
to achieve reform were called “crusades”. We are perfectly aware that this
term has a repugnant connotation in the Arab world. The instructor may
simply note that in the Christian world, moral crusades have been
practiced at home as well as abroad, and are an accepted part of that
culture.


Nellie Bly’s “Ten Days in a Madhouse” was the cornerstone of one such
crusade, to reform asylums. Her story captures key elements of crusading
journalism, which marries facts with emotions, shocking revelations and
graphic, outraged language, leading to an explicit demand for reform. (This
is not only an American phenomenon. Albert Londres’s exposé of the
horrors at Devil’s Island, _Au bagne,_ followed the same template. It is a _3_
structural constant of popular journalism.)


Some of these crusades, such as the decades-long campaign to end
slavery, can be defended as necessary and just, despite the tragedy of the
American Civil War that followed it. Others, such as the crusade against
alcohol that led to Prohibition in the 1920s, were counter-productive;
Prohibition merely led to the emergence of rich criminal syndicates. Still
others, including the crusade of the Hearst newspapers to launch a war
against Spain in 1898, or the attempts by the Administration of George W.
Bush to justify an invasion of Irak by the presence of non-existent
weapons of mass destruction, were more or less cynical attempts to
manipulate public sentiment. A current crusade on CNN against human
trafficking displays two other weaknesses of the genre – a tendency to
make promises that cannot be kept, such as “ending human slavery,”
while claiming an undue share of credit for the network’s contribution to
ameliorating the problem. _4_


We would argue that mobilisation is at best an incomplete strategy for
investigative media that seek to reform society. To achieve reforms, it is
not enough for a journalist to outrage the public, or to humiliate leaders
through the revelation of unsolved problems. The first question that
individuals who are touched by a problem will ask, once they are informed,
is: “What can I do about it?” Journalists, editors and publishers cannot
answer that question alone. Nor can they ignore the possible
consequences of their crusades, as we will discuss below.

a. Sharing sources, such as lists of contacts, or data, or access to witnesses.

b. Sharing powers and rights


Different actors in a story may have powers that are not legally available to
journalists. For example, the plaintiffs in civil or criminal procedures, and their
lawyers, typically enjoy access to evidence that reporters cannot easily attain. The
late great editor Harold Brown formed a de facto alliance with the families of victims
in an airline crash in order to obtain evidence from the ongoing investigation; Nick
Davies of _The Guardian_ was directly inspired by this example when he forged a link
to lawyers for plaintiffs who sued the Murdoch empire for tapping their phones.
Anne-Marie Casteret, worked closely (though not exclusively) with victims in the
Contaminated Blood Affair to gather information, and also to prevent the official
inquiry into the affair from ending prematurely, before anyone was sanctioned for the
crime of selling AIDS-contaminated blood products. All of these examples show how
reporters, by working with victims, gain access to resources of the judicial system.


The history of investigative journalism also contains numerous examples of reporters
who collaborated with public officials who were eager to fight corruption. Clark
Mollenhoff’s distinguished career began in the provincial capital of Iowa City, rotten
with gangsters and bribe-taking politicians. Mollenhoff formed a team with an honest
prosecutor and an honest policeman. The prosecutor built cases; the policeman
arrested the crooks; and Mollenhoff provided leads and support through his
published reports. _8_


The key insight here is that reporters do not send criminals to jail. Police,
prosecutors, judges and juries do that. Reporters are thus well-advised to identify
honest people in each of these occupations, and to consider how they can cooperate
without compromising their ethics, reputations or objectives.


The instructor can ask: _What is the risk in such cooperation?_


One risk is very well-known. Reporters who seek to cooperate with public officials, or
business people, or social actors like NGOs, may view their role as enabling the right
people to triumph over adversity or indifference. Eventually, many such reporters
openly share in the power that emanates from a public figure, as a follower. In the
end, the reporter may be blinded by that power, and incapable of seeing when it
grows corrupt. An extreme example, common in France, involves women journalists
who report on the activities of their Minister husbands.

c. Sharing the story


A fundamental insight of the coalition model, amply confirmed both by field
experience and research, is that an isolated reporter cannot succeed. The reasons
are quite practical. First, an isolated reporter can be safely ignored. Even if his or her
initial revelations are powerful, they will soon be forgotten in most cases. Unless the
reporter has compiled sufficient information to support a long series – for example,
when Anne-Marie Casteret published her first article in the Contaminated Blood
Affair, she had already planned a series of nine follow-ups using fresh information –
the story will become old news.


Equally important, a single reporter becomes very vulnerable. Pressure will be put on
editors and publishers to fire the reporter, or apologise for his or her story, or give the
reporter a new job cleaning out the same closet (or coffin) every day. If the reporter is
sued, judges and juries will note that no one seems willing or able to defend the
reporter’s revelations. Even if the case doesn’t get to court, the reporter may come
under attack by the targets of the exposé, their powerful friends, and their lackeys,
including consultants and journalists who wear a leash as if it were a diamond
necklace.


This was the fate of Gary Webb, an investigative reporter who reported in 1996 that
“for the better part of a decade, a San Francisco Bay Area drug ring sold tons of
cocaine to the Crips and Bloods street gangs of Los Angeles and funneled millions in
drug profits to a Latin American guerrilla army run by the U.S. Central Intelligence
Agency.”  Soon after the publication of his series “Dark Alliance” in the _10_ _San Jose_
_Mercury News_, the _New York Times_ and _Washington Post_ reported that they could not
substantiate Webb’s charges. Their reports were truthful, but had the effect of
isolating Webb without further investigation. The Mercury News apologised for his
story, and his career in the news industry effectively ended. His death at age 49 was
ruled a suicide. Following his death, other reporters confirmed parts of his story, and
his central argument – that the CIA had been aware of the traffic, and had protected
it – was considered “largely correct” by a scholarly observer who reviewed the case. _11_

It is not enough to be right. A wise investigative journalist will ensure, as well, that
he or she is not alone with the truth. In order to reduce this risk, the journalist
should ensure:


The facts of the story are not overstated or exaggerated. This was one reason
that Gary Webb found himself alone. According to his former editor, “He was
fundamentally a man of passion, not of fairness. When facts didn't fit his
theory, he tended to shove them to the sidelines."  Later on, when you begin _12_

to them that by making statements that their evidence cannot support, they
cut themselves off from other actors.


The facts, and a viable path to the facts, must be open to other journalists.
Anne-Marie Casteret faced this issue at the moment she was about the reveal
the Contaminated Blood Affair; she knew that other reporters could not
duplicate her exploit in documenting the scandal. Her solution was to
distribute key documents to other media.


In contrast, even experienced reporters could not verify key elements of
Webb’s story, in part because it was based on the testimony of criminal
sources. (A favorite defense of criminals is to say that someone else is
responsible.) Again and again, Webb’s story leads to a dead end, which he
suggests is due to the CIA’s intervention; but that is not positive proof.


Social actors can be engaged, as sources and as potential allies, before the
story is published. The reporter does not need to seal a formal pact with
them. The reporter does need to seek their challenges, insight and
information, and to alert them that a story which can affect their objectives
and interests is coming. Students may ask how it is possible for them to
become expert enough to collaborate with activists or other interests, without
being dominated by them. The answer is that by investigating, they too will
become expert.

B. Social forms of backlash against investigative reporting


We have already considered how American investigative journalists transformed
professional practice in the decades following Watergate. Another aspect of the story
deserves further discussion: The loss of public support for investigative reporters.
This phenomenon is distinct from official attacks on freedom of the press, and it is
recurrent in the history of investigative journalism. The Muckrakers of the early 20th
century attained the peak of their influence in the years 1906-1912, prior to the
election of a reformist President, Woodrow Wilson. In France, an investigative
reporting movement that began in the mid-1980s likewise lost influence and prestige
by the end of the 1990s.


These phenomena do not occur by accident. To a large extent, they result from
actions undertaken by adversaries of investigative reporting. (As Julian Assange of
Wikileaks once said, “Power pushes back.”) These actions include:


1. Cutting off advertising space purchases to punish a media.


This tactic aims to reduce the revenues available to a media. More than a century ago
it was employed against the weekly magazines that published the original
muckrakers, and it was employed against “alternative” weeklies in the _14_
post-Watergate era. It will be employed as long as media depend upon advertisers. It
can be highly effective, especially with media that lack a diverse and broad revenue
base.


The instructor can point out several solutions:


a.


b.


c.


Do not publish stories that offend advertisers on whom your media is
dependent. There are no doubt plenty of other stories that you can
publish.


Take the story to a media that is not dependent on the advertiser.


Since audiences tend to respect editorial independence more than
advertisers, seek to make circulation, donations or other revenues the
foundation of your media’s business model. This is the strategy adopted
by the _Canard enchaîné_, a French investigative and satirical weekly which
accepts no advertising but is nonetheless highly profitable. The _Canard_
derives about 50 million euros in annual revenue from a circulation based
of approximately 450,000 copies sold weekly. The fact that it carries no
advertising signals to the audience that the journal cannot be influenced
by advertisers.

2. Threatening editors or publishers with libel lawsuits.


Legal action against investigative reporters has gained in prominence since the
Watergate era, in the US and elsewhere. Whether or not a reporter has done a proper
job, the mere threat of such lawsuits can be highly dissuasive, because legal defense
is costly everywhere in the world. In the post-Watergate era, the great majority of libel
suits in the US – at least 60%, according to one study – were merely nuisance _16_
procedures without substance, aimed at harassing journalists who did a proper job.
Though the US Supreme Court generally protected the rights of journalists to tell
well-documented stories about the misdeeds of public figures, scholars documented
that editors and publishers were increasingly worried about the possibility of libel
suits following Watergate.  In the contemporary global era, this tactic has been used _17_
extensively by oligarchs from Eastern Europe who own residences or businesses in
the UK, where libel laws are quite favourable to plaintiffs and expensive to defend

3. The limits of adversarial reporting


The idea that reporters are a counter-power, a Fourth Estate, is deeply rooted in the
ethics and ideals of the profession. However, that role is very largely dependent on its
acceptance by the public, in more ways than one. If people do not believe that an
investigative reporter is trying to tell the truth about a matter of importance, they will
not help the reporter. If they believe that reporters are merely acting in a vicious or
vengeful way, they will grow afraid of them. (This is what happened to the _News of_
_the World_, and the ultimate result was a coalition of the media’s victims, including
celebrities, parliamentarians, journalists, and the parents of a murdered child whose
phone had been tapped. The coalition succeeded in destroying the newspaper’s
advertising base, and it folded immediately. ) _20_


In the late 1970s, investigative reporters appeared arrogant and aggressive to a large
share of the American public, as well as to their colleagues and leaders in the news
industry. Their denunciations of political, social and business leaders no longer
seemed exciting; they were merely depressing. One prominent scholar warned that
the unending stream of “bad news” could make both news consumers and reporters
mentally ill.  This was exaggerated, and one-sided: Allowing corruption to thrive can _21_
be extremely depressing, too. But Americans seemed to be weary of hearing that
their society was run by crooks and incompetents. In 1980, the election of a
conservative Republican, Ronald Reagan, to the US Presidency signaled the end of
the Watergate era, just as the election of Woodrow Wilson in 1912 had put an end to
the Progressive era. At New West magazine, a leading investigative journal of the
West Coast, owner _William Broyles_ advised his reporters that no one was interested
in their complaints. _22_

**Introduction**


We recommend that periodically, the instructor allow time for class discussion of
investigative stories in printed, filmed or online form. These stories should be
submitted to students for review before the discussion. We also recommend that the
instructor invite professional reporters who have published a noteworthy
investigation. In both cases, the purpose of the exercise is to form students’
experience, taste and judgment concerning investigative stories.


It is unfortunately a fact that very few students can remember the name of even one
investigative reporter. Even fewer take the time to study investigative stories
critically. These lacunae lead to regrettable consequences. Students will try to invent
solutions to problems of inquiry or composition that have already been developed by
professionals, and they will usually fail. They will regard professionals as natural (or
supernatural) talents, instead of seeing them as practitioners who can serve as
models in an ongoing apprenticeship. Professionals learn from each other. There is
no reason that your students cannot do the same.

1. Using the professor’s work as an example


The first professional visitor in the course may be the professor, if he or she has
published an investigation. The advantage of using the professor’s story as teaching

can be frank about a story’s failings, especially if he or she learned something from
the experience. However, under no circumstances must the professor present his or
her own work as the primary model in a course. (This is not an abstract issue. We

What documents did you seek (including electronic data)? Did any

solve to make it useable?


Is there a freedom of information law in your country? If so, did you use it?
If so, what were the results?


Please briefly describe your research strategy. Do you begin with documents,
or with human sources? Do you map out the sources and places involved in
the story? When do you organise the material you collect – as you go, at
periodic intervals, as you begin writing, or not at all? Do you use a particular
system to organise it?


If you were to advise someone about one or a few research techniques that
you consider fundamental, what would they be?


What was the response to your work? Did anything change? Did anyone
thank you? Did anyone sue or prosecute you? Have you run a correction or
clarification on the report? Has anyone come forward to challenge its
accuracy? If so, please explain.


What was the hardest thing you had to deal with on the story from a material
perspective? From a psychological perspective? From a political perspective?
How did you deal with it? (For example: did you have to do other projects at
the same time to pay for your work or keep your job? Did any sources annoy
you? Did anyone warn you not to do the piece or get cold feet about
publishing it?) Were any supporters or allies particularly necessary and
helpful?


What was the best piece of luck you had on the story? Why was it significant?
What preceded it? (In other words: what do you do to increase your chances
of getting lucky?)


Do you have a method for writing? Can you describe it? Have you found
anything that makes writing more efficient if not easier?


Finally, what did you do, if anything, to increase the impact of the story when
it was published? Did it work? Are you satisfied with the results of the story?

**Introduction**


The culmination of this class resides in the composition, by students, of a 4-6 page
story that follows on their previous oral presentations (see Chapter 14). This will be
their last assignment of the term, and it should account for a good share of their
grade.


In some ways the story may be modest. Because of time constraints, we cannot
expect students to produce the definitive investigation of a major social or political
issue. But we can expect that the story will be original, because it reveals something
that was unknown, or ignored. Its originality may also reside in the fact that it
exposes an official story as untrue, or incomplete. If it describes a problem, it will
point to a solution, or to the person or institution responsible for solving the
problem.


The professor can use this session to allow students an opportunity to update their
previous oral presentations, and to refine their proposals. In particular, the
instructor can critique the ambitions for a given project. Generally, students will see
a wide range of possibilities in a given project. Insist that they choose one of these
possibilities -- the most feasible -- and focus on it. Remind them that if they build a
masterfile, as you have shown them, they can always return to their material and
develop it further.


Numerous important investigations began when a reporter prepared a story on one
aspect of what turned out to be a much greater subject. In such cases a common
pattern emerges: As the reporter becomes sensitive to a subject,  simultaneously, he _1_
or she is developing new skills and confidence. In effect, the story trains the reporter.
It is thus of no importance whatsoever whether or not students undertake a story of
major importance. What matters very much is that by doing a story, they acquire
skills that they did not possess before. (For example: Instead of investigating how a
social problem affects the entire nation, students can investigate the way that it
affects the life of one individual. Instead of penetrating a crime ring, students may
investigate how crime affects the lives of individuals in one neighborhood.)


_1 Chris de Stoop’s landmark investigation of the traffic in women in Belgium, and Anne-Marie Casteret’s investigation_
_of the Contaminated Blood Affair in France, are two such examples. Both are documented in Mark Hunter,_
_Le Journalisme d’investigation en France et aux Etats-Unis, Presses universitaires de France, cool Que sais-je ?_
_1997._ _The instructor will know of other such stories and authors._


This may seem obvious, but we can assure you that every year, you will have at least
one student who dreams of finding a scoop that will make him or her famous
instantly. Unfortunately, these are often the students who fail miserably to produce
a viable story. Please use these final sessions to correct such tendencies. We do not
want to discourage our students, but we don’t want them to fail, either. Ask them
how they will achieve their goals. If they can’t answer in a plausible way, insist that
they revise either the plan or the goals.


These sessions also offer you the opportunity to remind students that their first task
and goal is to tell a story. Nothing will change if they fail at this, and part or all of
their work will be wasted. They are not compiling information. They are using
information to create true and meaningful stories. That is precisely what makes
them journalists.


The following techniques may help to simplify their work. They are focused on
structure, which is the single greatest problem that students face. Because
investigation generates large amounts of material, students who have not been
trained to manage it will drown in it. The requisite techniques are simple.


I. Completing and restructuring the masterfile


The work of writing begins before students compose a draft of the story:


Turning information into data -- that is, giving it a structure and order -prepares the subsequent creation of a narrative order.


Certain pieces of information -- in particular, great quotes from human or
documentary sources -- can be transposed directly into the final narrative.


Ideas or insights that occur to us as we research a story often turn out to be
highly useful as well.


All of these features will be found in a properly constructed masterfile. Please ensure
that your students understand how to build it. It would be wise to quickly review the
masterfiles they began in chapter 12. If they have added nothing to the file since, ask
why.

A. Choosing a structure


In _Story-Based Inquiry_ and _The Story Tells the Facts_, we describe two prototypical
narrative structures: A chronology, and an odyssey (or picaresque structure). The
former is appropriate for stories in which the passage of time is the key organizing
factor, such as reconstructions of crimes, biographies or profiles, or stories about
how policies go wrong. The picaresque structure is well-suited to stories in which
related phenomena occur simultaneously in multiple locations. It also works very
well for film, where we move from one scene to the next with the camera.


Your students should be asked to read the appropriate chapters of _Story-Based_
_Inquiry_ (or the ARIJ manual in Arabic), to describe which of these structures best
suits their subject, and to structure their masterfiles accordingly. In Chapter 15, we
showed how to build a masterfile chronologically, because it is the simplest
structure. To transform it into a picaresque structure, students may simply cut and
paste masterfile entries into an order determined by locales, themes, or actors. Two
writers in _The Global Investigative Journalism Casebook_ are very explicit about how
they do this. _2_


If this proves too difficult for students, you may suggest the following structure: a
comedic curve. The use of this structure in investigative reporting has been observed
at least since Herbert Gans wrote _Deciding What’s News_ (1957). You can represent it
on a blackboard or paperboard like this:


At one end of the curve, we are in a world where all seems fine. Then we discover that
things are not so fine, after all; what we know is a mistake, or a lie. We descend into
a darkness of disorder, injustice and confusion. Then, we understand what has gone
wrong, and how it may be fixed. Thus we rise again toward the light. Students can
order their material according to whether it leads us to darkness, or out of it, back
to the light.


Some students will insist that they prefer to compose spontaneously, letting the
structure arise naturally from their material. There are a very few individuals who
can work this way. In a lifetime of writing, we have met two. Students who follow this
path usually do so as an experiment, for the fun of trying something new, or because
it seems easier than making an outline. In the end it is not easier, because the story
falls apart.


_2 See the Afterwords by Barry Yeoman (pp. 32-35) and Mark Schapiro (pp. 74-76)._


B. Redacting the masterfile


When students have identified their structure, the next step is to set the material in
the masterfile in the order that best reflects this structure. This is a matter of cutting
and pasting. This principle also appears in documentary film, where transcripts are
first reviewed and sequences are selected before proceeding to a final edit and
composition of commentary.


Subsequently, students can cut the material. Above all, they should cut repetitions
and nuances. Typically, students repeat themselves from a lack of confidence. They
want their audience to know that they did a very serious job. Unfortunately, the
audience doesn’t care about that. The audience only notices that it is growing more
and more bored, more and more burdened under an endless stream of facts.
Students must learn to use their best material, and keep the rest in reserve.


Please note: before making any changes in the masterfile, students must save it
under another title. All revisions to source material should be saved in this way. We
recommend adding a date stamp in the file name, to ensure that a student can find
successive versions of a given file.


II. Rewriting the masterfile


Once the masterfile has been properly redacted, the work of writing a draft becomes
far simpler. The student will open the file, save it under another name, and rewrite
from the top. Ideas, changes or improvements will naturally occur as the student
proceeds, and they are welcome.

Over the years we have discovered that some students like to “write away” creatively
in their first draft, and concern themselves with relating the facts exactly later.
Others put the facts first, and only start thinking about storytelling in the second
draft. Both methods are fine. However, students should be told that they must
provide references for all facts stated in the article (see “quality control”, below). They
should also be reminded that journalists do not invent facts, and they are writing
journalism.


At this point in the process, most students will do good work. However, there are
several characteristic errors that students often make, to which the instructor must
be alert. We suggest that the instructor allow each student or project team the
opportunity to discuss such issues, in office hours or through another channel.


A student will seek to prove a hypothesis that is not supported by his or her
material.


1 8 6


Almost always in such cases, the student has discovered another story, but is
unaware of it. Or, the student may fear that he or she has failed, because the initial
hypothesis is not valid. In this case the instructor needs to look at the material and
see what it really expresses. The result is usually a much better story than the
student initially imagined.


However, some students simply insist on their initial ideas, whether or not they are
demonstrably true. The instructor must have not hesitate to sanction such work.
Opinions are one thing; twisting facts to support them is another.


A student will make a real discovery, but will be fearful of expressing it.


Sometimes the fear is unconscious: The student refuses to make a judgment,
without realizing that he or she is doing so. Often, this is because an authoritative
source has manipulated or intimidated the student. More rarely, it is because the
student instinctively recoils from saying that anyone could do anything wrong. They
seem to think: “Who am I to accuse someone?” They are not aware of it, but the effect
is that they put the interests of those who exploit society over their victims. (A true
example: A student began her story, “On January 15, European Union legislation on
food labels will change.” The true story: Food lobbies in the European Union changed
food labels to make chemical products appear natural and pure.) For some students,
this is an occasion to grow. For others, it is an obstacle that they will never overcome.
The best you can do is to point out the mistake, as frankly as your local cultural
norms will allow.


III. Quality control


The practice of fact-checking is more and more widely practiced in international
media. In many countries, however, it is not practiced at all. The two works cited as
advanced reading for this chapter both contain detailed guides to the process. If your
students do not learn something about it, they will be severely handicapped when
seeking markets abroad, and especially the best-paying markets. Editors and
publishers abroad are very wary of stories that cannot pass their local legal tests,
and fact-checking helps ensure that the stories pass.


We urge you to require that students prepare a footnoted version of their stories, in
which they provide a reference for every quote and fact. Interviews should be
accompanied by the date on which they occurred, as well as contact information for
the source (unless the source must remain anonymous). Documents should be
referenced with full bibliographic and page information.


Students should be encouraged to directly and nominatively cite any authors or
works to whom they refer. The practice of plagiarizing other journalists is both
widespread and repugnant. It is stupid; instead of praising someone who may
become a colleague and friend, the student risks their antagonism. The sloppy way
in which many journalists refer to their sources, and in particular reliance on
hearsay, is equally appalling. It is astonishing to read learned commentary on events
that never took place, but it is hardly unknown in the journalism profession.


1 8 7


Conclusion


Not all of your students will become investigative reporters. Some will have the
talent, character, desire and discipline to pursue this work. Others will lack one or
another of these resources. You can show them the discipline. You can inspire more
or less desire. You can’t give them talent, or change their character in one semester.

Every instructor deals with this situation differently. We tried two approaches: giving
everyone in a class equal attention, or focusing on the best students. Our experience
is that the worst students are the ones who demand the most attention. We learned
to refuse it, beyond a certain point. The greatest mistake we can make as professors,
in our view, is to deny our best students the support they need in order to advance.

We hope that you will help to discover and train great talents. Good luck.

**Definition**


_“When information was scarce, most of our efforts were devoted to hunting and_
_gathering. Now that information is abundant, processing is more important. We_
_process at two levels: 1) Analysis to bring sense and structure out of the never-ending_
_flow of data and 2) presentation to get what’s important and relevant into the_
_consumer’s head.” –Philip Meyer, professor emeritus, as quoted in The Data_
_Journalism Handbook._


Data journalism is a genre of journalism where data drives the story. It is a process
through which data is gathered, filtered and creatively visualized, relating a
compelling tale in ways that narrative alone may fail to do.


Let us address a misconception right away: “Data” is not a synonym for “numbers”.
Data consists of facts that have been archived and organized, and that can be
analyzed to uncover patterns and structures of fact. The facts may consist of
numbers, but they may also consist of events, judgments, names, places, or any
other category of information. Once they are organized so that they can be queried
and interpreted, they become a database. The massive cache of documents
concerning the U.S. invasion of Iraq that was released by Wikileaks contained rich
data about numerous aspects of the war, but that data had to be coded, searched
and analyzed before it made sense as a database.


With the rise of the Internet and the digitization and publication of governmental
records, non-governmental research findings and corporate figures, data is now
abundantly available. Add to that the sheer amount of information people willingly
post about themselves on social media sites – such as the pictures of Russian
armored vehicles that enabled the citizen journalists at Bellingcat to map the route
a missile launcher followed to Malaysian Airlines Flight MH17 – and you’ll
understand why data journalism is an increasingly important and sought-after
genre of journalism.


In fact, data scientists are in high demand today at many of the top media
organizations worldwide. The Guardian, the BBC, the New York Times all have their
own data teams. Additionally, data journalism institutions are rising as independent
entities, promoting causes, breaking stories and selling products to major media
organizations (e.g. Jumpstart in Georgia, Visualizing Palestine in Lebanon, or
Bellingcat in the U.K.).


The genre is not entirely new, however. Computer Assisted Reporting (or CAR) could
be considered the predecessor of data journalism. The widespread use of CAR can be
dated from the publication of Philip Meyer’s book Precision Journalism (1973), which
proposed using statistical methods appropriated from sociology to establish a higher
standard of veracity and proof in journalism. Beginning in the late 1970s,
investigative journalists began using sources like government data on housing and
mortgages, which allowed them to analyze and expose wrongdoing by banks in their
lending practices. Others, like Donald Bartlett and James Steele, constructed their
own databases, for example on the criminal justice system, to explore whether black
Americans were more likely to be convicted, and to serve longer sentences, than
white Americans (they were). By the early 1980s, CAR was the fastest-growing
specialty in American journalism, and a powerful driver of the national association,
Investigative reporters and Editors Inc. In the 1990s, as the Internet made new
sources of data widely and easily available, data journalism effectively redefined the
genre through new techniques and applications.


1 9 0


In this new way of doing journalism, data may provide highly convincing evidence,
as with CAR. However, data – its implications, the revelations it enables, the insight
it offers into subjects as diverse as government spending (through budget analysis)
or organized crime (through company registries) – may also be the core topic and
dynamic force of a story. Additionally, data journalism facilitates the use of
visualization programs that allow journalists to tell a story in compelling ways.
Infographics, videographics and other tools provide new ways of revealing the facts
and insights embedded in stories, in ways that traditional journalism cannot do so
directly.

borrows scientific rigor from the former, and storytelling from the latter. And, as is

question that leads to a hypothesis. Data is often a confusing, sometimes boring set
of raw information. If you do not have a starting point, or an angle, through which
you will examine and analyze documents or numbers, they will not mean much, and
will certainly not yield stories. Once again, you have to know what you are looking
for to find it.

How to search


Most of the data published by such sources is available on Excel sheets. Some are
also published in PDF format. How do you find such data? The answer is very simple:
Through Google and other search engines. We remind you of the steps students can
follow to make sure their Google Search yields the right results, which we have
discussed in detail in Annex to Chapter Seven: Advanced Internet Search.


Search by filetype: Google allows you to filter your search by type of document.
Punch in the word you are looking for, then add filetype:xls for Excel or
filetype:pdf for PDF. For example, if you are looking for suicide rates by
country, you can go to Google, and type the following: suicide rates by country
2015 filetype:xls. Results will be limited to Excel sheets.


Search specific sites: Let’s say you only want to check suicide rates published
by the UNDP. Go to Google and type the following: suicide rates by country
2015 filetype:xls site:www.undp.org. All the tables that Google will extract will
come solely from the website address you indicated.

Step 3: Cleaning the data


Once data sheets are located, you must make sure you clean your data. This means
getting rid of any mistakes, typos or blanks in the sheet, that could compromise your
calculations and confuse or even distort your story.


What does this process entail?


Let us say you have at hand a record of all persons employed at the Egyptian
ministry of interior. You would like to check, as a start, the percentage of males
versus females. You must understand that a human being most probably entered the
data. He or she had to enter hundreds of names, along with date of birth, gender and
occupation. Probably exhausted at one point, the government employee did not
notice that he or she used the words “male” and “man” at the same time. It will be
your job to clean the data by making sure only one word refers to the male gender. If
you do not do that, you will end up with distorted numbers and inaccurate findings.


How can one locate mistakes in the data?


The process is simple. Click on “data” (top of your Excel sheet), a drop-down menu
will appear. Click on “Filter.” Small arrows will appear near every column-category on
your Excel sheet (see picture below).


Click on the arrow. All answers listed in the column will appear. This will allow you
to see if all references to males are “male” or if the words “man” or “mal” are also
used. Note that blanks will also be listed.

rather than Excel sheets. One cannot clean data on PDF files and must make
