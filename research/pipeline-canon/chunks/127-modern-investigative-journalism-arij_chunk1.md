**Preface**


On the importance of creativity, curiosity and criticism – ARIJ’s contribution
to freedom

Dear reader,
In 1964, the Friedrich Naumann Foundation for Freedom (FNF) launched its first
international project: a training academy for journalists, hosted at the Foundation’s
very first foreign office in Tunis. Today, as FNF celebrates its 60th anniversary, it can
look back on a history of successful projects in the Middle East and North Africa,
which reflect an unbroken commitment to freedom of expression and freedom of the
press – central pillars of all free societies and functioning democracies.


It is against this background that I would like to express my sincere gratitude to the
entire ARIJ team, and especially to the founder and Executive Director,
Rana Sabbagh, for our productive cooperation and valued partnership. We are proud
to contribute to this university curriculum for professors and lecturers, and
delighted to use this opportunity to announce that we are establishing a partnership
on “Freedom of the Press in the Middle East and North Africa”.


The partnership will initially focus on hosting multiple workshops on investigative
journalism and human rights, as well as on composing policy papers on topics
including freedom of information and cybercrime law. We trust that it will make a
useful contribution to developing free and open societies in the MENA region, in
which journalists and the publications they represent are able to work without
censorship, repression and constant fear for their lives.


We see the work done by ARIJ as a milestone on the crucial path towards freedom of
expression and media freedom in the MENA region. In recognition of its important
work, ARIJ was recently awarded the Foundation’s Raif Badawi Award for
courageous journalists.

ARIJ is breaking new ground when it comes to encouraging the work of independent
Arabic journalists. The network promotes investigative journalism in the Arab world
by funding exceptional projects and journalists, as well as by offering training,
coaching and even legal support. By doing so, ARIJ plays a key role in providing
journalists in the region both with a platform for their work and with the necessary
tools for putting their ideas into practice.


This publication is an excellent example of the ARIJ vision. The innovative and
unique _Model Curriculum for an Introductory Course on Investigative Journalism at_
_Arab Universities_ is designed to teach university students both conceptual and
practical approaches to investigative journalism. Course participants are expected to
develop and realize an investigative story project and, in so doing, to develop a deeper
understanding of the role of journalism as a crucial source of information for all
citizens – as a form of public service for society, so to speak. The curriculum also sensitizes students to the importance and usefulness of digital open sources. The aim is
to create a tradition of shared resources in the MENA region in support of the overall
goal of improving the quality of journalism.


The need for such initiatives has never been greater. Globally, journalism is in crisis:
intellectual property is being undermined and quality standards are slipping. Other,
more specific threats emerge from the political and social circumstances in different
parts of the world. In the West, for example, interest in print media has shown a steep
decline, while online sources – more up to date, but usually offering only shallow
analysis bereft of vital correlations or contexts – are becoming vastly more popular.

Media consumption patterns are also changing, with people seeking to absorb many
small nuggets of information in the shortest time possible. In a desperate attempt to
reach more readers, even renowned print publications often choose to publish less
text and larger pictures while cutting costs and expanding their social media presence. As a result, journalism surrenders its intellectual aspirations, and newspapers
lose their unique identities.

Credibility represents another major issue for journalists and media professionals
worldwide, as expressed in the so-called “fake news” debate. In times when even
heads of state openly accuse renowned and independent media sources of lying and
presenting false evidence, journalists often find themselves in the position of having
to defend not only their integrity and reliability, but also their mere presence. While
the close examination of facts and news items undoubtedly remains an essential part
of journalism, the debate about “fake news” has created an atmosphere of distrust
and even hostility towards journalists – precisely the people whose role it should be
to serve society by critically examining the state and its representatives.

Without any doubt, journalists in the MENA region are confronted with far greater
and more obvious challenges. First and foremost among them is state repression and
censorship. According to the 2018 World Press Freedom Index, journalists all over
the Middle East and North Africa face persecution. Criticism of the state or state-affiliated authorities often results in arbitrary trials and year-long sentences: journalists
are regarded as enemies of the state and sometimes even accused of terrorist activities for merely doing their work.


Equipping journalists with the tools of investigative journalism therefore both
strengthens freedom of the press and freedom of expression in the region, and also
supports an essential pillar of democracy: insight into government action. The roles
journalists play can range from merely providing information to telling stories that
trigger unforeseen consequences on many levels. Investigative journalism therefore
always entails great responsibility while also providing the unique opportunity to
change people’s opinions and make a difference in the world.


In a world where violence, repression and suspicion against journalists are gaining
ground, investigative journalism plays a fundamental role in questioning the actions
of state actors. It functions as an alternative information source to state-owned or
state-affiliated media. Creating transparency by informing citizens, thereby empowering them to form their own opinions, is one of the most important mechanisms for
a thriving and prospering democracy.

Journalism does more than just transmit information. It also serves as an educational tool that promotes the establishment of an informed and educated citizenry, the
backbone of any democracy. Only when a state’s actions are transparent to its


citizens can the rule of law be safeguarded and ensured.


Let me close with a quote by one of the most renowned German liberal politicians, the
former chairman of the board of directors of the Friedrich Naumann Foundation for
Freedom, Ralf Dahrendorf (1929-2009): “As jesters of modern society, intellectuals
are virtually obliged to doubt anything that has not yet been doubted, to look astonished at everything that seems natural, to relativize all authority in a critical way and
to pose all of those questions nobody else dares to ask.” It is this spirit of creativity,
curiosity and criticism that characterizes the work of ARIJ and that needs to be cherished and preserved in media and journalism throughout the MENA region. Let us
hope that we are moving one step closer to open societies in which the media and
journalists are seen not as enemies, but as valuable allies and defenders of free societies and functioning –democracies.


_Dr. René Klaff_

_Head of International Department_

**I. Objectives of**
**this curriculum**


Within our lifetimes it was common to hear even journalism professors say that
journalism cannot be taught; it could only be learned through hands-on experience.
This was nonsense; any intellectual activity can be taught, if the professor and
students are not complete donkeys.

It was also common to hear journalists and professors say that investigative
reporting is merely “good, old-fashioned journalism”, properly done. This statement
was largely based in the desire of investigative reporters to encourage other reporters
to follow them. The truth, as John Ullman declared in his classic _The Reporter’s_

_Handbook_, _[1]_ is that investigative reporting requires skills and attitudes that most
reporters never learn.


Yet most reporters can indeed succeed at investigative work, if they acquire the
appropriate methods and the desire to use them. This course is designed to provide
both.


We set out _one_ way of teaching investigative reporting. We include references to
scholarly literature, as well as teaching notes for each session. The teaching notes
may be used as a how-to guide for instructors who have never taught this material;
the references can be used as a guide to further study. They include both freeware
and inexpensive handbooks that we created over the past ten years, including a
manual of practice, an anthology of investigative stories with afterwords by their
authors, and handbooks aimed at advanced methods. These materials were written
specifically to provide foundation texts for instructors. They will someday be
supplanted by other texts, but for the present we think they will be useful to you.


We are giving you a place to start; where you finish depends on your inspiration,
insight and passion for the subject. You may use, not use, or adapt any or all of this
material to suit your interests and strengths as a teacher. That said, we can
guarantee that by using this material, you can address the main concerns of your
students. We can make this guarantee because we have taught it successfully to
undergraduates and professionals in France, the UK, the US, Brazil, China, Tunisia,
Holland, Belgium, Norway, Iraq, Egypt, Senegal, Cameroun, Ivory Coast, South
Africa, Ukraine, and other countries and regions. This is a sturdy platform, and you
can dance on it.


The course is aimed at two different classes of instructors, who between them
account for the great majority of investigative journalism teachers:

1. Those who have scholarly training but limited or no experience of
in-depth reporting, writing and editing;
2. Those who have practical experience, but limited scholarly training, and
who are teaching investigative reporting in an anecdotal manner.


The first category is particularly prominent in the developing world. In many
countries that have only recently emerged from authoritarian governance, it was
virtually impossible for media professionals to practice investigative reporting. Thus
instructors often have only a theoretical knowledge of investigative reporting.


Even in countries where investigative reporting is an established practice,
instruction at undergraduate and masters levels may fairly be described, in most
cases, as thin from a scholarly perspective. The consequences go beyond failure to
access significant and useful knowledge. Students are typically taught the personal
methods that have worked for a teacher who possesses more or less practical
experience. Those methods may or may not reflect best practice. Nor do they always,
or even often, reflect deep knowledge of the history of investigative reporting
practices, not least because in many places, such histories were never written.


That is not a minor issue, if one judges by the fact that reporters familiar with the
history of investigative work have been responsible for some of the major
breakthroughs in our era. We are at a moment when historic practitioners, notably
those of the Watergate era, will soon leave us. If we do not capture their history, our
students will surely undergo the tiresome task of recreating it in ignorance.


The publication of _[2]_ UNESCO’s “Model Curricula for Journalism Education” in 2007
partially addressed this situation. In a syllabus for “in-depth journalism”, Yvonne T.
Chua, a first-rate practitioner of investigative reporting, articulated quite well
different aspects of the craft that must be covered in an introductory course.
However, she provided very little guidance for professors who lack practical
experience; surely because of space constraints, her curriculum includes no
teaching notes. Moreover, its focus on practice largely sets aside the study of
investigative reporting as an economic and political activity. We will say more about
this point below.


The divide between scholars and practitioners is a constant of professional
education; I have personally witnessed it in business schools as well as universities.
My personal feeling is that it is good for a practitioner to learn how scholars think,
and to appreciate the rigor and depth that characterise their best work. It is also good
for scholars to create resources that practitioners need, and this curriculum asks
them to take on that role, notably through research on open sources and models of
investigative reporting in regions that have so far been fully or largely absent from the
scholarly literature.

Nothing starts from nothing, but there is a big hole in our knowledge about where
investigative reporting starts from in most of the world. For that reason, we have
based our academic arguments largely on the deepest and most accessible
knowledge base in the field, which happens to be from the US. The references are
necessarily incomplete, and we will acknowledge by name in future updates scholars
who provide us with further references and insights that we can add to the text.


Let us now consider a principal innovation of this text – its focus on the elements that
define the sustainability of investigative journalism as a genre, a social force, and an
economic enterprise.


**A. Teaching economic and social aspects**
**of investigative journalism**


We know of no one-semester course in which students are taught a comprehensive
approach to investigative reporting, including key aspects of its history, aesthetics,
ethics and business models. From the perspective of their careers, that is a serious
oversight. Often, they will find themselves obliged to sell their work, or a unit that
does this work, to superiors in their organisations who have never performed an
investigation themselves. Always, they will have to explain what they do – as
investigative reporters did in France beginning in the 1980s _[3]_ - to a public that
regards their work as dangerous. We must therefore provide our students with
arguments that they can critique, explore and adapt for the purposes of their own
enterprises.


Two assumptions dominate instruction about investigative reporting as an
enterprise: First, that it is a form of public service, and by extension, its chief value
is social in nature; and second, that it aims at informing citizens so that they can
make their own decisions, rather than seeking to impose conclusions. Both those
assumptions can be challenged on theoretical and practical grounds.


1. Investigative journalism as a driver of individual and organizational value


Our first proposition is that economics is a central aspect of investigative reporting,
whether we view the work as a practice or as an enterprise. A corollary is that value
must be created for the reporter and the media that publish investigative reporting,
as well as for the public, or it will not be sustainable.
One reason behind these notions is that in two decades of teaching investigative
reporting at undergraduate, graduate and professional levels, someone in every
participant group asked us, while the others listened closely: “Can we make a living
at this work?”
The answer is yes, if they are shown how. Investigative reporting skills are a
tremendous asset in a student’s future career, and investigative material is highly
valuable to media enterprises that exploit it properly. There is no reason to be shy or
obscure about this, especially at a moment when most journalism students cannot
find jobs after graduation. In order to prepare them for their careers, we must

discuss the business of journalism, and how investigative reporting creates
resources and assets, and not merely costs. We can show them that if they learn to
investigate, they will always have a job, in addition to creating social value.


This leads to discussion of a major shift in the nature of journalism in general, and
investigative reporting in particular: the emergence of trans-national reporting
projects. It is no longer true that “all journalism is local.” Investigative reporting
increasingly pulls local elements into a global framework, and in the process creates
a global audience for major stories. Journalism that only meets local standards
cannot accede to this global market. By the same token it cannot influence global
debates. If we hope to train our students for the top ranks of their profession – and if
we don’t, what exactly do we think we’re doing? – we must open the door to
world-class skills for them.


2.Investigative journalism as a change agent


The core justification of objective reportage is that it enables citizens to make up their
own minds after providing them with facts. However, that idea is a fairly recent
development. Historically, the concept of objectivity enabled partisan newspapers in
the 19th-century US to accede to a larger, non-partisan public. In particular,
sensational crime or scandal stories were presented as “objective” and true

representations of contemporary life. _[4]_ Later, the claim of objectivity enabled the
reporters who undertook the Watergate investigation to claim that they were acting
in the public interest, and not merely seeking to destroy an elected President for
partisan motives.


Whether or not one considers objectivity a fully attainable ideal, the claim of
non-partisanship that defended investigative reporting in the post-Watergate era was
sincere. There are still leading theorist-practitioners who believe that the
fundamental task of investigative reporting is to provide unbiased information that
can support citizen decisions, such as Charles Lewis, founder of the Center for Public
Integrity, a major institution in the history of investigative reporting. Lewis’s
argument, diffused through numerous articles, has been essential to the rise of

non-profit investigative reporting centres around the world, _[5]_ which we will study
later in the term.


But there was always a conceptual and empirical counter-argument: Reporters don’t
just provide facts. They tell stories, and stories, by definition, _[6]_ always lead to a
meaning.

The meaning of investigative stories, almost always, is that something has to change.
In other words, investigative reporters do not only or merely invite their audiences to
make a decision about certain issues. They suggest, more or less strongly and
directly, what that decision ought to be. They make a judgment, and if they care
about society, they want that judgment to be followed by effects. This desire is deeply
embedded in practice. Even reporters who claim to respect entirely their audience’s
democratic rights will file applications for prizes whose criteria include whether or
not their stories achieved “results.”


We are aware that in many countries, developed or not, this desire can serve as a
license to lie. Since at least the origins of the English-language newspaper, a
primordial function of the news has been to advance partisan objectives. That
partisan function can be supported either by leaving out certain facts (in modern
terms, “spin”), or by falsifying facts. Neither of these procedures is compatible with
world-class investigative work. The highest professional standard, which is the one
we want our students to learn, requires taking all available facts into account before
a judgment is made. But it can, and often must be made.


Investigative stories, no matter how profound or objective they may be, do not
achieve results by accident or simply by being displayed. As Protess et al. showed in
their classic book, _The Journalism of Outrage_, _[7]_ such success requires either
mobilizing the public’s anger, or building coalitions that use investigative revelations
as levers to affect change. If neither of those strategies is enacted, or if it fails, nothing
changes.


In the 20 years following publication of _The Journalism of Outrage_, practice has
validated the insight that coalitions of social forces are necessary to obtaining change
through investigative reporting. In the process, it has been shown that neutrality is
not always essential to either credibility or impact. Investigative reporting is
increasingly undertaken by non-governmental organisations, like Greenpeace or the
Project on Government Oversight, who transparently proclaim the objectives of their
work. _[8]_ Leading news media, such as The Guardian, have openly allied with such
organisations on at least two landmark occasions (the Wikileaks and Trafigura
affairs) in the past decade. We have to take into account such glaring evidence that
though investigative journalism makes use of rigorously obtained facts for the
purpose of effecting change, the desire and ambition to change society is inherently
subjective.

This new environment and its ambiguities, far more complex than a world in which
objectivity was the journalistic standard, cannot be wished away. It must be
presented in such a way that students can navigate and thrive in it. No instructor,
anywhere in the world, can predict or provide answers to all the consequent

**B.** **Teaching economic and social aspects**
**of investigative ournalism**


At the time Yvonne T. Chua created her model curriculum, the various processes of
investigative work – the conception, planning, researching, organization and
composition of a story – were thought of as separate tasks which must be completed
in a sequence of discrete steps. In 2009, the publication of _Story-Based Inquiry:_
_A Manual for Investigative Journalists_ by Arab Reporters for Investigative
Journalism and UNESCO demonstrated how they could be fused into a single

continuous workstream. _[9]_ The benefits include economies of time – an explicit reply
to the notion that investigative work is always slow and costly – as well as increased
quality of information and storytelling.


This curriculum is the first to fully integrate that manual and its various
supplements. It is also the first to include teaching notes that tell an instructor how
to present the material, and how to engage the class in discussion. Of course, there
is nothing obligatory about this material. Experienced practitioner-instructors will
surely have developed their own exercises and tools, and some may be better than
those presented here. We have cited several such inventions, with permission, from
other practitioner-scholars. (Whenever we have the chance, we watch our colleagues
teach. As many of you already know, so should you. This is a craft, and all crafts
require apprenticeship.) Even beginners in this field will have insights that can be
added to our work, or objectives that make cutting some of it necessary. We are
building a floor under the class, and we invite you to put the ceiling wherever you
please. We also invite you to share your discoveries with us. We systematically give
credit to colleagues who contribute to projects such as these, and in future editions
we will be glad to cite yours.


**C.** **Toward an archive of models and open**
**sources**


No one course in investigative reporting, and no one institution, no matter how
excellent its work and how admirable its goals, can suffice to make investigative
journalism a sustainable, well-rooted feature of the landscape. This curriculum can
also be read as a call to participate in that enterprise. We hope that you and your
students will conduct essential research that contributes to creating an Arab
tradition and shared resources.

In particular, we ask you to contribute to knowledge of open sources. An open source
is any information or archive that may be freely accessed in the public domain. The
compilation of extensive open sources in the US was a key initiative of John Ullman,
the first director of Investigative Reporters and Editors, Inc., and was vital to the

institutionalization of investigative- reporting in the United States. _[10 ]_ There is no
reason that such resources cannot be created in other regions, too. The Investigative
Dashboard of the Organised Crime and Corruption Reporting Project has
demonstrated that publicly-available information on businesses in different
countries can be compiled, indexed and rendered useable on a global scale. The same
can be done at national and regional levels.


We have provided some open sources in this curriculum. Equally important, we have
provided templates that can serve instructors as class exercises in finding and
cataloguing open sources. By collecting and sharing this material, instructors and
students will help to create a regional source archive comparable to the open source
archives developed in the US and Europe. This will also help to answer an objection
which you will surely hear from your students: That in many Arab countries there
are no open sources at all. It is certainly true that there is never enough open, public
information, wherever a reporter is working. But it is also true that there is always
more open information than one would expect, and that one will never find it unless
one looks for it.


A further task to which all users of this curriculum may contribute is in the domain
of journalism history: the discovery and analysis of models in the genre. These
models may be individual journalists, ground-breaking media or formats, or singular
works that embody the ideals and best practices of investigative reporting at a given
moment. Some of the models may belong to the realm of fiction, in the use of reality
to enrich art, and of art to empower truth-telling. Some will be due to the efforts of
activists, militants or non-governmental organisations who surpass timid official
media in seeking for truth. Some will have been created by journalists who defied the
mediocrity around them, and distinguished themselves by their courage and
tenacity.


We know this: When we ask participants in our seminars to name such models, they
often hesitate. Sometimes it is because the models are rare; they were crushed or
obscured by authoritarian regimes. Sometimes it is because people did not realise
how important it is to have someone to admire, who preceded us on our path.
(Believe us, it is very, very important.) Sometimes it is because the chaos of life makes
it difficult to reflect. And sometimes, unfortunately, it is because people are too lazy
and arrogant to consider the work of others as anything else but competition, to be
ignored or forgotten. We must get past these obstacles. We must train our students
to seek inspiration on the emotional and practical levels from journalists close to
them, from within their historical, national and professional traditions.


That is one way of using the material, and there are others. You may decide to change
the order of chapters, in order to begin practical work before students learn the
history and economics of investigative reporting. In some schools, students get all
theory and no practice, and are burning to get into the field. You can give them what


_10_


they want, and weave our scholarly content among the practical exercises. You will
find the best way to lead your class.


We have provided a number of model stories in this curriculum, from freely available
national and international sources. We have also shown how they may be used in the
classroom, as objects of analysis concerning their methods, narrative structures,
and social or legal impact. The focus must always be on how students may improve
their own practice and ethics through the study of models. Some models may be
imitated; others contain techniques that can be appropriated; others simply inspire.

This material is only a start, and we ask you to extend it, in the classroom and your
scholarly research. We hope that you and your students will seek out other works
and journalists who are worthy of emulation, and take the trouble to understand
exactly what makes them admirable. We hope that you will leave the record of this
search in the scholarly literature, for the next generation of instructors and students.
And we hope that you will share your discoveries with others in the region, as you
share your open sources. As with open sources, we have provided a template that can
serve as a guide for student work on models.


Let us now consider the structure and content of this course in detail, one class
session after another. The overall shape and strategy of the course is to proceed from
general principles and history, and then to move into practice. We have observed that
students who are thrown immediately into practice, on the assumption that ‘learning
by doing’ is the best way forward, often become confused and hesitant. So first we
will tell them what they are going to do, and how practice has evolved in the history
of journalism. We will also give them an idea of the markets and careers that may be
open to them. Then, when they are eager to enter this new domain themselves, we
will show them how to do it.


Annex:


Introduction to Investigative Journalism: Course description and outline


_Note: As with other material in this curriculum, this course outline can be modified to_
_create a final syllabus, according to the standards of your institution or the instructor’s_
_personal preferences._


Investigative journalism can be defined in several ways: as a reportorial method, as
a literary and documentary genre, as an organizational and personal capability, as a
driver of social and political change, and as a powerful economic current in the
history of media. This one-term (37.5 contact hours) introductory course aims to
survey these different aspects of the field. By the end of the semester:


Students will have a firm understanding of how investigative reporters
conceive, research, organise and write powerful stories. They will be familiar
with examples of high-level investigative reports from their region and the
world. They will learn the fundamentals of story-based inquiry, at this writing
the global benchmark method in the field.

Their skills will include developing open sources, as well as interviewing

The first purpose of this class session is to define the differences between
investigative journalism and conventional news reporting. These differences
involve techniques, professional standards, ethics, and outcomes.


A second purpose is to allow and encourage students to reveal their own
conceptions (and misconceptions) of the genre and what they hope to
accomplish by studying it. The instructor must therefore take extra care not
to dominate the conversation; instead, he or she can guide and inform it,
allowing students to enrich the discussion. This can set the tone for the entire
term.


**A. All journalism is not investigative journalism**


It is not true, as people (including some professors) sometimes say, that “all
journalism is investigative journalism,” or that “investigative journalism is just good
old-fashioned journalism properly done.” There is nothing investigative about
watching a tennis match and writing a report on it. The report may be well-written
and entertaining, even insightful, but it is based on observation only. The same
applies to any reportage based on observation, or rewriting of materials provided to
the reporter (such as the text of a speech or PR release), which account for up to

two-thirds of the “news” in a typical newspaper. _[1]_


As for the idea that “good old-fashioned journalism” will suffice for an investigation,
this is dangerously mistaken. Of course journalists trained only in news writing can
also do investigations, but they will have to acquire new skills and new muscles in
order to succeed. There is a major difference, for example, between conducting an
investigative interview that lasts for hours and attending a brief press conference.
Likewise, an investigation becomes more difficult and costly when it is not supported
by methods for archiving information, and few news reporters are familiar with such
methods. The instructor can say here that it is of course possible to be a good
journalist – meaning a journalist who seeks to find and report the truth about
events, personalities and society - without being an investigator. Close and
passionate observers of society, editorialists or columnists, highly skilled and
courageous interviewers, and reporters who cover wars or disasters are obvious
examples of worthy colleagues.

Not every student in the class will go on to become an investigator. **What is**
**important, above all, is that students learn to appreciate investigative**
**journalism as a method, a business and a social force.** Investigative journalism
is not an infallible, divine art. But many of the criticisms, as well as much of the
praise, heaped upon investigative journalism are more or less ignorant nonsense (for
example, that investigative reporters are solitary adventurers; that investigation is
always very slow and costly; that it is about destroying reputations, rather than
reforming society; and so on). Thus the fundamental goal of this course is to bring
students into the **reality of investigative reporting, to allow them to decide**
**whether and how it will fit into their own careers.**


Most professionals would agree with the following definition of investigative
reporting:


The reporting, through one’s own initiative and work product, of matters of
importance to readers, viewers or listeners. In many cases, the subjects of the

reporting wish the matters under scrutiny to remain undisclosed.” _[2]_


Note three key elements here. First, the reporter does not merely reprise or leak an
investigation conducted by someone else. Any revelations are due to the reporter’s
own efforts. Many will probably not understand that this is not quite the same thing
as receiving the confidences of a more or less well-placed individual. Such “scoops”
may contain exclusive information, but if the story ends with the interview, they do
not amount to investigation.


The second element is that the story matters greatly to viewers; it concerns
something that may change their lives, if it has not already. This part of the definition
is very elastic, in theory as in practice. It can be argued that the lives of celebrities
matter greatly to many viewers, and so celebrities are legitimate objects of
investigation. However, the impact of celebrities is typically symbolic. Investigative
journalism is usually more concerned with subjects that have a direct, material
impact on the public or individuals.

The third element is that in some cases, someone would like to hide the truth that
the reporter exposes. Certainly, much investigative reporting is concerned with
revealing secrets. And, as Julian Assange of Wikileaks said, when information is
secret, its economic value automatically rises.


However, much of what we call secrets are simply facts to which no one paid any
attention, or that appear meaningless. By investigating these facts, we reveal their
hidden or ignored meaning. This is particularly true of investigation into archives.
The contents of archives may not be secret, but they are often effectively buried in
neglect. Yet they may contain information of great public interest. The instructor will
find two examples of such investigative work in The Global Investigative Journalism

There is indeed great pleasure in knowing something that very few people know.

**C. The importance of narrative in investigative reporting**


What the public wants from us is not a catalogue of information, but the meaning
of the information that we have catalogued. That meaning is captured in a story. In
other words, investigative reporting is not merely about finding information. It is
about telling a story based on new and important information.


Please note: We have never met a student who could not find interesting
information. We have met many who could not use it to tell an interesting
story.


We often use a very simple technique to communicate the difference between facts
and a story.


We ask a student if he or she has an address book or contact list. Of course
they do.


We then ask if they can recite the first five names on the list, along with their
emails and phone numbers. Of course they can’t.


We then ask, “If you have the list in front of you, could you tell me how you
met those people?” They usually say yes, at least some of them.


That is the key difference between facts and stories: We forget facts alone, but
we remember facts that are embedded in a story


In the Manual excerpt for this session, which we urge you to study closely, we note
the ways in which the practice of investigation diverges from the typical practice of
news reporting. These differences appear in terms of i) research, ii) source relations
and iii) outcomes.

1.Research: Going wide and deep


Conventional journalism rarely involves stories that involve more than a few days to
research and write. Investigations can last for weeks or months. They also involve far
more information than is generated by research for most news stories. Thus a higher
level of organization is required. The organizational work becomes ever more
important in direct proportion to the amount of material that the reporter collects.
You can promise students that later in the term, they will learn how to manage
information, and that it is a skill they will use for the rest of their lives.


2.Source relations: from dependence to challenge


American reporters like to joke, “Don’t trust anyone but your mother, and whatever
she says, check it.” The reality of their work is very different. Even sources who say
things that cannot be verified, and that would immediately appear as false if they
were verified, are quoted in daily reports. (This is surely one reason that news media
are regarded by a majority of the public as lacking credibility; put simply, they allow
sources to lie.) In particular, high-ranking official sources are quoted without
challenge.


_2_

This “quasi-institutionalized dependence” of reporters on official sources was
weakened, in the United States, during the Vietnam War, when it became apparent
that officials were misleading the public, and using journalists to do it. However, in
recent years most reporters have lost the habit of challenging officials. One reason is
that they do not have time to gather information to support challenges; most news
organisations are understaffed. A second reason is that reporters in many places see
themselves as guardians, not critics, of the State and its values. In the 19th century
the founder of the New York Times, Adolph Ochs, said that his newspaper would “air
and support” those responsible for governing. In 1992, the medical afffairs reporter
of Le Monde, Jean-Yves Nau, sought to counter revelations that the State had
distributed blood products contaminated by the AIDS virus, on the grounds that the
news would destroy France’s health care system.


Moreover, most reporters who claim to practice investigation are merely channels for
sources who provide them with scoops. They find nothing for themselves; their

stories are rewrites of reports compiled by government or civil society organisations. _[4]_


In contrast, authentic investigative reporters do not accept something as true simply
because someone, and in particular an official, gave them a report. Instead, they
verify the information through other sources, and challenge information that cannot
be verified. Nor do they consider someone more important because he has an official
title. The importance of a source to an investigative reporter is based on the validity
of the information that the source can provide.


The promise you can make to students here is that they will learn to respect
themselves more in encounters with public officials and other sources. By learning
how to prepare for interviews in depth, they will be better able to challenge sources
who speak down or lie to them, as well as sources who repeat mistaken information
in good faith.

3.Outcomes: Proving the need for change


The investigator’s job is to arrive at a judgment – that something should never have
happened, that it must never happen again, or conversely, that something
worthwhile has been unjustly terminated. This principle has emerged in every
country we know of with an investigative tradition. Later in this term, you will lead
your students to find examples of such work in your own country and region. This is
vital foundation work for any investigative movement, and you are helping to build
such a movement.


In the history of American journalism, the insight that a journalist can, and
sometimes must, make a judgement did not result from theory. It occurred in the
midst of the Watergate crisis, when the Washington Post made its own independent
accusations against the White House. Thus were exposed “journalism’s colliding

_6_

traditions”, as investigation moved beyond objective neutrality. This same collision
will be of crucial importance to your students, and you will find it necessary to evoke
it with them. Otherwise, they will confuse opinion with investigation. The elements
below may be useful to you in preparing for that discussion.


What is objectivity, in practice? Conventional reporting aims first at balance:
All sides of an issue are quoted and represented in a story. Of course, many _7_
reporters do not make even this effort; they quote only sources they agree
with, or allow celebrities to say whatever they like without challenge.
Nonetheless, students can agree that seeking comment and information from
all sides ought to be standard professional practice.


Conventional reporting then strives for neutrality. The reporter does not
choose sides in a debate, unless the story is clearly labeled as opinion.
Instead, the reporter leaves the viewer to decide which side of the debate has
better arguments. Many students will believe that this is always best practice.


However, the investigative reporter “refuses to accept the world as it is. The
story is aimed at penetrating or exposing a given situation, in order to reform
it, denounce it or, in certain cases, promote an example of a better way” (see
the Manual assignment for this class).


Your students will now become confused – especially if you push them: How
can a reporter, who is supposed to be objective, refuse to accept what people
say as the truth? The answer is that a reporter can and must be neutral
toward the facts: He or she can recognise that a given fact is true, whether or
not the reporter would like it to be true or not. But the reporter can then use
these objectively verified facts in order to arrive at a rigorous judgment about
a given situation. Put another way, the reporter earns the right and the
confidence to make a judgment about the meaning of the facts, through an
investigation.

**A. Tips, leftovers and observation**


The great Belgian reporter Chris de Stoop said: “You don’t choose the subject. The
subject chooses you.” He meant that every reporter is better disposed and equipped
to understand some stories than others; thus people who want those stories to be
known and investigated eventually find the reporter.


More practical is the schema developed by Nick Davies, the independent reporter
who revealed the News of the World phone-tapping scandal for The Guardian. Davies
suggests that investigative reports tend to begin with one of several categories of
information. We refer to them as i) “tips”, ii) “leftovers”, or iii) observations and
questions.

A “tip” is information that a human source provides to you. The reason for

you will be the instrument. Maybe the source is very excited about a new
development or invention that deserves wider attention. In any case, the
source provides the initial clue that becomes an investigation.

**Introduction:**


**The key tool of contemporary investigative reporting: creating hypotheses**


From the 1970s until roughly the year 2000, investigative journalism had not
yet been conceptualised as a method. In the past decade this situation
changed dramatically. It is now understood that investigative reporters
employ methods, and that their methods distinguish them from other
reporters. _1_


The most basic of those methods is the one that you are now going to teach
your students: The use of hypotheses to frame and guide investigative
research. This method emerged from within the Global Investigative
Journalism Network, soon after its founding in 2003. Instead of a theoretical
construct, it is derived from field research and practice, and embodies the
solution to a practical difficulty: _We cannot investigate efficiently unless we_
_know what we are investigating, and what we are looking for._ Otherwise we will
either pile up information until we drown in it, or wander around helplessly
with no idea of where to find the information we need. Therefore, we need an
idea of what a _story might be_, before we can investigate whether or not it really
occurred that way, or in another way.


In other words, we need a hypothesis: “a supposition or proposed explanation
made on the basis of limited evidence as a starting point for further
investigation.” _2_


In this section you will be shown, in detail, how the method works, and how
to explain it to your students. Subsequently, you will help your students to
form investigative hypotheses of their own. These hypotheses, in turn, will
frame and orient their first investigations under your guidance. They will thus
provide you with a means to gauge your students’ progress – in other words,
a project management tool.

**A. Explaining the use of hypotheses in journalism**


Consider a scientist who seeks to verify or disprove the origin of an epidemic. The
scientist first assesses the available facts – say, how the current events resemble
past epidemics the scientists has witnessed or read about, where the current
outbreak appears to have begun, where it appeared next, how it may have travelled
between those places – and creates a hypothesis that accounts for these facts. The
scientist then seeks to verify specific elements of the hypothesis, while seeking for
new facts.


The scientist accepts that the hypothesis may change as new facts emerge. The
hypothesis exists to be tested and verified, even disproved, so that a better
explanation may take its place. It is not a final truth; it is only a tool that enables him
or her to approach the truth.


In the same way, as investigative journalists we create a hypothesis – _a story that we_
_have reason to believe might be true_ - and then set out to verify that story. As we
proceed, we will discover either that our initial idea was more or less accurate, or we
will uncover evidence that points to another story.


Many journalists have difficulty understanding this approach. They would argue
that one cannot ethically say _what happened_ until one knows all the facts. That is
true, but only in a very limited sense. Of course one cannot tell the _final_ version of a
story without finding and assessing the pertinent facts. But you can nonetheless use
your _imagination_, based on what you have heard, read and experienced about the
subject, to deduce what may have occurred, and then test your deduction. Besides:
it is very, very hard to investigate a story by compiling an enormous number of facts,
and then trying to make sense of them. The usual result is drowning in a chaotic sea
of details. In order to investigate efficiently, we need a provisional idea of what we
are investigating.


Often journalists create hypotheses in a reflexive, unconscious way. Such a
journalist will go to the place where a story is happening with a preconceived
opinion, or even more than one opinion, about what he or she will find. If the
journalist is lazy or careless, he or she will then proceed to collect only the facts that
support the initial idea, and leave it at that. If the journalist is lucky, no one will be
injured as a result, beyond the indirect damage of misinforming the public about a
more or less important matter.


The outcome can be far richer if the journalist accepts that the initial idea may be
false, and is willing to replace it with one that better fits the facts. For example,
Thomas Friedman has said that when he investigated the massacres at Sabra and
Chatilla during the 1982 Israeli invasion of Lebanon, part of him wanted to prove
that the Israelis were responsible, and part of him wanted to prove that they were
not. Both of these initial ideas are hypotheses. They cannot be equally true; one _3_
must be false. But either of them is a good starting point for an investigation,
because one of them _might_ be true. Moreover, both of these hypotheses are useful,
because they focus the reporter’s attention on a key question: How did the massacre
come about?


This is of crucial importance, because otherwise the reporter may concentrate solely
on the carnage around him, and ignore its cause. It is also important because if we
are not looking for something, we are less likely to find it. In the end, Friedman
concluded that the Israelis were indeed responsible, and the result was a report that
earned him a Pulitzer Prize. _4_

From our standpoint as journalists, a hypothesis is what we think may have
happened in a given situation. By verifying the hypothesis, we find new information
that gives us a truer idea of what happened. We then change the hypothesis to take
this new information into account. In the end, the verified hypothesis and its
supporting data become the core of the story.


**B. Standards for making hypotheses**


Any useful hypothesis must meet several tests:


1. _It takes the best information we have into account._


A hypothesis is not mere speculation. For example, we do not hypothesise the
existence of extra-terrestrial beings to explain how the Pyramids were constructed,
on the grounds that human beings alone are incapable of such majestic work. (This
was actually the key thesis of a best-selling book.) Nor do we look at an event and
say, “This can only be the work of a conspiracy,” when we have no concrete
information to suggest that a conspiracy was involved.

We do not invent facts to explain what we cannot see. We create a theory that
accounts for what we already know and what we can see. Then we deduce the facts
that might explain what we do not know, and seek to verify them.


For example, suppose that after a hard rain, houses on a city hillside collapsed. We
already know that in principle, houses are built in accord with building regulations.


**We then hypothesise:**


_The regulations were not observed, either by the builders of the houses, or by_
_the inspectors who certified that the houses were properly built, or both;_


_Or, the regulations are deficient, because they do not take into account_
_exceptional conditions like torrential rains._


2. _The hypothesis must contain assertions (or terms) that can be verified._


Your students almost surely suffer from a very common misconception among
journalists – the belief that in order to obtain valuable information, it suffices to call
someone on the telephone and ask them to reveal the truth. To demonstrate the
typical absurdity of this tactic, ask one of your students to pretend to be a building
inspector, then ask: “Did you accept a bribe to certify that these awful buildings were
safe?” The student may say, to be funny, “Of course, I always do!” To which you may
reply: “Thank you, I am going to put you in jail, unless you are killed first by relatives
of the victims.”


This is the point: No one of any intelligence volunteers information to a reporter
simply because the reporter asks for it. However, many sources will confirm or deny
information that is already in the reporter’s possession. By creating a hypothesis, we
see clearly what information we must acquire before we speak with anyone. These
facts will make us more powerful when we proceed to interviews.

Students may object that a reporter can sometimes gather critical information
without prior investigation – in particular, by questioning witnesses or victims at the
scene of a catastrophe or crime. That is certainly true. But witnesses to the collapse
of buildings will probably not have knowledge of or access to information that will be
very crucial in coming days, as we move beyond reporting the disaster to
understanding its causes. For example, they are not likely to be experts in building
regulations.


Our hypothesis tells us that we should take into account building regulations as well
as weather conditions. While our competitors are reporting official reactions of the
disaster and interviewing survivors and rescue crews, we can get ahead of them by
using open sources:


How much rain fell? (We can get this information from whoever collects
weather data.)


How steep is the hillside? (We can go to the scene and look at the slope.)


Who owns the houses? Who built them? (Of course, neighbours may be able
to tell us the name of the owner or builder. However, we can also obtain
information on the owner from property records. If there are no records, then
we have already discovered evidence that the building is illegal. Which raises
the questions: Why was the construction allowed? By whom?)


What do building regulations say about ensuring that foundations are safe?
(We can obtain building rules from the library or online.)


Who enforces them? (This too is in a library or online.)

Were the houses inspected? (We will find out if inspection records are public.)


Our hypothesis must enable us to understand which facts we can verify, and how,
or it is of no use. If our hypothesis states only, “The buildings fell down because
inspectors were paid bribes,” then we are focused only on whether or not we can find
a witness to that transaction. The hypothesis is therefore useless, because it does
not lead us to sources of information that enable us to advance in the absence of
confidential information. No one with direct knowledge of how bribes were paid is
going to speak to a reporter, because those sources must be involved in the crime.


3. In its initial form, the hypothesis must be written in three sentences or less.


Students love to show off the facts they have heard or discovered for themselves, and
will present them as if they were a story. Unfortunately, facts are not stories. A story
combines facts into a sequence of events that leads to a meaning. It can, and must,
be easily summarised. Otherwise your students do not understand what they are
trying to prove.


The instructor will find it necessary to remind students of this rule more than once.
The rule is not important in itself. What matters is that students acquire the habit of
thinking in terms of stories. If they think you only want them to find facts, they will
try to please you by pouring buckets of facts over your head every time they see you.
You must make it clear to them that you do not want only facts. You want stories,
which begin with hypotheses that are supported by facts. If there is no story, the
facts do not matter.

(on a blackboard or projected Word-file). Encourage the students to start every
question with “ _How…_ ” We think it is better to focus students on _how_ things occur,
than on _why_ they occur. The “how” points to concrete, verifiable actions, while the
“why” ends with unverifiable motives.


The hypothesis: _Landlords are cheating us on rental apartments_ led to these
questions:


How does the racket work?


How is the university involved?


How can this be stopped?


**G. Now ask your students to** _**answer these questions,**_
**using their imagination. Point out that every answer is**
**hypothetical, and, indeed, part of a hypothesis.**
**Encourage them also to start every answer with a human**
**subject, followed by an active verb. We find this a useful**
**‘trick’ to provide concrete answers - and concreteness is**
**what we need, if we will ever be able to prove the story.**


In the case of the cheating landlords, students came up with this extended
hypothesis:


_Landlords demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits that are never_
_returned, for rooms that are in terrible condition. The landlords advertise and use_
_university housing services to find renters. The university makes no attempt to identify_
_cheating landlords, or to sanction them, and provides no help to cheated students._


Point out to your students that they have just created a list of concrete elements that
may play a role in their story – rents, deposits, rooms, landlords, advertisements,
etc.


4 1


**H. Now ask the group to specify which of the**
**different points in their hypothesis is easiest to verify.**


Many students will approach their topic from the opposite direction – that is, they
will focus on a secret that can only be explored through a heroic adventure, like
penetrating a criminal gang. Please inform them that every investigation becomes
more difficult as it proceeds; if they cannot do the easy work first, they will never be
able to do the hard work.


At this point in the discussion, put their hypothesis in view again. Tell them you will
read it to them slowly, and ask them to stop you every time that they hear something
that can be verified. Then, ask them how they will verify that point.

In the preceding example:


We can verify that landlords demand exorbitant rents and deposits by
comparing student rents with market prices for other renters.


If landlords advertise to students, we can locate these advertsements in media
(such as magazines or billboards) that are targeted at students.


We can also locate students who have complained about landlords to
university housing services, with no result.


Of these three verifications, the easiest will be to find rental prices and landlord
advertisements, because they are open sources. The hardest task will be to locate
students who complained, and to confirm that the university does nothing about the
complaints.

**I. Concluding remarks**


Once your students have created a viable investigative hypothesis – a story line that
contains elements that can be verified, including from open sources – you may want
to make the following points:


1. Making a hypothesis seems time consuming. But with practice, it can be done in
minutes. The time lost when you have no hypothesis is measured in days, weeks and
months. The larger point is that news reporters are trained to do everything fast, and
that is their default mode. Investigators must be less reactive, and more pro-active;
they must take control of the story from the start.


2. At some point in the session, a student will ask: “I would like to keep an open
mind. Won’t a hypothesis increase the chance that I miss something important, by
focusing me on one set of facts?”


In fact, a hypothesis can blind a reporter. This usually occurs in two ways:


A reporter turns up evidence that contradicts the hypothesis, but refuses to
consider that evidence;


The reporter is using the hypothesis in an unconscious or reflexive way, in
order to make the work faster and easier. Typically, the reporter has a
preconceived idea, and a minimum of evidence to support it, and is happy
with that state of affairs.


You can alert your students to these dangers, and offer a solution: An honest
reporter constantly tests his or her hypothesis against the facts that emerge in the
investigation.

In the following sessions, we will show students how to create a timeline and a
source map. These two tools will then become the basis for a “masterfile” – a
database that the student can use as a working asset throughout his or her career.

**Introduction:**


**The key tool of contemporary investigative reporting: creating hypotheses**


After the hypothesis, the timeline is the most important tool used by
investigators, and probably the most widely used. It helps us to make sense of
complex matters, by revealing causality and consequences of related events.
It shows us what is missing from our story—that is, what might have
happened before and after events that we have confirmed – and keeps the
information we have gathered in a clear, strong order. A timeline also creates
a foundation for the simplest and strongest narrative structures, as we will
see later. In fact, the timeline enables us to begin creating our narrative while
we are still conducting research.


For all these reasons, you must insist that your students learn how to create,
enrich and maintain a timeline. In successive sessions, you will show them:


1. How to identify specific events related to the hypothesis;


2. How to deduce and verify other events that must have occurred;


3. How to compile these events, and their supporting documentation, into a
database.


You can tell your students:


_In order to verify our hypothesis, we need to understand:_
_What specific events may have taken place;_
_What evidence, or documentation, those events created;_
_How we may obtain that evidence._


You may also tell them: _To do this, we need to use our imaginations_ . In the rest
of the session we will encourage them to do just that. We recommend that you
read _The Hidden Scenario_ for further insight into the techniques
recommended in this chapter; they had not been developed when _Story-Based_
_Inquiry_ was published.


4 5


**A. Defining a sequence of events**


Start by asking: _Who can explain what a “timeline” is?_


Answer: A timeline (or chronology) is a list of events that follows the order in which
they occurred. In a timeline, one event follows another.


You can tell your students: _Every hypothesis contains a timeline, because it refers to_
_a sequence of events._


You may now take one of the hypotheses that students created in previous sessions
and write it on top of a blackboard or Word-file that you project.


Ask your students to take their hypothesis apart; to identify the seperate events that
make up their hypothesis, and put these events in a list. They can easily identify the
separate events by counting the verbs in the hypothesis: every verb is the expression
of a different action.


Please be sure, before you do it in front of them, that you have identified the number
of verbs in the hypothesis, and the events to which they refer.


To make the exercise clear, we will show you how it works with the hypothesis that
students created. We urge you very strongly to create your own examples from
local events. You will know better than we do what stories are of interest to your
students. Here is our example from the previous chapter:


_Landlords demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits that are never_
_returned, for rooms that are in terrible condition. The landlords advertise and_
_use university housing services to find renters. The university makes no attempt_
_to identify cheating landlords, or to sanction them, and provides no help to_
_cheated students._


Notice that this hypothesis refers to a number of events. The events show up in the
hypothesis every time that a verb appears, because verbs signify action:


1. Landlords demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits.


2. Landlords decline to return this money to students.


3. Landlords advertise for renters.


4. Landlords use university housing services to find renters.


5. University housing services help the landlords find renters.


6. University housing services tolerate cheating landlords.


7. University housing services deny help to cheated students.


4 6


Now let us remake this list according to the chronological order in which the events
must have occurred. First, landlords must find renters:


1. Landlords advertise for renters.


2. Landlords use university housing services to find renters.


3. University housing services help the landlords find renters.


Now the landlords take advantage of the renters:


4. Landlords demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits.


5. Landlords never return this money to students.


Finally, the university allows them to get away with it:


6. University housing services tolerate cheating landlords.


7. University housing services deny help to cheated students.


Now ask your students: _What actions must occur before and after these events?_ If we
focus on the consequences of our events, we may imagine that:


8. Landlords continue to dupe more students.


9. Students sue landlords with the help of lawyers.


Already, in our hypothetical sequence of events, each event enables the next.


Landlords advertise and use university services in order to find students
whom they can cheat.


By declining to sanction cheaters, the university enables the scheme to
prosper at the expense of students.


Finally, some students strike back through lawsuits.


The class will be much more lively if you perform this exercise using hypotheses
provided by students. (You will require them to apply this procedure to their own
hypotheses in any case.) Whatever hypothesis you use, follow the procedure
described above:


Set out the list of events contained in the hypothesis.


Put those events in the probable chronological order in which they occurred.


Imagine other plausible events that must have occurred, either to enable
known events, or as a consequence of those events.


4 7


Some of the events that they propose will be of less interest than others. Do not be
concerned about that for the moment. Instead, concentrate on getting your students
to elaborate a rich timeline between the moment when the story begins, and the
moment that it generates newsworthy consequences. When the imagination runs
dry, or the timeline contains far-off or implausible events, it is time to stop. Ideally a
timeline consists of between 5 and 10 important events.


**B. Formulate the events as concretely as possible**


Point out to the students that the next step will be to verify the events in their
timeline. They must therefore formulate every event concretely, so they know exactly
what they are trying to verify.


It helps to begin every sentence with a human subject. It is easier to
investigate human beings than abstract problems or systems. Moreover,
at some point, students will need human sources to verify the events. In
short, students should ask not “What do I want to investigate?” but
“Who do I want to investigate?”


Make sure that the character in the event is engaged in an activity. Only
activity leaves traces that we can follow up to prove that something
happened. “Landlords cheat” is less easy to prove than “Landlords
demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits.” “Demanding” is a
real action (the landlord is talking to the student), while “cheating” is an
abstract summary, and a subjective opinion at that.


Finally, urge your students to stay clear of negatives and predictions.
For instance, the statement above, “Landlords never return the money
to students” states what does not happen. Things that don’t happen are
hard to prove, so let’s change the sentence to state what does happen
instead: “Landlords refuse to return the money to the students.”
Likewise it is impossible to prove something that has not happened yet

      - for example, “Landlords who cheat form a syndicate to control the
market.” Perhaps they will, but at the moment, this is speculation, not
hypothesis.


**C. Identify the maximum and minimum story**


As a general case, your initial hypothesis, and the timeline that you derive from it,
will contain very few verified facts. (In the example used for The Hidden Scenario,
concerning toxic chemicals that are used to manufacture water pipes, only one fact
was known at the outset: scientists had discovered that plastic water pipes leak
chemicals into drinking water.) In the example above, we know only two facts, which
we have learned through tips from students:


Landlords demand exorbitant rents and large security deposits.


Landlords never return this money to students.


4 8


4 6


Every other “fact” in our timeline is hypothetical, and remains to be verified. This will
surely recur when you work with students’ hypotheses, and it will worry some of
them. They may object: _How do we know that this or that event occurred?_ If they do
not ask, you may pose the question yourself.


Your answer: _Some of these events are only hypotheses, which we will verify. They are_
_what we think must have happened. If they did not, we will discover what really_
_happened when we attempt to verify them._


The students may then ask: _What if our hypotheses are not true? Do we still have a_
_story?_


Whether or not they ask, we need to address this question, too. The answer is:


_The hypothesis should be framed to reveal the most important story we can find here,_
_without merely speculating. We need to know what the most important story may be,_
_so that we can decide whether or not it is worth exploring. If the most important story_
_we can hypothesize from the available facts is neither very interesting nor very easy,_
_we abandon it._


In the case above, students might suggest that university officials are being bribed
by landlords. You may add this event to your timeline; however, you may also note
that it is not necessary to go that far in order to have an interesting story. In fact, you
may point out that one of the common, and gravest, errors made by investigative
reporters is to make accusations that their evidence cannot fully support, in order to
make the story appear more dramatic. _1_


We faced this exact situation in one of our classes: A student hypothesized that
university housing officials were taking bribes from students desperate to find an
apartment. She became expert in how the housing office functioned, but found no
bribes – only a multitude of example of how students manipulated a broken system.
She thought that she had failed, because she could not prove that housing officials
were criminals. Instead, she had proven that their office was completely
dysfunctional. Once she realised that this was a valuable story, she wrote it and
published it.


**E. Take-home assignment: Creating a timeline**


Before the next class session, your students should repeat the above procedures for
every one of their hypotheses, creating a timeline. Make clear to them that this work
must be done as a word processing file, and not merely freehand. Explain to them
that before long, this preliminary work will expand into a database.

**A. Introductory lecture: How document**
**searches define investigative reporting**


Following the resignation of Richard M. Nixon from the US Presidency in 1975,

investigative reporters bathed in “exaltation.” _[1]_ They were folk heroes and models for

other journalists, in the US as well as other countries. _[2]_ Not least, the reaction to
Watergate made clear that investigation is hardly “good old-fashioned journalism,
properly done”. Other journalists at the time regarded investigators as “inquisitors”
(an allusion to medieval Spanish torturers of the Catholic Church) whose success in

obtaining confidential documents was due entirely to “inside” human sources. _[3]_


This was flattering, and it was also nonsense. No one is obliged to talk to a journalist
under threat of torture, and journalists do not need inside sources to obtain a great
many documents. In this session we are going to show students that they can obtain
significant information before they talk to anyone at all.


1. The institutionalization of document-based reporting


Your students need to understand the true relevance of the post-Watergate
investigative journalism movement to their own skills and careers: Journalists
realized that instead of simply recording what official sources told them, they could
conceive and document a story on their own. They could thus be critical in a more
powerful and positive way than by stating an opinion. Instead, they could influence
society through obtaining and publishing facts that moved individuals and
institutions to action. (Stubborn students may again observe that lies may have the
same effect. This is true, but if they wish to spend their lives lying, they are in the
wrong place.) This insight had two important methodological implications:


The focus of their work shifted from gaining access to powerful figures to
confronting powerful figures with facts.


Reporters were thereby forced to become expert at finding and
documenting facts before, not after, they asked questions.

Donald L. Bartlett and James B. Steele were reporters at the Philadelphia Inquirer

racially-biased justice in Philadelphia (1972). Their report involved reviewing literally
thousands of pages of court judgments and transcripts, as well as interviewing
victims and officials of the system. They urged other journalists to develop “a
document state of mind”— to always think of what documents might be available to
support their stories — and they were right.


The creation of Investigative Reporters and Editors (IRE) Inc. in 1975 provided
journalists with a platform to diffuse such best practices. IRE’s awards, conferences,
publications, and training seminars set a standard for the practice of investigative
reporting and publicised the necessary methods. In the view of investigative
journalism historian James L. Aucoin, IRE was a key reason that investigation

changed from a specialty to a broadly-based “social practice” in the US. _[4]_
Document-based research was the core of that social practice.


Two signs of the progression of this movement were that environmental _[5]_ and
business news, previously regarded as graveyards of the profession, became
dynamic sectors for ambitious reporters. In 1979, a survey of US business editors at
newspapers selling more than 50,000 copies daily showed that two-thirds had

published investigations in the preceding year. _[6]_ At some US journals business

investigations doubled during the 1970s. _[7]_ Thus investigative reporting techniques
that originated in political exposés spread constantly into new sectors, until they

were applied to every corner of public life, from business to zoos. _[8]_

**B. A contemporary example from Syria**


Your students must be able to see that document-based investigative reporting
entails a competitive advantage, because most reporters are incapable or unwilling
of doing this work. Usually, reporters rely on documents provided to them by
someone else; they do not seek to obtain documents on their own. A reporter capable
of these practices will always find rich sources.


At a minimum, the instructor can instill respect for the determination and creativity
required to discover documentary evidence of true stories. At most, the instructor
can provide young professionals with a method that will serve them for the rest of
their lives.


Lina Joudy’s story, “Gharghesia archeological site disappears under village in East
Syria”, demonstrates documentary techniques in a very hard place for a reporter to
work. Her story details the destruction of a superb archaeological site in Syria, by
villagers who wished to use its stones in their own houses. The story begins with a
reference to a published book. Other books are cited as she proceeds. She also refers
to official documents, including a law, a 1977 government report, and a recent

decree. All of these documents could be obtained in a library. _[13]_ Your students should
be advised that Internet is not a substitute for the library.


The instructor may ask students to perform the following analysis of this story before
coming to class:


On one side of a sheet of paper, list the reporter’s documentary sources.


On the other side, list the human sources.


Now, draw a line connecting human sources to the documentary sources with
which they are probably familiar, or to which their activities are related. (Not
all human sources will be connected to documents.)


This exercise should demonstrate that the reporter’s documents clearly inform
and guide her use of human sources. The documents are connected to institutions
where her sources work. By knowing their contents, the reporter can engage in
detailed and precise discussions with experts and officials. Fort example, from the
start of her investigation, Lina Joudy can say to villagers: “There was a castle here.
Scholars wrote about it in detail. It is gone now. How did this happen?”


Ordinary reporters ask their sources, “What happened?” Investigative reporters can
also ask their sources: “Isn’t this what happened?” The second question is much
more powerful than the first. It shows the source that the reporter cannot easily be
lied to – at least, not as easily as most reporters can be lied to. It also makes the
source’s task, of answering the question, much easier. Sources do not like to explain
everything to reporters. Nor do they like to volunteer information that a reporter
clearly does not possess, and may not be capable of appreciating. However, sources
often confirm information that they would not volunteer. And they often enjoy
speaking with someone who has taken the trouble to learn something before asking
questions.

**Introduction: What are open sources?**


It is a misunderstanding that journalism is about finding facts; instead, it is about
finding reliable _sources_ . Once you find the right documentary and human sources,
the facts will emerge from them. However, hunting for sources consumes the largest
share of a typical investigation. It is fair to say that 90% of journalists use only 10%
of the available sources, precisely because finding new sources takes time.


So careful planning is necessary. Generally, a plan should begin with open sources,
because they are the easiest to consult.


“Open sources” are people and documents that are freely and easily accessible. They
include:


People who seek public attention, and therefore willingly talk to
journalists. They may include: victims, lawyers, counselors, politicians,
competitors, campaigners, unionists, scientists, researchers, or
analysts (such as financial analysts).


Documents and data that are freely available to the public.


B. Putting documents first


If you ask your students how they can prove their hypotheses, nearly all of them will
reply: _Find someone who knows the answers to our questions._


For investigators, it is usually far more efficient and fruitful to seek information from
public documents first. The information they contain can confirm whether or not a
story is plausible, before much more time is invested in it.


You can expect that students will ask: “Why would we take the effort to obtain
documents? Why not stick to interviews? People are the best sources. Besides, the
information in public documents is already known!”


Possible answers include:


The fact that something is open to inspection does not mean that anyone
inspects it. Often, publicly available documents contain answers to
important questions, but no one has bothered to study the documents.
Story-Based Inquiry contains several examples of important stories that
were based on documents that no journalists bothered to consult, such
as statistics on premature birth, voting lists, scientific studies, etc.

Open sources, in particular documentary sources, can be consulted
without raising an alarm. Calling an official for an interview will attract
attention, and doors may close.


6 3


Documents often contain facts that interview subjects will not
spontaneously reveal.

Documents often save you the trouble of interviewing. Experts and other
sources have often expressed their experiences and opinions long before
you, as a journalist, think to interview them.


Even if someone is willing to speak with you, he or she will provide better
information if you are visibly capable of understanding it. The source
will observe whether or not you understand the language and concerns
of the people involved in your story. You must therefore do at least some
documentary research in order to prepare for interviews.


C. How to search for open sources


In order to search efficiently for documents and friendly human sources, we can use
the following method:


First we identify the people who are concerned by our story, and the
open sources that may be related to them.


Then we plan a route among these sources concerned by our story, and
the open sources that may be related to them. if we know whom we
expect or hope to meet, we have a better chance of being prepared when
an opportunity appears. If we have a strategy to reach them, our
chances of making contact increase.


1. Using a timeline to identify open sources

Women fall into poverty.


They commit crimes in order to survive.


They are arrested by police.


The court sentences them to prison.


They serve their sentences.


When they come out of prison, they are abandoned by their families, and
return to crime.


We can imagine these other, following steps:


Scholars study the problem.


NGOs try to help them, but lack the means.


The government and parliament try to solve the problem.

School or employment records that help to explain how a woman

C. Mapping a source village


The timeline shows us actors who are visibly involved in the story. But there may be
others who are affected by the story, or who are interested by the problems that it
raises. In the case above, they might include parents and friends of the unfortunate
women, confidants to whom they reveal their feelings, government officials who
regret the absence of a policy to help them, and others.


We can imagine whom these other characters may be by creating a “source village”.
Along with actors in the story, the “village” contains various witnesses to events or
their consequences. An example of a source village map, which indicates the
different actors involved in an unfolding tragedy centered on handicapped children,
can be found in Chapter 4 of Story-Based Inquiry. You will see that this is a very
simple technique.


Yet it is also powerful. Besides helping us to think of new sources, a source village
can help us to visualize the sources we already know, and their relations to each
other.


This is particularly useful when investigating business or criminal networks.
Complex chains of crossed ownership or alliances can be very difficult to define. By
situating the actors in space, we can have a far better idea of what we are dealing
with. It is no coincidence that the Organised Crime and Corruption Reporting Project
(OCCRP), a world leader in the field, has developed free software to create such

maps, which they call “Visual Investigation Scenarios.” _1_ (Scholars of network studies
have their own, expensive software to map complex interactions involving hundreds
of actors.)

### **Advanced** **Internet** **Search**

**Introduction**


Compared to most other investigative resources, the Internet appears relatively new
and still much in development. This makes it harder to give instruction on how to
use it. Websites that proved practical for researchers yesterday may have
disappeared today. Tricks that seem valuable today, may become obsolete tomorrow.

Nevertheless, some useful websites and tricks have proven sustainable. In this
chapter we suggest several that may be taught to students. In no way do we claim to
be complete or final. Instead, the goal is to demonstrate to students that their
Internet search can become far more efficient and powerful than it previously was.


The core strategy here is to put sources before information. Students can be
trained, instead of looking for an answer on the Internet, to look for reliable sources,
and then to look for the answer in these sources. The Internet is not a source. It is a
container of sources, like a library. As in a library, one first must decide which
source to pick before one starts reading.


Students often think that Google has all the answers, without realising that Google
shows answers from all kinds of sources, from pretty reliable to plain rubbish. To the
extent they decide what they want to see, they can eliminate a great deal of wasted
time and useless material. Students should be advised that “search costs” - the time
and effort it takes to find the solution to a given problem - are a main reason that
investigation is regarded by editor and publishers as slow and costly. By improving
their efficiency, they make investigative reporting a viable enterprise.


The most frequent problem that students encounter is that their search requests are
not precise enough. They should understand that Google’s default search mode is
Boolean - that is, Google will automatically search for documents containing all of
the terms in a request. To narrow the number of results, the easiest solution is thus
to repeat the request with additional terms. Many students are also unaware that by
putting terms between quotation marks, they direct Google to search for that exact
phrase. The difference is easily demonstrated: If students search for their full names
without quotation marks, everyone who shares either their forename and family
name and is indexed by Google will appear in the results.


Below, we have described common situations that journalists encounter in using the
Internet for research. Any or all of these situations may serve as the basis for
classroom exercises in which students use or add to the tools we provide below. We
have on occasion offered extra credit to students who discover useful new techniques
or websites. In any case, students should understand that their ability to use such
tools can make a great difference in their careers.


7 1


**I. We know exactly who can answer**
**a particular question**


A. Using an organisational website


Go to a website owned by the source or subject and see if you can find the answer
there. If you, for instance, want to know how much profit the Coca-Cola Company
makes from its soft drinks, you turn directly to their official website
(www.coca-colacompany.com) rather than search through thousands of results in
Google.


If a protagonist or antagonist in a story is a company, a NGO, an organisation or a
public figure, then he or she probably owns a website that publishes “official”
information.. Not all of the information will be reliable, of course, but there is
probably also stuff that is useful, like:


Organisational purpose (“mission statement”)


Financial reports


Organigram or structure of the organisation


Organisational history


Project or product information


News and press releases


Speeches and statements


Calendar of upcoming events


Links or alliances with other organisations

Names of possible sources with contact information


**II. We know what type of source can**
**answer a question**


A. Refining the search with “operators”


In formulating a search request for Google, an “operator” means a formula that
directs the search engine to focus only on particular locations and materials.
Operators are highly useful, but students will need to be shown how to use them; few
will make the effort for themselves. The examples below can serve as the basis for
classroom exercises.


7 2


1. The Minus sign (-)


The minus sign tells Google to exclude words from the search. Suppose we are
searching for health effects of Coca-Cola. To be fair to the firm, we start by searching
for: “Coca-Cola” is healthy. This brings us 1.35 million results, many sponsored by
the firm. Now let’s try it using a minus sign on “healthy”, and adding the term
“unhealthy”. This search is designed to cut out the firm’s promotion, and to highlight
critical opinions: _“Coca-Cola” is -healthy unhealthy._ That brings us 768,00 results,
from which the company’s promotion is largely absent.


2. The “site” operator


Google often finds more results in an official website than the embedded search bar
on that website. This is because of restrictions that webmasters often program into
their internal search engines. An external engine like Google, on the other hand, has
no such restrictions.


Thus, instead of using the search bar that is embedded on a website like
Coca-Cola’s, we can ask Google to look inside any website for key words. The syntax

A. Overview


Investigative journalism has evolved in cycles, and there are two primary drivers of
these movements. Either is sufficient to sustain the genre. When both appear
simultaneously, there is explosive development of investigative content, formats and
audiences.

2.


One driver resides in the development and exploitation of new reporting
methods. Sometimes these methods follow the appearance of new sources of
information that can be readily accessed. They may also result from the
reporter’s willingness to adopt a different role or approach to the work.


A second key force resides in the willingness of a significant public to hear what
investigators say, and to support their calls for change. Such publics are not an
unchanging, faceless mass. They may include specific classes, sectors or
communities within society, or particular groups within ruling elites (as is often
the case in China). The public’s attention is never guaranteed, and it is never
permanent. Journalists and their allies must continually seek to attract the
interest and backing – financial, social and ethical – of influential audiences. A
key factor here is the ability of journalists to find novel ways of telling stories.
Another is their ability to form alliances with other social actors.

entirely) on the United States.  The instructor should be clear with students that the _1_

B. “Colliding traditions”: Partisanship, Objectivity, and Crusading


The origins of the English-language newspaper were commercial, in the form of
letters offering confidential information to business interests, and partisan, in the
form of propaganda sheets for Puritan revolutionaries. Business and political
factions remained key publics for the news media.


a. Journalism as a moral force


In the early 19th century, some US and English newspapers sought to enlarge their
publics beyond partisan groups by focusing on crime and scandals. They claimed
that they presented an “objective” image of society, while protecting its interests from
wrongdoers. Later in the century “crusades” against corruption, or to repair the _4_
effects of natural disasters, or to promote causes like the Spanish-American War,
became prominent features of the Anglo-American press. We understand that the
term “crusades” has unpleasant meaning for Arab students; yet that was the term
employed at the time, and it was indeed deeply tinged with Christian moralism.
Journalists became guardians of public morality, exposing vice to promote virtue.

while claiming to use it in the service of reform. Both also innovated in terms of
journalistic technique. However, their innovations included deceptive methods, in

reporting in more detail later in the term. At this point, you may note that a major

b. The emergence of document-based advocacy reporting


At the end of the 19th century a new trend became visible simultaneously in the US

The trend emerged as reporters sought methods that would offer them an advantage

b. Social turmoil and investigative style


In the US and Europe, the 1960s were a time of social upheaval, protest and
mobilization – not unlike what happened in the Arab Spring. Such turmoil always
represents an opportunity for innovation in the media. Just as Tunisian
revolutionaries turned Facebook from a social media into a news media in 2010-11,
muckrakers in the 1960s created new media or remade existing media into vehicles
for their work. There is no divine law which specifies that investigators can only use
one form of media to distribute their stories.


In the US, the key innovations of the 1960s involved content, style and distribution.
One channel that combined many such innovations was the “alternative” press –
mainly free weekly newspapers, typically published in cities with large student
populations, and supported by advertisers whose customers were those same

political and social issues that mainstream media ignored or misinterpreted, and
their success was lasting. Muckraking articles were among their key features. A
famous example is The Great Speckled Bird, an “underground” newspaper from
Atlanta, Georgie, USA, whose archives can be consulted free online.  The magazine _22_
Rolling Stone may be considered a variant of this formula. In the 1990s, free _23_
English-language weeklies became vital media in the former Soviet republics and
client states of Eastern Europe.  In other words, the “alternative weekly”, distributed _24_
free and supported by advertising revenue, has proven to be one of the most
successful, diverse and sustainable (though rarely wealthy) formats in the history of
investigative news media.

c. The revival of consumer watchdog reporting


It is a great error to believe that investigative reporting is the sole property and
heritage of the news media. Recall that _The Jungle_ (1906), a novel, was among the
most influential works of the Muckraker era. Likewise, one of the most powerful
investigations of the years before Watergate was written by an independent activist,
and published as a book. In 1965, Ralph Nader showed in _Unsafe at_ _Any Speed_ that
US car manufacturers sold vehicles that they knew contained deadly design flaws.
Nader linked those flaws to thousands of deaths, and warned: “The roots of the
unsafe vehicle problem are so entrenched that the situation can be improved only by
the forging of new instruments of citizen action.” The importance of car _28_
manufacturers as advertisers was surely one reason that until Nader appeared, news
media had failed to expose the story.


Nader became a popular speaker at universities, a novel revenue source for
investigative reporting. He also founded an association of consumers to fight for
reforms, supported by public donations. Eventually, he became a candidate for US
President, and though he lost, he also attracted millions of voters. His success
greatly influenced the news industry, where investigative reporting swiftly became a
feature of consumer columns.


Currently NGOs or civil society organisations aimed at consumer protection, as well
as commercial enterprises, publish magazines or websites focused on protecting
consumers from fraud or dangerous products. In nearly any market, there is room
for such a publication, or even several of them. The instructor may refer to examples
of such media, in particular:


_Consumer reports_ (http://www.consumerreports.org/cro/index.htm). This

medications to counterbalance the aggressive promotional tactics of
pharmaceutical companies. Prescrire has exposed genuine scandals involving
unsafe medications.

_Annex to Chapter 8:_
### **The Emergence** **of Arab** **Investigative** **Reporting**


By Dr. Saba Bebawi


In the following essay, Saba Bebawi undertakes the first comprehensive
overview of the historical development of Investigative Journalism in
the Arab region. The instructor can use this unique material
in at least two ways:


First, this authoritative essay answers the question, “What about Arab
investigative journalism?” It provides a balanced and documented
assessment of the achievements of Arab journalists as they sought to
extend the boundaries of their craft under often difficult conditions.


Second, the text provides a foundation for further research, by the
instructor as well as the students. In particular, the instructor can ask
students to prepare a presentation on one of the journalists mentioned
in Bebawi’s text, following the guidelines in Chapter 16, “Finding
models: Class discussion of reading assignments and guest lecturers.”


When going through the historic development of Arab journalism, two observations
emerge:


The first observation is that the tradition of journalism that historically
evolved in the region, developed as a result of individual journalists
playing an active role in transforming the field. Today, investigative
journalists once again find themselves needing to transform the field
and becoming independent drivers of change.


The second point that emerges is that although Arab journalism evolved
based on Western traditions of journalism by borrowing many of their
values, it eventually transformed to become a form of journalism that
was specific to the region formed by political, social, economic and
cultural influences.


Today, Arab investigative journalism is being shaped in a similar way in the region:
by becoming a form of investigative reporting that is adopting Western teachings and
techniques of investigative reporting, whilst accommodating cultural practices
specific to the Arab culture of journalism. With these two observations in mind, I will
provide an historical account of how Arab journalism evolved in general, and
investigative journalism specifically, in the region.


9 2


In _The Press in the Arab Middle East: A History_, Ami Ayalon provides a
comprehensive and detailed account of the rise of journalism in the Arab world,
which I will rely on here as a background to understanding the development of
investigative journalism in the region. Generally speaking, journalism in Arab states
operated in an environment of state governance and hegemony. Ayalon explains:


The ruler was expected to govern effectively, and it was desirable that he
be just; the ruled were required to obey him, regardless of the quality of
his justice. The basic principle underlying this concept was that
effective government was essential for the stability and proper
functioning of the community, and obedience was vital in assuring such
effectiveness (Ayalon, 1995: 109).


The media were therefore regarded as playing a focal role in assuring the
effectiveness of people’s obedience. Arab journalism, therefore, has broadly acted as
the mouthpiece of the state, thus avoiding political, religious and cultural
questioning. The earliest legislation to affect Arab press came under the Ottoman
Law of Printing and Publications in January 1857. This law stipulated the need to
gain permission from the Council of Education and the Ministry of Police in order to
establish a press. Yet what was particularly vital to the notion of free press in this
case, was that this law obliged anybody who had a press licence, to submit all texts
that were intended for publication for council approval. A year later a penal code
imposed punishments, which included closures and fines, to those who did not
submit material for prepublication approval. (Ayalaon, 1995: 111). As Ayalon states,
‘[l]icensing, prepublication censorship, and prescribed punishments—a set of
preventative measures—would become the cornerstone of government control of
printed self-expression by individual subjects’ (Ayalon, 1995: 112). This,
unavoidably, led to journalists’ self-censorship of what to report on and what to
avoid in their publications. They were fully aware of political, religious and social red
lines they were not allowed to cross. Having said this, ‘[t]he rules for what was
permissible or prohibited were seldom consistent, and interpretation depended
largely on the understanding, or ignorance, of the censors. This resulted in endless
ridiculous situations’ (Ayalon, 1995: 114). These situations included, for example,
the monitoring of words that could have double meanings.


Hence throughout the nineteenth and twentieth century, the region’s leaders
‘were irritated not only by the conduct of the press but by its very existence as well.
Freedom of expression, a novel concept and a newly coined phrase in Arabic, was
part of an alien system’ (Ayalon, 1995: 117). Nonetheless, there were Arab free
thinkers and intellectuals, such as Rifa’a Rafi’ Al-Tahtawi, who rose to promote
enlightenment thought in Egyptian society in the 1830s and beyond. There were
those who ‘were drawn to the press under the inspiration of a foreign example [and]
pursued journalism successfully in the 1860s and 1870s’ (Ayalon, 1995: 216). They
‘showed that it was possible to publish opinions on public affairs and have an impact
without antagonizing the government beyond an acceptable point, produce a
saleable commodity, and even make a living out of it—in other words, to turn writing
on current affairs into a career’ (Ayalon, 1995: 216).


9 3


The development of the profession itself, therefore, began with the introduction
of Western concepts and teachings of journalism into Arab journalism. Such
concepts included notions of objectivity and fairness. Yet within such a tightly
monitored Arab media environment such concepts were not always easy to apply.
This meant that Arab journalism was forced to develop in a different way to that of
Western journalism. Accordingly, Arab ‘journalism developed as a variation distinct
from the European prototype in the social and political roles it filled’ (Ayalon, 1995:
215). Prominent journalists who rose to become influential included Mustafa Kamil,
Ahmad Lutfi Al-Sayyid, Muhammad Al-Tabi’i, Amin Al-Rafi’i, Mohammad Hassanain
Heikal, Abbas Mahmuod Al-Akkad, Sa’d and Fathi Zaghlul, Abdul Qadir Hamza, and
Mahmuod Azmi from Egypt; Jibran and Ghassan Tuwayni, and Muhi Al-Din
Al-Nusuli from Lebanon; the Rayyis cousins, Muhammad Kurd Ali, and Yusuf Al-Isa,
and Nassuh Babil from Syria; Tawfiq Al-Sam’ani and Rafa’il Batti from Iraq; and Isa
Al-Isa from Palestine (Ayalon, 1995: 217). The number of newspapers established by
the end of 1929 rose to three thousand Arabic periodicals (Ayalon, 1995: 217). At the
time, there were minimum requirements for setting up a non-governmental private
newspaper, whereby licence conditions would include a minimum age of 20-25
years, no criminal record, and financial guarantee (Ayalon, 1995: 217). In some
cases, ‘a writer would establish a paper, and slowly, as other like-minded people were
attracted to it, they formed a party that became active on the political scene’ (Rugh,
2004: 148), especially during French and British rule. Later in the century, however,
there were numerous restrictions which meant that only the state had access to the
media. Currently, the media space is opening up within the Arab world with the
advent of the internet, however independent news websites are continuously being
closed down as witnessed during and post the recent Arab Spring uprisings.


One interesting aspect to Arab journalism was that the culture of journalism at
the outset was focused on the ‘desire to satisfy literary cravings’, and writers
regarded newspapers as an outlet to produce literary work (Ayalon, 1995: 219). The
explains why Arab journalism today is literary in its nature, and why producing
investigative journalism which relies on facts, simple language, and is straight to the
point, is one of the challenges that trainers are facing with investigative journalists
today. The literary expressions in Arab journalism were fuelled further with political
motivation, especially in the face of French and British imperialism of Arab states
that came about as a result of the Sykes-Picot Agreement to partition the Arab world
in 1918 after the defeat of the Ottoman empire. However, ‘the British were more
tolerant of partisan papers than of organized parties’ (Rugh, 2004: 148), and
therefore in some regions of the Arab world journalism played a strong political role
against foreign imperialism. For example, before 1945 in Egypt, the Wafd party
utilised its newspaper as a political tool against the British military, as it was not
possible to challenge the occupation through military means (Rugh, 2004: 149).
Rugh notes that ‘[e]xcept for the interlude of wartime censorship, the press was able
to criticize policies, expose corruption in government, and reflect diverse
philosophies and interests’ (Rugh, 2004: 149). Journalists, therefore, became
idealists in their work, where there was a keen ‘desire to serve the community, the
state, and the homeland’ (Ayalon, 1995: 219). Ayalon explains this further:


Virtually every opening statement of each new journal (and, for that
matter, every journalist’s memoirs) contained the cliche: a desire to
serve the public good. Yet in spite of the commonplace usage of such
formulas, there is no reason to doubt their sincerity (Ayalon, 1995:
219).


Accordingly, as Ayalon describes, journalists during this time became active agents
of cultural and social change (Ayalon, 1995: 216). After the British and French rule
ended in the second half of the twentieth century, however, censorship was once
again imposed on Arab media.


9 4


Investigative reporting in the Arab world is not new. In the past there have been
individual investigations that were carried out by a few reporters, and although most
often they were not labelled as ‘investigative’, they were seen as attempts in
liberalising the press and revolutionising traditional methods of reporting. Such
examples include the work of the Amin Brothers in the 1930s and 1940s in Egypt.
Mustafa Amin and Ali Amin, who were twin brothers, were ‘advocates of
Western-style democracy’, Western liberalism, free enterprise, and free press, that
would help liberalise Egypt (Jehl, 1997). Prior to the nationalising of Egypt’s press in
1960 by President Gamal Abdul Nasser, the Amin brothers were publishers of five
publications, which included Al Akhbar and the weekly Akhbar Al Youm. Their
journalism critiqued those in power, which led to frequent imprisonment. Mustafa
Amin was imprisoned in 1939 for criticising King Farouk, and in the early 1950s
during Nasser’s rule (Jehl, 1997).


Ihsan Abdul Quddoos was also prominent in writing investigative stories for the
publication Rose Al Yusif, which was founded by his mother Fatima al Youssef. He
syndicated columns for government newspapers and magazines. His column,
entitled At a Café on Politics Street, addressed current issues circulating in cafes in
Egypt at the time, thus extending discourses from the physical public sphere to that
of the mediated public sphere. In the 1960s and 1970s, he was also editor of Akhbar
Al Yawm and Al Ahram newspapers (New York Times, 16 January 1990).


Mohammad Hassanain Heikal was another Egyptian journalist who was
influential in his investigative reporting. He was editor-in-chief of the Cairo-based
newspaper Al-Ahram, was keen on developing a tradition and practice of
investigative reporting at the paper, and therefore would train graduates in
investigative journalism and employ them. Yet, unlike the Amin brothers, Heikal had
strong ties with President Gamal Abdel Nasser and therefore gained access to
information. Accordingly, he had consent from authorities to publish his work, in
addition to his investigations. However, during the rule of Anwar Sadat following
Nasser, Heikal continued to report yet ‘when he spoke out too obviously in opposition
to government policy he simply lost his platform’ (Rugh, 2004: 153).


Recent individual attempts at investigative reporting have also existed. An
example of this is the work of Rana Al-Husseini in Jordan who set out to uncover
honour killings and bring them to the attention of the mediated public sphere. This
was significant given this was a taboo topic to be addressed and critiqued in public.
Working for the Jordan Times, Husseini would visit various police stations and
inquire, follow up and uncover various cases of honour crimes, investigate why they
were done and how they were dealt with. Through a series of investigative stories
that created social awareness to the issue, she exposed the legal deficiencies in
dealing with this social practice, thus calling for a legislation reform. As a result, a
National Jordanian Committee was formed to eliminate crimes of honour, and
managed to collect 15,000 signatures in the form of a petition demanding stronger
punishment for honour crimes (Husseini, 9 January 2015). Between 1999 and
2003, amendments to the law were presented to the Jordanian parliament but have
been rejected a number of times. Despite not achieving a concrete change in this
case, Husseini’s work brought the magnitude of the problem to local and global
attention.


9 5


It is necessary to point out that all the historic and recent individual cases of
investigative reporting were isolated, and not sustainable, since there were no
institutionalised units for investigative reporters. Nonetheless, media organisations
in the Arab world now understand the importance of investigative journalism and
are giving it some attention. The reason behind this is because they can see the
effects of investigative reporting and understand its power. In recent times, there has
been a renewal of investigative reporting through media institutions such as Al
Jazeera. Yosri Fouda, who was Al Jazeera’s chief investigative correspondent,
conducted investigative stories through the programme Sirri lil-Ghaya, translated to
‘Top Secret’. Fouda tells how the programme began:


After the launch of Al Jazeera in 1996, I summoned my courage and put
an “outlandish” idea across to its management: to be allowed to
disappear for two months every other month in return for a bi-monthly,
45-minute investigative piece. Sarcastically, the norm in Arab TV then
was that you were only allowed to disappear for 45 minutes if you
promised to come back with two months’ worth of rushes. Rather
expectedly, the proposal generated some kind-hearted laughs and I
almost entered yet another cycle of professional depression (Fouda,
2009).


Despite this rough start, the programme began its broadcasts in 1998 and ran for
ten years. In June 2010, Al Jazeera announced the establishment of its investigative
unit. The Director General at the time, Wadah Khanfar, said this was a:


groundbreaking initiative, at a time major news networks [were]
retreating from investigative journalism the new unit represents an
important step forward for the Network, not only to expand the breadth
of our reporting, but also to drive further into stories for deeper
narratives (Press Release, 29 June 2010).


The unit consisted of three investigative teams whose aim was to conduct their own
investigations, and also commission stories from external sources for output across
all Al Jazeera channels, platforms and languages (Press Release, 29 June 2010).
Another Al Jazeera investigative unit was established in April 2013 as part of Al
Jazeera America, and in January 2011, the network launched the Al Jazeera
Transparency Unit (AJTU), which ‘aims to mobilize its audience - both in the Arab
world and further afield - to submit all forms of content (documents, photos, audio
& video clips, as well as “story tips”) for editorial review and, if merited, online
broadcast and transmission on our English and Arabic-language broadcasts’ (Al
Jazeera Investigates, 9 January 2015). Al Jazeera’s investigative unit deals with
regional and global issues, and it is necessary to note that the investigations it
conducts are therefore conducted at a regional and global level in contrast to the
training and reporting conducted at a more local state level, such as the investigative
units that have been set up with the assistance of the Arab Reporters for
Investigative Journalism (ARIJ).

The ethical issues facing investigative reporters are often less black and white, than
gray. Our stance is that rather than try to discern the ideal or normative ethical
position in a given situation, reporters may find it simpler and more effective to
consider the risks involved. What dangers may arise, for the reporter and for others,
from a given course of action? How can the reporter manage, reduce or eliminate
those risks?


The key ethical risks that face investigative reporters involve three main themes:
revelation, sourcing and conflicts of interest. Let us consider all three from the
standpoint of risk.


**A. The implications of revelation**


1. Reporters are blamed for the truth


Journalists (or for that matter, artists) who reveal injustice, corruption or simple
incompetence are always accused of undermining progress and public order. By
raising awareness of problems, according to this argument, they create even worse
problems.


Thus in the 1980s, journalists who exposed the corruption of the ruling Socialist
Party in France were accused of helping the neo-fascist National Front party to
progress, and in the 1990s, the revelation that a government agency had knowingly
sold blood products contaminated with AIDs to hemophiliacs was denounced as an
attempt to destroy the national health system, motivated by sympathy for the
extreme right. _1_


The instructor will no doubt know of similar cases, in which reporters are accused of
acting against the national interest by exposing injustice, closer to home. If such a
case has been documented in the press or the scholarly literature, the instructor
may copy the report and give it to students as advance reading. If not, the instructor
may use “Ethical Conflict and Investigative Reporting” (Hunter 1997) as advance
reading. _2_


The instructor can address this issue by presenting students with the story of a
recent scandal in their country, and asking:


_Should this be revealed? Or is it better to leave the story untold? What stories should_
_not be told?_


The instructor can offer examples – say, corruption, marital violence, or poor
government services – until someone is willing to say that it does no good to tell the
story.

Rather than ask why, the instructor can ask: _Is the problem real?_


The instructor can then ask: _How will the problem be solved if no one acknowledges_
_that it exists?_


If students say that whether or not a problem is revealed, nothing will change, the
instructor may ask: _Who benefits most when we denounce injustice?_


The answer is: the victims. Whether or not something changes, they take comfort
from knowing that they are not alone. This is the minimum result of an investigation
into injustice or corruption, and it fully justifies telling a story that evokes
corruption, incompetence, social or governance problems. Recall that in his preface
to Story-Based Inquiry, Yosri Fouda says that nothing is sweeter to an investigator
than when someone says, “Thank you for showing the story.” Many of those who say
it are victims who have given up hope of recognition, let alone justice.


The instructor can then ask:


_Should the reporter only show the problem, or should the reporter also propose_
_solutions?_


This question addresses the core of the practice of objectivity: An objective reporter
must not take sides. The ethical underpinning is that the reporter cannot be fair to
a subject if he or she has a stake in the outcome. The reporter’s only goal, by this
theory, is to report the facts, fully and fairly. The reporter must then allow the public
to conclude what the story means, and in particular, what the story shows must
change, and how to do it. This has been called the “mobilisation model” of how
investigative reporting achieves results and sets public agendas (Protess et al.,
1991).  It proposes that the reporter’s revelations of unknown or unappreciated facts _3_
arouses public opinion and desire for reform, and institutional actors – regulators,
prosecutors, legislators, executives – act to satisfy that desire. We will say more
about this later. You can tell your students that this is why, at the end of their book,
_All the President’s Men_, Woodward and Bernstein do not say explicitly that President
Richard Nixon must resign or be impeached and thrown out of office; if they do, they
will be taking sides. Some of the public will think so anyway, but they must try to
keep a distance from all sides in any case.


However, there is another tradition in investigative reporting. It is exemplified by
Albert Londres, the French grand reporter of the post-World War I era, who after
exploring the penal colony of Guyana, closed his series with a demand for four
specific reforms: _“I am done. The government must start,”_ he wrote. All four of
Londres’s proposed reforms eventually became law. The Muckrakers also sought
specific reforms. This tradition goes beyond denouncing injustice, to propose
changes.

In the 1990s, some scholars argued that proposing solutions to contemporary
problems is a core mission of news media.  We agree, on condition that the solutions _4_
are supported by serious study and investigation of alternatives (and not merely to
support government or a party, as is commonly done by tame journalists). This
approach is also compatible with current developments such as the entry of large
NGOs, notably Greenpeace, into investigative journalism. Greenpeace does not
merely tell the world that the environment has problems; it actively researches and
promotes solutions to those problems.


We realise that many students will confuse their role with that of activists or
advocates. The instructor can make several points when this confusion arises:


Reporters who propose solutions must be transparent about their interests
and objectives for a given issue.


They inevitably assume responsibility for the consequences of their work, for
better or worse.


Conversely, reporters are frequently accused of negative effects on the
societies where they work, whether those effects are real or not, intended or
not.


Investigative reporters must never be merely the servants of a cause. They
must first seek to discover the truth of a given situation. Positive change
cannot be founded on illusions, no matter how noble they may seem.


The reporter may learn of possible solutions from sources, or imagine them,
then verify whether or not they are workable. This is a worthy goal, and a fair,
thorough investigation allows one to attain it.


2. Investigative revelations may hurt someone


Investigative reports frequently identify specific individuals as responsible for
particular injustices or wrongdoing. These people may suffer consequences: They
can lose their jobs, or their business can lose customers. They may face social anger
or criticism. They may also be prosecuted, if their wrongdoing involves crimes.


The instructor will know of such a story in his or her region. It can be evoked with
students. The instructor may ask: _Under what conditions should we denounce_
_someone? What can justify the damage that we may do to someone’s reputation or_
_livelihood?_


_Is it enough justification that we do not like someone?_

_Is revenge a valid motive?_

Students may ask if avenging injustice is mere revenge. Anger at injustice is a valid

would do it “cleanly”. Put another way, you do not allow anger to take you beyond

Here are some questions you can discuss with your students. Always ask them to
give you an example to support their opinion. If the example is not a fact – if no one

_What if they are harming others?_


_Are they doing harm deliberately, or by mistake?_


_Have they tried to conceal their acts, which makes it more difficult to repair the_
_damage they have caused?_


_Do they hold a position of responsibility that requires a higher than usual standard of_

To show how these questions are answered by a master reporter, the instructor can
discuss “Torture at Abu Ghraib”, by Seymour Hersh. In this story about torture in an

criminal acts, but he does not stop with this shocking material. He focuses his most
intense attention on the commanders and policy makers who created the situation.
This is a high target, and he hits it. Thus his greatest effort is given to the greater
evil. This is also an ethical stance. As Andrew Jennings says: _Who would you rather_

**B. Sourcing is an ethical process**


1. Sources have the right to reply


In investigative work, as in all reporting (see the SPJ “Code of Ethics”), anyone who
is implicated in a story as an actor or target must be given the opportunity to reply
to any questions raised by the story, and to explain any facts concerning him or her.
The only exception is when speaking with the source will result in immediate
physical harm to the reporter. However, even gangsters can be interviewed, as the
Organised Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (occrp.org) has proven. _6_


In general, student work which does not include interviews with individuals accused
of wrongdoing or incompetence should be graded unacceptable.

2. Scoops must be corroborated


Very frequently, reporters are offered “scoops” that are essentially disinformation,
based on the testimony of one or another parties to a scandal, or on a purportedly
“secret” document. In such cases the information should not be used unless the
reporter can verify it independently.


To make this point, you can obtain an unsourced newspaper or blog story
containing scandalous or sensational content. (For example, several Arab
newspapers recently published the “news” that a female Israeli minister slept
with diplomats from foreign countries to get their consent for Israeli policies.)
Hand it to your students and ask, “Should we publish this too?” They will
almost surely begin to debate the content. When they have done so for a few
moments, ask them: _“How do you know that this news is authentic? How will_
_you verify it?”_ If they cannot answer that question, then the document is
worthless.


3. Anonymous sources may be used with precautions


The use of anonymous sources always concerns students, as well as professionals,
particularly in the Arab world. There are good reasons for a source to request
anonymity. Sometimes a source runs a risk of revenge, for example. From the
source’s standpoint, a reporter thus represents a risk of exposure and retribution.
We will consider this case in the next section.


Conversely, sometimes the source merely seeks to manipulate the reporter or
damage someone else with impunity. Thus from the reporter’s standpoint, an
anonymous source represents a higher risk for the reporter’s own credibility. If the
story is false, a mere manipulation or mistake, the reporter’s career can be severely
compromised.  Let us consider this risk first. _7_


Corroboration serves that purpose: What other sources support the claims of the
reporter’s anonymous sources? They can be found through documenting the
source’s claims, or by confirming with three other human sources, anonymous or
not. Both techniques are visible in this extract from “The battles for ‘Bab Tabbaneh’
and ‘Jabal Mohsen’: Banter and Barter, and Bullets” by Carole Kerbage. The core of
the piece is composed of interview material. However, notice how the reporter weaves
separate facts into the interviews, to confirm a coherent image:


A commander’s confession is supported by a popular phrase that shows how
typical are his acts;


The complete commercial chain of the “traffic” is mapped out;


Details of a known, similar case are provided;


And the reporter shows us an untouched shop owned by a man who plays the
game.

4. Insiders and whistleblowers


Students often imagine that the key to investigation is getting access to insiders in
an organization or an event. Indeed, the “inside” source was the trademark of the
most profitable show in the history of broadcasting, _60 Minutes_, as the film _The_
_Insider_ recounted. This tactic can deliver great drama, and great stories.


But it also carries big risks. Some of them fall on the source; news media soon lose
interest in whistleblowers, who may then suffer retribution. Or, they may simply be
forgotten as the reporter moves to the next story. (This, in effect, is how The Insider
ends.)


Another risk is that the inside source is a fool, or a fake. For example, _60 Minutes_
correspondent Lara Logan broadcast a story about the 2012 attack on the US
embassy in Benghazi that was based on the recollections of one man. However, her
source recollected events quite a different way in a written report prior to contacting
her. Logan's story created a political scandal before it was retracted. _[8]_

Once again, students must understand that reliance on an anonymous source, no

may be blamed for things they have never done. Note that in _All the President’s Men,_

5. Put the source above the secret


a. Protecting the source


Nearly every country defines the possession or revelation of certain official
documents as a crime.  Typically, the documents concern judicial or security affairs,
though in some countries, the scope of official secrecy may be very wide. In some
places, the rules are deliberately left unclear, to allow authorities greater scope for
action; or they may be enforced arbitrarily, even illegally.

If a journalist is offered such documents, he or she is probably wise to accept them.
However, the journalist will be even wiser to keep the documents in a safe place,
outside his or her home or office. The journalist must also take care to ask the
provider of the documents if he or she can be identified as their likely human source.
If so, the journalist must never refer or allude to the documents.  The information _9_
they contain must be documented by other means.


The instructor can make this point by asking a student to volunteer. The instructor
will hand the student an official report and say, _“I obtained this from a government_
_worker. It contains important information. Do you want it?”_


The student will of course say yes (if he or she does not, ask if anyone else does until
someone says yes.)


The instructor can then withhold the report and say, _“What will you do with it?”_


The student, of course, will study it and write a story if the information is valid.


The instructor should then ask, _“What will happen to the worker who gave me this_
_document? Will she get caught? Will she lose her job?”_
