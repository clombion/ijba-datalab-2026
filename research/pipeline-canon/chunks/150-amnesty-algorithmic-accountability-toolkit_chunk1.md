## Overview

This toolkit is designed for anyone looking to investigate and challenge the use of algorithmic systems in the public sector. It synthesizes the learnings of Amnesty International's Algorithmic Accountability Lab, which specializes in researching, campaigning and advocating on issues related to state use of automated systems.

**Target audience:** Civil society organizations (CSOs), journalists, community organizations, researchers, communications experts, campaigners, advocates, and technologists.

**Four core elements of the approach:**
1. Human rights frameworks and research (grounding harms within IHRL)
2. Centering people's stories (lived experiences over technical language)
3. Sociotechnical framing (technology within political, social, economic contexts)
4. Research to seek change (driving concrete advocacy, litigation, or policy shifts)

## Glossary

- **AI Lifecycle**: Sequential stages of AI development — model conceptualization, data collection, data processing, model design, model implementation, and model evaluation.
- **Algorithm**: A list of mathematical rules which solve a problem in a defined order. Algorithms are the building blocks of AI and ML.
- **Algorithm Registers**: Consolidated directories providing information about algorithmic systems used by public agencies in different jurisdictions.
- **Algorithmic Bias**: Systematic and repeatable errors in an algorithmic system that create unfair outcomes due to biases embedded in data, model design, or deployment context.
- **Algorithmic decision-making system**: An algorithmic system used in (support of) various steps of decision-making processes.
- **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: No consensus definition; broadly describes any technique or system that allows computers to mimic human behaviour.
- **Automated decision-making system**: An algorithmic decision-making system where no human is involved; the decision is taken solely by the system.
- **Biometric (surveillance) technologies**: Technologies used to identify human body characteristics using biologically unique markers such as fingerprints, eye retinas, voice patterns, facial patterns, and hand measurements.
- **Black-box algorithm**: An algorithmic system where the inputs and outputs can be viewed, but the internal workings are unknown.
- **Facial recognition technology (FRT)**: Computer vision technique used to identify faces based on images used for prior training.
- **Fairness**: Numerous suggested methods for embedding fairness into AI systems to avoid algorithmic bias; generally considered a limited tool in and of themselves.
- **Machine Learning (ML)**: A subfield of AI; a technique to provide AI with the capacity to learn from data to perform a task.
- **Predictive algorithms**: The use of AI techniques to make future predictions about a person, event or other outcome.

## Introduction

Over the past decade, most governments have adopted digital transformation strategies, increasingly adopting data-driven technologies with automated, algorithmic or AI-driven components. Examples include systems that:
- Assess or "predict" the risk that welfare claimants are committing fraud
- Predict the risk that a person commits a crime
- Triage visa applications

These automated decision-making (ADM) systems have been widely reported to: exclude people from essential services; replicate inequities along racial, gender, migration status, disability and socio-economic lines; give impacted people limited ability to challenge decisions; and deploy mass surveillance technologies that particularly impact marginalized communities.

### Four Core Elements

**1. Human rights frameworks**: IHRL is legally binding on states and provides existing commitments for challenging systems through campaigning, litigation and advocacy. Where states partner with private companies, the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights apply.

**2. Importance of people's stories**: Centering people's and community stories demonstrates issues in accessible ways. Research solely focused on technical aspects without meaningful participation of impacted people can gesture towards false notions that issues can be solved through technical fixes alone.

**3. Sociotechnical framing**: Looking at technology within the political, social, economic and cultural incentives and environmental factors. The word "system" denotes that these tools are "intricate, dynamic arrangements of people and code" that cannot be reduced to the technology component.

**4. Research to seek change**: Documenting evidence alone is not enough. Post-publication, researchers must continue to pursue accountability through advocacy, litigation and other means.

## Project Scoping

### Scoping Phase Aims

A fact-finding mission to build a picture of:
- Where and by whom algorithmic systems are being deployed
- Whether they are having detrimental impacts on people's lives
- What information is available in the public domain
- What further research avenues are possible
- Options for challenging systems and seeking change

### Considerations for Global Organizations

- **Geography**: Does the project centre on a specific technology or area? Consider highest-stakes deployment contexts or under-researched areas.
- **Avoiding duplication**: Build relationships with local activists, journalists and organizations. Only proceed if your work is additive to local efforts.
- **Community asks**: Community organizations may have specific requests where international support is useful (e.g., Serbia's A11 Initiative approached Amnesty for support investigating the Social Card Law).
- **Advocacy opportunities**: Proposed regulation (e.g., EU AI Act) presents opportunities for civil society input.
- **Accountability opportunities**: Consider existing regulation/legislation, oversight bodies, domestic court willingness, regional legal instruments.

### Participatory Methods

Involve stakeholders, community members, and affected people as early as possible. Participatory research approaches include community-led data collection, collaborative workshops, and mapping exercises. Build a shared understanding of algorithmic harm and allow communities to shape the project and its goals.

### Considerations for Community Organizations

- Consider whether a long-form investigation is needed vs. advocacy/campaigning
- Partner with researchers or journalists for research capacity
- Draw on international examples (India's Aadhar, Denmark's welfare tech)
- Use resources like TechTonic Justice's guidance to understand if people you work with are subject to algorithmic systems

### Knowledge of the System

Early steps:
1. **Desk research**: Reviews of official government communications, strategy and procurement documents
2. **FOIs**: Submit broadly to relevant public sector agencies (Lighthouse Reports submitted 100+ FOIs for their Suspicion Machine investigation)
3. **Speaking to impacted communities**: Engagement with individuals subjected to new technologies, obtaining copies of official decision communications

### Viability Checklist

- Important human rights concerns present
- Existing or potential access to information about the system
- Internal and external partner support
- Additive to impacted communities' work
- Contributes to advocacy goals
- Upcoming regulatory opportunities
- Capacity for follow-up advocacy
- Potential for data collection on system impact

## Project Goals and Lifecycle

### Holistic View

Investigating potential human rights impacts requires both sociotechnical analysis and human rights-focused research. A holistic approach considers harms along the lifecycle of the technical system from inception to use.

Analysis of civil society reports, news reports, and interviews with impacted communities can surface key human rights harms even if technical research cannot be undertaken.

### Affecting Change

Discussions around change should precede any investigation:
- Who are the impacted people and communities?
- What are the harms?
- What is the best way to serve and support communities?

A **theory of change** incorporates goals, channels of influence, key messages, tactics and activities, with mechanisms for monitoring and evaluating success.

### Stakeholders to Include

Impacted individuals and communities, local journalists and researchers, digital rights CSOs, organizations working with affected communities, relevant politicians, legal case workers, academics, service delivery practitioners, technologists, government officials, ombudsman officials.

## Project Ethics and Principles

### Community Participation

Embed participatory approaches throughout a project's duration. After scoping, ensure regular consultation and reshape the project to align with community needs. Published research should be translated and made accessible (Easy to Read versions, explainer videos, presentations to communities).

### Anti-Discrimination and Intersectional Lens

Embed racial justice and anti-discrimination within the strategy. Interview individuals with diverse identities. Intersectionality examines how different forms of discrimination overlap and interact — critical for analyzing how algorithmic systems compound harms against specific groups (e.g., women of colour with disabilities).

### Shared Commitments

- Locally led: needs and priorities of impacted communities guide the work
- Prioritize communities most at risk
- Intersectional: look beyond symptoms to structural and systemic issues
- Prioritize allyship and collaborative work
- Accessible and understandable to impacted communities

### Security Considerations

Prioritize safety over revelation of information. Invest in planning to assess risks and benefits for communities and the research team. Develop risk mitigation strategies in partnership with stakeholders. Do not proceed if the risk of harm is too high.

## Obtaining Access to Information

### Direct Engagement with Government Officials

Where security allows, direct engagement should be the first port of call. Provides opportunities to interrogate the system and understand policy drivers. Example: Amnesty's research into Denmark's Udbetaling Danmark included meetings and interviews about algorithmic systems in social protection.

### Freedom of Information Requests

Critical for circumventing lack of publicly available information. Template requests should cover:
- Technical documentation (system architecture, algorithmic design)
- Data collected/exploited
- Example model inputs and outputs
- Data Protection Impact Assessments
- Equalities or human rights impact assessments
- Bias or statistical assessments

Example model inputs/outputs and technical documentation are essential first ports of call.

### Subject Access Requests (SARs)

Data protection laws grant people the right to access information organizations hold about them. SARs can reveal system inputs and data processing when working with people subjected to algorithmic systems.

### Expert Interviews

Interview academics, analysts, practitioners, and front-line workers. Start by looking for relevant media and academic articles, reaching out to authors and quoted experts.

### Alternative Data Sources

Map out the "dream scenario" data and triangulate publicly available information with FOI requests. Public records, survey data, and quarterly/yearly reports from public sector agencies can be valuable.

### Procurement Research and Supply Chain

Map the supply chain from computing hardware to cloud providers to AI companies to government deployment. Useful sources:
- Tender notices and Requests for Proposals
- Published government strategies
- Loan agreements or MoUs with international organizations
- Public company information, marketing materials, and demonstrations
- Companies search databases and job advertisements
- Research letters requesting responses from private companies

## Human Rights Research

### Methods and Data Sources

- **Testimonies and focus groups**: The backbone of evidence on algorithmic harms
- **Legal analysis**: International human rights law, UN reports, domestic law, court transcripts
- **Discourse analysis**: Media reporting, government interviews, policy documents
- **Survey data**: Short surveys with people subject to the technology

### Sources of Human Rights Law

International treaties: ICCPR, ICESCR, ICERD, CEDAW, CRC, CRPD. Regional treaties: American Convention on Human Rights, African Charter, EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, ECHR. Data protection treaties: Convention 108+, African Convention on Cyber-Security, GDPR.

### Right to Privacy

Guaranteed under ICCPR (174 of 193 UN states). Digital welfare states require merging multiple government databases, amounting to mass-scale extraction and processing of personal data.

**Case Study — Denmark**: Amnesty's research found Denmark allows mass-scale extraction and processing of personal data for fraud detection, including merging government databases, use of fraud control algorithms, unregulated social media monitoring, and geolocation data — all collected without consent. This data processing only amounted to 30% of fraud investigations, raising necessity concerns.

### Right to Equality and Non-Discrimination

Guaranteed under ICCPR and most other treaties. AI systems can lead to discrimination when used to classify, differentiate, rank and categorize.

**Direct discrimination**: Explicit distinction between groups (e.g., algorithm includes protected characteristics).
**Indirect discrimination**: Neutral presentation but disproportionate impact. **Proxies** — inputs correlated with protected characteristics (e.g., postcode correlating with socioeconomic status and race).

**Case Study — Serbia**: The Social Card Law (2022) introduced automation into social assistance eligibility. The Social Card registry processes data from multiple government databases. Amnesty found the system is not designed to factor in challenges faced by communities most dependent on social assistance, including Roma, people with disabilities, and women. Multiple cars registered to someone living in extreme poverty would be flagged without understanding these may be sold for scrap metal.

### Other Rights at Stake

- **Right to Social Security and Adequate Standard of Living** (ICESCR): Automated welfare systems that reduce access to benefits directly impact these rights.
- **Right to Freedom of Peaceful Assembly** (ICCPR Articles 21-22): Facial recognition technology fundamentally incompatible with human rights. Documented abuses in Occupied Palestinian Territories, Hyderabad, New York City, France, Netherlands, and Hungary.

## Algorithmic Auditing through Empirical Investigations

### Statistical Bias and Fairness Testing

Rooted in traditional social science auditing of racial and gender discrimination. The core idea: compare accuracy of algorithmic decisions across demographic groups. Measuring fairness is not purely technical — proposed measures must be interrogated from legal, philosophical and sociological perspectives.

**Key fairness metrics:**

1. **Demographic Parity**: Equal proportion of positive predictions between groups. Example: In COMPAS, the proportion denied bail would be equal across demographic groups.

2. **Predictive Parity**: Equal precision (proportion of correct predictions among positive predictions) across groups. Example: Among those predicted "high risk," the proportion who reoffend would be the same across groups.

3. **False Positive Error Rate**: Equal false positive rates across groups. Example: Among people who don't reoffend, the system incorrectly labels the same proportion as "high risk" across groups.

Even when a specific definition of fairness is met, discrimination may still be present.

### Reviewing Documentation

- Compile a checklist of what you can learn (inputs, processing, models, outputs)
- Interrogate design choices — these are not neutral decisions
- Examine redactions (not always applied correctly)
- Use information for follow-up questions
- Corroborate with testimonial evidence

### Audit Scenarios

**Scenario 1 — Full access (White Box)**: Rotterdam Municipality's welfare algorithm. Lighthouse Reports obtained source code, variable lists, model evaluations, documentation, trained model file, and raw data, enabling direct stress-testing and bias analysis.

**Scenario 2 — Code access (Reverse Engineering)**: France's CNAF risk-scoring algorithm. Researchers obtained redacted documentation and source code, reconstructed the system, ran tests using publicly available demographic data, and created profiles to assess which people were most at risk of discrimination.

**Scenario 3 — Outcome data only (Black Box)**: Sweden's Social Insurance Agency. Without access to source code or inputs, researchers requested data on outputs and outcomes, conducting statistical bias and fairness testing. Required: how the system makes decisions for demographic groups and whether decisions were correct, plus a comparison group.

## Affecting Change through Advocacy

### 1. Building Power

Equip your organization and partners with knowledge and expertise. Develop guidance documents, policy briefs, and workshops. Build alliances across digital rights, human rights, social justice, environmental, racial, migrant, gender, disability, and intersectional justice movements.

### 2. Shaping Narratives (Communications)

Counter pro-innovation and anti-regulation narratives dominating mainstream media. Key considerations:
- Center lived experiences of impacted people
- Demystify the technology (simple language, graphics)
- Challenge false dichotomy between innovation and regulation
- Present positive, rights-affirming vision of technology's role
- Co-design key messages with partner organizations
- Time publications strategically (avoid quieter days)

### 3. Influencing Policy

Push for higher legal protections, effective implementation of existing laws, and push back against weakening of protections. Key considerations:
- Define advocacy goals with partners
- Choose between direct engagement with policymakers vs. wider campaigning
- Understand political context and public debate
- Identify key dates for influencing
- Map target audiences, allies, and adversaries

## Routes to Accountability and Justice

### Strategic Litigation

Deliberate selection of legal proceedings to create long-term societal change, using the legal system to pursue remedies, challenge unjust practices, and set precedents.

**Case Study — France (CNAF)**: Amnesty International and 14 coalition partners led by La Quadrature du Net submitted a complaint to the Council of State demanding withdrawal of the CNAF risk-scoring algorithm. The system assigns risk scores to all recipients of family and housing benefits, discriminating against people on low income, unemployed, in disadvantaged neighbourhoods, and those with disabilities.

### National Supervisory and Equalities Mechanisms

**Case Study — Sweden**: Following Lighthouse Reports and Svenska Dagbladet's disclosure that Försäkringskassan's algorithm disproportionately flagged women (1.5x), people with foreign backgrounds (2.5x), and people without degrees (3.31x), advocacy with the discrimination ombudsman led to an open call for cases. The Swedish Data Protection Authority (IMY) opened an investigation, resulting in the system being shut down.

### Building Power and Mobilizing Communities

**Case Study — NYC (Ban the Scan)**: In September 2020, Amnesty filed a FOIL request for NYPD records on surveillance of BLM protests. After NYPD refused, Amnesty and S.T.O.P. filed a lawsuit in July 2021. In February 2022, Amnesty mobilized 7,000+ digital volunteers to map facial recognition cameras across NYC, revealing that neighbourhoods most at risk of stop-and-frisk also had the highest concentration of FRT-compatible cameras. In August 2022, the New York Supreme Court ordered NYPD to disclose nearly 2,700 documents on procurement and use of FRT against BLM protesters.
