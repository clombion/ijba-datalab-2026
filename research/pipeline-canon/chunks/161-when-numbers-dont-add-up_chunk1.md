**Sue Green** **[1]**


**Abstract**
Journalism educators recognize the need to prioritize teaching data journalism
to ensure their students are industry-ready, particularly given the exponential
growth in data, data availability, collection and analysis. However, research indicates
that falling staff numbers and skill levels, lack of available sessional expertise and
insufficient room in existing course curricula are among the problems faced in
accommodating data journalism. This article outlines the efforts of Swinburne
University of Technology’s journalism programme to meet these challenges and
the solutions implemented. The programme staff sought expertise through their
existing international contacts and within their own university but also reached
beyond the silos of their own department and faculty into computer science
disciplines. The outcome means students in a programme which promotes its
practical nature and industry-experienced staff will be taught by staff with data
analytics expertise but limited journalism experience. This has, however, opened
up the possibilities for collaboration between the analytics and journalism staff
members and a team approach to the teaching of data journalism.


**Keywords**
Data journalism, journalism education, data analytics, future-ready

The rich story potential inherent in the vast quantities of data created and captured
daily is an opportunity lost to journalism students if they do not learn how to
source this data, to analyse and to storify it. It is clear that data journalism skills
are now not only a means for journalists to find stories but also they increase the
attractiveness of new journalism graduates to employers at a time when the
employment market is particularly challenging. In April 2016, US research found
employers wanted ‘superpowers’; they valued skills such as data journalism
ahead of the basics of newswriting, editing and reporting. ‘Coding/development,
audience development/user data and metrics, visual storytelling, digital design,
and social media distribution were the top five hiring priorities among the newsroom leaders surveyed’ (Stencel & Perry, 2016). While no comparable study of
Australian media employers is available, the Business Council of Australia identifies data analysis as among the minimum expectations Australian business has of
an individual’s skills on applying for a job or soon after starting (BCA, 2016).
Seven years ago the World Wide Web inventor Sir Tim Berners-Lee said:
‘Journalists need to be data-savvy … now it’s also going to be about poring over
data and equipping yourself with the tools to analyse it and picking out what’s
interesting’ (Arthur, 2010). In that time news organizations have increasingly
realized the importance of this and, albeit with varying levels of enthusiasm,
success and some inertia, begun giving priority to data journalism in their newsrooms and their hiring (Appelgren & Nygren, 2014; De Maeyer, Libert, Domingo,
Heinderyckx, & Le Cam, 2014; Fink & Anderson, 2015; Tabary, Provost, &
Trottier, 2016; Stampfl, 2016). By 2016, Borges-Rey found data journalism units
were in place in most of the mainstream British newsrooms she sampled:


In cases where a data unit was not in operation, a minimum of one data journalist was
appointed—or worked informally—to write data-driven journalism or to collaborate
with other specialist correspondents in co-authored pieces. In this respect, data journalism practice is fully ingrained within the news cycles of the majority of the mainstream
organisations studied. (p. 841)


This reinforces the need to prioritize data journalism in journalism education
courses. Davies and Cullen noted that the themes emerging from the monitoring
of data journalism by journalism academics ‘include recognition that DJ is an
essential part of contemporary news work’ (2016, p. 133). However, they also
note that while data visualization was being taught in some way in 72 per cent of
the universities they surveyed, coding was being actively taught in just three.
Coding and extraction of meaningful information from big data are only being
taught in some lectures and activities or in modules or units in 12 per cent (three)
of the responding universities, with some mentions in further 28 per cent and
52 per cent (13 universities) not teaching it (Davies & Cullen, 2016, p. 138).
It is becoming evident that this lack of coding ability is an issue for graduate journalists. When Australia’s Walkley Foundation offered 20 Sydney and
Melbourne Coder Factory scholarships for journalists in 2017, 139 applications


_Green_ 3


were received. Powell (2017a) reports the applicants were ‘deeply aware of the need
to learn how to code’ making the selection process difficult and leading to the
realization that more opportunities were needed for those who missed out. BorgesRey (2016, p. 840) notes this as an issue in UK newsrooms: ‘Amid claims of
journalistic authority over computational proficiency, at least half of regional data
journalists felt limited by their inability to write software code, regarding the skill
as a powerful enabling agent in data journalism’.
Predictions about the exponential growth in data and data collection reinforce
the importance of data journalism. In April last year, a white paper by market
intelligence company International Data Corporation (IDC) forecast: ‘What the
next decade will bring using the power of data is virtually limitless’ (Reinsel,
Gantz, & Rydning, 2017, p. 2). The paper’s predictions about the staggeringly
large future growth of data demonstrate the necessity for our students to master
finding, understanding and analysing data, then presenting it in a readable form:
‘IDC forecasts that by 2025 the global datasphere will grow to 163 zettabytes
(that is a trillion gigabytes). That’s ten times the 16.1ZB of data generated in 2016’
(Reinsel et al., 2017, p. 3).
Interestingly, a Quebec study (Tabary et al., 2016, pp. 77–78) found that a
majority of the journalists who worked on the data journalism projects samples
did so as researchers and had minimal computer skills, while those with graphic
design, statistics and computer skills did most of the complex project creation.
Journalism education programmes around Australia are coming to grips with
equipping journalists to meet the challenges of such projects, including undertaking more of the technical aspects, with Davies and Cullen denoting data journalism as one of ‘the current hot topics in journalism education in Australia’ (2016,
p. 144). The challenge recognized by journalism educators is, therefore, to provide
students with the specialist teaching and courses they need at a time when staff
numbers and resources are under pressure.
These challenges appear to be common to journalism schools around the world.
While most of the studies in the emerging body of research in this area focus on one
country or even one course, there is a consistency to the issues identified. These
include a lack of staff expertise and of available, skilled sessional teachers, the
need for upskilling of busy existing staff, maths aversion in students and staff and
a lack of available time and space in existing courses (Appelgren & Nygren, 2014;
Berret & Phillips, 2016; Davies & Cullen, 2016; Splendore et al., 2015).
Hewitt (2016, p. 132) noted other key factors to consider: ‘Points such as computing resources, the learning environment, group size, links with colleagues in other
disciplines, and how the data journalism teaching will interface with other modules
or courses. Teaching and administrative workloads must be addressed, too.’
Nonetheless, despite these acknowledged and ubiquitous difficulties, some of
the literature is extremely positive about data journalism’s potential and importance in the challenging current and future media environment. Despite warning
about the dangers of ‘bad data journalism’, Howard predicted (2014, p. 79):

‘As people struggle to make sense of what matters or is true in a tsunami of new
media, data journalism will be held up as a way to provide trustworthy insights to
debunk pseudoscience, propaganda, misinformation, and online rumor’.
Borges-Rey (2016, p. 841) noted the disruptive power of data journalism, with
greater interactivity and content linked to analysis of data. Her survey of UK data
journalism lead her to conclude:


Data journalists have also innovated by institutionalising a whole new range of norms
and conventions to both legitimise their practice, and overcome generalised public
mistrust of journalism. In this respect, data journalism uses methods reinforced by
values such as numeric infallibility, scientific rigour, computational neutrality, crowd
sourced collaboration, intra-and extra-newsroom cooperation, and hyperlocal empathy
to generate exclusives that are generally perceived as more accurate and transparent
(2016, p. 842).


But there are warning bells. Knight (2015, p. 70) cautions over ‘the appropriation
of the language of investigative data journalism for somewhat less rigorous
material’ while Flew, Spurgeon, Daniel and Swift (2012, p. 167) argue that data
journalism is only as good as its data.
The strength of computational journalism relies on the integrity, quality and
reliability of the data available: a central concern for both journalism and information technology. Statistical anomalies, a lack of sense, misinterpretations,
conflicting data standards, incomplete data, skewed results or malignly altered
data pose serious problems.
Other potential issues are identified by Splendore et al. (2015, p. 139), who assert
that the teaching of data journalism ‘frequently neglects fundamental journalistic
topics such as ethical issues, transparency, accountability, and responsiveness’.
So while Australian journalism educators grapple with the logistical difficulties of offering data journalism, the importance of doing so seemingly a given, the
challenges should not be underestimated. However important is teaching students
to find and analyse data, equally important must be teaching them to verify and
assess the reliability of that data.
This appears to be a rich area for future research, particularly in this era of
‘fake news’ and lack of trust in the media. There would be great value in research
findings which cross national boundaries to consider issues such as the ethics and
credibility of data journalism.


**The Challenge of Incorporating Data Journalism**


This is particularly demanding for a journalism education programme such as
that offered at Swinburne University of Technology. Convinced of the importance
of data journalism, the five full-time programme staff were keen to integrate it


_Green_ 5


into the journalism course. This was seen as essential to avoid a loss of currency
in the newly refreshed major available to students undertaking a Bachelor of
Arts or the Bachelor of Media and Communication, which was introduced in
2015. The staff were aware that other universities including the University of
Melbourne (Christiansen, 2013), RMIT University (RMIT University, 2017) and
the University of Queensland (The University of Queensland, 2016) had introduced dedicated data journalism courses. This was, therefore, a particular priority
given that one of the Swinburne course’s strengths is its practical nature, with
all tuition undertaken by staff with extensive industry experience. Also, the
university is committed through its _Strategic Plan 2025_ to creating ‘future-ready
graduates’ (Swinburne University of Technology, 2017). Finding a means of
achieving this, however, was far from straightforward.
With just eight core subjects in the journalism major, one of eight majors
offered within the Media and Communication Department, there was little scope
to add subjects. This would necessitate dropping an existing subject and undertaking the lengthy process of accrediting a new one. Also, a postgraduate data
journalism subject, albeit introductory, had just been included in the new masters
of media and communication and was scheduled to begin in 2018.
It was, therefore, decided to commence by integrating data journalism into as
many undergraduate core units as possible, including first-year subjects such as
newswriting. Most subjects would lend themselves to this and several, including
investigative journalism and reporting public forums already had small, introductory data journalism components, which students had shown limited enthusiasm
in exploring further through their stories. In the reporting public forums’ local
government module, for example, students were encouraged to draw on the substantial quantity of comparative data available on the Victorian Government’s
know your council website. Few, however, appeared confident about tapping into
and analysing this rich and readily available information.
This planned cross-subject integration posed its own challenges: A major with
just eight core units necessitates packed curricula to cover all that is essential to
produce employment-ready journalists. Adding new modules to existing subjects
was problematic; so too was diverting hard-pressed staff to new duties with none
feeling qualified to teach data journalism—a situation common to Australian
universities, with Davies and Cullen noting that from 25 universities surveyed,
‘only one respondent said that the existing staff at her university had been skilled
enough to teach DJ’ (2016, p. 140). They reported on the upskilling efforts across
journalism schools, but Swinburne staff were keen to avoid a situation where the
teacher is just one step ahead of the student.
The issues faced at Swinburne have also confronted journalism schools in
many countries, as discussed earlier. Swinburne University’s journalism staff are
all former senior journalists employed either directly from industry, or with very
recent industry experience, but none with specific data journalism experience.
All not only had full workloads, which meant adding duties would necessitate
dropping others, but found the prospect of teaching data journalism daunting, a

situation faced by many Australian journalism education programmes (Davies &
Cullen, 2016, p. 145). A key Berret and Phillips (2016) research finding summed
up the Swinburne situation: ‘Many journalism programs do not have a faculty
member skilled in data journalism. Hiring professional journalists as adjuncts
may pose many challenges, one of which is that job openings outnumber qualified
applicants’.


**Meeting the Challenge**


In considering how to proceed—not doing so was not an option—programme
staff were guided by the recommendations contained in the literature, and the
solutions found so far by other journalism schools facing the same issues. Despite
the relatively short time during which data journalism has been a priority for
journalism education, there is a considerable body of research with findings as to
how programmes across the Western world have come to grips with these issues.
Solutions have focused (Berret & Phillips, 2016; Davies & Cullen, 2016; Hewitt,
2016) on the use of guest lecturers, upskilling of existing staff, the use of sessional
teachers, other external assistance such as summer schools and massive open
online courses (MOOCS).
Hewitt notes (2016, p. 129):


Journalism schools typically supplement their core staff by involving practising
journalists as visiting lecturers, which helps to address questions of currency. But the
pool of individuals with current experience as data journalists is small, and an employer’s
demands on their sought-after skills and experience may make it difficult to accommodate teaching.


He cites the example of the MA interactive journalism at London City University,
which has used data journalists, some of whom have taught previously, to fill
this gap.
Berret and Phillips (2016, p. 10) further recommend: ‘Journalism programs
can integrate alternative teaching methods to help fill the gaps in their own faculty.
Examples include cooperative teaching among different university departments,
online courses, and independent tutorial packs’.
While programmes have sought outside help from within their own university
and beyond, including industry experts, the 2015 collaboration on an investigation of gambling between Auckland University of Technology and Canterbury
University in New Zealand went further (Treadwell, Ross, Lee, & Lowenstein, 2016).
Their reach was international and their pilot—a two university—postgraduate
project and an undergraduate paper at Auckland University of Technology—
demonstrated the potential of a collaborative approach for overcoming the issues
faced in introducing data journalism to journalism education programmes.
‘The postgraduate project demonstrated the value of collaborative teaching to
help instructors tackle new curriculum content, including both data analysis skills
and innovative approaches to their delivery’ (Treadwell et al., 2016, p. 306).

Facing the same kinds of challenges as the Swinburne University journalism
programme, including starting from a low base of student and staff data skills,
they reached out to the global journalism community.
A visiting Fulbright specialist supported both initiatives, helping to develop
staff and student data skills, kick-start a joint investigation by students and lay
the groundwork for future international collaborations. Thanks to his visit,
New Zealand educators and students were able to seek support from a global
community of journalists and journalism educators working in data journalism.
(Treadwell et al., 2016, p. 297).
This can be compared to the Walkley Foundation’s contribution to solving the
shortage of Australian journalists skilled in coding (Powell, 2017b). Albeit local
rather than international, it turned to a coding trainer, the Coder Factory Academy,
in each of the two major cities to offer eight-week training programmes in Ruby
on Rails. In response to frequent questions about why such scholarships were
being offered, Powell (2017b) wrote: ‘We’re offering the scholarships because
knowing how to code and build basic apps is an increasingly crucial skill for most
workers, and a particularly sought-after one by reporters’.
These graduates could have an important future role to play in persuading
reluctant, maths-averse undergraduates of the importance of data journalism.
In addressing future challenges, Hewitt (2016, p. 132) suggests: ‘Engaging successful alumni (as well as current students and perhaps employers) may have an
important role to play here in addressing the concerns of prospective students’.
The need for computational journalism was foreseen by Flew, Spurgeon,
Daniel and Swift almost a decade ago. Describing this (2012, p. 157) as ‘active
engagement with techniques for the large-scale manipulation of data using computing software to enable new ways to access, organise and present information’,
they concluded that this major development required ‘not only a certain level of
new ICT skills, capacities and literacies of journalists, but a new understanding of
how journalists can work with, and in, the new economies of distributed and
co-creative production’ (2012, p. 168). While they noted the challenge posed by
software costs, more recently an open-source software has opened the data
journalism door to every journalism student. However, the true value of this free
resource can be exploited only with appropriate tuition and it appears Australian
journalism schools have yet to fully meet this challenge.


**The Swinburne Solution**


With only three faculties following a restructuring in 2014, Swinburne University
has advocated the breaking down of silos and cooperation across disciplines
and schools. In 2017, it launched five new research institutes, including a Data
Science Research Institute, with research staff across the university encouraged to
collaborate with these institutes. With this policy and the solutions found by other
journalism schools in mind, the journalism programme staff discussed outsourcing

by seeking qualified sessional teachers and the possibility of applying for funding
or grants to bring in expertise. They decided to begin by looking within the
university but beyond the Faculty of Health Arts and Design. The hope was to
draw on the resources of the wider university.
Also, the staff looked to the programme staff’s contacts, including internationally, as the New Zealand educators had done. Swinburne University offers a wide
range of study abroad opportunities, enabling students to substitute a semester at
Swinburne for approved subjects in an approved university abroad. Birmingham
City University was recently included, as a result of contacts made by a member
of the Media and Communication Department staff. Birmingham City University
offers a practise-based three-week international data journalism summer intensive
from July to August, which includes data journalism and data visualization fundamentals, ways of telling data-driven stories and a live project (Birmingham City
University, 2018). Swinburne’s exchange programme made it possible to offer
journalism students the opportunity of undertaking that summer school in 2018
and receiving credit for it equivalent to a Swinburne elective unit.
Staff contacts were also used to identify colleagues with relevant skills in the
Faculty of Business and Law. I, as acting coordinator of the journalism programme,
and Media and Communication Department acting chair Dr Carolyn Beasley,
then made contact with Dr Ashir Ahmed, an information systems lecturer whose
teaching experience included data visualization and data management systems.
Although he had no experience of teaching data journalism, he was enthusiastic
about the topic, its importance and potential and agreed to meet with us to discuss
possible collaboration and guest lectures.
At that meeting, he provided the unit outline for his third-year subject business
analytics, offering students a guided opportunity to explore big data analytics,
to learn data mining techniques and social network analysis, and also covering
topics such as predictive analytics, data warehousing and future trends. He was
enthusiastic about encouraging journalism major students to undertake his unit as
an elective. With an assessment comprising solving analytic problems, reflective
practice essays and a group business analytics project worth 50 per cent of the
semester mark, this subject lent itself to study by journalism students. Instead of a
business analytics group project, they would undertake a journalism project.
Dr Ahmed also agreed to give guest lectures beginning in 2018 in two subjects,
reporting public forums and investigative journalism, with the prospect of expanding this further in future. His own experience related particularly to analytics and
he had extensive software knowledge and development skills. The use of data
visualization in journalism was, however, new to him and he was excited by the
possibilities, particularly after viewing examples such as _The South China_
_Morning Post Belt and Road_ initiative, best data visualization project at the World
Digital Media Awards 2017 (Duhalde, Arranz, & Hernandez, Long, 2017).

**A Non-journalist to Teach Journalism**


For some time, Swinburne Media and Communication Department staff, particularly those in the social media major, had been urging the appointment of a staff
member with expertise in media data analytics. Concurrently, with the approach
to information systems, faculty approval was given for a new position, the appointment of a senior lecturer in this field. The successful applicant would teach and
research across and beyond the department, but in particular teach into journalism
and social media, including at the postgraduate level. The coordinators of both
disciplines were included on the selection panel. The key selection criteria required
teaching experience and curriculum development, as well as research skills; however journalism experience was not specified.
None of the applications received was from a data journalist and none of the
shortlisted applicants had journalistic teaching or industry experience. However,
in recognition of the importance placed by the department on both undergraduate
and postgraduate data journalism teaching, each member who was shortlisted was
asked to present to the four-person selection panel on teaching data journalism
to maths averse students. The three candidates met this challenge admirably—
surprisingly so, given that for each data journalism teaching was a new challenge.
Each separately explained that to describe students as mathematics averse and to
think of data journalism as about mathematics was a misconception. Rather, it
was more accurate to think of those daunted by it as numbers averse, given that
modern software, including sorting and scraping tools, meant very little mathematics was required. Similar advice has been provided through the Walkley
Foundation by SBS data journalist Jason Thomas (2016) whose advice to mathsinsecure journalists reporting on surveys is that ‘basic maths and statistics are just
journalism’.
While data journalism has been a key component of some of Australia’s most
prominent and award-winning investigative stories—obvious examples include
the Panama Papers and Paradise Papers investigations—much of the data journalism being undertaken involves data visualization. This is the case on data journalism sites such as _The Age_ or _The Sydney Morning Herald_ Datapoint—‘news
by numbers’—and _The Guardian_ Australia edition data blog ( _The Age_, 2017;
The Sydney Morning Herald, 2017). As discussed earlier, Davies and Cullen (2016,
p. 137) found most of the universities they surveyed teaching data visualization.
The appointment of the new data analytics lecturer to Swinburne’s Media and
Communication Department now means its journalism programme is able to offer
students tuition well beyond data visualization and Excel spreadsheets. Dr Ahmed
was the successful applicant for the job. His presentation to the panel included a
full data journalism teaching package which he had devised. He has expressed
considerable enthusiasm for teaching students to find and extract stories from
big data.
The appointment of an information systems specialist to teach data journalism
does, however, raise questions as to whether a lack of journalism experience and

media knowledge will be disadvantageous. A pan-European data journalism study
found this lack of industry and journalism education experience was not unusual
there. ‘Educators in data journalism do not necessarily have a media-related
background’ (Splendore et al., 2015, p. 146). In countries with limited university
journalism training, data journalism teachers’ previous careers ranged from teaching and political science to the public service and agriculture, the study found.
It noted that in countries with more professionalized journalism training, collaboration between journalism and computing educators helped bridge the gap and
minimize competition between disciplines.
It seems likely that such a collaboration will be needed and indeed desirable at
Swinburne, but between Media and Communication staff, rather than across
faculties as was the initial plan. Dr Ahmed is willing to work collaboratively with
members of the journalism programme and development of this team approach is
already in train with integration of data journalism into two units in semester one,
2018. Using an open-source software which the students could download during
his classes, he has begun with a module in the third year investigative journalism
unit, comprising two lectures, tutorial exercises and a requirement that data analysis
be incorporated in the student’s final major assignment. A smaller data journalism
component has been incorporated in the reporting public forums unit, specifically
in the module relating to coverage of parliament and politics. In a single lecture
towards the end of semester, the students are shown the wide range of data on
political issues available to them and encouraged to explore these. It is intended
that the data journalism component of this subject will be increased in future and
that it will be introduced in the core first year newswriting unit. Although now
physically located in the Media and Communications Department, Dr Ahmed
continues to undertake some teaching in the Faculty of Business and Law, increasing cross-faculty cooperation.


**Conclusion**


Ideally, Swinburne University’s journalism programme would create a new lecturing position for a data journalism specialist and establish a new accredited subject
or subjects at the undergraduate level. But for a programme of its size, this was
simply not feasible for the reasons discussed earlier. This is a situation in common
with other Australian universities, as also previously discussed and as evidenced
by the research (Davies & Cullen, 2016, p. 140). Nonetheless, by drawing on the
lessons offered by other journalism programmes internationally and the findings
of the available research in this area, the programme has developed a solution
which ensures it will not lag in data journalism teaching.
Also, this is a solution which assists in breaking down the silos across the
university by reaching out to expertise beyond the department and even the
faculty. It illustrates that members of one department or faculty may be completely
unaware of the staffing resources and pool of talent existing within another.

Several lessons have been learned from this process as the programme looked
beyond its relatively small staff numbers:


  - Other university departments or faculties may have expertise which can be
drawn on beyond those administrative silos.

  - Guest lectures and small teaching modules inserted into existing courses
may overcome issues of a lack of staff expertise and packed curricula.

  - International collaborations can provide opportunities for student intensives
like summer schools.

  - A collaborative team approach between journalism and data analytics
educators may assist in overcoming the shortage of skilled data journalism
lecturers and sessional teachers.


In a journalism programme in which all staff members are industry veterans,
there is an element of the experimental about the Swinburne approach to teaching
data journalism. Students will be taught by a senior staff member with an information systems PhD and a mastery of the analytics but no industry or journalistic
knowledge or experience. Berret and Phillips warn (2016, p. 10):


Journalism schools can collaborate across the university to meet the burgeoning need
for instruction in data and computation but should be wary of trying to outsource too
much—while understanding how to do math, statistics, or computer programming is an
important component, data journalism is much more than that.


However, the teamwork and collaborative approach this necessitates may ultimately
be rewarding for the staff and ensure a richer educational experience for the
students.
