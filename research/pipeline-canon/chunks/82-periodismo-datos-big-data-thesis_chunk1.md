**ABSTRACT (SPANISH)**


Precision Journalism (PJ) and Computer Assisted Reporting (CAR) are terms that have accompanied, from the last two decades of the twentieth century, data journalism (DJ) and Data Driven Journalism (DDJ), both in the professional and academic fields. However, since the late 2000s and more specifically since the first massive leaks of Wikileaks in 2010, data journalism has undergone significant development to the present day by incorporating elements of a technological and sociocultural context that did not exist when PJ and CAR emerged in the previous century: _big data_.

In Spain this has been reflected in the birth of several data journalism teams in both traditional media outlets and recent digital-native ones, which in turn are related in some way to the first collectives of entrepreneurs and transparency activists who first practiced this discipline in our country. As a phenomenon, DJ has been growing in Spain in parallel with the enactment of the first transparency law. For this reason, we have set out to address the study of Spanish DJ and its relationship with _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_, taking _big data_ into consideration as the sociocultural and technological context in which it has been developing.

This research seeks to differentiate DJ as a journalistic discipline from others already in existence — such as PJ and CAR — from the perspective offered by Science, Technology and Society studies, by centering the news as a boundary object between journalists, programmers, designers and other cultural actors who are now part of the news production process. Furthermore, taking into account that, according to the sociology of computational journalism, its practices depend on various social institutions and technological artifacts that lie outside its field, we seek to determine which elements of the Spanish transparency law have affected the practice of DJ and how this professional sector has responded to these effects.

To achieve these objectives, 14 semi-structured in-depth interviews were conducted between 2015 and 2017 with Spanish data journalists (8), from the USA (1) and Finland (1); with academic experts from Spain (1) and Finland (2) in PJ, DJ and transparency; and with one expert in transparency laws and access to public information in Spain, Europe and Latin America. In addition, a content analysis — based on _agenda setting_ — was conducted with a sample of 231 units of analysis, which describes the way in which some Spanish media with data desks (_Eldiario.es_, _El Mundo_ and _Elconfidencial.com_) positioned transparency as a relevant issue for public opinion between June and December 2015, as opposed to a media outlet without a data desk (_El País_). The dates of this sample were ideal for measuring how media with data desks influenced the political agenda through public opinion, coinciding with the emergence of new political forces in mid-2015 and the general elections at the end of that year. During this period, the first anniversary of the functioning of the transparency portal also falls, representing months of experience by data journalists in requests for public information through it.

In this way, while through the in-depth interviews we were able to demonstrate that _big data_ is a differentiating element of DJ because it is a sociocultural context in which the open data philosophy, free software, collaborative and team work form part of its identity, both through the interviews and the content analysis we managed to prove the following: 1— _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_ has promoted data journalism in Spain despite its inadequacy and non-compliance; 2— the inadequacy of the law has stalled DJ in a still incipient state; and 3— non-compliance with the law is due to a lack of a culture of transparency in the Spanish public administration. In addition, through the content analysis we also verified that the data journalism sector in Spain has influenced the political agenda by exposing to public opinion the same inadequacies of the law identified in the in-depth interviews.

KEY WORDS: data journalism, _big data_, transparency, open data, visualization, precision journalism, computer assisted reporting.

**INTRODUCTION**


The use of social science methods and computational tools for the analysis of databases in journalism has received various definitions since Philip Meyer popularized precision journalism (PJ) in the 1970s. Terms such as computer assisted reporting (CAR) accompanied PJ throughout the rest of the twentieth century until the emergence, at the end of that century and the beginning of the current one, of other terms more closely related to the internet, such as digital journalism and journalism 2.0, among others. The convergence in newsrooms in recent times and the new journalistic culture as a consequence of the use and processing of large quantities of data on the network (Barbosa & Torres, 2013) over the last decade brought with it a term that seemed to encompass all of this: data journalism (DJ) or database-driven journalism (DDJ) [1]. However, it was not until the Wikileaks leaks in 2010 that DJ differentiated itself substantially from other terms related to the processing of networked data in journalism.

In this sense, DJ incorporated elements of the transparency culture of the _big data_ era — a technological and sociocultural environment that, in addition to entailing dizzying technological advances and the emergence of numerous disruptive technologies, implies the influence of the _open data_ movement across various fields of knowledge, civil society and the administrative structure of contemporary nation-states.

1 DDJ stands for _Data Driven Journalism_ — data-driven journalism, referred to by many as database journalism or data journalism.


**_Big data_ and journalism**


Today, _big data_ is not conceived as "merely a technological transition towards a data deluge" (Lewis & Westlund, 2015: 448), in that it is not considered a unitary phenomenon (Boellstorff, 2013). Boyd and Crawford (2012:663) define it as a cultural, technological and academic phenomenon that lies in the interaction of technology, analysis and mythology. In the journalistic sphere, _big data_ needs to be open (Elías, 2015) and is associated with transparency, accountability, accessibility, public use, free access and reusability. According to journalism and communication scholars Seth Lewis and Oscar Westlund, _big data_ implies "a complex amalgam of digital abundance, emerging analytical techniques, mythology about data-driven insights, and a growing critique about the totality of the consequences of big data practices for democracy and society" (Lewis & Westlund, 2015:447).


**Data journalism and transparency**


In the various contemporary societies organized as nation-states with a right of access to public information, transparency tends to be linked to the need of these societies to know the rules, norms and procedures of the various spheres of the State, as well as to the obligation of their governments to account to citizens for their actions. This is how journalism and transparency have always been linked in some way through one of the most important functions this profession performs in democratic societies: accountability.

The first country in the world to recognize and regulate with a specific law the fundamental right of access to public information was Sweden, which in 1766 legalized public access to documents of Parliament and the Government in what was called the age of liberty, so named by the Swedish historian Jonas Nordin because for two generations Sweden was governed by Parliament (Altares, 2016). The first law of this type in Spain did not come until 1992, with _Ley 30/1992, de 26 de noviembre de Régimen Jurídico de las Administraciones Públicas y del Procedimiento Administrativo Común_ (BOE, 1992), which according to the transparency NGO Access Info Europe was a provision that did not establish access to all information held by public entities, but rather a much more limited access that did not meet internationally established minimum guarantees (Access Info Europe, 2010). In this sense and taking the legal definition of Transparency as the duty of all public authorities to account for their actions and to explain their conduct in a reasoned and reasonable manner (Arenilla & Redondo, 2011), one cannot speak of transparency laws in the European Union until _Reglamento (CE) nº 1049/2001 del Parlamento Europeo y del Consejo, de 30 de mayo de 2001, relativo al acceso del público a los documentos del Parlamento Europeo, del Consejo y de la Comisión_ (DOCE, 2001).

From this regulation and the national transparency laws derived from it in the EU context, it can be asserted that access to information and its dissemination are conditioned by the means used to carry them out, where technological progress has modified legal doctrine (Lasagabaster, 2010). It is a perspective that responds to visions and practices characteristic of a culture where relationships of trust are established between those who request or demand transparency and those who grant it — characteristics of a social practice enhanced by new communication technologies and guided by sincerity and accessibility to all related information. It is a culture of transparency specific to the network society that Himanen (2001) associates with the hacker work ethic, by affirming that money ceases to be an incentive and benefit is measured in goals such as the social value generated by the work undertaken, its transparency and free access to the created work. In this sense, the collectives linked to digital and transparency activism have helped transform the way a part of journalism is understood by incorporating many of the elements characteristic of the culture of transparency and the _big data_ era into their practices. The result is what is known today as data journalism.

This doctoral thesis takes this environment into account and aims to discover whether in the professional practice of data journalists the culture of _open data_ — of open data and transparency — is reflected, as well as to learn the opinions of leading experts. This would allow us to differentiate data journalism from other forms of journalism that work with database analysis to tell journalistic stories. Likewise, bearing in mind that DJ contains a culture of transparency characteristic of hacker activists or _hacktivists_ with a hacker work ethic (Himanen, 2001) that was not evident in other forms of journalism, we will study the relationship between _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_ (BOE, 2013) [2] and the emergence and subsequent development of data journalism in Spain.

**CHAPTER I**


**JUSTIFICATION AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK**


One of the most significant moments of political-civic mobilization in Spain in recent times was the 15M movement, or the movement of the indignant of 15 May 2011, which not only led to citizen assemblies in various Spanish neighborhoods but also promoted the subsequent global mobilization of 15 October 2011, or 15-O, coinciding with the Occupy Wall Street movement. Following this global mobilization, the activists who formed part of the encampments and assemblies began to create thematic collectives, among which those dedicated to transparency stood out, such as OpenKratio, Qué Hacen Los Diputados, Sueldos Públicos, Filtrala.org and the Fundación Ciudadana CIVIO. All these collectives were linked in one way or another to the emergence of data journalism in Spain, by incorporating elements of the culture of transparency and techniques from the _big data_ era into their activities.

In this context, the data journalism working group at Medialab Prado in Madrid was born, where one of CIVIO's founders, David Cabo, together with one of the most internationally visible Spanish data journalists and a member of the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ), Mar Cabra, teamed up with several figures from the world of transparency activism and current data journalism in Spain. In this sense, the current director of the Transparency Project of the Madrid City Council and former member of one of the world's most prestigious transparency NGOs (Access Info Europe), Victoria Anderica, along with the now-renowned Spanish infographics journalist and data journalist of the _Miami Herald_, Alberto Cairo, formed part of this team.

Members of this group were already part of activist movements for free digital culture and citizen empowerment — such as Xnet-X.Net — linked in turn to investigative journalism groups such as the ICIJ. From then on, the term data journalism began to be heard increasingly in Spain and figures such as Mar Cabra gained growing notoriety both in the journalistic field and in digital activism. DJ and transparency reference projects in Spain such as Tu Derecho A Saber (Access Info Europe) and España en llamas (CIVIO) launched in 2012 as part of the vanguard of Spanish data journalism that began in 2011, with many of the aforementioned figures involved in these projects and paving the way for the inauguration in 2013 at Medialab Prado of the first _Taller De Periodismo De Datos: Convirtiendo Los Datos En Historias_ (Medialab Prado, 2013), a space in which journalists who are now part of various data units in different media outlets participated.

This is how, from its very beginnings, Spanish data journalism was linked to digital activism and transparency, with 2013 being a crucial year for its subsequent development. Not only was it the year when the first data journalism teams appeared in newsrooms (_Eldiario.es_ and _Elconfidencial.com_), but it was also the year when the Transparency Law was published, there being thus an evident parallel between the emergence of Spanish DJ in media outlets and the law.


**I.1. Transparency Law and data journalism in Spain**


The first data journalism teams in Spanish newsrooms — such as the Lab of _Eldiario.es_ and the Data Unit of _Elconfidencial.com_ — emerge practically in parallel with the publication of the Transparency Law on 10 December 2013. The law came into force in stages: at the national level on 10 December 2014 and in local entities on 10 December 2015. This means that until December 2014 it was not possible to submit requests for public information through the transparency portal, which is a relevant fact when one considers that from the first newsroom teams in 2013 and 2014, there was an explosion in this journalistic discipline throughout 2015, just when public information requests could already be made.

The gradual opening towards greater transparency in Spain coincided with the gradual popularization of this journalistic discipline in the media, with the appearance of data units in 2013 — _Eldiario.es_ and _Elconfidencial.com_ —, the Media Lab of _Vocento_ in December 2014, El Mundo Data of _El Mundo_ in January 2015, and the data journalism team of _La Sexta_ in March 2015 (Gaibar, 2015). At first glance it might seem that the Transparency Law has promoted DJ in Spain, though if we take into account that from its inception this law has received numerous criticisms — such as the failure to include it as a fundamental right, the exclusion of access to many types of information and the double administrative silence when refusing a request for public information — we might be faced with a different type of scenario if we consider that the criticisms to this day are not only the same but are in fact compounded by non-compliance with the deadlines for responding to a request, the electronic national ID requirement for requesting information through the transparency portal, and the non-implementation of the law in some autonomous communities and local bodies.

Criticism of the law began before its publication in December 2013, from journalism and academia (Ventura, 2013; Magallón, 2013), as well as from transparency activists in various media (Anderica, 2013b; Anderica & Cabo, 2013). That year, Jesús Lizcano Alvarez, president of Transparencia Internacional España and professor at the Universidad Autónoma de Madrid (1991–present), stated that, despite the law's important role in social progress, "it will not at all be the best possible law, since it contains a series of deficiencies that have not been corrected in its final parliamentary processing" (Lizcano, 2013). Two years later, Transparencia Internacional España published its report _Recomendaciones y consideraciones de Transparencia Internacional España al Reglamento de desarrollo de la_ Ley de Transparencia, in which, in addition to highlighting the same deficiencies that the law had received from the beginning, it criticized the role and dependence of the Consejo de Transparencia y Buen Gobierno, the functioning of the transparency units and the obstacles to the right of access to public information, among which it highlighted "the absence of a regime of infractions and sanctions for non-compliance" and "the regime of negative administrative silence" (Transparency International España, 2015:4). This made it clear that two years after the law's approval it not only continued to present the same problems that had been criticized before its publication, but that there were also other conditions limiting its correct compliance that came to light following its entry into force.

Three clear examples of this situation were projected in different media outlets between 2013 and 2018. On 11 September 2013, Victoria Anderica stated in an article titled "Una ley de transparencia que no nos saca de la opacidad" that the law continued to present the same problems from the outset of its draft: "it does not recognize the right of access to information as a fundamental right, it excludes many types of information (…) it establishes a double negative administrative silence (…) and the Transparency Council is not independent" (Anderica, 2013a). On 6 January 2016, in an article titled "Una transparencia de muy difícil acceso", Elena Sevillano reported that the "Defensor del Pueblo criticizes the 'excessively complex' and 'dissuasive' system for requesting information from the Government" (Sevillano, 2016), referring to the electronic national ID and other elements that made it difficult to request public information through the transparency portal. Recently, on 5 September 2018, José García Badía published an article titled "El Defensor del Pueblo critica que la Ley de Transparencia omita a los ayuntamientos", in which the lack of capacity of the Consejo de Transparencia de Murcia to mediate before local administrations was criticized: "the Defensor del Pueblo has initiated proceedings so that the Ley de Transparencia y Participación Ciudadana de la Región grants powers to the Consejo de la Transparencia de Murcia with respect to the 45 municipalities" (García, 2018).

All these criticisms, continued over time, have come about as DJ pieces and data units in newsrooms have increased considerably between 2013 and 2018, to the point that in several media with data units it is practically impossible to quantify the total of DJ pieces: in _Elconfidencial.com_ there are some 196 DJ pieces between 22 September 2015 and 14 September 2018; in _Eldiario.es_ there are 195 pieces on which the data journalist and infographics journalist Belén Picazo worked between September 2013 and September 2018; El Mundo Data has more than 1,700 tweets related to its DJ pieces between January 2015 and early 2019; and CIVIO has been working on seven macro DJ projects for several years, with hundreds of articles related to the following areas: power, justice, contracting, the public sector, healthcare, transparency and the environment.

It thus became increasingly clear that the relationship between the Transparency Law and data journalism should have been studied from a perspective that did not imply causality but correlation, in which the new DJ and its link to transparency activism would be differentiated from earlier forms of journalism using data that operated without open data (_open data_) and transparency laws as we know them today. To this end it became necessary to first delve conceptually into the term data journalism in relation to PJ and CAR before studying the relationship between Spanish DJ and the Transparency Law.


**I.2. Data journalism in the academic field**


Despite the boom in data journalism, the academic field had until recently been divided between different approaches that positioned it, on the one hand, as a discipline with several decades of existence (Tejedor & Dader, 2011; Egawhary & O'Murchu, 2013) and, on the other, as something new (Ferreras, 2013; Chaparro-Domínguez, 2013; Antón, 2013; López & Martínez, 2014). This conceptual diversity — which has at times been contradictory — has occurred, in our view, because DJ as a term already existed alongside _Data-Driven Journalism_ within what has been known as PJ and CAR for several decades. In this sense, data journalism was considered to be the quantitative drift of journalism that broke into the United States in the 1970s — PJ — and which had its origins in Philip Meyer's use of computers in his investigations as a journalist for the Miami Herald in 1959. However, as has already been noted, the sociocultural and technological context in which data journalism has developed as we understand it today is very different from that of the other models mentioned. Our research bears on this point by clarifying the definition and characterization of this form of journalism in comparison to similar ones that precede it and coexist with it, for which purpose fieldwork was carried out based on in-depth interviews with an international focus. All this considering that, given the credibility crisis affecting media and the journalistic profession, the presence of journalists in newsrooms with a mindset close to the fundamentals of _open data_ — the hacker ethic, free software and collaborative work — could contribute to improving the quality of journalism.

This line of thinking already emerged with precision journalism when it employed social science techniques, though now it is also supported by the mythologies of _big data_. Furthermore, the fact that the experience of working in multidisciplinary teams in DJ is positively valued is a good indicator that journalists could work closely with more technical professions in media newsrooms in the future. For these reasons, the cultural aspects reflected in the professional practice of Spanish data journalists in our research will be analyzed from a global perspective, with the vision of professionals and experts from the USA, the United Kingdom, Spain and Finland — countries where the _open data_ movement, through organizations such as Access Info Europe and the Open Knowledge Foundation, has actively participated in the opening up of public data and in the training of data journalists.

For this we will rely on the fact that in PJ and CAR the journalist's efforts were focused on seeking and collecting information in a world where information was scarce, while in data journalism the efforts are concentrated on data processing rather than on the search and collection of information. Furthermore, in DJ one can perceive a culture of transparency characteristic of _hacktivists_ that was not evident in precision journalism and computer assisted reporting, where elements of the philosophy of free software and _open data_ movements underlie. In this sense, the publication of data by journalists could also be considered a transparency exercise, but, moreover, these publications, sometimes leaks, involve subsequent "refinement processes among journalists" (Lesage & Hackett, 2014:41).

All these implications around data journalism as it is understood today stem from an exhaustive and systematic study of the scientific literature on precision journalism, CAR and DJ, set out in greater detail in the conceptual framework of our research (Chapter III). Bearing in mind that our hypotheses and research objectives are divided into two parts — the first related to the differentiation of data journalism from other forms of journalism that work with database analysis to tell journalistic stories, and the second focused on the emergence and subsequent development of data journalism in Spain and its link to the Transparency Law — we will now present two theoretical frameworks with their respective hypotheses, objectives and related research questions.


**I.3. Theoretical framework around _big data_ as a differentiating element of data journalism**


Science, Technology and Society (STS) studies have developed concepts to explain how diverse communities come together and exchange knowledge and resources and coordinate productively while maintaining their distinctive social and cultural identities (Galison's, 1997; Star and Griesemer, 1989). According to Stark (2009) in his work _The Sense of Dissonance_, innovation does not arise from homogeneous cooperation but from assemblages of heterogeneous and even discordant individuals and ideas. American scholars Seth Lewis and Nikki Usher (2016) use this conceptual basis to extrapolate to Computational Journalism what Galison's (1997) called trading zones and boundary objects in the study of cooperation between different kinds of physicists. According to them, boundary zones serve perfectly to describe the productive possibilities at the intersection of and even disagreements between heterogeneous actors:

"Groups with different identities, expertises and occupational backgrounds can come together around particular objects to communicate and collaborate, without losing any alignment to their own field and their particular frame of reference toward those objects. In that way, boundary objects are flexible enough to facilitate a variety of meanings across knowledge" (Lewis & Usher, 2016:5).

Taking news as a boundary object, news can mean certain things for programmers and developers, who have their own ideological and occupational foundations, while meaning something else for journalists and something different again for designers and infographics specialists. In this research we propose to study DJ by focusing on the news as a boundary object between programmers, designers and infographics specialists, journalists and other actors who form part of the news production process. This difference is fundamental when it comes to separating it from precision journalism and computer assisted reporting: while in PJ there was a trading zone between social scientists and journalists who had news as a boundary object, in CAR computational thinking was added with the mass adoption of personal computers in newsrooms and universities, adding a new actor — the programmer — in the conception of 'news' as a boundary object among these three cultural actors.

In the case of DJ we must incorporate how _big data_ is linked to representations of digital journalism such as infographics, interactive data visualizations and custom probabilistic models (Howard 2014; Smit, Yael & Buijs, 2014), since these products "contain epistemological assumptions about how audiences might acquire knowledge, since users are encouraged to play with the data to understand a particular and personalized version of the news narrative" (Lewis & Westlund, 2015:453).

Similarly, we will take into account that where DJ is practiced, efforts have been made to make datasets more accessible, transparent and exploratory in keeping with the ethos of free software and open government and _open data_ activism (Daniel, 2010; Bounegru, Chambers & Gray., 2012; Egawhary & O'Murchu, 2013; Rodríguez-Brito & García-Chico 2013; Parasie & Dagiral 2013; Lewis & Usher 2013; Lesage & Hackett 2014; Quesada & Ribas, 2016; La-Rosa & Sandoval-Martín, 2016a, 2016b and 2018; Martínez, 2016; Subires, 2016; Varona, Plaza, Pulido & Camarero, 2016). Therefore, we must incorporate the strengthening of the culture of transparency as a fundamental aspect of data journalism and as a differentiating element from PJ and CAR, insofar as the _big data_ era creates ideal conditions for other actors related to the hacker work culture (Himanen, 2001) — such as _hacktivists_ with various professional backgrounds — to join as new cultural actors.

All this despite the fact that, in relation to transparency, the use of databases in news production has already been linked to practices of collecting and structuring data that political observatories set in motion from the 1970s onwards (Schudson, 2010). Bearing in mind that Manovich (2001) already asserted that databases are to the digital era what narrative was to the modern age of the novel and cinema, Schudson linked the advancement of government monitors with "the new availability of databases for public interest research: together, these developments represent just one of the characteristics of the future of news" (Schudson, 2010:107). This future of news, nearly a decade old now, is a reality today in various newsrooms around the world, where data units use databases published by governments or created from requests for public information, as well as large datasets leaked by interested parties, activists or concerned citizens.

In this sense we have been inspired by work such as that conducted in Sweden on DJ in that country (Appelgren & Nygren, 2014), as well as a study on the future of the journalistic profession due to the great influence of _hacktivism_ on data journalism, in discourses and practices of various actors — journalists, editors, computer scientists and activists — in the French-speaking part of Belgium (De Maeyer et al., 2015). Approaching the subject from this sociological perspective allows for new questions to be raised around the transformation of journalistic values and practices, where the inclusion of these actors — programmers, designers and hacktivists — and the new identities arising from their collaborative work have implied a new conception of what data journalism is, differentiating it from other similar forms of journalism. In this way we propose to fill a gap in the research conducted to date on data journalism by incorporating the sociocultural environment in which it develops.


**I.3.1. Hypotheses, objectives and subordinate research questions**


Our first objective is to detect — empirically through in-depth interviews with professionals and experts — the existence of the interconnections described in the theoretical framework between data journalism as we know it today and the environment in which it develops. This environment encompasses the rise of other phenomena associated with _big data_ that go beyond the enormous volume of data available with current technologies and the ease of managing them, such as _open data_ (and the culture of transparency linked to the philosophy of open data), free software and collaborative work. These technological, sociological and cultural phenomena characteristic of the _big data_ era, reflected in DJ practices, can be considered as differentiating factors with respect to earlier forms of journalism that worked with databases — PJ and CAR — thus contributing to the characterization of DJ.

The second objective focuses on describing the profile of the professionals who carry out their work in DJ and detecting whether they work in multidisciplinary teams in the production of journalistic stories. Starting from the premise that the news is a boundary object between programmers, journalists and designers, we want to detect the possible consequences of collective work in a profession that until recently was basically solitary (Heinrich, 2011), above all in the field of investigative journalism. Among these repercussions, we are interested in discovering whether the journalists who integrate into these teams have incorporated routines characteristic of the culture surrounding _open data_, as well as ethical questions related to transparency, collaborative work, data visualization and the possibility of data reuse by other journalists. To achieve these objectives we have put forward the following research questions:

  - Research question 1: Is _big data_, from a technological and sociocultural standpoint, a differentiating element of DJ with respect to earlier forms of journalism that worked with databases?

  - Research question 2: What technological and sociocultural differences exist between DJ, PJ and CAR?

  - Research question 3: Does DJ contain elements of the mythologies that accompany the sociocultural context of _big data_?

  - Research question 4: Is the new philosophy of open data and free software in DJ the product of the incorporation of other professions characteristic of the digital era — such as programmers, developers, infographics specialists and designers — into the news production process?

These questions are designed to test the following main hypothesis, which we will call Main Hypothesis I (H1), and two subordinate hypotheses (H1A and H1B):

  - H1: _big data_ is a differentiating element of data journalism compared to other forms of journalism that have traditionally worked with data analysis and databases to tell journalistic stories — Precision Journalism (PJ) and Computer Assisted Reporting (CAR).

  - H1A: In the professional practice of data journalists the culture of _open data_ and transparency characteristic of the _big data_ era is reflected.

  - H1B: The presence of journalists with a mindset close to the fundamentals of _open data_ — the hacker ethic, free software and collaborative work — has caused one of the mythologies characteristic of the _big data_ era, namely the objectivity and veracity of data, to prevail in the work of data journalism teams.
**I.4. Theoretical framework around the influence of the Transparency Law on Spanish data journalism**


Taking into account that, after comparing the gestation and development of _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_ (BOE, 2013) and the emergence and subsequent development of data journalism in Spain, a temporal correlation has been observed between both processes, we have set out to study the relationship between the culture of transparency and Spanish DJ by integrating _big data_ and _open data_ as part of a definition of DJ that we will set out in greater depth in the conceptual framework (Chapter III) of our research. To this end we propose to investigate the links between the practice of DJ in Spain and the Transparency Law based on what Anderson called "the sociology of computational journalism: politics and public policies", which studies "the decision-making policies that allow computational journalism to be delayed or expanded" (Anderson 2012:1011).

This political approach to the sociology of the computational in journalism is grounded in the premise that computational practices within it depend on a number of social institutions and technological artifacts that lie outside its field, and therefore urges researchers to pay special attention to the ways in which public policies and struggles for data and open-government transparency affect journalistic production. According to Anderson, this approach should not be made at a macro or state level, but rather at a bureaucratic level and that of political initiatives that can influence forms of computational and data journalism. For this reason we have chosen three very specific elements of public policies related to the culture of transparency and the practice of DJ in Spain: the Transparency Law in force, the government transparency portal and the line separating the transparency of a public official from their privacy.

In the same way, we have also wanted to invert Anderson's approach of how politics and public policies affect the development of computational journalism, by also wishing to find out in what way the culture of transparency related to the practice of DJ is influencing or seeks to influence the politics and public policies related to its development. Approaching the object of study in this way will allow us to obtain a more complete picture of how DJ has been influenced by the Transparency Law passed in Spain in 2013. This will result in the application of a combined research methodology.


**I.4.1. Hypotheses, objectives and subordinate research questions**


The first main objective of this part of our research consists of determining how the Transparency Law and the Spanish government's transparency portal have affected data journalism. To this end we have set ourselves as a first subordinate objective to verify the correlation between the creation of DJ teams in Spain and the creation and entry into force of the Transparency Law. A second subordinate objective will be to establish the state of DJ in Spain and its relationship with the correct exercise of the Transparency Law, in which the line separating the transparency of a public official from their privacy will form an important part of the analysis. A final objective will be to analyze how this journalistic sector has responded, in its public-facing discourse, to the way in which the Transparency Law, the government transparency portal and the line separating the transparency of a public official from their privacy help or constrain the development of DJ. **RESEARCH METHODOLOGY**


In order to achieve the objectives of our study we have applied a mixed research methodology, combining the use of in-depth interviews with content analysis. In this chapter we will explain both methodologies and their application in our study, which is why it will be divided into two parts. The first part will be dedicated to the methodological process by which we were guided to conduct, code and analyze our in-depth interviews, while the second will delve into the theoretical basis, components and methodology of our content analysis.


**II.1. Methodology of in-depth interviews**


The in-depth interview is a methodology used when we want to investigate deep feelings, motivations for behaviors and lived experiences. It draws on contact with protagonists, scholars and experts on the subject of study to access these feelings, motivations and experiences, from which we will extract data relevant to our research. The approach to this type of interview used in our research is based on the interactive-relational approach, which has succeeded in overcoming the tension between conducting an empirically focused interview and the relational interview, resolving the differences between the empiricist criterion prevalent in twentieth-century social sciences and the hermeneutics and postmodern construction of meaning in the social sciences that emerged strongly in the second half of that century. Its development implied overcoming the methodological biases observed in the empiricist criterion:

"Formal and traditional interview approaches often position a distance between the interviewer and their interviewees, predisposing the interviewer to evaluate the interviewee: to generalize, embellish, exaggerate, stereotype and simplify. As a result, these approaches do not provide a complete and concise picture of the participant's contribution, their motives, developments, strengths and weaknesses" (Chirban, 1996:xii).

Taking these possible biases into account, some experts in qualitative research methods have emphasized in recent decades that there is no need to sacrifice the professional interview when the researcher relates to the interviewee, since rather than detracting from the research it adds to it from an epistemological standpoint:

"High-refinement interview technologies rationalize, standardize and sanitize the process, but, despite their methodological sophistication, they persistently ignore the most fundamental epistemological question: Where does this knowledge come from and how is it derived?" (Holstein & Gubrium, 1995)

For this reason the interactive-relational or I:R approach "provides a plan for tackling the goals as well as a way of maintaining a constructive relational dynamic" (Chirban, 1996:xiii). This is achieved as long as the interviewer maintains the following components: "(a) self-awareness, (b) authenticity, (c) attunement, (d) positioning in interaction, (e) participation in relational dynamics and (f) interaction of their person in the interview process" (Chirban, 1996:xv).

All these components speak to the need to prepare the interview in order to establish a clear positioning that does not prejudice the research objectives when we begin to interact with the interviewee. In this sense, the preparation of a questionnaire of questions related to our object of study is the best way to maintain the components mentioned by Chirban (1996), together with a certain flexibility in the formulation of questionnaire questions once interaction with the interviewee begins. This flexibility is provided by the standard non-programmed interview, which forms part of a classification of interview type "generally accepted in the academic sphere" (Valles, 2001:24): (1) Standard programmed interview, (2) Standard non-programmed interview and (3) Non-standard interview (Richardson, Snell & Klein, 1965).

The standard non-programmed interview, according to the I:R approach, draws on the mutual understanding between interviewee and interviewer to overcome taboos that could obstruct the collection of data through the in-depth interview: "If there is mutual understanding between interviewee and interviewer, it is easier to address certain questions that could be a taboo subject when approached with other methodologies" (Wimmer & Dominick, 2010:139). Our study will be based on the stages of an interview-based investigation proposed by the professor of educational psychology, director of the Qualitative Research Center at the University of Aarhus in Denmark and professor at the Saybrook Institute in San Francisco, Steinar Kvale:

"(1) Thematizing: formulating the purpose of an investigation and describing the concept of the topic to be investigated before beginning the interview. The _why_ and _what_ of the research must be clear before the _how_ question — method;

(2) Designing: planning the design of the study taking into consideration the seven stages before beginning the interview: carrying out the study with a view to obtaining the intended knowledge and taking into account the moral implications of the study;

(3) Interviewing: based on a guide with a reflective approach to the knowledge observed and the interpersonal relationship of the interview situation;

(4) Transcribing: preparing the material for analysis;

(5) Analyzing: based on the purpose and topic of the research, as well as the interview material, which methods of analysis are appropriate;

(6) Verifying: how consistent are the results and whether the interview investigates what it was intended to investigate;

(7) Reporting: communicating the findings of the study and the methods applied in a way that survives scientific scrutiny, considers the ethical aspects of the research and results in a readable product" (Kvale, 1996:88)

Although the purpose of our study was already explained in the preceding chapter, in order to fulfill the first stage of the interview-based investigation proposed by Kvale (1996) in this chapter, we will enumerate below the research objectives we intend to achieve with this methodological tool:

1— Detect the existence of interconnections between the various actors who form part of data journalism teams and the environment in which it develops.

2— Describe the profile of the professionals who carry out their work in DJ and detect whether they work in multidisciplinary teams in the production of journalistic stories.

3— Detect technological, sociological and cultural phenomena characteristic of the _big data_ era reflected in DJ practices.

4— Determine how Law 19/2013, the Spanish government's transparency portal and the separation of a public official's private sphere from the transparency related to their office have affected the practice of DJ.


**II.1.1. Design of the standard non-programmed interview**


After clarifying the thematization we will explain what the design of our measurement instrument consists of. This will be done clearly and concisely to enable other researchers to reproduce the interview-based research method applied step by step, which will allow results to be replicated in similar studies. Furthermore, this design — aimed at obtaining intended knowledge as presented in the interview thematization — has respected all interviewees, always bearing in mind their willingness to answer our questions while knowing at all times the objectives of our study. With all of this, the moral implications of our study have been made clear both to the reader and to the interviewees who form part of the sample.

Taking into account the seven stages put forward by Kvale (1996), we will develop below the interview questionnaire, in whose justification it will be possible to observe the third stage, the interview: "based on a guide with a reflective approach to the knowledge observed and the interpersonal relationship of the interview situation" (Kvale, 1996:88). **II.1.3. Transcription and analysis of the interviews**

Although in the seven stages of interview research proposed by Kvale (1996) transcription and analysis are presented separately as stages four and five respectively, according to this author there are six steps that constitute the analysis of the interview, distinguishing the analysis of stage five as step 4.3:

"(1) The subject describes their lived world; (2) The subject discovers for themselves new relationships and sees new meanings in what they do and experience; (3) The interviewer condenses and interprets the meaning of what the subject describes and sends the meaning back; (4) the transcribed interview is interpreted by the interviewer: (4.1) structuring the long and complex material for analysis, (4.2) making the material amenable to analysis by eliminating superfluous material such as repetitions and digressions, as well as distinguishing between the essential and non-essential in accordance with the purpose of the study, (4.3) the analysis itself, developing the meaning of the interviews and bringing the subject's own understanding to light; (5) Re-interviewing, which consists of giving the interpretations to the subjects so that they may comment and make self-corrections, allowing them to make their own statements; and (6) a possible sixth step: including actions arising from the interviewee's new introspections" (Kvale, 1996:189-190).

According to Kvale the first three steps usually take place during the interview, and in our case they even led us to change some of the designed questions during the interview itself, taking advantage of the I:R approach to probe unexpected relationships and meanings raised by the interviewees during the interview. From the outset we knew this might happen, which is why the standard non-programmed interview was chosen. The fourth analytical step and its sub-points form part of this sub-section, while steps five and six proposed by Kvale will not form part of this research because we have incorporated other theoretical approaches in the transcription and analysis that render them unnecessary.

**II.1.3.1. Transcription of the interviews**

The debate around the interpretation introduced by the hermeneutic tradition in the social sciences brought with it debates about the uses of qualitative methods, among which the transcription of interviews has been an important part. Noting that the different rhetorical forms of oral and written language are frequently overlooked during the transcription of interviews in the social sciences, Kvale (1996) states that "sociolinguistics and ethnomethodology have brought to the center of the debate the differences between oral and written languages" (Kvale, 1996:166). In this regard, according to one of the most recognized sociolinguistic historical studies, _Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of the Word_, the thought and expression of a primary oral culture is close to the human lifeworld, situational, empathetic and participatory, additive, aggregative, agonal, and redundant; while a written culture is characterized by being analytical, abstract, with objectively distanced forms of thought and expression (Ong, 1982). In his work, Walter Ong (1982) refers to the Homeric culture of pre-alphabetic Greece as a primary oral culture, while by written culture he refers to the Ancient or classical Greece of Plato, already fully alphabetized.

Following this line of thought, the author considered one of the founding fathers of the Toronto School of communication, Eric Havelock, argued that the widespread introduction of the alphabet in ancient Greece, and the preservation of Greek knowledge and identity through writing, made Homeric education through mimesis unnecessary, since Plato introduced the critical, rational thinker — the philosopher — onto the scene (Havelock, 1963). These are differences between oral and written culture that also arise between oral and written language from the perspective of interview transcription:

"A transcription is a transgression, a transformation from one form of narrative — oral discourse — to another — written discourse. To transcribe means to transform, to change from one form to another. Attempting a literal transcription produces hybrids, artificial constructions that are adequate neither to the living oral conversation nor to the formal style of written texts. Transcriptions are translations from one language to another; what holds true in the hermeneutic tradition of translators also applies to transcribers: traduire treaittori — translators traitors" (Kvale, 1996:166).

Although from this statement it would seem evident that the literal transcription of an interview is inadvisable, Kvale notes that this could be modified depending on the research: "if the transcriptions are made to serve as material for psychological-sociolinguistic analysis, they should be as detailed as possible, that is, literal" (Kvale, 1996:171). However, this would entail specific techniques that are not relevant here, since our study is neither psychological nor sociolinguistic in nature. For this case, he recommends the following:

"If they are made to give a general impression of the subjects' points of view, paraphrasing and condensing the statements may be the order of the day. Moreover, if the analysis is prepared in a way that categorizes or condenses the overall meaning of what has been said, a certain amount of editing in the transcription is desirable" (Kvale, 1996:170-171).

Apart from this, there are ethical implications involved in the transcription of interviews. Beyond matters of subject confidentiality and the proper structuring of the study, the ethical obligation to present the interview transcription to the interviewees could result in refusals to allow the use of what subjects said in the interview, as well as problems of stigmatization that could be detrimental to the veracity of the research:

"Some subjects may experience shock as a result of reading their own interviews. The literal transcription of oral language can appear as a discourse so incoherent and confused as to suggest a low level of mental functioning. Subjects may feel offended and refuse any future cooperation and the use of what they have said… Bear in mind that the publication of literal transcriptions of incoherent and repetitive interviews can lead to the stigmatization of specific individuals or groups of people" (Kvale, 1996:172-173)

For all these reasons, the interview transcriptions in our study will not be literal: they will involve a degree of condensation and editing that will allow: (1) easy exposition of the interviewees' points of view, (2) the future categorization of the interviews, and (3) the avoidance of future refusals in view of future research.

**II.1.3.2. Interview analysis: meaning condensation**

The interview interpreted by the interviewer through the structuring of material to make it amenable to analysis will result in our study in a transcription in accordance with what the representative of the Duquesne School and orthodox practitioner of the phenomenological research method, Amedeo Giorgi, called the natural meaning unit (Giorgi, 1985). In our analysis we will combine Giorgi's natural meaning unit with the meaning condensation explained by Steinar Kvale:

"The form of the results in words will be primarily meaning condensation, interpretation and narrative analysis, possibly with some narrative structuring figures… Meaning condensation implies an abridgement of the meanings expressed by the interviewees into shorter formulations. Long statements are compressed into briefer ones in which the main sense of what has been said is paraphrased in a few words. Hence meaning condensation entails a reduction of a lengthy interview text into a shorter and more succinct formulation" (Kvale, 1996:192).

The way in which Giorgi achieves this meaning condensation from the natural meaning unit is perfectly exemplified in his article "An application of phenomenological method in Psychology" (Giorgi, 1975), where this author presents in a table how this process is carried out:

|NATURAL UNIT|CENTRAL THEME|
|---|---|
|1. The first thing that comes to mind is what I learned about interior decoration from Myrtis. She was talking to me about the way you see things. Her way of seeing different rooms has been altered. She told me that when you enter a room you generally don't notice how many horizontal and vertical lines there are, at least not consciously. And yet, if you took someone who knows what happens in the field of interior decoration, they would intuitively feel that there are the correct number of horizontal and vertical lines.|1. The role of vertical and horizontal lines in interior decoration.|

This table shows only the first extraction of a central theme from a first natural unit of meaning, which is why both are preceded by the number 1. In Giorgi's example the numbering continues until all the natural units of an interview are exhausted. According to Kvale, the construction of this table involved three of a total of five analytical processes:

"The natural meaning units of the subject's responses are shown in a left column and their central themes are presented in the right column. Five steps are involved in this empirical phenomenological analysis: first, the entire interview is read to obtain a sense of the whole. Then, the natural meaning units as expressed by the subjects are determined by the researcher. Third, the central theme that dominates the natural meaning unit is expressed as simply as possible… the fourth step consists of interrogating the meaning units according to the specific purpose of the study… in the fifth step, the essential one, non-redundant themes from the entire interview were linked in a single descriptive paragraph" (Kvale, 1996:194).

Following this process, all our transcriptions are central meaning units that have generated some central theme related to the research objectives set out in this chapter. For this reason, the results of our in-depth interviews are a grouping of the same central themes raised by different interviewees in a specific order that allows us to make generalizations. Likewise, each verbatim quotation referring to the transcription of an interview will constitute a natural meaning unit.

**II.2. Content Analysis Methodology**

After having chosen in-depth interviews for data collection on the political decision-making policies that allow data journalism to be delayed or advanced from the sociology of computational journalism, the information gathered will be complemented with a methodology capable of identifying how Spanish data journalism has sought to influence those policies. In this sense, since the interviews will identify how political decision-making policies have influenced the practice of DJ in Spain through Law 19/2013, the Spanish government's transparency portal, and the separation of a public official's privacy from the transparency related to their office — a methodology that will also allow us to identify elements of a transparency culture specific to DJ — in order to investigate how this culture of transparency is influencing or intends to influence that policy and the public policies related to its development, we will conduct a content analysis.

This methodological tool will allow us to determine whether outlets with data units have attempted to exert that influence on public opinion regarding the politics and public policies of transparency related to Law 19/2013 and the Spanish government's transparency portal, since for its application we will rely on the theoretical frameworks of _agenda-setting_, the _priming_ effect, and the _framing_ effect.

**II.2.1. Theoretical framework of content analysis**

From _agenda-setting_ we have taken the influence that media have in establishing which events, topics, and persons are important, in order to identify through our content analysis the extent to which outlets with data units have sought to position transparency as a relevant issue. We also draw from the _priming_ effect the analysis of the way in which transparency is treated in relation to some aspects of politics at the expense of others, while from the _framing_ effect we have incorporated the way in which the journalist focuses on this theme. Below, we will present a brief explanation of the theoretical elements underlying our content analysis before detailing the application of this methodological tool in our study.

**II.2.1.1. Agenda-setting**

The idea that the audience is shaped by the information it receives was noted by William James (1890) in his work _The Principles of Psychology_ and developed in 1922 by Walter Lippmann in _Public Opinion_, where he compiled various topics about how the objective characteristics of the world often bear little relation to the beliefs people hold about it (Lippmann, 1997). Lippmann put forward for the first time the idea that the media represent forms of public perception through images and mental representations, grounded in the concept of public opinion that would serve as a point of reflection for other authors such as Bernard Cohen (1963:13), who argued that "the press may not be successful much of the time in telling people what to think, but it is stunningly successful in telling its readers what to think about." Thus, the concern to understand the influence the media have on society is relevant, since they generate endless positive and negative connotations at the same time, in which consumers not only learn about a problem, but also about the importance attached to the informational content of a news item and its placement (Cohen, 1963:13-14).

The current theoretical foundations of media agenda-setting in a large number of scientific articles today can be found in authors such as Rogers and Dearing (1988) and McCombs and Shaw (1972). Specifically, agenda-setting theory, in contrast to the minimal effects theory of the media, was the product of research by McCombs and Shaw (1972), who observed that the mass media attempted to establish the relationship between community issues — which voters considered important — and the content of messages appearing in the media — apropos the US presidential election campaign of 1968.

The authors concluded that the media exert a considerable influence on those issues that voters consider most important, and that there is a strong relationship between the media agenda and the public agenda with respect to the main media — television, press, and radio (McCombs & Shaw, 1972). For Rogers and Dearing (1988), the relationship among the three components of the _media agenda process_ — public agenda, political agenda, and media agenda — relates as follows (Figure 1):

political agenda. Source: Rogers & Dearing (1988:557).

As can be seen in the relationship among the different agendas set out by Rogers and Dearing (1988:557), the media agenda influences the public agenda, producing changes that can affect the political agenda. In this sense, beliefs and value judgments are the result of the media agenda, which can even affect public behaviors in society that generate a climate of political action. This research tradition was grounded in arguments made in advance by the _Hutchins Commission_ report (1947) on the homogenization of news around a set of topics addressed by all members of the media, as well as in the early studies on media agenda-setting by Lazarsfeld and Merton (1948).

In the relationship between the public agenda and the political agenda, the public agenda holds an influential power over politics in governmental decisions and in citizens' opinions, while when the political agenda relates to the public, it hints at intentions that the population translates as distrust, combined with undertones of political inefficacy. In this way, the influential power of the public agenda over the political agenda can be read as the influence of the media agenda on politics.

According to an evolution of agenda-setting theory by McCombs and Shaw (1993), there are four phases of research in this field: 1 — the one that would confirm that, contrary to the minimal effects theory, the media do influence audiences; 2 — the one that studies the conditions that limit or increase the framing of the media agenda; 3 — the one that studies effects beyond the mere transfer of topics and investigates whether the media succeed in influencing public opinion (at two levels); 4 — the one that studies how the media's own agenda is configured. López-Escobar and Llamas (1996) tell us that the third phase described by McCombs and Shaw (1993) is divided into two: "the transfer of the agenda of topics is then called 'first dimension' or 'first level' of the effect, and the transfer of aspects 'second dimension' or 'second level'" (López-Escobar, Llamas & Rey, 1996:10).

Our content analysis begins from the confirmation of the first phase: the media do influence audiences and therefore public opinion. From there, we take into account that the media process information at a first level, which says what to think about (topic transfer) and suggests what the public should think based on the coverage they wish to give each news item (López-Escobar, Llamas y Rey, 1996). The second level, which attempts to go beyond thematic cognitions (aspect transfer) and studies how the media suggest to audiences how they should think about the topics raised at the first level (Escobar, Llamas y Rey, 1996), is also taken into account, although we do not intend to measure the effects of the message on the Spanish audience, but rather to see how transparency has been positioned in the agenda of outlets with data units. For this we rely on the _priming_ and _framing_ effects of _agenda-setting_.

**II.2.1.2. The _priming_ effect**

_Priming_ is a technical concept from psychology that was transferred to political communication as part of _agenda-setting_ by Iyengar, Peters & Kinder (1982), and is known today as the _priming_ effect. In essence, it is a cognitive process in which the media prepare the audience on those aspects they have selected. From a social perspective, it is linked to the process by which people perceive the world through mass media and is often called political media primacy. According to Alger (1989), by drawing attention to some aspects of politics at the expense of others, the media can serve to set the terms in which political judgments are constructed. However, it must be borne in mind that this type of effect does not influence all individuals equally, since the selected topics are those in which many subjective elements participate that determine the audience's final perception of that context.

Since our content analysis does not seek to measure effects on audiences but rather to examine how outlets with data units position transparency on the public agenda, the _priming_ effect in our content analysis is reflected in the establishment of topics and sub-topics around transparency, where the relationship of topics and sub-topics in each outlet will yield relevant results regarding which aspects of Spanish politics related to public transparency policies are most prominently positioned in comparison to others. This will provide material suitable for comparison between outlets that is highly relevant to our research.

**II.2.1.3. The _framing_ effect**

Framing theory emerged in the field of psychology through Erving Goffman (1986), who included sociological nuances in his work that are still used today in communication studies. According to Belén Amadeo, most contributions from these studies in Spanish have been designated by their English name, _framing_, due to an eclectic stance that attempts to reconcile the various possible translations, such as _enfoque_ (focus), _encuadre_ (frame), _marco_ (framework), and even _formato_ (format) (Amadeo, 1999). Adapted to the construction of the media message and inherited from the tradition of interpretive sociology and psychology, framing theory broke with objectivist pretensions by introducing the subjective element as a fundamental part of the communicative process — that is, news items are not a reflection of reality but a representation of it. In this sense "frames linked to political cultures or identities bring their own meanings to social reality, in which the journalist constantly moves" (Sábada, 2001:161), which means that when a journalist recounts what happens, they frame reality and contribute their own point of view.

In our content analysis, when a data journalist recounts what happens regarding transparency, they do so framed within a reality different from that of the traditional journalist: within a data unit. This is a perspective very much in keeping with the vision of Gaye Tuchman, who used framing theory to penetrate the media as organizations in order to discover working methods, and for whom the elements that determine the _frames_ of media outlets are their organization and the professional attitudes of the journalists who work in them (Tuchman, 1978). However, the _framing_ effect in our content analysis does not seek to discover the organizational structure and professional attitudes of the data journalists in the analyzed outlets, but rather to detect whether outlets with data units have employed an informational treatment that reflects professional attitudes toward transparency different from those of outlets without data units. This would be reflected in the attempts — and in the manner in which these are carried out — to influence transparency policy and public policies through the creation of a media agenda that influences public opinion.

**II.2.1.4. Quantitative and qualitative perspectives in content analysis**

Content analysis as a research tool has been so widely used that it carries various definitional nuances depending on the field of knowledge in which it is applied, the researcher using it, and above all, the scientific paradigm to which the author belongs. In our case, we will present some of the definitions most accepted by the scientific community in the field of communication that also contain conceptual elements that can be adapted to our research, beginning with definitions ascribed to the rationalist scientific paradigm and moving toward some of the self-styled qualitative methodologies.

Among the former, where the quantitative method predominates in social research (Berelson, 1952; Kerlinger, 1986), we will highlight that of Kerlinger, who defines content analysis as "a method of studying and analyzing communication in a systematic, objective, and quantitative manner, with the purpose of measuring certain variables" (Kerlinger, 1986:447). This definition, based on an earlier one formulated by Berelson (1952:18), forms part of a vision ascribed to the rationalist scientific paradigm that was widely criticized throughout the second half of the twentieth century due to the restrictiveness of the term "quantitative." In this way, similar definitions that excluded the quantitative term began to predominate, such as that of Hostil (1969), who defined content analysis as "a research technique for making inferences by objectively and systematically identifying specified characteristics within a text" (Hostil, 1969:5).

Among the most accepted definitions at present is that of Klaus Krippendorff, who stated that this methodological tool is "a research technique for making replicable and valid inferences from texts (or other meaningful matter) to the contexts of their use" (Krippendorff, 1990:28). According to this author, the intellectual roots of content analysis lie in the conscious use of symbols and voices, especially since writing came into existence, when human beings began to replace the magical use of language with the ancient disciplines of philosophy, rhetoric, and cryptography. However, today it has three distinctive characteristics in scientific research:

"First, content analysis is an 'empirically grounded method', exploratory in process and predictive or inferential in intent… second, contemporary content analysis 'transcends notions of symbols, content, and intentions'… third, contemporary content analysis 'has been forced to create its own methodology', one that enables researchers to plan, execute, communicate, reproduce, and critically evaluate their analyses whatever their particular findings" (Krippendorff, 2004:xvii-xx).

These distinctive characteristics of contemporary content analysis are put forward by Krippendorff after this technique underwent various moments of conceptual transformation, during which various authors attempted to overcome the shortcomings of quantitative analysis for capturing deeper meanings through a qualitative methodology that emphasized the capturing of meanings (Kracauer, 1952; Ritsert, 1972; Mostyn, 1985; Wittkowski, 1984; Altheide, 1996). However, we note that, like Krippendorff, we consider that we can no longer speak of a distinction between quantitative and qualitative content analysis, since "all reading of text is qualitative, even when some characteristics of the text are subsequently converted to numbers" (Krippendorff, 2004:16).

Another point to take into consideration is that, as Jaime Abela (2002) tells us, content analysis
"is on the border of other techniques such as linguistic analysis, documentary analysis, textual analysis, discourse analysis, and semiotic analysis" (Abela, 2002:9), and today all these techniques are combined according to the type of research being conducted. In this sense, the approaches of self-styled qualitative methodologies in content analysis have offered protocol alternatives for exploring texts systematically, where discourse analysis has been one of the most widely used approaches in the study of messages in the field of information sciences. In this regard, Van Dijk (1991) studied how racial minorities appear represented in the press and how racial conflicts are described, while Wonsek (1992) investigated how television news programs manifested a particular ideological vision of the economy.

By focusing on how a particular phenomenon is represented, discourse analysis — or content analysis of discourse according to Abela — carries significant weight in our research, since we study how outlets with data units represent transparency in their attempt to influence public opinion. It is a perspective that uses content analysis to approach the study of messages and "understand their genesis or formation process, obtain precise descriptions of their structure and components, analyze their flow or patterns of exchange, trace their evolution, and infer their impact" (Igartua, 2006:180).

**II.2.2. Components of content analysis**

Many theorists and researchers have outlined the various steps to follow in conducting a content analysis, although at present most are careful not to provide restrictive prescriptions. For this reason, the authors most frequently used as the basis for research with this methodology speak of components, which according to Krippendorff (2004:83) is "merely a convenient way to divide, conceptualize, talk about, and evaluate the design of content analysis step by step." According to Jaime Abela (2002) these components would be the following: "1- determine the object or topic of analysis; 2- determine the coding rules; 3- determine the category system; 4- check the reliability of the coding-categorization system; 5- inferences" (Abela, 2002:11). For Krippendorff (2004) the components of a content analysis are divided into: unitizing, sampling, coding, abductive inference from the context of the phenomenon, and narration of responses to the research questions (Krippendorff, 2004:83). Both partitions have similarities that we will point out below, since it is on this basis that we have designed the content analysis for our research.

According to Abela, determining what the object or topic of analysis is means asking the following questions: "What do we want to investigate? What bibliography or prior knowledge exists? What theory or theoretical framework does it fit into? What text or texts will be used? And what is the unit of analysis to be used?" (Abela, 2002:11). With the exception of the second question, the methodology of our content analysis has addressed the first three questions in previous chapters and sections. The bibliography and prior knowledge will be set out in greater depth in the conceptual framework (Chapter III) and the state of the art (Chapter IV). In this sense, all that remains to be clarified in detail in this chapter is which texts and unit of analysis will be used. For Krippendorff, unitizing is the systematic differentiation of segments of text that are of interest for the analysis — that is, the unit of analysis. In content analysis "the method of unitizing must be justified, and to do so, it must be shown that the information needed for the analysis is represented in the collection of units" (Krippendorff, 2004:83).

If we compare both versions of the components, we can note that point two identified by Abela — _determining the coding rules_ — coincides with point three of Krippendorff — _coding_ — so we can assert that sampling is considered by Abela as part of the first component. In this sense, Abela (2002) speaks of observed universe, sampling units, recording units, and context units as part of the first component. The observed universe is clarified when the texts to be used in the analysis are explained, while the other three units are types of units of analysis. In this way we can establish that Krippendorff's (2004) sampling coincides with Abela's (2002) sampling unit. According to the latter, "the sampling units are those portions of the observed universe that will be analyzed" (Abela, 2002:13), which allow the analyst, according to Krippendorff, to "economize research efforts by limiting observations to a manageable subset of units" (Krippendorff, 2004:84). This author elaborates further on this point and states the following:

"In qualitative research, samples are not designed according to statistical guidelines, but the quotations and examples that qualitative researchers present to their readers serve the same function as the use of samples. Citing typical examples in support of a general point implies that they represent similarities when they are not isolated cases" (Krippendorff, 2004:84)

Being a qualitative study, our content analysis subscribes to this way of presenting the sample selection. For this reason, the first component of our content analysis is entitled _Unitizing and sample selection_, which will encompass the first component identified by Abela (2002) and the first two components identified by Krippendorff (2004), with the nuance noted by the latter author regarding content analyses ascribed to the qualitative tradition.

**II.2.2.1. Unitizing and sample selection**

Taking into account that "the media exert a powerful cognitive influence" (Igartua & Humanes, 2004:243-245), our content analysis will study how Spanish media outlets with data units have sought to make the Spanish audience believe that transparency is a relevant issue. In this way, relying on the theoretical frameworks of _agenda-setting_, the _priming_ effect, and the _framing_ effect, the quantification of articles on this topic will be grounded in the following premise:

"People tend to include or exclude from their own knowledge what the media include or exclude from their own content. The public tends to assign to what it includes an importance that reflects the emphasis attributed by the _mass media_ to events, to problems, to persons" (Shaw, 1979:96-105).

For this reason, and because our study will analyze the policy and public policies affecting computational journalism in Spain from the Transparency Law and the transparency portal, the quantification will be carried out through a systematic newspaper archive search in Spanish media dealing with transparency and good governance. All of this keeping in mind that our content analysis aims to detect how outlets with data units have sought to influence transparency policy and public policies through the establishment of a media agenda on these policies in public opinion. In this sense, and taking into account that the unit of analysis "is each of the elements that are in fact quantified" (Wimmer & Dominick, 1996:179), with each unit of analysis being "a whole that the analyst distinguishes and treats as independent elements" (Krippendorff, 2004:97), in our content analysis we are interested in finding all journalistic pieces published in Spanish media that contain the word "transparencia" (transparency) and "buen gobierno" (good governance).

For this purpose the digital newspaper archive _MyNews_ was used, which compiles publications from all modern Spanish press since 1996 and operates through specialized keyword searches in the informational body of the text and the headlines. The keywords used were "transparencia" and "buen gobierno," since with these we will find all publications that are related in some way to Law 19/2013 and the government's transparency portal. Since _agenda-setting_ is "a process that can affect both the topics we think about and the way we think about them" (McCombs y Evatt, 1995:379), the way in which the journalist frames reality, providing the public with a way of interpreting events, is fundamental to our analysis. For this reason, it will be more qualitative than quantitative in nature, given that we are also seeking the capturing of meanings, the definition of situations, and above all, the emitter's point of view: in this case the data journalist or the outlet with a data unit.

A fundamental aspect for our study is obtaining a sample at a point when data journalists would know with certainty, from practice, how the transparency portal functioned in relation to public information requests. For this reason the periodization of the sample would need to be limited to dates after December 10, 2014, the moment when the Transparency Law of December 9, 2013 came into force.

Taking into account that transparency formed part of the campaign for the Spanish general elections of December 20, 2015, and that December 10, 2015 marked one year since the Transparency Law came into force, we have limited the sample of articles to December 19, 2015, the last date on which articles related to the general elections of that year were published. The latter is due to the fact that in Spanish elections there is a period of reflection, which among other things stipulates that the electoral campaign ends at midnight on the day before election day and media outlets cannot publish information related to it.

Another aspect taken into account in the periodization and sample selection were the regional and municipal elections of 2015. On May 24 of that year, regional elections were held in thirteen of the seventeen Spanish autonomous communities and in most municipalities, the results of which brought to national political life left-wing coalitions such as Podemos — although it did not stand under its own name — Izquierda Unida, Equo, and citizens' platforms linked to the 15M movement that were integrated into some of these coalitions' candidacies. Taking advantage of 1,700 corruption cases and more than 500 accused or investigated individuals (EP, 2014) — the vast majority of whom were in some way connected to a political party from the two-party system: PP and PSOE — as well as the abrupt fall of Spain in the Corruption Perceptions Index from position 37 to 58 out of 180 countries (Transparencia Internacional, 2015), these coalitions made transparency their electoral campaign banner ahead of the general elections held in December of that year, which is why we decided to begin the sample on June 20, 2015.

The selection of this date is due, among other things, to the fact that by then the majority of those elected in the municipal and regional elections would have taken office, making it easier to find publications related to transparency ahead of the December 20 general elections by the new political figures. The use of transparency by individuals and institutions between June 20 and December 19, 2015 would facilitate the capturing of valid and extrapolable sampling units in our study. The choice of December 20 as the final date of the sample was also informed by the convenience of dividing the sample into six-month periods, running from the 20th of one month to the 19th of the next.

However, the main reason for choosing these dates is that it was an ideal year for media outlets to establish an agenda capable of influencing the political agenda through public opinion. The emergence of new political forces as a starting point and the December 20 elections as the objective of the various political forces, passing through the Catalan regional elections of September — whose independence forces had fueled the discourse of some political forces at the national level — as well as the coincidence that on December 10 it was one year since the law came into force, are the main reasons that accompanied the most important factor of all: it is a period that meets the requirement that data journalists would know with certainty, from practice, how the transparency portal functioned in relation to public information requests. It must be borne in mind that, among other things, this would shed light on the relationship between the culture of transparency specific to DJ and the practice of this discipline in Spain.

In this sense, if we bear in mind that the research objective related to this part of our study is to analyze how the data journalism sector has responded in its public-facing discourse to the way in which Law 19/2013 and the Spanish government's transparency portal have aided or constrained its development, our content analysis is interested in the point of view of the emitter — namely, the outlet with a data journalism team. For this reason, the universe of media in the sample should consist of outlets that had a data journalism team during the selected dates: June 20 and December 19, 2015.

Although at present there are numerous data journalism teams in various media outlets, those with the longest track record in this discipline are two of Spain's leading digital-native outlets: _Eldiario.es_ and _Elconfidencial.com_. As already noted, these two outlets have had data journalism teams since at least the last quarter of 2013, dates at which they began to participate prominently in Madrid's most important annual data journalism forum:

"We want to open a collaborative space to work with data more calmly and turn it into compelling stories… The eight groups will meet in person at Medialab-Prado during the production workshops, which will take place from October 25-27 and December 13-15, 2013… Eldiario.es will participate in the project selection and will choose at least one of them to work actively with the team and help with the development of the work, which will ultimately be published on its website" (Medialab Prado, 2013).

If _Eldiario.es_ had the greatest prominence in the first data journalism workshop organized by Medialab Prado in 2013, the following year it was the turn of _Elconfidencial.com_ at the II Data Journalism Production Workshop. In this, coordinators Mar Cabra and Adolfo Antón Bravo carried out the "project selection together with the data journalism team (@ECLaboratorio) of El Confidencial" (Medialab Prado, 2014). Another outlet with a data journalism team during the selected sample dates has been _El Mundo_, whose existence can be confirmed through its _Twitter_ account from at least January 2015:

The inclusion of _El Mundo_ in the selection of the sample's media universe also responds to our interest in including a traditional reference outlet, with print publications, that has managed to adapt to the new digital media ecosystem. In this way we will be able to see the similarities and differences between how transparency is positioned as an important issue in traditional and digital-native outlets with data journalism teams, thus achieving a somewhat deeper inquiry into the research objective related to this part of our study. The selection of the final outlet as part of the sample's media universe relates to the fact that this content analysis aims to find out how outlets with data journalists are positioning the issue of transparency, and that one of the best ways to do so would be through a comparison with a major outlet without a data journalism team in its editorial structure. For this reason, _El País_ was chosen as part of the sample's media universe, since at the selected dates it did not have any data journalism team.

The advanced search in the _Mynews_ newspaper archive using the keyword "transparencia" and the exact phrase "buen gobierno" (Image 16) yielded a total of 293 units of analysis between June 20 and December 19, 2015, to which a total of 11 publications not captured by the digital newspaper archive were added through an advanced Google News search during the same dates with the same keywords. The 304 publications that our newspaper archive search yielded were reviewed one by one to purge errors from the sample. In this way the sample was composed of 231 publications from _El País_, _El Mundo_, _Eldiario.es_, and _Elconfidencial.com_ between June 20 and December 19, 2015. The errors purged were the following:

1. Repeated articles: the _MyNews_ newspaper archive database stored several repeated articles, the majority of which belonged to the traditional outlets in the sample, _El País_ and _El Mundo_, which tend to have digital versions of their print newspaper publications. In these cases we chose the digital publications as part of the sample, since they contain updates made throughout the day after the article's print publication. However, print publications not found on the outlet's digital page were included in the sample from the print articles collected by _MyNews_.

2. Articles in supplements of major newspapers: the _MyNews_ database collects all the supplements of the print editions of _El País_ and _El Mundo_, including those of their regional and sports versions. However, it does not do the same with the special print magazines of _Eldiario.es_, so we had to remove all information from print supplements to obtain a more homogeneous sample suitable for comparison between the different outlets.

3. Corporate transparency articles from the business section: almost all of these articles were excluded from the sample, with the exception of the few publications that bear some direct or indirect relation to the Transparency Law, its portal, or some characteristic of the politics or public policies of transparency and good governance.

4. Book reviews: there was in fact only one book review published by _Eldiario.es_, but it had to be removed from the sample as it did not meet the characteristics of the journalistic genre categories chosen for this study, which will be explained later.

**II.2.2.2. Coding and categorization of the sample**

In this section we have decided to encompass components two and three of the content analysis described by Abela (2002) — namely, determining the coding rules and determining the category system. Both steps form part of component three described by Krippendorff (2004), coding, since, as part of this, in chapter seven of his work he "suggests designs for the creation of text records in a medium adapted for subsequent analysis" (Krippendorff, 2004:125), while in chapter eight he addresses the language of data in this coding process for subsequent analysis:

"Categories and measurements are the entry point for empirical research. We treat their particular organization as data language, which is conceived as having a syntax and a semantics. The semantics of a data language anchors the data to the phenomenon of the observed world, to the coder's reading of texts, while the syntax of the data language links the data to the computational process of analysis" (Krippendorff, 2004:150)

In this sense, as a design for the creation of text records we have chosen an analysis sheet prepared using Microsoft Excel (Image 17).

**CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK**

**III.1. Evolution and current state of the concept of Data Journalism**

Convergence in newsrooms and the new journalistic culture resulting from the use and
processing of large quantities of data on the network has in recent years led to the study of data
journalism as a growing phenomenon (Barbosa & Torres, 2013; La Rosa, Sandoval-Martín,
2016). Until recently, its study in the academic field had been divided among various approaches
that positioned it differently. Some authors studied it as a discipline with several decades of
existence (Tejedor & Dader, 2011; Egawhary & O'Murchu, 2013), while others approached its
study from a perspective that situated it as a novel journalistic phenomenon, albeit with
antecedents in precision journalism and computer-assisted reporting (Ferreras, 2013; Chaparro
Domínguez, 2013; Antón, 2013; López & Martínez, 2014).

Throughout our study of the origins of the term DJ, we have observed how this conceptual
differentiation in the academic field has been accompanied by a lack of concern for defining this
discipline in the professional field from its very beginnings, a fact well exemplified in the blog
post by data journalist Adrian Holovaty under the title "The definitive two-part answer to the
question: Is data journalism?", whose answer was: "1. Who cares? 2. I hope my competitors
waste as much time as possible arguing about this" (Holovaty, 2009). This pragmatic stance
taken by data journalism professionals regarding deeper conceptual engagement with DJ came in
the year that preceded its first major impetus with the WikiLeaks revelations in 2010 about the
war in Afghanistan, which in turn promoted the academic study of this phenomenon in
subsequent years.

In this regard, among these early studies there were authors who positioned data journalism as
the evolution of precision journalism without mentioning computer-assisted reporting or CAR
(Chaparro-Domínguez, 2013), authors who considered PP and CAR as the same antecedent of
data journalism (López & Martínez, 2014), and manuals with excerpts from various authors and
practitioners holding differing views — some of whom considered CAR and PP as the same
thing as data journalism, while others treated each of these disciplines as something distinct
(Gray, Bounegru & Chambers, 2012). Other authors did differentiate data journalism from PP
and CAR, though without delving deeply into each of these terms and their possible definitional
discrepancies (Ferreras, 2013; Coddington, 2015; Ferreras, 2016).

This diversity of definitions — or lack of depth in them — does not stem from a lack of
knowledge about the origins of DJ, but rather from the shared knowledge surrounding these
origins and one specific name: Philip Meyer. Considered the foremost promoter of precision
journalism from its inception, Meyer was among the first journalists to support his investigations
with the use of computers. Although his beginnings in this innovative field started in 1959, it was
throughout the 1960s and 1970s that his investigations had the greatest media impact, at which
point he began to be mentioned in the academic field under the term precision journalism. This
early use of computers in journalistic investigations by Meyer meant that in later years there was
a tendency to label any use of computers in journalism as PP, which led one of the most cited
authors in this field in Spanish — the Spanish professor José Luis Dader — to assert that PP is
the socio-informatic pathway for discovering news (Dader, 1997).

From an academic standpoint, after having studied the use of Data Journalism as a term, it is
our view that the current terminological confusion has arisen because for several decades now
the term DJ has coexisted alongside that of data-driven journalism (DDJ, by its English
acronym), within what has been known as PP and CAR. These two disciplines had traditionally
been understood as the quantitative drift of journalism that emerged in the United States in the
1970s and which originated in Philip Meyer's use of computers in his investigations as a
journalist at the Miami Herald in 1959.

The oldest scientific articles in two of the most widely used databases in communication
studies — *Web of Science* and *Scopus* — that linked journalistic practice to the search and
processing of large quantities of data are "Precision Journalism and the 1988 US elections"
(Meyer, 1989) and "Database organization for qualitative-analysis - the nudist(tm) system"
(Richards; Richards, 1992). On the one hand, Meyer's article describes how the use of survey
methodologies in journalistic pieces generates large quantities of data that would help improve
the democratic system of the United States. On the other hand, two researchers from La Trobe
University in Australia, Tom and Lyn Richards, pointed out that the way in which data analysis
software used by social scientists and humanists typically organized their databases in qualitative
research was insufficient, and in some cases even distorted the information.

Both articles contain the terms DJ and DDJ in their texts, but neither refers to what we
understand today by data journalism, since they do not include defining characteristics such as
the consequences of the mass proliferation of personal computers, the exponential growth in
their processing capacity, the invention of the World Wide Web protocol, the exponential
growth of internet users, and the resulting information flow implied by the ever-increasing use of
mobile devices. All of these are characteristics of a sociocultural and technological phenomenon
that was not part of precision journalism or CAR: *big data*.

**III.1.1. Defining Data Journalism**

In addition to the vertiginous technological advances and the emergence of numerous disruptive
technologies, in data journalism there has been a turning point derived from the influence of the
*open data* movement and the WikiLeaks revelations in 2010. Indeed, although there were several
DJ projects in earlier years — such as those carried out by *ProPublica* in 2008 and the Data Blog
of *The Guardian* in 2009 — the term became popularized from the enormous quantity of
compromising files revealed on Julian Assange's website. That year *The Guardian* multiplied its
production of DJ pieces and began calling them as such: Simon Rogers stated that there was then
"a spreadsheet hell: 92,201 rows of data… This was the WikiLeaks war logs" (Rogers, 2011).
Hence Alberto Cairo used the term in 2011 in his work "The Functional Art" (Cairo, 2011) and
Bradshaw published that same year the inverted pyramid of data journalism on
OnlineJournalismBlog.com (Bradshaw, 2011).

Image 21. The inverted pyramid of DJ. Source: Bradshaw (2011)

This popularization of the term in newsrooms and new digital media drove both its academic
study at universities and the formation of independent data journalism teams and data units
within newsrooms, accompanied by a growing trend toward transparency that brought with it
the release of additional databases through governmental transparency portals, which in turn
generated more DJ teams and academic studies, forming a feedback loop that continued to feed
the popularization of the term, greater demands for transparency, the formation of even more
teams, and its investigation in the field of communication and journalism studies.

From the early days of the study of what we now know as data journalism — around 2008 —
the use of computers in journalism has been studied by many experts as computational
journalism and has evolved as the term DJ gained traction, incorporating computational thinking
and a culture of transparency, among other aspects, as fundamental components of its definition.
Below we will approach the evolution of this concept and link it to its best-known antecedents,
precision journalism and CAR, in order to shed light on the conceptual confusion that has
characterized DJ in communication studies in recent years. By doing so we aim to clarify the
differences between what is understood today as DJ and what has been understood as PP and
CAR.

**III.1.1.2. From Computational Journalism to Data Journalism**

In 2009 two academics from Duke and Stanford universities, Hamilton and Turner (2009),
defined Computational Journalism (CJ) as the combination of algorithms, data, and social
science knowledge to support the accountability functions inherent in journalism, while asserting
that public and private data sources were re-expanding exponentially and transparency advocates
were demanding greater openness:

"Data scientists are rapidly creating algorithms to make sense of large amounts of data.
Social scientists are also working with real-time data (…) pursuing social problems alongside
journalists" (Hamilton & Turner, 2009:2).

The fact that data scientists were creating algorithms to make sense of large datasets speaks
to a change in the nature of data, whose sheer volume no longer allowed it to be made sense of
in the way it had traditionally been handled in certain social sciences. This increase in large
quantities of data was related to the speed at which they were transmitted across the network. It
is also noteworthy that by 2009, the processing of these large datasets was bringing social
scientists closer to the working methods of some journalists, and not the other way around. This
is remarkable insofar as, as we shall see later, PP was born when journalism adopted techniques
from the social sciences in its investigations.

All these facts are indicators not only that technology had once again changed the way of
investigating in journalism, but of a sociocultural change that was identified with the term *big
data*, which was gaining increasing prominence between 2010 and 2011 due to its implications
across all fields of knowledge. In this regard, by 2012 the professor at the Methodist University
of São Paulo, Walter Lima, stated the following when linking *big data* to journalism:

"The configuration of the internet is being shaped by the increase in data transmission
speed… the need for an understanding of computational logic will enable data journalists to
use new possibilities in the processing of information and data, finding new angles on
information of social relevance, new facts and alternative information sources" (Lima,
2012:207-208).

As can be observed, it was no longer only a matter of working with large datasets as was done
in CAR, but of the speed at which these were transmitted. We are talking about one of the Vs of
*big data*: velocity. To this day there is talk of up to 7 Vs of *big data* depending on the field of
knowledge. However, four are constant across all definitions: Volume, Velocity, Variety, and
Veracity (Marr, 2015). All these Vs — along with a fifth: the Value that can be extracted from
data — form part of what is understood today as data journalism, with veracity being one of the
most discussed Vs in recent years with the rise of so-called *fake news* and disinformation
hoaxes spread through social media.

**III.1.1.3. *Big data* as a sociocultural phenomenon and its implications for journalism**

Cambridge academics and Microsoft Social Media researchers Danah Boyd and Kate Crawford
(2012) warned in 2012 that something important was changing in the nature of data: in the
volume and variety of its digital representation, in its collection and analysis at a massive scale,
and in its ultimate potential to generate social, cultural, and monetary value, even as these
developments simultaneously raised troubling questions about privacy, accountability, and ethics
(Boyd & Crawford, 2012). Part of this change in the nature of data in journalism, as explained
by Philip Meyer himself in a quote from the *Data Journalism Handbook*, can be seen in the
changes in working routines from the early days of precision journalism compared to those of
today, due to the enormous amount of data available: "When information was scarce, most of
our efforts went into the hunting and gathering. Now that information is abundant, processing is
more important" (Bounegru, Chambers & Gray, 2012:21).

This change from the perspective of journalism has been accompanied by other challenges at
the technical level, as warned by computer science academic Shan Suthaharan from the
University of North Carolina: when the volume, variety, and velocity of data increase, "the
techniques and technologies might not be able to store and process data if they do not adapt to
that new reality" (Suthaharan, 2014:70). This means that *big data* as a phenomenon has implied
a change in the way we view data and a change in the way technology stores and processes it. It
also tells us that, from both a cultural and a technical standpoint, if we understand *big data* solely
in terms of the abundance of data, we would not be capturing all the implications it entails. As
Boyd & Crawford (2012:670) stated: "out of context, it loses its meaning."

Indeed, at this point, *big data* is not considered merely as a technological transition toward a
data deluge (Lewis & Westlund 2015:448) and there is no singular *big data* phenomenon
(Boellstorff, 2013). Boyd and Crawford define it as a cultural, technological, and academic
phenomenon that lies in the interaction of the following aspects:

"(1) Technology: maximizing computational power and algorithmic accountability to collect,
analyze, link, and compare large data sets. (2) Analysis: carried out on large data sets to
identify patterns in order to make economic, social, technical, and legal claims. (3) Mythology:
the widespread belief that large data sets offer higher forms of intelligence and knowledge that
can generate precise insights previously impossible, with an aura of truth, objectivity, and
accountability" (Boyd & Crawford, 2012:663).

This is a view shared by two scholars of *big data* and journalism, Seth Lewis and Oscar
Westlund (2015), who, as already mentioned in the "*Big data* and journalism" section of the
introduction, have asserted that *big data* amounts to "a complex amalgamation of digital
abundance, emerging analytic techniques, mythology about data-driven insights, and a growing
critique of all consequences of big data practices for democracy and society" (Lewis & Westlund,
2015:1).

In the journalistic sphere, *big data* needs to be open (Elías, 2015) and the term *open data* is
associated with transparency, accountability, accessibility, public use, and being free and
reusable. Underlying this philosophy are the free software movement and hacker activism —
hacktivism. The publication of data is intimately tied to a transparency culture inherent to the
network society, which Himanen (2001) links to the hacker work ethic when he argues that
money ceases to be an incentive and the benefit is measured in goals such as the social value
generated by the undertaking, its transparency, and free access to the created work. As a result
of the *open data* movement worldwide, we have governmental transparency laws. The publication
of data by journalists could also be considered a form of transparency work and, although on
occasion it results from leaks, it involves subsequent "refinement processes among journalists"
(Lesage & Hackett, 2014:41).

Despite the apparent advantages that *big data* offers to journalism, writing a journalistic story
from a vast quantity of data has become a new challenge, not without difficulties and ethical
considerations to be taken into account. Carlos Elías (2015:94) alerts us to the difficulty of
"constructing a theory or a narrative when only many numerical data exist without any
explanation," and for such cases considers grounded theory a possible solution: "it is useful for
generating a hypothesis from the data obtained in an investigation." The author of *big data y
periodismo en la sociedad red* demystifies one of the mythologies of *big data* — that of the
veracity conferred by data — because deontological corruptions can occur that deliberately
distort reality. Quantity is sometimes confused with quality, when so-called small data —
interviews or expert opinions — explain reality better than a dataset. He also insists on the
importance of explaining data in their context, outside of which *big data* loses its meaning (Elías,
2014), as Philip Meyer (2011) has also noted: "Today we have an endless stream of data, more
than we could ever understand, so the need for explanation becomes paramount."

**III.1.2. Antecedents of Data Journalism**

As already noted, antecedents of DJ such as precision journalism and CAR have been treated as
the same thing by various authors for several decades, due in part to the fact that Philip Meyer
published his first precision journalism pieces with the help of computers, and that Investigative
Reporters & Editors (IRE) — one of the organizations that most helped promote the benefits of
precision journalism for improving the quality of investigative journalism — founded the National
Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting (NICAR) in 1989, whose mission is "to foster
excellence in journalism, particularly as it pertains to data journalism" (IRE, n.d.). Thus, for a
long time the equating of both terms was never questioned, either in investigative journalism
practice or in the academic field that studied it.

The term Data Journalism was associated with PP and CAR, which were often not
differentiated from each other in many circles, despite the fact that in the era of the first
computer-aided reports produced by Meyer, computers were very expensive and nonexistent in
newsrooms. It was not until the use of computers began to proliferate among the ever-growing
users of personal computers that one can speak of a CAR embedded in a sociocultural context
distinct from that of PP, which coincides precisely with the founding of NICAR by the IRE in
1989.

**III.1.2.1. Precision journalism**

As a term, PP was first mentioned in the academic sphere by Everette Dennis in 1971, in
contrast to the literary approach of the New Journalism (Galindo Arranz 2004:98), in a
monograph titled "The Magic Writing Machine, Student Probes of the New Journalism" (Dennis,
1971), where he linked precision journalism to certain quasi-scientific rigorous reports produced
by *Fortune* magazine in previous decades. This business magazine was one of the first news
organizations to familiarize itself with the marketing techniques popularized during the Second
World War (Blanchard, 1999), and is thus seen as one of the precursors of precision journalism.
However, it was not until Meyer (1973) published his work "Precision Journalism: A Reporter's
Introduction to Social Science Methods" that the term became internationalized, becoming
associated with the computer-aided reports produced by this journalist from 1959 onward at the
Miami Herald, and above all with the report on the Detroit riots of 1967 that earned him the
Pulitzer. The year after the publication of Meyer's seminal work, Everette Dennis (1974)
published "Other Voices, the New Journalism in America," in which precision journalism was
defined as the new journalistic form in which information-gathering tools — such as survey
research and computational analysis — were less important than the stylistic effort to produce
reports that drew on the best of the scientific method and humanistic writing (Dennis, 1974). It
was thus that PP began to be differentiated from other forms of specialized journalism such as
that of *Fortune* magazine.

As a journalistic discipline, precision journalism stems from a self-critique of the journalistic
method of detecting, classifying, and ranking any aspect of current events, and grows out of its
approximation to sociological research (Dader, 1997). Meyer defines it as "the application of
scientific methods of social and behavioral science research to the practice of journalism"
(Meyer, 1989:96), meaning that mastery of the scientific methodology proper to the social
sciences and its possible informational application constitutes its backbone (Galindo, 2004).
Dader provides a more complete definition, asserting that it "is based on developing and
interpreting empirical data collected, cross-referenced, and verified through scientific methods
of socio-statistical and computational research" (1997:156).

From this latter perspective, there would seem to be no significant difference between precision
journalism and CAR, given that computational research is incorporated as part of the definition
of PP. However, more than 20 years elapsed between this definition and that first book by Meyer,
so the context in which Meyer's PP developed is not the same as that to which Dader refers in
the 1990s, since one of the most important technological changes that occurred between the
1970s and the 1990s was the beginning of the computerization of media newsrooms (typewriters
gave way to computers, among other advances). Here lies an important cultural change with
respect to the first precision journalism practiced by Meyer, which required the use of computers
located exclusively at universities and other large institutions: the mass proliferation of personal
computers brought with it a journalistic culture quite different from that practiced by a select
group of journalists with access to the latest technologies during the 1960s and 1970s.

**III.1.2.2. Computer-assisted reporting (CAR)**

To understand the cultural change in journalistic practice when speaking of computer-assisted
reporting, we must go back to 1975, the year that saw the birth of the IRE. This non-profit
organization was founded with the aim of constituting a cross-disciplinary community of
professionals (Aucoin, 1995), breaking the individualistic and particularistic nature of
investigative journalism (Dader, 1997). From its inception it focused its activity on improving
the quality of investigative journalism, and was therefore always closely linked to the promotion
of PP and the training of investigative journalists during the two decades in which the personal
computer was gaining an ever-greater number of users (the 1980s and 1990s). By the time the
IRE founded NICAR in 1989, the majority of newsrooms in the United States already had
computers and the sociocultural context in which the investigative journalists affiliated with this
institute worked had diverged considerably from that in which the first precision journalism had
developed. As an example of this difference we can mention the formation of a multidisciplinary
unit at *The New York Times* in 1989, whose presentation to other editors at the outlet was
witnessed by the now full professor of journalism José Luis Dader, thanks to a Fulbright
fellowship that year:

"The idea of collaboration already existed, which can occur on two levels: collaboration
among specialists and collaboration among methodologies. Both things were already present
at *The New York Times* in 1989 when a new unit was created comprising a sociologist, a
computer scientist, a statistician, and a journalist." (J.L. Dader, personal communication,
20 October 2010)

Another sociocultural characteristic that was changing journalistic practices between the early
years of precision journalism and the year NICAR was founded was the spread of database use
in news production through data collection and structuring practices by institutions that Michael
Schudson (2010) called political observatories. This scholar linked these political observatories
to "the new availability of databases for public interest research: together, these developments
represent only one of the characteristics of the future of news" (2010:107).

This technical-cultural characteristic — one of the defining features of what we today know as
data journalism — had its roots in a decade in which journalists began to organize themselves in
the production of collective knowledge, and whose subsequent development came accompanied
and reinforced by the mass proliferation of personal computers among journalists.

This is the beginning of a culture of transparency that we today consider key to the
development of DJ.

**III.1.2.3. From the network society to *big data***

The year that saw the birth of NICAR was the same year that marked the beginning of public
commercial use of the internet. A year later Tim Berners-Lee created the World Wide Web. By
the end of 1990 the HTTP protocol and HTML language were established, and the first web
server was launched, along with nearly all the features through which the internet would achieve
the cultural and commercial impact we already know. The direct and indirect influences of
digital technologies on journalism generated analytical frameworks such as the network society
(Castells, 2006) and cultural convergence (Jenkins, 2008), among others that have also been key
in communication studies.

In this period the business model changed radically when classifieds, advertising, and
subscriptions became less sustainable online (Meyer, 2004) and many journalistic sources
became media outlets in their own right, leading to the steady loss of journalist jobs at traditional
media while press offices were gaining them (Elías, 2015). More audience- and network-focused
production models for news began to be adopted, resulting in greater interaction (Boczkowski
2005; Deuze 2005). This increase in digitalization and interactivity through hyperlinks, audience
comments, and the opening up of sources and documents contributed to greater openness and
transparency (Karlsson, 2010; Bennett, 2014), and this journalistic culture that had customarily
been associated with precision journalism and CAR took on new dimensions in a context of
open data and *big data*: transparency became a standard element in the new data journalism.

**III.1.3. Fundamental aspects of data journalism framed within *big data* as a sociocultural
phenomenon**

Access to large volumes of data, their processing, and their publication are three of the
fundamental elements of data journalism (Ferreras Rodríguez, 2013:119); likewise, as Philip
Meyer (2012) has told us, "today's emphasis falls on data processing" (Bounegru, Chambers &
Gray, 2012:6). From a sociocultural standpoint we know that all these elements are part of one
and the same phenomenon, despite how vaguely *big data* is defined as a term in most
disciplines: "*big data* is a loosely defined term used to describe data sets so large and complex
that they become difficult to work with using normal statistical software" (Snijders, Matzat &
Reips, 2013:1). To avoid such vagueness, we will briefly unpack some of the fundamental
elements that form part of DJ as a discipline embedded within *big data* as a sociocultural
phenomenon. These would be: 1- data processing and the databases that make it possible, 2- the
publication of data and its relationship with open data philosophy and transparency, and finally,
3- data visualization, differentiating in this last point the new interactive applications from the
classic infographics of journalism.

**III.1.3.1. Data processing and databases**

Some academics have delved into the study of database use in journalism in a context of
information digitalization well before *big data*. As we have seen in the previous section, today's
data journalism is unthinkable without the new technologies and digital tools. However, most
branches of journalism can continue to exist without these new digital tools. Declarative
journalism, for example, has absolutely no need for databases to process information, and some
investigative journalism pieces can be produced with just a telephone and a recorder, with no
need whatsoever for the processing and storage of large quantities of data or public information
requests to public administrations.

In the case of DJ, the use of databases is a *sine qua non* condition, since it is directly related
to the processing of data. This is a condition inherited from the development of ICT, and various
academic articles have examined it in depth, as in the case of the article "Automation of
databases: potentialities of basic tools for another possible journalism" (López-García,
Toural-Bran, Pereira-Fariña & Barbosa, 2009), in which the following was affirmed:

"With the emergence of new information and communication technologies (ICT), the routines
of journalism professionals have been affected: immediacy, one of the essential characteristics
that ICT bring to the practice of journalism, has turned it into a field in which databases and
the advantages they offer in day-to-day practice take on great importance. Journalists handle
a large number of references related to databases and have the possibility of accessing an
enormous quantity of information from the internet. Furthermore, databases have become
structuring agents of knowledge, of content, and in many cases even the backbone of media
discourse." (López-García et al. 2009:308)

This vision of databases as structuring agents of knowledge and content, as well as the
backbone of media discourse, emerged in 2009, when the term Data Journalism had not yet
become popularized in the academic sphere. By the time this term broke into the research
agenda, Brazilian academics Suzana Barbosa and Vitor Oliveira Torres, in their article "The
'Digital Journalism in Databases' paradigm: modes of narration, formats and visualizations for
content" (Barbosa & Torres, 2013), merged the terms DJ and DDJ into one, asserting that they
are part of the JDBD paradigm — an acronym for Digital Journalism in Databases in Portuguese:
*Jornalismo Digital em Base de Dados* — which they propose as the model from which DJ
derives:

"The consolidation of databases as structuring agents of journalistic activity and as singular
agents in the media convergence process allows us to affirm their preponderance (…) One of
the most visible [extensions to the proposed paradigmatic model] is what is being called
Data-Driven Journalism or Data Journalism." (Barbosa & Torres 2013:153)

Many authors had already shared this vision, such as Mark Briggs when he said that
sophisticated databases are helping all professions, including journalism, where opportunities
abound to use databases, spreadsheets, and other forms of structured data in news and story
coverage (Briggs, 2010). Handbooks such as *The Online Journalism Handbook* suggest that
readers use databases and spreadsheets such as Excel or Google Spreadsheet to find stories in
data (Bounegru, Chambers & Gray, 2012), a process that of course takes place after searching
for, collecting, and cleaning the data (Bradshaw & Rohumaa, 2011). In other words, what data
journalism experts recommend is to compile and clean data and then to contextualize and
combine them (find stories) through the use of databases — that is, to use databases to carry out
three of the first four steps of the inverted pyramid of data journalism (see Image 15).

The emphasis that DJ places on data processing over searching for and collecting data is a
direct consequence of *big data*, just as the processing of these large data volumes is impossible
without databases that help manage so much information and make sense of it — a reality that
has been taking root in an evident way in the media since the first WikiLeaks revelations.
However, data journalism is not the only field that was swept along by this reality in the wake of
the aforementioned leaks. In the article "Model trees with topic model preprocessing: An
approach for data journalism illustrated with the WikiLeaks Afghanistan war logs," academics
in economics and business from the universities of Vienna and Linz assert that the data
processing challenges facing data journalism are the same as those facing the social sciences, and
especially computational social sciences:

"Generating stories from complex data is a contemporary issue for journalists and (social)
scientists, especially when the amounts of data are very large and cannot easily be processed
by humans (…) Data journalism and computational social sciences both use statistical and
computational methods to deal with the problem of processing many complex data (often in the
form of text documents) and presenting them in an accessible way." (Rusch et al. 2013:3)

Although this article does not focus on DJ and devotes itself to building a statistical model, it
is a testament to how the WikiLeaks revelations so profoundly changed the landscape in
journalism and the social sciences — especially computational and statistical ones — that data
processing, already important before the leaks, has now become the central axis of DJ. If in the
field of the social sciences — which has numerous expert eyes well-versed in the world of
statistical methodology — academics must resort to complex methodologies for the processing
of data that would otherwise be unintelligible, in DJ this processing must lead to products that
are easy for the general public to understand. It is at this point that one can see how *big data* as a
sociocultural phenomenon has standardized values of transparency and open data in DJ, without
which it would not have been possible either to communicate complex stories to the general
public, or to acquire through *open data* — major leaks being one example — the data that make
it possible to carry out the first phase of the inverted pyramid of DJ: compiling (see Figure 1).

**III.1.3.2. Publication of data: open data philosophy and transparency**

In any journalistic piece, the publication of the story worked on or investigated is the final
objective, although in data journalism this publication usually implies the publication of the data
on which the story is based. When we speak of data publication we do so in two senses: (1) the
publication of stories based on data and (2) the publication of the raw data themselves, or *Raw
Data*. The publication of stories based on data does not refer solely to those presented in classic
journalistic genres that may have been data-driven, but also to visualizations and interactive web
applications that continue to innovate in new presentation formats today. These last two ways of
publishing stories from data are those that attract the most attention in DJ, although as *The Data
Journalism Handbook* tells us, they are not the only ones: "there are lots of different ways of
presenting your data to your audience, from simply publishing the raw data with stories to
creating beautiful visualisations and interactive web applications" (Bounegru, Chambers &
Gray, 2012:178).

In other words, and according to British journalists from the Centre for Investigative
Journalism — CIJ — Elena Egawhary and Cynthia O'Murchu, DJ is "the ability to analyse and
scrutinise numbers, and to know how to handle large datasets and read them correctly… [to] find
and support your stories" (Egawhary & O'Murchu, 2013:1). That is to say, stories are the raison
d'être of DJ, not visualizations or interactive web applications. This does not mean that some
stories are not better told to the general public through visualizations and applications, but these
remain in service of telling stories or communicating. It is also necessary to note that in the
majority of cases where DJ is used to tell stories without visualizations or interactive
applications, these stories are supported by the raw data on which they are based.

These two main strands — the publication of raw data to support stories, and the use of data to
design visualizations and applications — are well illustrated by the views of two well-known
data journalism experts: Aron Pilhofer (*The New York Times*) and Steve Doig (Walter Cronkite
School of Journalism, Arizona State University). According to Pilhofer, sometimes data can tell
a story better than words or a photograph, which is why the following occurs:

"Terms like 'news applications' and 'data visualization' have had something of a buzzword
status in so many newsrooms of late." They are also fuelling interest the abundant harvest
(usually free) of new tools and technologies designed to help even the most reluctant journalist
to turn data into visual storytelling pieces" (Bounegru, Chambers & Gray, 2012:178).

As can be observed, this data journalist from *The New York Times* highlights the proliferation
of free digital tools to help journalists turn data into stories, which makes evident in his
professional experience how some elements of the transparency culture inherent to the free
software movement had already reached mainstream media by 2012 across all journalism, not
just DJ. He also links this new conception of data for creating stories to another fundamental
aspect that we will address later: data visualization.

For his part, Pulitzer Prize-winning data journalist and journalism professor at Arizona State
University, Steve Doig, highlights the publication of raw data as a transparency practice
characteristic of data journalists, similar to the way scientific knowledge works — which
typically describes the methodological process by which a problem has been approached so that
any other researcher can replicate the results. However, his conception of transparency goes
beyond publishing data so that any other professional can replicate results, for he underlines that
such data are paramount in encouraging users to verify results and generate collective
intelligence, as well as in building reader loyalty:

"Giving news consumers easy access to the data we use for our work is the right thing to do
for several reasons. Readers can assure themselves that we are not torturing the data to reach
unfair conclusions. It opens our data in the social science tradition of allowing researchers
to replicate our work. Encouraging readers to study the data can generate tips that can lead
to subsequent stories. Finally, engaged readers who are interested in our data are likely to
come back again and again." (Bounegru, Chambers & Gray 2012:179-180)

Thus, in these statements by Doig about the practice of data journalism one can observe
elements of a transparency culture similar to that of the social sciences — very much in the
style of precision journalism — as well as elements characteristic of the transparency of the
network society, when he highlights that tips from users could lead to subsequent stories. That is,
the use of the collective intelligences inherent to *open data* that form part of the transparency
culture of the free software movement.

The publication of data is intimately tied to a culture of transparency inherent to the network
society, which Himanen (2001) linked to the hacker work ethic by arguing that money ceases to
be an incentive and the benefit is measured in goals such as the social value generated by the
undertaking, its transparency, and free access to the created work. Thus, the publication of data
to the public by journalists is not the only "transparency work" in data journalism, but "involves
refinement processes among journalists" (Lesage & Hackett, 2014). In this way, various
researchers linking transparency values and open data to DJ (Karlsson, 2011; Gray et al., 2012;
Egawhary & O'Murchu, 2013; Rodríguez-Brito & García-Chico, 2013; Parasie and Dagiral
2013; Lewis & Usher 2013; Lesage & Hackett 2014; Bennett, 2014; Lesage & Hackett, 2014;
Howard, 2014; Smit, Yael & Buijs, 2014; Elías, 2015; Quesada y Ribas, 2016; La-Rosa y
Sandoval-Martín, 2016a; Martínez, 2016; Subires, 2016; Varona, Plaza, Pulido & Camarero,
2016; La-Rosa y Sandoval-Martín, 2016b) made statements similar to the following about their
objects of study:

"Subsequent *Guardian* initiatives have included: APIs (application programming interfaces —
or software that allows interaction with other software); a data store (where data can be
obtained by subjects); a data blog and initiatives during the British elections. *The Guardian* is
an open platform, making its digital tools and resources available for reuse." (Daniel, 2010)

It is evident, without needing to search for more particular examples, that the transparency
implicit in the process of producing information with data implies a development or
"refinement" — borrowing Lesage & Hackett's term — in journalistic work, which in the case
of *The Guardian* has led it to become one of the world's leading references in data journalism.
For this reason the following statement comes quite close to what we understand as DJ:

"The development of data journalism encapsulates the processes of digitalization; the
philosophy of *open data* and transparency and public access to information; statistical and
visualization tools; and the investigative skills of the journalist of all time for compiling,
filtering, contextualizing, contrasting, organizing, ranking and telling a story in an appealing
way." (Rodríguez-Brito & García-Chico, 2013)

This article is the result of 5 DJ case studies in the American continent: the special feature
"Subsidies to collectives in Argentina" by the Argentine newspaper *La Nación*; the "Retratos
Paraná" application by the Brazilian *Gazeta do Povo*; the "Illinois School Report Cards" by the
American *Chicago Tribune*; the "Government Employee Salary Database" by the also American
*The Texas Tribune*; and finally, the work "Terrorists for the FBI" by the independent American
outlet *Mother Jones*. Through the article by Rodríguez-Brito and García-Chico one can
corroborate how none of these examples would have existed without the collectives linked to
digital and transparency activism that have helped transform the way of understanding one part
of journalism, incorporating many of the elements inherent to the open data philosophy of the
*big data* era into the practices of investigative journalism.

This open data and transparency philosophy is a fundamental aspect of the practice of data
journalism and, like every aspect that forms part of the same phenomenon, is related to others
alongside which they constitute a whole. That is why, in DJ, the great majority of visualizations
and interactive web applications are accompanied by the raw data underpinning them, owing to
the transparency culture within which they are embedded.

**III.1.3.3. Data visualization: infographics and interactive applications**

According to the work "Milestones in the history of thematic cartography, statistical graphics,
and data visualization," data visualization is the science of visual representation of data as
information that has been abstracted in some schematic form (Friendly, 2008). Viewed as such,
data visualization has a tradition of more than two hundred years according to several authors
(Chen, 2004; Tufte, 1997; Ware, 2000), and in journalism, "since the appearance of the
American newspaper USA Today in 1982, these elements now called infographics (from the
English 'informational graphics') have revolutionized design, especially journalistic and
editorial design" (Leturia, 1998).

Simon Rogers himself — data editor at Twitter in San Francisco and editor of the Datablog and
Datastore at *The Guardian* — goes back to 1854 to assure us that John Snow's map of the
London cholera epidemic (Image 17) changed the way we see diseases — and gave data
journalism a model of how to work today (Rogers, 2013). This map shows the population of
Soho, part of the West End of London, in 1854. Its author, John Snow, published in 1849 a
small pamphlet titled "On the Mode of Communication of Cholera" in which he proposed that
the "cholera poison" reproduced itself in the human body and was spread through the
contamination of food and water (Snow, 1854). It must be remembered that at the time it was
believed that cholera was transmitted by inhalation of miasmas or contaminated vapors.
According to Simon Rogers, "Snow did something that data journalists do today: he mapped the
cases" (Rogers, 2013). Each death is represented on the map with a bar, and through this
mapping Snow was able to identify where the majority of deaths were concentrated and how the
remaining outliers were in some way near the water pumps.

This graphical representation was part of a more extensive statistical work and conforms to the
definition of data visualization given by Friendly above. This example also illustrates how, both
in cartography and in statistics, charts are visual ways of discovering and displaying data: in
journalism's case, it is a way of showing stories. However, if we limit ourselves to the definition
of data visualization or infographic in the journalistic sphere, we would have to include all
data-based visualizations prior to the digital era. It must be remembered that, in addition to
being framed within the sociocultural context of *big data*, the visualizations of data journalism
are part of a proposal born on the web:

"It is not the first time that data or statistical analysis have been used in journalism — it also
occurred with scientific or precision journalism; nor is it the only journalistic proposal to have
emerged from the web, since citizen or participatory journalism was combined with the web in
journalism 2.0." (Antón-Bravo, 2013:100)

According to various expert data journalists, data visualization is a fundamental part of the
production process in DJ. Such is the case of Mirko Lorenz, information and journalism
architect at the data journalism agency Journalism++; and Paul Bradshaw, author of the Online
Journalism Handbook and professor at City University's School of Journalism in London. Based
on a conference on journalism innovation (Lorenz, 2010) and an article from *The Guardian*'s
Datablog (Bradshaw, 2010), the article "Data visualization in online journalism and its
implications in the production process" assures us of the following:

"Data-driven journalism is based on large amounts of data with the purpose of writing news
stories. Data journalists describe the journalistic process as a workflow that consists of digging
deeply into the data, analyzing and filtering the data found, visualizing it and forming a story"
(Weber & Rall 2012:349).

According to these experts, data visualization is a fundamental part of the production process
in DJ. However, they give no detail whatsoever about the differences between visualizations in
data journalism and other forms of visualizing information in journalism. Weber & Rall do
anticipate in their article that one visual format in data journalism is interactive information
graphics, also called interactive features or interactive graphics (2012:349). Other authors are
more explicit when pointing out differences between print and network-society visualizations:

"The difference between a printed and an online graphic lies mainly in three aspects:
animation, interactivity, and multimodality. These three additional aspects fundamentally
change the ways in which the user perceives, clicks, and navigates through graphic
information on the web." (Burmester et al. 2010:361)

Given that graphical information has the value of displaying complex information that is
difficult to explain verbally through visualizations — and this information being far more
complex to gather and contextualize in the *big data* era — the predominance of interactive
visualizations over traditional ones in DJ responds to the journalistic interest in bringing such
data to the ordinary citizen, who is not an expert in the statistical interpretation of data. This
direct relationship between *big data* and the new visualizations for the general public in data
journalism has been studied by various authors, in which visualization as a sense-making tool for
large quantities of data is paramount:

"As a consequence of technological advances and solutions, the speed, volume, and even the
diversity of data generated every day grow exponentially and new challenges arise for
managing them and making sense of them — that is, for turning them into information."
(Sánchez-Bonvehí & Ribera 2014:312)

In addition to the challenge of making sense of large quantities of data, there is the issue of
decision-making based on the information obtained from these visualizations. In this regard, in
the article "Interactive analysis of big data," Heer and Kandel (2012) propose visualizations of
large quantities of data to combat the information overload of the *big data* era and to provide
citizens with tools for making decisions based on accurate information:

"Another notion of big data that has a relevance to the end user is that the data are too much
… Faced with the data deluge, what remains relatively constant is our own cognitive ability to
make sense of the data and reach decisions based on trustworthy information." (Heer & Kandel
2012:50)

This is an important point where, according to the final phase of the inverted pyramid of data
journalism (see Figure 1), communicating integrates data visualization as a tool alongside a
publication of the data that forms part of an open data and transparency philosophy — which
would not have been possible without another fundamental aspect of DJ: data processing and
databases. These latter aspects make possible the other initial phases of the inverted pyramid of
data journalism: compiling, cleaning, contextualizing, and combining data. According to
information processing experts Claudia Sánchez Bonvehí and Mireia Ribera (2014), visualization
implies a communicative effort that goes beyond simply disseminating data:

"Information visualization emerges to change the communicative process and strategically
restore primordial prominence to the visual. It is not about vulgarizing the data, but about
delving deeper and taking advantage of the benefits offered by human perception and
interaction, with the objective of transforming complex and abstract realities into simple ones."
(Sánchez-Bonvehí & Ribera, 2014:312)

Taking all this into account, we subscribe to the following definition proposed by Wibke Weber
and Hannes Rall when speaking of interactive visualizations in data journalism:

"…we define interactive information graphics as (1) a hybrid presentation of different
combined modalities: text, photos, audio, video, tables, maps, charts; the interaction of these is
more than the sum of the parts. (2) In addition to multimodality, interactivity and
hypertextuality are defining characteristics of interactive information graphics. (3) Each
interactive information graphic focuses on one story, one topic." (Weber & Rall 2012:361)

What these authors call an interactive information graphic implies making sense of data within
the context of *big data* and converting them into information by means of an interactive,
multimedia, and hypertextual visualization. For our purposes, this transformation of data into
information through this type of visualization with the characteristics already cited includes
so-called web applications, provided they are focused on one story, one journalistic topic. Thus,
these interactive information graphics by Weber and Rall, and the web applications that share
these characteristics, are referred to in this study as interactive visualizations, since an
application focused on presenting complex data in a more digestible way for the general public is
a form of visualizing information.

For this reason, interactive visualizations form part of the most visible aspects of DJ, although
it must be stressed that DJ can exist without them, as we have already mentioned above.

**III.2. Proposal for an updated definition of Data Journalism**

Taking into account the points addressed in section III.1 of this chapter, we can assert that,
although other forms of investigating with databases existed in journalism — such as precision
journalism and CAR — data journalism differs from these variants in various respects that, to a
greater or lesser degree, are a consequence of the sociocultural context within which this
discipline is embedded. In this context, information on the web is abundant and the efforts of the
journalist or data journalism team focus more on data processing than on searching for and
collecting information. This aspect, together with a culture of transparency inherent to the
network society that Himanen (2001) linked to the hacker work ethic — in which cultural
practices of the free software movement and hacktivism underlie — have fostered practices in
DJ that lead us to reaffirm a definition proposed in partial publications of our current research:

"Data journalism is the journalistic proposal born on the web that shares with precision
journalism and CAR a methodological rigor similar to that of the social sciences for the
extraction, systematization, and processing of data for the publication of journalistic stories. Its
fundamental difference from these two branches of journalism lies in the incorporation of
problems and techniques that emerged in the context of *big data*, which in turn has led to the
establishment of the following fundamental aspects:

1- its context within the *big data* phenomenon;

2- the need for data processing and databases in this context;

3- the open data and transparency philosophy viewed from two perspectives:

a- that required of public and private institutions for the correct investigation in this branch
of journalism, and

b- that which the journalist must possess as part of their code of ethics;

4- data visualization by means of infographics and interactive applications;

5- citizen journalism as support in the face of a lack of transparency,

6- collective work viewed from two perspectives:

a- that related to citizen journalism and

b- that related to the collective production of information within a data journalism team."
(La Rosa, 2015:91)

Several of these fundamental aspects will be able to be confirmed empirically through our

in section I.3.1.

**IV.1. The use of databases in journalism: from PP and CAR to the *big data*-journalism
relationship in DJ**

After the first mentions made by Everette Dennis (1971) and the first introduction to social
science methodologies for reporters by Meyer (1973) — reprinted and commented on that same
year by other authors (Hvistend, 1973) and updated by Meyer himself (1980) years later — the
public-interest benefits of this branch of journalism were studied from computer sciences (Reck,
1976), as well as its credibility from the field of communication (Mosier and Ahlgren, 1981).
These studies were accompanied in the 1980s by practical guides for the correct practice of PP
(Ismach, 1988), taking into account both its credibility and the benefit that a greater practice of
this journalistic discipline would bring to society. By the time CAR was at its peak, there were
authors who sought to update PP with the realities of CAR, such as Singer (1994) and her article
"The New Precision Journalism," in which the author described the new CAR techniques of her
day as the update of precision journalism, or the new precision journalism.

DJ and DDJ as terms implying the use of databases in journalism continued to be studied in a
very cursory manner throughout the 1980s with works such as the already mentioned article by
Meyer (1989) on coverage of the 1988 US elections. Studies that already included CAR as a
term began to appear in the following decade with research such as that conducted by Leonard
(1992) on American newsrooms, with notable studies such as that of Foley (1997) which
investigated the successful CAR strategies of its time. PP and CAR as antecedents of the change
that media underwent with digitalization and the internet boom (Martin, 1999) have coexisted
alongside proposals to use CAR to improve journalism (Yang, Ha & 박현수, 2004; Paulussen,
2007) and other branches of communication (김경희 & 정희선, 2003) in the first decade of the
21st century.

The most recent publications on the use of databases in journalism and outside of what we
today know as DJ have been proposals for a historical periodization of PP going back to the
early 21st century (Galindo, 2004), and whose most recent ones, at the beginning of the second
decade of this century (Armentia & Ganzabal, 2010; Ortiz, 2010), coincided temporally with
the first boom of DJ as a term already in the process of differentiation from PP and CAR. It was
in these years that the terminological debate among DJ, PP, and CAR (Tejedor & Dader, 2011;
Nerentz, 2014) began to proliferate in the academic field, developing across various branches of
knowledge — in addition to information sciences, communication, and journalism — until its
resolution with studies that successfully linked *big data* and journalism (Heer & Kandel, 2012;
Lima, 2012; Boellstorff, 2013; Rusch, Hofmarcher, Hatzinger & Hornik, 2013; Lewis &
Westlund, 2015; Neubauer, 2015; Renó & Renó, 2017; Sandoval & La-Rosa, 2018; Neves,
2018; Revuelta, 2018), which in turn explains the resurgence of articles on DJ as we know it in
the last decade.

**IV.2. DJ as we know it on the research agenda**

The dates of some articles that mapped the DJ ecosystem (Flores, 2012) and the journalistic
future through data journalism (Du, 2014; Gaber, 2014; Miller, 2015) are examples of how
academia incorporated belatedly the studies surrounding a journalistic practice that had been
exercised since approximately 2008. However, this type of article that at the time sought
different angles — such as viewing DJ as a specialization of news organizations (Flores, 2013) —
contrasted with others of near-current relevance, having studied web analytics (Thelwall, 2013)
and DJ *fact-checking* (Goasdoué, Leblay, Karanasos, Manolescu, Katsis & Zampetakis, 2013),
especially if we take into account recent publications such as "ContentCheck: Content
Management Techniques and Tools for Fact-checking" (Manolescu, 2017), which disseminate
data verification techniques to guard against so-called *fake news*, and other more recent studies
on the automation of *fact-checking* (Sarr & Sall, 2018). These advances in the study of data
journalism at that time later consolidated its investigation as a discipline with a disruptive
impact thanks to emerging technologies (Murray, 2015), in which the link between *big data* and
DJ began to be studied based on relationships with some of the particularities we now know
form part of this technological and sociocultural context — such as DJ and the semantic web
(Pellegrini, 2012; Antón Bravo, 2013), the way information is reported in the digital age (Abad,
2015), how data journalism is defined according to the context of the digital tools of the
information age (López & Martínez, 2014; Aslan, Bayrakci, & Kucukvardar, 2016), the
characteristics of data-based news (김진희 & 임종섭, 2016), or our understanding of
quantitative journalism (Anderson, 2015). Thus, the incorporation of elements that form part of
*big data* as a sociocultural context into journalism studies has today reached a point on the
research agenda where there are studies on interdisciplinary learning in DJ (Zhu & Du, 2018)
and the relationship between data science and DJ (Okazaki, 2018).

This does not mean that there have not been, or that there are not still, authors who have
considered (Pease, 2012) and who still consider DJ as part of PP or CAR (J.L. Dader, personal
communication, 20 October 2016), but it is a declining trend owing to the most recent studies of
a more sociological bent — such as those that study this phenomenon from the perspective of the
epistemological tensions it creates in investigative journalism (Parasie, 2015) or field theory
(De Vuyst, 2018). These investigations delve into new formats and trends from a perspective
that seeks to explore DJ as a term more deeply, with clear examples such as hypermedia narrative
through its origins in DJ (Colussi, 2017), the new professional profiles in journalism according
to the Spanish academy (Gómez-Calderon, Roses & García-Borrego, 2017), and recent studies
such as "Journalism meets games: Newsgames" (Meier, 2018), in which sociological categories
such as boundary work are used to differentiate news games journalism from other branches of
digital journalism, taking it for granted that it forms part of DJ.

**IV.2.1. Concerns about the training of the data journalist on the research agenda: data
literacy**

As can be observed, although data journalism as we know it today did not exist until
approximately 2008, from 1990 onward numerous scholars examined the use of databases in
journalism newsrooms. A study that compiles a large part of these investigations has been
"Journalism's 'Dirty Data'. Below Researchers Radar," an article in which Messner and
Garrison (2007) compiled nearly all the academic research on the use of databases and
quantitative investigation in journalism through a content analysis of the most prestigious
reference publications on communication. Their article was a wake-up call regarding the way
databases were being used and the significant errors that journalists typically made in the course
of their investigations due to a lack of technical knowledge.

This concern — which remains relevant in studies on data journalism as we know it today —
has generated an entire branch centered on the education and training of data journalists (An &
Lugo-Ocando, 2016; Burns, & Matthews, 2018; Chaparro, 2014; Davies & Cullen, 2016).
Examples include studies on training in DJ techniques for extracting data and creating stories
(López-García, Rodríguez-Vázquez & Pereira Fariña, 2014; Charski, 2015; Graham, 2015;
Griffin and Dunwoody, 2016; Yang & Du, 2016; Alazañez-Cortés et. al., 2017; Green, 2018;
Heaton, Mills & Ansell, 2018; Ojo & Heravi, 2018; Welbers, Opgenhaffen & Janssens, 2018),
research on the experiences of teaching data journalism through MOOCs (Plaue & Cook, 2015;
Franco y Martul, 2016), as well as research on the university programs that teach or attempt to
teach DJ and their pedagogical strategies (Radchenko & Sakoyan, 2016; Splendore, Di Salvo,
Eberwein, Groenhart, Kus, & Porlezz, 2016; Treadwell & Lowestein, 2016; Hannis, 2018;
Heravi, 2018, Weiss & Retis-Rivas, 2018).

This branch on education and training in data journalism shows an evolution since Domínguez
(2013) raised the absence of DJ training in the academic sphere, not being the only one to share
the concerns of Messner and Garrison (2007) regarding the lack of technical knowledge on the
part of journalists who use databases in their investigations. An example of this is the call for a
radical change in the selection of newsworthy topics (Newsgathering) with statistics
and their interpretation to improve democratic debate (Cushion, Lewis & Callaghan, 2017),


the study of identifying connections between heterogeneous data sources so that data
journalists can obtain useful information (Chanial et al., 2018), and research


around the difficulties of data journalists in accessing public data
(Appelgren and Salaverría, 2018; Álamo, Cruz & Elías, 2018), as well as the ever-present


concern for training journalists in new knowledge and skills in DJ
(Bradshaw, 2018; Gray, Gerlitz, & Bounegru, 2018; Splendore et al., 2016).


In recent years, project-based teaching approaches have been proposed for
generating journalistic stories (Graham, 2018), which had already been recommended in earlier


years by practitioners invited to academic conferences (Grasso, 2016). These are advances in
the education and training sector for data journalists that have been accompanied by


technological improvements for its practice (Bonaque et al., 2015), such as the automation of
databases (López-García, Toural-Bran, Pereira-Fariña & Barbosa, 2009), database


and statistics management software (López-García, Toural-Bran & Rodríguez
Vázquez, 2016), artificial intelligence (Broussard, 2015), and the automatic extraction of


data from statistical spreadsheets (Cao, Manolescu & Tannier, 2017), which shows
us how there has always been a special emphasis on data literacy understood as


technical, computational and statistical development (Nguyen & Lugo-Ocando, 2016). However,
a shift in approach is emerging through studies such as "Data infrastructure


literacy" (Gray, Gerlitz, and Bounegru, 2018), which proposes viewing data literacy
beyond professional competencies, taking into account the data infrastructure


that makes it possible, and thus cultivating a sociology, culture and politics of data in
training programs.


**IV.2.2. Sociological perspective in DJ studies**


The latest educational approaches mentioned in the previous section are part of a
current that studies data journalism from a more sociological perspective.


Research studying reporters' habits, their source-use practices and
institutional norms (Wihbey, 2017), the impact of commensuration processes in DJ


(Lowrey & Hou, 2018), and the use of DJ as a tool to increase data literacy
among information professionals (Vrabec, 2015) are just a few examples of this current. Many other


studies pay attention to aspects such as the practices of reporters in the digital age
(Veglis & Bratsas, 2017), the retrospective and prospective view of media in our current context


(Yaang, Chen, Zhu & Destech, 2017), and the evolution of DJ and its tools (Vállez & Codina, 2018),
with some authors from this perspective also seeking to generate new classifications of data journalism


(Stalph, 2018), to define new cultural trends and practices through the study of
mobile device metadata in the context of the evolution of network society


(Rodríguez & Soengas, 2017), and to study its sustainability in the near future (Stalph &
Borge-Rey, 2018).


These studies stem from the growing popularity in academia of one of the three
strands that according to Fink and Anderson (2015) were part of the research agenda in 2015.


At that point, these scholars divided DJ research as follows (Fink
& Anderson, 2015:1–2):


The first strand, which for a long time has included the majority of studies in
computational journalism, focuses primarily on professional journalists and


points toward practical concerns (e.g.: Cohen S., Li C., Yang J., et al. 2011;
Daniel A, Flew T, & Spurgeon C 2012; Nguyen 2010; Hamilton JT, & F Turner 2009). The


second strand maps the infrastructures and spaces that enable the connection between
data scientists and journalists, focusing on the relationship between DJ and the rhetoric and


institutional structures of the open-source software movement (Lewis, S. C., & Usher, N.
2013), the technology department of the New York Times (Royal, C. 2010; Weber, W., &


Rall, H. 2013), the organizational roles of data journalists in Chicago (Parasie,
S., & Dagiral, E. 2012), and public press collaboration as infrastructure (Ananny,


2013). A third strand historically documents current developments, examining the
links between DJ and other earlier forms of data-driven news production


such as CAR (Parasie and Dagiral, 2012; Powers 2011).


As can be seen, these 3 strands retain a certain relevance in the current research agenda,
although the growth in the number of DJ studies in recent years has more to do


with the proliferation of research of a more sociological nature, which we could
locate as part of the second strand identified by Fink and Anderson in their study. In this regard,


studies on ethics in the practice of DJ have become increasingly important
(Bradshaw, 2014; Craig, Ketterer, & Yousuf, 2017; Shilina, 2017) and its challenges


(Del Campo Lozano & Chaparro-Domínguez, 2018), as well as those that
relate DJ to: big data (Heer & Kandel, 2012; Lima, 2012; Boellstorff, 2013;


Vallance-Jones, 2013; Lewis & Westlund, 2015; Zhi, 2016; 정동우, 2016), network society
(Elías, 2015), networked narratives (Bounegru, Venturini, Gray, J., & Jacomy, 2017), and


open data code (Lewis & Usher, 2013; Tunney & Thomas, 2015; Valencia, Becerra
& Gutierrez, 2016). These types of studies have placed a special emphasis on the


collaborative work of journalists through social networks (Heravi & Harrower, 2015, 2016;
Appelgren, 2016) and the way in which journalists use these networks to influence public opinion


(Dubois, Gruzd and Jacobson, 2018). These are studies that take into account the impact of
computational exploration in changing ways of thinking (Gynnild, 2014) and in which we can observe


changes in cultural practices of journalism already identified by Parasie and Darigal (2013a;
2013b) and Hammond (2017) when discussing data journalism and big data in


comparison to the CAR of previous decades.


Some of these studies seem to intertwine at least two of the strands
identified by Fink and Anderson (2015), such as those analyzing the ethical implications of


visualizations (Cairo, 2014), the ethical and legal aspects of data journalism in
Tatarstan (Doroschuk, Gurianova, and Mantashashvili, 2017), or the ethics of DJ in the codes


of Spanish media outlets (Díaz-Campo, Chaparro-Domínguez and Segado-Boj, 2016).
However, in the vast majority of these studies one can observe how the relationship between


journalists and other professionals—such as designers, infographers and programmers—is
in some way related to their object of study, with notable studies on the


relationship between journalists and hacktivists (Baack, 2018), and those that, by studying big data
as a phenomenon, have concluded that it enables DJ practices through


shared computer code and research methodologies reproducible by other
cultural agents and journalists (Broussard, 2016).


**IV.2.3. Data visualization in DJ and its connection to other branches of knowledge**


Many of the studies with a more sociological perspective within communication and journalism studies
have grown out of a first wave of articles that focused on


visualizations in DJ and their origins. Studies attempting to link the visualizations
of the early twentieth century in the United States with the new visualizations of


DJ (Anderson, 2017), together with others examining the multidisciplinary nature of
information visualization (Do Carmo Botelho, 2017), are a direct consequence of this


wave, which has produced a broad consensus on the multidisciplinary nature of
information visualization:


"The multidisciplinary nature of information visualization is today widely
agreed upon in both professional and academic communities: data analysis,


information design, storytelling, among other topics, are common motivations
in this field. The study of this cross-fertilization, evident in the way concepts


are defined, varies according to the perspective adopted, representing an
important and much-needed contribution to the critical mass of a relatively recent area


of knowledge" (Do Carmo Botelho, 2017:1).


Sociological concepts such as cross-fertilization, which seek to delve deeper into
realities that blend perspectives from various areas of knowledge, have permeated


the study of data visualization in DJ—or other objects of study in which DJ
data visualization is fundamental—with research ranging from the analysis


of the tools that make it possible (Wiedeman, Yimam & Biemann, 2018), to
socioeconomic investigations drawing on their use (Smith, 2016). In this regard, the


perception of users of interactive infographic information (Burmester, Mast, Tille, &
Weber, 2010), the use of DJ visualization techniques in geolocation of


information (Sandoval-Martín & Espiritusanto, 2016), work in geographic spatial science
(Brunsdon, 2014; Salovaara, 2016), the visualization of news in conflict geographies


(Corby, 2014), and its relationship to the democratization of data (Sánchez-Bonvehí & Ribera,
2014) have required that studies of visualization in DJ incorporate categories and


concepts from other fields of knowledge. These studies have been accompanied
by work concerned with the need for other disciplines to assist journalism


in the correct visualization of data (Rodríguez & Devezas, 2015; Sidiropoulos,
Konstantinidis & Veglis, 2016; Reilly, 2017), and in recent years have pointed to the


visualization and linking of DJ data as the "new order" in new journalistic enterprises
(Dowd, 2016), which has complicated the study of news data with


perspectives from many other fields of knowledge that use this technology:


"At the start of the twenty-first century, Tim Berners-Lee envisioned linked data, known as
Web 3.0, as the basis for semantic search engines and resource sharing. However,


linked data is now used in ways unimaginable at the time, especially
across news enterprises, media corporations and advertising companies. The


use of data in news-gathering has also extended to mobile phones, live media,
and unmanned aerial vehicles, adding to the complexities and tensions in media and


contemporary journalism" (Dowd, 2016:97)


Some studies have even gone so far as to examine from a quasi-historical perspective how
interactive visualizations reflect particular social aspects, such as a work by


Dominguez (2013) that analyzed the implications of the economic crisis in the iconographic
production of certain Spanish media outlets between 2007 and 2013. In this way we see how one


of the fundamental aspects of DJ has been among the most affected by
multidisciplinary study in the effort to understand new realities in big data as a socio-cultural


phenomenon, which is intimately linked to the need for sociologically grounded terminology in order to
encompass different approaches and generate results from them.


**IV.2.4. The hybridization of different fields of knowledge in the research agenda on DJ**


The hybridization of different fields of knowledge is not exclusive to visualization
in studies on data journalism. The article "An illusion of interactivity: the paternalistic side


of data journalism" (Appelgren, 2018) is a perfect example of this
when it asserts that DJ is a hybrid combining elements of engineering culture and journalism:


"technologists question paternalistic design elements that journalists consider
justified in their decision-making as part of what journalism is" (Appelgren,


2018:308).


From those studies that searched within big data for ways to make sense of

Yuan, 2018). A paper presented at the Thirteenth Ibero-American Congress
on Artificial Intelligence IBERAMIA 2012 stood out in this regard at the start of the


present decade, as it highlighted how data journalism techniques could be used in
the analysis of hydrological data (Molina, 2012). Along similar lines we can observe


research on the use of DJ for political and electoral analysis (Teruel & Blanco,
2015) and the use of data journalism skills for digital political campaigns


(Fuller, 2018).


Many of these studies are based on fieldwork in countries such as Sweden
(Appelgren and Nygren, 2014; Appelgren, 2014), Spain (Ferreras, 2016; La-Rosa & Sandoval

Trottier, 2015; Hermida and Young, 2016), USA (Fink and Anderson, 2015), England (Borges

Uruguay (Rodríguez, 2016); Australia (Wright & Doyle, 2018), China (Zhang & Feng, 2018),
Korea (임종섭, 2016; 이긍희, 2017; 남재일 & 오대영, 2018), India (Nirmala & Aram, 2018);


as well as in several countries at the same time, such as Brazil and Greece (Charbonneaux &

some way incorporate at least three key dimensions in the incorporation of data into
DJ practices, dimensions developed from mediation theory by


Lesage and Hackett (2014): the problem of scale, transparent working, and access to open data.


**IV.2.5 Cultural hybridization, open data and democratic values in DJ**


Diversity of approaches in DJ studies collected in books and compilations such as the
monograph "Periodismo de datos" (Blanco Castilla & Quesada, 2016), which have accompanied


a large number of manuals over recent years (Bounegru, Chambers & Gray,
2012; Crucianelli, 2013; Egawhary and O'Murchu, 2013; Pery, 2016; Davies, 2018), have


linked data journalism to democratic values. Studies on the public's perception
of data journalists as promoters of democratic conversations


(Boyles & Meyer, 2016) are one example of this, as are studies that
addressed the links between social networks, DJ and activism seeking democratic


accountability (Casero-Ripollés, & López-Meri, 2015).


This connection of DJ to democratic values aimed at improving society has been studied
by incorporating one of the mythologies of big data, as the title of the following study


indicates: "Opportunities and challenges of data journalism: computing for humanity:
information technology for the advancement of society" (McGreor & Halevy, 2014). However,


there is no shortage of research challenging the mythologies of big data, such as
that by the academic Carlos Elías (2014) on the truthfulness conferred by data, since


deontological corruptions can arise that deliberately distort reality. This is something
McNair and Flew (2016) also addressed by asserting that data have led media experts toward


interpretation by intuition, which has led some experts to call for more independent
scrutiny of news (Cushion, Lewis, & Callaghan, 2017) and of


algorithms, applications and big data (Flores, 2018). [6]


In many of these studies it has also been observed how access to open data and
the participation of activists mentioned by Appelgren and Nygren (2014) and


Radchenko and Sakoyan (2014) are crucial for the development of data journalism, in which
the hybridization of different professional profiles (Hermida & Young, 2017) has manifested itself in


the creation of multidisciplinary data journalism teams. In this context of collaborative work,
the separation between technician and journalist is increasingly narrow (Degan, 2013), and as


Parasie and Darigal (2012) affirmed, the participation of programmers in newsrooms
in social movements related to free software and open government initiatives


has had a considerable bearing on this process of cultural hybridization.


It is in this way that the importance of transparency in the practice of DJ has gained increasing
importance since the first studies by Hamilton and Turner (2009) on algorithmic accountability


and the implications of data protection laws in
these contexts (Rodriguez, 2009). Academic theses at various universities that have


studied the relationship between open data and big data in DJ (Mariño, 2012) and that view
open data as a fundamental aspect of data journalism (La Rosa, 2015), have since then


accompanied studies ranging from algorithmic accountability

**V.1. Data journalism is, above all, journalism: journalistic values in DJ**


One of the first things that all Spanish data journalists emphasized in the
interviews is that data journalism is, above all, journalism. Without journalism, all that would remain


would be techniques for collecting, systematizing and analyzing large quantities of data
and their corresponding visualizations. There would be no relationship whatsoever between these techniques and the


intrinsic values of journalism. Frank Belyeu, editor of the CEPID, stated that DJ is
in-depth journalism, distinguishing it in any case from investigative journalism (IJ)


and from precision journalism on the basis of method, but "in the end all of it [DJ, IJ and PJ] is doing
in-depth journalism, it is investigating." In this regard, all interviewees made


numerous references to the intrinsic values of journalism, highlighting how DJ has reinforced
these values.


For Adrián Blanco, of _Elconfidencial.com_, "DJ has brought much greater rigor to
journalism (…), greater accuracy and makes it possible to produce other types of articles and investigations that


you could not demonstrate before"—a rigor that data journalist Paula Guisado of _El Mundo_
relates to the requirement of fact verification, asserting that "good DJ replicates the


good values and good practices of journalism in general." In the case of Belén
Picazo, of _Eldiario.es_, it is a rigor directly linked to new technologies:


"now there are technological improvements, which means [DJ] can be more rigorous." This is a view
shared by her colleague at _Eldiario.es_, David Ruiz, who emphasized that in


data journalism "it is something that has always been done: digging into data and sources,
analyzing. What is different is that now we have much better tools, we have much


more experience than before."


Another point highlighted by several interviewees was the need to verify
information. In fact, Adrián Blanco noted that if "you have data that are tangible, that


are supposed to come from a source, and you take it for granted that they are true… many times
they are not," and DJ helps to verify this. As Paula Guisado noted, DJ "includes


positive aspects such as the requirement of fact-checking, since in data work it is
fundamental to review the process."
