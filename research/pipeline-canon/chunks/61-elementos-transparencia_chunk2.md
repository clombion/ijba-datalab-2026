One of the few studies that examines transparency in DDJ is that of Rodrigo Zamith


(2019). By analyzing about 150 articles from _The New York Times_ and _The Washington_


_Post_ in the first half of 2017 using the concepts of transparency, interactivity,


diversity, and provenance of information, Zamith (2019) concludes that the DDJ practiced in


everyday work is not open and shows itself to be highly dependent on official sources. The investigation


also showed that it is rare for data journalists themselves to carry out the


collection of information. The author therefore understands that DDJ still needs to be improved,


characterizing it as a practice that is evolving but not revolutionary.

The results of Zamith's research converge with the critique made by Träsel


(2014): by omitting the original statistical models and databases, data journalists and their respective outlets fail to encourage the public auditing character that


transparency seeks to enable. The author illustrates that, in the case of science —


probably the main reference for DDJ —, articles are peer-reviewed and submitted to methodological evaluation by others, validating or not the findings made. "In the case of DDJ, access to the original databases would allow the audience and other


reporters to conduct their own analyses and judge the validity of the conclusions derived from a given journalistic piece" (TRÄSEL, 2014, p. 128).


It is clear that, in the face of political and public health issues, such as the Covid-19 pandemic itself, pressure increases for the opening of public data and methods used in analyses. In a study on DDJ coverage of the pandemic, Brenol and Träsel


(2020) analyzed reports using DDJ techniques and detected, among the results, the


lack of availability of consolidated information about Covid-19, pointing to the still


present difficulty of obtaining access to public information in Brazil. In this way,


according to the authors, journalists not only exposed these limitations but also adopted


a critical tone regarding access to data. There is a fear that this scenario is not


exclusive to the Covid-19 pandemic, but indicates greater opacity in the Brazilian state,


despite advances in digital public transparency in recent decades.

It is observed that, in recent years, journalistic initiatives anchored in the


principle of transparency have emerged. Journalists themselves, as we will see below, present


this characteristic markedly in their professional _ethos_. In the wake of collaboration and


the hacker ethic diagnosed by Träsel (2014) in Brazil, transparency adds itself as


a value invoked by the data journalism community.

In Brazil, the _Vortex Media_ initiative, launched in October 2019 by Diego Escosteguy, emerged to offer what it claimed to be a "journalism in motion" under the


discourse of "radical transparency of methods and intentions" (GEHRKE; BENETTI, 2020), but


never revealed its forms of financing and partially ceased operations two


months after its launch. As a result, the team of data journalists, one of the main differentiating features of the new enterprise, had only a short trajectory.

On January 6, 2020, after the closure of the _Vortex_ data team, the former editor of that small group, journalist Sérgio Spagnuolo, created together


with Alexandre Orrico the _Núcleo Jornalismo_. The outlet's mission, according to its own


page [89], is to place transparency at the center of everything in order to foster the use of "[...] mainly public data, through investigations, analyses, and applications that will inform


and shed light on public policies, institutions, and political and social events"


(NÚCLEO JORNALISMO, 2021, n.p.).


In this context, _Núcleo_ seeks to practice, internally, the same transparency it demands


from public authorities. "[...] the transparency we want from institutions is also what we must apply internally. This means corporate transparency (who funds us and who we are) and process transparency (how we do our journalism)" (NÚCLEO JORNALISMO,


2021, n.p.). To achieve this, the journalistic company declares a commitment to the systematic referencing of sources, including descriptions and links; transparency about public policies; and explanation of the importance of a topic.


In the United States, the digital native _The Markup_ [90], which describes itself as a nonprofit institution, created its own methodology to cover the area of technology and how


the decisions of major players (_Facebook_ and _Google_, for example) affect life in society. The _Markup Method_, according to editor Julia Angwin (2021), consists of the detailed publication of the methodology used in the reports. There are three main steps toward the scientific method: the elaboration of original data, peer review by specialists, and the communication of the data.

**5 ELEMENTS OF TRANSPARENCY: METHODOLOGICAL PATH AND RESULTS**


Monitored by the press worldwide, the Covid-19 pandemic demanded an explanatory and specialized coverage from journalists. Data-Driven Journalism


teams were called upon, at first, to count and contextualize the


number of confirmed cases and deaths from Covid-19. The lack of tests and reliable data


proved to be a problem in different countries, challenging journalists to work with


the available information and to acknowledge the limitations of official statistics. Mainly


in the second year of the pandemic, the numbers on vaccination also began to be


consulted, adding challenges inherent to the quality of public data [93] and the need


for scientific knowledge to understand and explain the efficacy of vaccines.

In Brazil, the federal government became the biggest obstacle to qualified journalistic coverage, especially at the beginning of the pandemic. At the time, the Ministry of Health


presented a limited dashboard [94], using only aggregated data and


disregarding the progression of the disease in municipalities. It also ignored the need to record a


historical series of cases and deaths. This type of longitudinal record is essential for


understanding the progression of Covid-19 in different parts of Brazil and adopting strategies for combating the spread of the virus.


In early June 2020, President Jair Bolsonaro was responsible for promoting a kind of "data blackout," ordering the Ministry of Health to delay


the timing of information releases — which harmed the press and the compilation of


numbers for news programs — and to modify the Covid-19 dashboard, limiting


public access to daily data without reporting the accumulated total of cases and deaths.

**5.1 Multiple case study and as illustration**


This thesis, with its qualitative approach, presents two main methodological inspirations: the multiple case study (STAKE, 2006) for the analysis of phenomena, and the


hybrid modality of the case study as illustration (MACHADO; PALACIOS, 2007) adopted by researchers of the Online Journalism Research Group (GJOL) at the


Federal University of Bahia (UFBA). Both procedures are particularly useful for


the systematic observation of digital empirical objects still in development.


In the hybrid methodology of the case study as illustration, three main stages


occur, according to Machado and Palacios (2007, p. 343): a preliminary review of the bibliography and analysis of journalistic organizations; delimitation of the object with the formulation of hypotheses and the case study combined with research within journalistic organizations; and, finally,


the elaboration of categories of analysis for processing the collected material and conceptual definitions about the objects of study. "Our methodology allows the researcher,


on the one hand, to review the current bibliography on the object and, on the other, enables this


conceptual production to be tested in specific case studies." In this thesis, I take the


hybrid methodology as an inspiration because there is no observation of the productive processes within journalistic newsrooms as such. Here, I analyze only the product.


The multiple case study (STAKE, 2006), in turn, is used to examine


a phenomenon that can be explored from different angles or parts. This is


an effective approach for the analysis of phenomena. In this thesis, the phenomenon studied


is transparency in Data-Driven Journalism, whose intentional sample


(GIL, 2019b), which is non-probabilistic, involves the selection of cases that are similar to each other. In the


present research, this materializes through informational journalistic products, namely news articles, reports, and data-driven projects.

Although in this methodology each case is studied in a distinct manner, the aim is


to understand how individual cases can contribute to the understanding of the phenomenon


as a whole. In the multiple case study, the object intended for study is called


the quintain (_quintain_, in English), formed by singular cases that share some


characteristic in common. In this study, all the news articles or reports that make up the


_corpus_ have elements of transparency as a common thread. In general, four to ten cases are selected to compose an investigation guided by this method. This is because, in a single product, it becomes difficult to find all the characteristics one intends to study.


For Stake (2006), one of the advantages of adopting the multiple case study is the


fact that it can take into account the context or the system in a broad manner, characteristics that


are sometimes lost in the conventional case study. Both, of course, present specific limitations — both the case study and the multiple case study correspond


to a particular reality and do not present external validity, even though they may recur in other circumstances. This type of method is therefore adopted to understand


a specific phenomenon belonging to a particular frame. Thus, it is an alternative


for obtaining answers motivated by a central object that is not necessarily broad.

At this point, the hybrid methodology proposed in the case study as illustration


(MACHADO; PALACIOS, 2007) converges with the purpose of the multiple case study methodology. As there is no exhaustive description of the objects as occurs in the conventional case


study, the case study as illustration is a stage in the methodological process used to illustrate arguments and demonstrate the validity of or refute hypotheses. In this


way, this methodology presents a somewhat open format, which allows for


future formulations to encompass aspects hitherto disregarded by researchers.


As indicated earlier, the empirical corpus of the thesis is made up of informational materials published in 2020 and 2021 by Brazilian reference newspapers and digital natives, with the Covid-19 pandemic as their main topic. The analysis period of the thesis corpus begins in March 2020, when the first


reports on the topic were detected, and ends in August 2021, due to the thesis deadline, since


the coverage of the Covid-19 pandemic has no set end date. I analyze only


journalistic material already published, without taking into account the stage of news production.


To select the texts that make up this convenience sample, I conducted, throughout the pandemic, free browsing and systematic observation that allowed me to identify, at the product level, aspects related to transparency in journalism in light of


theory. And, for the purposes of methodological rigor, I observe guidelines for corpus composition


for qualitative research, in which materials present only one thematic focus and, therefore, relevance, homogeneity — the analysis is restricted to texts, for example — and


synchronicity, which refers to the presence of the time frame in history (BAUER; AARTS, 2015).

Regarding the _corpus_ selected to compose the multiple case analysis, the segmentation is quite clear: synchronicity is ensured by the selection of texts referring to the

**5.2 Transparency and its levels: organizational and operational**


Unlike its precursor known as Computer-Assisted Reporting (CAR), in which data were treated as just another source without necessarily taking center stage in the report, Data-Driven Journalism includes


transparency as a key aspect of its processes and results (CODDINGTON, 2014),


since, as we noted earlier, it is anchored in a philosophy of open source


and collaboration (TRÄSEL, 2014), derived from the community of developers and computer scientists.


For transparency to sustain itself as an **operator of an ethical practice** and a **discursive resource**


**of legitimation**, it must, strictly speaking, be an element that resonates both at the organizational level and at the operational level — in this case, it must also be reflected in


the product. This means that, ideally, it is not enough for journalists to adopt and demonstrate clarity in their methodological path. Journalistic companies must


offer clarity on broader topics, such as the error correction policy, the


diversity policy adopted in the company, and, no less importantly, their sources of financing. Knowledge about the origin of funding tends to be a good ally for


audiences in evaluating the type of coverage made (or ignored) by a newspaper.

As we will see below, it is unlikely that a single journalistic outlet will present, on its own, all possible and viable elements of transparency. Even though Covid-19 coverage is significant at the operational level, it is necessary to consider that the publication of


reports is part of a broader context of decisions and editorial processes present


in the newspaper as a whole. For this reason, it is impossible to ignore this prior stage, inherent to editorial decisions that reverberate throughout the entire organizational structure (the organizational level of transparency), and not only in the informational content of Data-Driven Journalism (the operational level of transparency).
of Data-Driven Journalism (the operational level of transparency).

Furthermore, the use of one or more elements of transparency does not guarantee,


necessarily, that the media outlet is attentive to the scientific premises linked


to Data-Driven Journalism, or that it follows all the methods suggested for this practice. The elements presented below aim at the systematization of knowledge and exemplification, and do not intend to point out that one media outlet is more or less transparent than another.


I present below the topics corresponding to the two levels verified with respect


to transparency in Data-Driven Journalism: **organizational** (regarding the outlet, also


involving aspects about the journalist who works in that organization) and **operational**


(regarding the method).

The listed examples follow the methodological inspiration of the multiple case


study (STAKE, 2006) and the case study as illustration (MACHADO; PALACIOS, 2007).

Due to the methodological choices of this thesis, the elements of transparency are listed


and analyzed individually and exclusively — that is, without the detailed explanations that normally occur in the case study. In some cases, each element


will have only the most significant example for its section — as recommended


by the multiple case study methodology, for which the understanding of a phenomenon


does not demand quantitative but qualitative exploration; there are some exceptions, however, in which more than one case is presented per element for contextual purposes.


It should be clarified, finally, that I am not evaluating whether a given element of


transparency is consistently used and updated within a journalistic company.

This research, like other qualitative studies, represents a broad snapshot of the moment at which the analysis was undertaken.


**5.2.1 Organizational level: the newspaper**


At the organizational level, transparency appears as a **discursive resource of legitimation** — that is, the degree to which a newspaper shows itself as transparent through its strategies


of engagement with audiences. In this section, I present eight elements related to the


posture of the professional and the media outlet: **byline, profile, and expertise**; **contact**;


**error correction**; **sources of funding**; **editorial guidelines and editorial principles**;


**diversity policy**; **privacy policy**; and **type of content**.


**5.2.1.1 Byline, profile, and expertise**


Indicating the name of the professional responsible for reporting a story is


typically a basic requirement present in editorial guidelines. As a transparency strategy, however, it must go further, also including information about the journalist's profile, such as work previously developed by them; alongside this description, aspects of their own training should appear, indicating their credentials.


If the skills and knowledge of the author of a report are publicly known,


the reader will be able to judge the level of specialization of the professional and


how prepared they are to cover a given topic.


For this reason, in this section, we present three characteristics that are intertwined: the


**byline**, which refers to the clarity of disclosure of the name of the person responsible for the


text; the **profile**, which concerns the possibility of accessing a page of the history of other reports published by that same person; and **expertise**, which shows the


competencies developed by the professional. Not all journalistic outlets will present all of these characteristics; the most common is to offer only the reporter's name,


without further detail.

entitled _TrateCov indicava cloroquina e outros remédios sem eficácia a quase todos pacientes_ [TrateCov Indicated Chloroquine and Other Ineffective Drugs to Almost All Patients], published by _Jota_ (MARCELINO, 2021).

**5.2.2 Operational level: the method**


At the operational level, transparency appears in the journalistic product as an **operator of an ethical practice** — that is, the degree to which it demonstrates, in its methods and procedures, information about sources consulted, original documents, and permission for


verification and reproducibility of the methodology. In this section, I present seven elements:


**publication date**; **documents**; **data sources**; **degrees of uncertainty**; **reporting markers**;


**methodology**; and **reproducibility and open source**.


**5.2.2.1 Publication date**


The publication date of content is an indispensable element, as it encompasses


the idea of currency and of a verifiable truth, subject to constant updates.


Although it may seem obvious, the publication date of a news article or report is not always


clearly displayed on a news portal, or does not appear at the top of the page for quick and immediate identification by readers.

More than just reporting a basic piece of data, the availability of the publication date


is particularly useful for combating disinformation, since one of the main ways


this occurs in the digital environment is through the recirculation of old content. During the Covid-19 pandemic, this becomes even more dangerous, since outdated data


can be circulated out of context.


In **Figure 21**, the _Núcleo_ site, which specializes in Data-Driven Journalism,


presents a highlighted notice, emphasizing that the article in question, entitled _Na verdade, a qualidade da conexão à internet parece ter melhorado na quarentena_ [Actually, Internet Connection Quality Seems to Have Improved During Quarantine] (SPAGNUOLO, 2020), was published a year before the date the reader accessed it.

**5.2.2.6 Methodology**

The methodology of DDJ has scientific inspiration and therefore typically begins with the detection of a problem or hypothesis testing – even though this level of detail is generally not communicated. The methodology tends to be presented in narrative form and sometimes makes use of hyperlinks, but does not always involve the disclosure of programming code.

**5.2.2.7 Reproducibility and Open Source**

Inspired by the scientific strand of Precision Journalism and with a community of professionals mobilized by the collaborative ethos present in the computing field, Data-Driven Journalism tends to make the source code used to build projects and analyses available in its reports. This means that the lines of code that enable the creation of algorithms are documented and made available for consultation.

In addition to the possibility of describing the working method only in narrative form, as demonstrated in topic **5.2.2.6**, one of the distinguishing features of DDJ is precisely organizing its methods in code format and, in this way, enabling the reproducibility and verification of information. Thus, other journalists and newsrooms are able to verify the content produced, as in a kind of peer review, and eventually reuse the code; it also allows readers with technical knowledge to evaluate the construction of a data narrative and the relevance of its information. The availability of source code occurs in both news articles and more substantial journalistic projects. As we will see ahead, this topic is in dialogue with the idea of open science.

**6.1 Transparency Protocol in Data-Driven Journalism**

The Transparency Protocol in Data-Driven Journalism that I propose here is inspired by earlier studies (SILVA; MAIA, 2011; TRÄSEL; LISBOA; REIS, 2018) and is grounded in the theory associated with transparency in journalism, developed by various authors (KARLSSON, 2010; KOLISKA; CHADHA, 2018; PLAISANCE, 2007; KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014; LISBOA; FRANZONI, 2018; MEIER, 2009; WEINBERGER, 2009). It unfolds from the transparency elements found in news articles, reports, and projects conducted using Data-Driven Journalism techniques during COVID-19 coverage, already examined in this thesis.

As extensively discussed in the previous chapter, we understand that transparency is anchored in two guiding principles: as an **operator of ethical practice** and as a **discursive resource for legitimation**. Its pragmatic character, in turn, presupposes the existence of two levels: **organizational**, linked to the communication outlet where the content was published, and **operational**, where attention is directed to the step-by-step reporting process.

Together, the two levels present 15 elements. The first, organizational, has the following parameters, in alphabetical order: **byline, profile and expertise**; **contact**; **error correction**; **funding sources**; **editorial guidelines and principles**; **diversity policy**; **privacy policy**; and **content type**. Regarding the second level of analysis, **operational**, the transparency elements to be observed are the following: **publication date**; **documents**; **data sources**; **degrees of uncertainty**; **traces of reporting**; **methodology**; and **reproducibility and open source**. **Table 4**, presented in the previous section, allows each of these parameters to be observed in detail.

This protocol aims to assist researchers and interested parties in evaluating the transparency levels of a journalistic organization and its published reports. If the researcher considers it pertinent, they may implement a scoring system according to the fulfillment of the criteria detailed below, which will be presented in alphabetical order. To carry out the first stage, information available on the homepage of the newspaper's website under study must be collected. The second level comprises the evaluation of Data-Driven Journalism reports. In this case, fulfillment of the indicators – with emphasis on sources and methods – is evaluated through the reading of the material to be analyzed. Although this protocol is intended for the analysis of data-driven reports, it can be adapted for other contexts and journalistic productions.

Below, we present each element and a set of indicators, in the form of questions, that guide the evaluation of transparency for that topic. New indicators may be added based on characteristics specific to the object.

**1) Organizational level**

**Byline, profile and expertise**

- Is the text signed?
- Does it allow access to a profile with the author's biographical information?
- Does it allow access to other works by the author?
- Is there an indication of the author's level of knowledge on the topic?

**Contact**

- Does the newspaper's website display a contact list that is easy to locate and access?
- Are there multiple forms of contact (email, messaging apps, telephone, etc.)?
- Is there an ombudsman or reader's representative?
- Is there an audience or reader engagement desk?
- Is there a reader comments section? Is this section moderated by the outlet or does it only redirect to a social media network?

**Error correction**

- Is there a clear policy on error correction?
- Is the description of this editorial policy easy or difficult for readers to access?
- Is there a dedicated section for corrections?
- Is there an indication in the original text that some type of error occurred?
- Is error correction made clearly in the original text (indicating, for example, the date and time it was made)?
- Is there a button indicating the possibility for the reader to report errors?
- Is there a concern on the part of the outlet to provide adequate space for communicating errors, so that the correction receives significant visibility and actually functions as a correction?

**Funding sources**

- Does the newspaper's website explicitly state its funding sources or sponsors?
- Is there an accountability section indicating revenues and expenditures?
- Is it possible to access a detailed balance sheet?
- Is the revenue generated by audience subscriptions monitored and made available?
- When part of a corporation or conglomerate, does the newspaper provide broad reader access, indicating the other companies that compose it?

**Editorial guidelines and principles**

- Does the outlet clearly present its editorial principles?
- Is transparency one of the principles cited by the newspaper?
- Is an editorial style guide available to readers?
- In this guide, is there concern with making the journalist's ethical conduct and their relationships with sources explicit?

**Diversity policy**

- Does the newspaper present a diversity policy regarding journalists, topics, and sources?
- Is there a diversity desk to address this policy?
- Are there resources or tools for readers to monitor compliance with the diversity policy?

**Privacy policy**

- Does the reader have access to clear information about the handling of their personal data when accessing the newspaper's website?
- Does the outlet detail the type of information collected by the website?
- Does the newspaper present data on its audience profile?

**Content type**

- Are there markers that allow readers to identify whether content is informational or opinion-based, distinguishing it from paid content?
- In the case of opinion content, is it clear that the text is an analysis made by a specialist?
- Are there text markers indicating editorial content?
- Does the outlet invest in projects or initiatives for reader literacy, so that readers can identify differences between information and opinion?
- Does the outlet invest in projects or initiatives for reader literacy, so that readers understand the news and report production process?

**2) Operational level**

**Publication date**

- Is the publication date of the text visible?
- Is the date of any updates or corrections visible, with differentiation from the original publication date?
- When content has been recirculated, is there a visual highlight referring to the original date, alerting the reader to observe this point?

**Documents**

- Are the documents used as sources clearly identified?
- Is there access to the full text of these documents, with the possibility of consulting the original materials?
- Are the documents made available alongside the body of the text, for full reader access, or via hyperlink, directing to another page?

**Data sources**

- Are the data sources clearly identified?
- Does the text provide access to the original source, such as the spreadsheets used, including an external hyperlink?
- Are there supplementary materials to the original source (such as data dictionaries), providing the reader with the context of production and a guide for understanding the variables adopted?

**Degrees of uncertainty**

- Does the text make explicit the gaps and the provisional nature of the information consulted?
- Does it present the limitations of the data and methods employed?
- Is there clarity about what still needs to be verified or explained?

**Traces of reporting**

- Does the text make the journalist's reporting and verification work visible?
- Is there mention of the difficulty in accessing the data or the path taken to find them?
- Does it make explicit the unsuccessful attempts to contact an important personal source?
- Does it clearly state when it was necessary to invoke the Freedom of Information Act and the waiting time for a response?

**Methodology**

- Does the text include a methodological note?
- Are the procedures for data selection, collection, and analysis described (e.g., filters applied to spreadsheets)?
- Is it possible, through the methodological description, for the audience to replicate the analysis undertaken?

**Reproducibility and open source**

- When a programming language is used, is the source code disclosed, allowing for its reproduction?
- Does the source code provide access to the step-by-step process employed in selection and analysis, including the files used?
- Is the source code available in an open repository and does it offer the possibility of reproduction under a _creative commons_ license (whereby use occurs freely upon citation of the original source)?

**6.2 Proposed Method with a Focus on Transparency and Reproducibility**

In early 2017, under the coordination of professors Luciana Mielniczuk (_in memoriam_), Ilza Girardi, and Marcelo Träsel, we tested in the Environmental Data Journalism course (PPGCOM/UFRGS) what we intuited to be a fundamental component of this type of practice: the formulation of a hypothesis as a starting point for journalistic investigation and the methodological description of the data-gathering process in databases for subsequent verification and reproduction by peers and readers.

The course began with theoretical contributions on digital and data journalism and environmental journalism, a stage that preceded journalistic practice and the generation of a product – the _Guaíba Dados_ portal [127], which in addition to being a repository became an extension project at UFRGS. The graduate students, myself included, investigated at the time different segments related to Lago Guaíba (such as politics, industries, pesticides, etc.) that could be observed using public data. The lake is fundamental to the water supply of the Porto Alegre Metropolitan Region, and was therefore chosen as the main topic.

All sections of the website, made available at the end of the course, share a common textual structure: _proposal_, _data found_, _paths_, _dead ends_, and _future paths_. This guiding thread is, therefore, a method developed for data-driven productions. The _proposal_ is linked to the idea of a hypothesis in which the development of a given topic is speculated. The _data found_, in turn, corresponds to the process of searching, analyzing, and contextualizing quantified information. After communicating the

data found, the text for each segment indicates the _paths_, i.e., the methodological description of the step-by-step process adopted to obtain the indicated results, and the _dead ends_, showing the limitations of the data or points the report could not cover. Finally, there are the _future paths_, suggesting proposals for continuing the investigation.

The establishment of a common method for all was fundamental to ensuring uniformity of reporting among the graduate students themselves, since the work was developed in pairs and trios within the same editorial section. Had any one of us followed their own method instead of a pre-established step-by-step, we would not have obtained the same results. Thus, an exhaustively descriptive documentation helped avoid doubts and setbacks.

The proposal depicted in **Figure 32** presents a series of steps for journalistic work. It starts from a **problem** (1) and a **hypothesis** (2) for which information must be sought through **research** (3), and whose databases present a **context** (4) about how they were produced and where they are situated. After examining the feasibility of using the information found, the process moves to the stages of **collection** (5) and **analysis of information** (6), which require the elaboration of lines of **open source code** (7) for subsequent **reproducibility**. The code is related to the **product** (8) to be created – which could be a data visualization or some interactive content that highlights data for readers, for example – and to **replicability** (9), which concerns the possibilities for use and reuse of the developed material, including information about any conflicts of interest between the journalistic organization and the topic covered. Finally, all stages of the method are subject to **supplementary documentation** (10), i.e., the detailed description of the step-by-step process, offering readers information that can help in understanding the journalists' decision-making during an investigation. An analogy can be made with the package insert that accompanies medications: it is through this insert that the public gains access to the recommendations and caveats inherent to the product.

This methodological proposal carries two important concepts on its horizon: science is the guiding thread of the methodological step-by-step (arranged on the vertical axis), while transparency (arranged on the horizontal axis) accompanies the consolidation of two main stages: **open source code and reproducibility** and **supplementary documentation**. In the case of transparency, it can be said to activate both categories discussed in **Chapter 3**. It concatenates aspects such as **operator of an ethical practice**, insofar as it reveals its working methods, and transparency as a **discursive resource for legitimation**.

Before presenting each stage of the method, I explain some fundamental concepts for understanding the proposal as a whole. **Open source** refers to the free consultation, distribution, and modification of a product, without the need for payment to use it. Currently, the programming languages _Python_ and _R_ are among the most widely used in journalism and data science because they can be used freely and because they have a large community of users. The more users dedicate themselves to learning and using a programming language, the more libraries or packages – sets of instructions that seek to solve a specific problem, according to Machlis (2019) – are made freely available. Furthermore, the spirit of collectivity allows for the exchange of information and ideas about solving problems in code [131], and both _Python_ and _R_ feature user-friendly interfaces for developing code, such as _Jupyter Notebook_ and _R Studio_, respectively.

Open source is an essential functionality for the **replicability** or **reproducibility** of the method. To explain the differences between the terms, I draw on the explanation by Trielli and Diakopoulos (2019): while _replicability_ presupposes the use of code for new analyses, or could even refer to a methodology explained narratively, _reproducibility_ involves the execution of step by step, allowing for the complete verification of results and their validation, similar to the premises adopted in open science – which will be discussed at the end of this chapter.

The method proposed in this section is intended for the production of data-driven informational content and is executable in cases where the reporting team has extended time to investigate a topic. It should be noted that a journalist will rarely be able to develop all work stages alone (BRADSHAW, 2017), although it is advisable that the professional be familiar with the entire news production chain. In the late 1960s, when he implemented a hypothesis-testing and analysis methodology for covering the Detroit protests, the then-reporter Philip Meyer had the assistance of academics to design the research.

The proposal I present here aims to serve as a guide for the critical analysis involved in using data and documents in DDJ at its various stages, emphasizing that data function as a source and should therefore be consulted with caution. Thus, it is not mandatory for all journalists to learn a programming language – since mandating it could even generate work precarity and task overload – but it is a fact that this is a path that brings Data-Driven Journalism closer to open science, and that it is advantageous to know the possibilities and limits inherent to code development. **6.2.1 Problem**

Problem is the term that characterizes the scenario of journalistic investigation on a topic of public interest. According to Guerra (2008), the detection of a problem represents the beginning of the journalist's interpretive journey [132], i.e., when the object of investigation is established. Once the scenario to be covered is characterized, the professional is ready to create hypotheses related to the problem. The solution to this problem, then, is the final understanding that is presented in journalism in the form of a news article, report, or investigative project.

The choice of the word _problem_ in this methodological proposal is intentional insofar as it approximates science. In academic research, Gil (2019a) associates the existence of a problem with a topic or scope that is insufficiently explored and needs to be investigated in a given field of knowledge. Furthermore, in science, it is necessary to find ways to test a problem – which can be structured in the form of a question – through empirical verification, with rigorous and systematic procedures. One strategy for thinking about the problem is to initially indicate a theme to be addressed. In this way, in a second step, it becomes easier to establish the problem derived from the theme.

The problem must have some type of delimitation – temporal or spatial, for example – so that it is viable to develop it into hypotheses. The approach that establishes a problem is common in entrepreneurship processes. In this case, the idea is to find solutions to problems that are relevant to the target audience and that generate an impact on social life; research strategies are used to measure this reach. The mapping of problems, in this context, also allows entrepreneurs to fail and quickly correct the course of their action by reflecting on and adjusting the problem and the hypotheses derived from it.

132 The journalist's interpretive journey, says Guerra (2008, p. 189), corresponds to the journalist's knowledge of a fact. It is the movement conducted by the professional to "[...] weave together and establish a set of connections among things, persons, texts, and audience expectations [...]." In addition to the journey itself, classified by the author as the act of interpretation, journalists make use of what he calls "cognitive techniques," i.e., the knowledge they activate when interpreting facts.

For journalism, thinking from a problem-based perspective is also useful for better understanding the audience to whom the product is directed. The objective of initiating a process with such reasoning brings an approach that differs from the elaboration of the traditional journalistic assignment (_pauta_), which is generally pre-defined with angles and sources, without the journalist being able to truly understand the panorama of a situation to be investigated. From my perspective, after the problem is identified, it becomes viable to create and test hypotheses.

**6.2.2 Hypothesis**

The formulation of a **hypothesis** is central to scientific investigation. It functions as a kind of guide for the researcher, directing focus toward specific questions to be answered, and for the reader, who has a kind of plan and set of premises for understanding the results presented (CRESWELL, 2010). In the daily practice of journalism, the preparation of an assignment (_pauta_) normally substitutes the hypothesis, since it emerges from an uncontrolled observation of reality rather than from a previously established theoretical system (MEDITSCH, 1992).

Data-Driven Journalism, however, presents some differences relative to journalism practiced daily. It produces _news-as-relations_ (NIELSEN, 2017), i.e., contextualized content that seeks a more lasting character, unlike the ephemeral _news-as-impressions_ (i.e., alerts about a new event, normally published on digital social networks) and _news-as-items_, which have a lifespan of approximately 24 hours.

The hypothesis has been suggested by researchers to guide investigative journalistic projects (HUNTER _et al._, 2013) that naturally demand a greater amount of reporting time. In general terms, it is believed that journalism could benefit from structured scientific thinking, starting from a guiding statement to test the existence of a phenomenon within a slice of reality. According to Gil (2019b), hypotheses are statements that are tested and accepted, or tested and refuted, according to the results obtained, and can either relate cause and effect or possess an explanatory character. Observation, consultation of other studies, and theoretical knowledge are at the basis of their formulation.

In journalism, according to Hunter _et al._ (2013), the use of hypotheses is beneficial because it allows a very specific aspect to be verified. In this way, instead of working with an entanglement of data about which the journalist must generate meaning without a starting point, the reporter makes some assumptions that will need to be tested. If it is concluded that there is no newsworthy fact, one can start over and examine other hypotheses and possibilities. It also represents a way of quickly changing the course of an investigation.

In previous research, we suggested that the formulation of hypotheses is foundational for investigations in public databases (GEHRKE; FREITAS, 2021), called open sources by Hunter _et al._ (2013) and also known as documentary sources (GEHRKE, 2018a). Even in cases where the primary source consists of documents leaked by third parties, journalists are able to formulate initial hypotheses and modify them later.

The adoption of hypotheses in Data-Driven Journalism can be considered an inheritance from Precision Journalism, as it integrates the first steps toward fulfilling the scientific method in journalistic practice, allowing events to be measured (MEYER, 2002). As reported in **Chapter 3** of the thesis, the coverage of the Detroit protests in 1967 by Philip Meyer involved hypothesis testing and data collection through a survey conducted with residents rather than simply consulting scholars or gathering residents' impressions. To prepare the questionnaire and design the sample to be surveyed, Meyer had the support of academics from the University of Michigan and Wayne State University (DAVIS, 2021). As stated in the previous section, it is not expected that a single journalist or group of journalists will carry out all the expected procedures. Collaboration, a hallmark of DDJ, can be a positive aspect to be explored with the assistance of scientists accustomed to these processes.

Wihbey (2019) has an optimistic view of journalism's approximation with science: for him, the use of hypotheses in journalism means that this practice is apt to receive and recognize new evidence and, in this way, operate from a scientific perspective – open and closer to people's reality, establishing spaces for dialogue with the audience and acknowledging, above all, the provisional nature of data and information about an event, eventually showing its uncertainties. In line with the idea of scientific practice, the information-search stage, explored below, is called research.

**6.2.3 Research**

Hypothesis testing, as explained in the previous stage, depends on the availability of data – which is generally supplied by public documentary sources (GEHRKE, 2018a; GEHRKE; FREITAS, 2021) or open sources (HUNTER _et al._, 2013), including the passive dimension of the Freedom of Information Act.

**Research**, at this stage of the method, is related to the search for information, i.e., the understanding of the topic to be addressed and how data can be obtained. There is, therefore, an initial phase of abstraction and critical analysis, in which it is necessary to identify which potential sources may assist in testing the formulated hypothesis. In a second stage, it is possible to think about the forms of the extraction itself, as we will see in the following sections.

When the hypothesis that the journalist wishes to test is not supported by existing public data, one alternative is the creation of an in-house database, structured within journalistic organizations. For this, it is advisable for journalists to think systematically about how to collect information in their area of specialization, such as politics, economics, health, and sports. Bradshaw (2013) suggests that journalists reflect on their practice as if they were librarians, cultivating the habit of systematizing and storing information that may be useful later.

By way of illustration, we can think about how this suggestion could be implemented in the area of public security. It is feasible, for example, for reporters to systematically record data on fires, as suggested by Holovaty (2006) in the early days of Data-Driven Journalism, or as carried out by the team at _Diário Gaúcho_ in 2016 to circumvent delays in the release of public data by the Rio Grande do Sul Public Security Secretariat on robberies, homicides, and robbery with homicide (GEHRKE, 2016b). The creation of a collaborative spreadsheet developed among the team's own members allowed the newspaper to cover not only specific cases of violence, but generated a dossier after five years of data collection.

Creating one's own database for subsequent consultation and testing of new hypotheses is viable in various areas. Depending on the situation, journalists may even involve the audience in a collective reporting process (_crowdsourcing_), as in the mobilization carried out by the newspaper _La Nación_ in Argentina regarding the 40,000 intercepted audio recordings that contributed to the journalistic investigation into the death of prosecutor Alberto Nisman [133] in 2015. In addition to enabling hypothesis testing independently of whether official data exist, this type of collection allows, above all, the journalist to identify patterns – a fundamental action in data analysis.

Whether public or developed within a journalistic organization, a dataset must be accompanied by comprehensive documentation so that the audience is able to understand how the information was collected and how it is presented. The sources, in turn, present their own **context** of production and the limitations inherent to the process.

**6.2.4 Context**

In this methodological proposal, **context** is associated with the scenario of production and availability of data. As Lückman and Fonseca (2017, p. 163) state, context refers to "[...] the idea of an interrelation of circumstances that accompany a fact or situation." By choice or due to limitations in structuring their own databases, data journalists frequently use official sources. In general, they point to official statistics as reliable, rarely verifying the numbers used (LAWSON, 2021). When analyzing coverage of the humanitarian crisis, for example, the author found that, rather than verifying the numbers, journalists tend to rank the sources that provide the data, ultimately calling upon the institutional source they consider most reliable.

In my perception, the lack of verification and contextualization of data is a move detrimental to journalism – while Data-Driven Journalism seeks to escape a declaratory practice, it ends up reproducing this flaw by other means when it relies solely on official sources, especially if it disregards their context of production. As pointed out by Lawson (2021) in the title of the article discussing this topic, in doing so professionals "hide behind the sources." Decades earlier, Tuchman (1972) had already

133 Available at: https://www.niemanlab.org/2017/04/how-the-argentinian-daily-la-nacion-became-adata-journalism-powerhouse-in-latin-america/

detected this type of procedure in journalistic newsrooms, calling it "objectivity as strategic ritual" – i.e., when professionals seek to protect themselves from possible errors by using direct quotations from the sources consulted, in an uncritical manner.

The insertion of information into a spreadsheet or other type of document involves an initial human effort and is therefore subject to mistakes. Thus, data must be treated as any documentary source and, for this reason, must be evaluated in their totality. I argue that the context of data must be studied as soon as the journalist finds the evidence they intend to work with. One alternative for avoiding mistakes is to create a kind of "data biography," as suggested by data scientist Heather Krause (BELISÁRIO _et al._, 2020). This means that, in addition to locating the statistics to be analyzed, the professional must pay close attention to the documentation accompanying these data (generally called data dictionaries) in order to understand the use of variables and the methodology adopted in a study. It is therefore necessary to record aspects concerning the origin, collection, scope, and limitations of the figures.

Belisário _et al._ (2020) recommend an approach similar to what would be used with human sources, in which the professional should ask: what are the source's interests, areas of expertise, and activity? What is the track record of this source as an informant? Is there a connection with people or groups capable of generating conflicts of interest or some type of controversy? Is this source typically consulted by other journalists? Rusbridger (2020) adds: where does the data come from, who collected it, and why? The answer to these and other questions makes up the data biography, which represents the contextual detailing of structured information. This is an approach similar to the principle of source criticism (STEENSEN, 2019), a premise according to which every source has a bias – and discovering what that bias is, by contrasting it with other sources, is essential to the reporting and verification process in journalism.

In my dissertation (GEHRKE, 2018a), I analyzed the sources in 60 news items produced using DDJ techniques. Of the total of 315 sources activated, 213 were documentary sources – i.e., sources that presuppose a different approach from traditional human sources, since they cannot be consulted through interviews but must be observed without being subjected to direct questions and answers. This data suggests that there is recurring use of official databases as raw material in DDJ, particularly of repositories with already-available data. Historical series, descriptive statistics, indicators, rates, and reports represented 67.61% of the sources found in the survey.

Considering the context of data production is one of the seven principles identified by D'Ignazio and Klein (2020) in the work _Data Feminism_, whose proposal aims to discuss the uses and limitations of data, beginning by recognizing that power is not distributed equally in the world. To weigh the context of production, then, is related to acknowledging that data are often products of unequal social relations, and recognizing this scenario is connected to conducting an accurate and ethical analysis. The authors report, for example, that minorities are often underrepresented in demographic data. When these data are used uncritically, without recognition of their limitations, they end up being taken as a faithful portrait of reality, potentially generating news, products, and other forms of knowledge that do not correspond to reality. In such cases, it is necessary, for example, not only to look at the structured data, but at their forms of collection – the limitations are sometimes found at a stage prior to the publication of this information in public repositories.

To prevent data from being used without rigorous analysis or out of context, researchers such as Diakopoulos (2019, p. 11) have been discussing the term "_algorithm accountability_," i.e., knowing and recognizing how data are collected, structured, and what kind of information they can generate. In highly automated processes involving the use of machine learning, this kind of algorithm audit becomes even more fundamental for detecting biases of gender and race, for example. The presence of algorithms in various social processes also alters people's relationship with the decision-making process – which includes criminal justice and education, for example.

The analytical effort is essential for continuing to the following stages of this methodological proposal, avoiding errors and problems in data collection.

**6.2.5 Collection**

Having observed the context of data production and availability, as well as the documentation accompanying them, one can finally proceed to the **collection** stage of structured information. Data extraction can occur in an automated manner – in which a programming language assists in creating a sequence to perform data scraping – or non-automated, such as the simple download of a spreadsheet (or set of spreadsheets).

On this topic, Bradshaw (2013) suggests that the data journalist try to _think like a computer_ when collecting information. This means that if the database will likely be used in future queries, it falls to the journalist to think of ways to automate the extraction so that there is no duplication of effort. In such situations, it is possible to work with forms such as Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) present in large databases.

Data collection must be related to the problem and the hypothesis proposed at the beginning of the method, and therefore may involve combining different sources. Data collection is not always simple and direct – in some government portals, for example, data are made available in closed formats (such as .pdfs, which make it difficult to extract information). In these cases, we say that the data are available, but not accessible.

At this stage of obtaining information, the journalist must also be attentive to how the information is organized to guard against any typing errors or missing information. This requires, therefore, database cleaning, involving the detection of errors before proceeding to the analytical stage. For this, there are technical manuals that assist with these details.

As explained in **Figure 32**, it is from this stage onward that one can begin thinking about structuring open working code, providing a detailed account of how the data were collected and how they will be analyzed subsequently.

**6.2.6 Analysis**

The **analysis** stage concerns the results that will be obtained from the cross-referencing of structured information. The problem and the hypothesis formulated at the beginning of this method are defining, and associated with them may be the use of some simple statistical strategies, such as calculating the mean, mode, or median, or even the use of correlations and complex models such as certain types of regression.

Data analysis requires multiple precautions. It is first necessary to verify whether the figures form part of a dataset that exhibits a normal distribution. Numbers that do not belong to this orderly distribution are called _outliers_ and, although journalism has a tradition of reporting singular cases, a single absolute or isolated number is rarely able to explain a phenomenon. Therefore, as described in the previous stages, one must consider that numbers are situated within a broader context, and in order to make sense they sometimes need to be subjected to the use of rates, indices, and other strategies for standardizing results.

When spreadsheet software is used for data analysis, formulas, filters, and pivot tables are generally employed – processes that tend to remain hidden in the results. When the analytical stages are structured in a programming language, the tools allow the step-by-step to be commented and documented, making the procedures clear and accessible so that they can be replicated or reproduced later. For this reason, the use of programming code is recommended in this methodological proposal, as we will see in the next section.

Data analysis also presupposes that there is specific theoretical knowledge about a topic of investigation, since the cross-referencing of variables only makes sense when prior knowledge about a subject exists. For this reason, it is suggested that journalists reflect on their work stages with the assistance of specialists in the area they wish to cover.

**6.2.7 Open Source and Reproducibility**

Spreadsheet software and open-source programming languages such as _Python_ and _R_ are among the most common working tools used by data journalists (BELISÁRIO _et al._, 2020). However, as seen in the previous topic, spreadsheets, even if simpler to handle, present limitations in terms of reproducibility, since the step-by-step of filters and formulas adopted is generally not documented. "Formulas – which do most of the analytical work in a spreadsheet – are only visible when clicking on a cell. This means that it is harder to review the actual steps taken to reaching a given conclusion" [134] (LEON, 2021, p. 149).

In this methodological proposal, the use of **open source code and reproducibility** constitutes the sixth methodological step because it brings together different stages: **collection**, **analysis**, **product**, and **replicability**. For the process to be fully reproduced, all programming code must be accessible and commented step by step. Depending on the complexity of the project, it is common to use different repositories for the source code so that all fronts of work are available for consultation.

**6.2.8 Product**

The stage entitled **product** is situated at the end of this proposal and also presupposes the use of open source code and methodological detailing. Although visualization (the production of charts and maps) is one of the most common results in Data-Driven Journalism, it is not the only alternative for illustrating informational content of this nature, which also encompasses the creation of other interactive resources. For this reason, we believe the term is more appropriate for this stage of the method, since visualization can be undertaken in earlier stages when one wishes to consult the distribution of data in a sample.

Regardless of the product to be generated, data continue to be its raw material. For this reason, the stages presented previously in this methodological proposal call for caution and analytical performance to ensure the reliability of the analyses and products generated. To prevent a visualization or other material from suggesting mistaken patterns, Cairo (2019) notes that rigor in data verification and care in organizing information are essential in this process. The choices of what one intends to show or omit, then, go far beyond aesthetics.

The creation and methodological disclosure of a product, as stated, is related to the journalistic organization's willingness to open up its processes. This "letter of intent" can be identified in the replicability stage.

**6.2.9 Replicability**

**Replicability** in this methodological proposal refers to the narrative description of decisions made in Data-Driven Journalism, including aspects concerning the editorial transparency of an outlet. The interpretive journey (GUERRA, 2008) previously mentioned, which corresponds to the journalist's work in obtaining information, also appears here: the establishment of relationships between data and sources, as well as the hierarchization of content, are operationalized by professionals in a rational manner. Thus, the choices made in coverage can be justified – and even communicated to the public when more transparent procedures are advocated.

When a newspaper has a policy of using open source code and making it available in a public repository, it is naturally aligned with the rules for sharing information – i.e., it allows the code to be verified and reused by third parties. Under a _Creative Commons_ license, open source code normally published on _GitHub_ can be reused as long as the original source of the collection and/or data analysis is cited. In this way, it represents an opportunity for communication outlets to add information about the nature of data collection, including any conflicts of interest regarding a topic or investigation, as is customarily presented in scientific research.

Both replicability and the other stages of the process must be detailed and made available to the audience as supplementary documentation, opening the possibility of consultation across the entire journalistic process.

**6.2.10 Supplementary Documentation**

In this methodological proposal, the step-by-step of the entire process must be written and made available in open source along with **supplementary documentation** that shows the choices made by data journalists in relation to a coverage topic.

In addition to incorporating an educational approach raised by Leon (2021), so that non-specialists can engage in a technical reading of the procedures that occurred, it becomes interesting to include premises from Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). Practices belonging to HCI are used mainly in computing research and are identified by Trielli and Diakopoulos (2019) as beneficial for journalism. For reproducibility to be possible, the authors list several points that must be explained in the code and/or in the accompanying material: are the methods explained? Are the data and codes used by the authors available online? Is there an explanation of how to run the code or replicate the results in the future?
Public databases generally come accompanied by data dictionaries with a detailed breakdown of variables, and I propose that the topics covered in this chapter can be compiled into a format of supplementary documentation to facilitate the reproducibility of the method, with a sufficiently detailed description of the variables. Repositories normally present only a document titled "_read me_" with limited information about the variables used in the analysis. The idea, in this case, is to provide the maximum number of subsidies.

Thorough documentation is key to ensuring that everyone can follow the same method, which facilitates, for example, the process of networked investigation, frequently carried out in Data-Driven Journalism – as in cases such as _Panama Papers_, _Paradise Papers_, and _Implant Files_, coordinated by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ).

**6.3 Limits and Perspectives for Reproduction**

Data-Driven Journalism is identified by Coddington (2014) as the specialty with the greatest potential for methodological openness when compared to earlier practices such as Computer-Assisted Reporting (CAR), and even to contemporary practices such as Computational Journalism.

While CAR is still strongly associated with investigative journalism, in which some sources are rarely revealed, Computational Journalism is naturally tied to the use of algorithms and software that are often opaque. In general, they are used for at least four major processes: prioritizing, classifying, associating, and filtering (DIAKOPOULOS, 2019), and the automation of procedures often operates on the same data, involving only already-available information and avoiding certain coverage topics for which no information exists.

DDJ places the analysis and publication of data at the core of its practices – even though part of the press continues to keep detailed materials to itself, deviating from basic principles of transparency. In his thesis, Träsel (2014) mentions that by not presenting this information, professionals are failing to encourage the public audit of their work, which would occur through the Open Source movement and peer review, as happens in science. Mazotte (2021), in turn, points out that by omitting the step-by-step, journalists deprive readers of the means to understand a news story – a form of comprehension that is even more desirable at a time of information abundance.

As seen in the section on open source, one path toward public technical understanding is the adoption of a method that unites computing language and human language, allowing programming code to be commented and explained step by step, including documentation of the packages and data used. A qualified audience can be decisive in pointing out, even technically, problems in journalistic reporting (DALMASO; MIELNICZUK, 2011). Thus, journalistic transparency goes beyond procedures of the journalistic community directed only at its peers.

The implementation of better transparency practices requires a change in mindset throughout the entire news chain and also demands reflection on the forms of journalism financing. While methodological openness appears to be gaining the sympathy of some data journalists (BEILER; IRMER; BREDA, 2020; GEHRKE, 2020), it is not necessarily a priority in journalistic organizations, especially in the reference press, for reasons of inter-company competition. It is also observed that journalists see themselves as transparent professionals (CHRISTOFOLETTI, 2021), activating transparency as an **operator of an ethical practice**, but that this does not necessarily materialize in their work environments.

In the method proposed in this chapter, I seek to open space for demonstrating any limitations or conflicts of interest on the part of newspapers, since a journalistic organization can simultaneously encourage its team's transparency and act opaquely regarding the relationships maintained with financial and political groups, as found by Christofoletti (2021).

Although any type of change encounters resistance in traditional organizational structures, digital natives have demonstrated that establishing an honest relationship with the audience – including disclosing their funding sources – is a significant part of transparency and the cultivation of trust. Even so, it must be acknowledged that organizations will only share the information that is in their interest, particularly that which reinforces their appearance as an organization concerned with transparency, in this case activated as a **discursive resource for legitimation**.

Despite the problems identified in relation to transparency, disclosing information, even partially, is a fundamental step toward bringing Data-Driven Journalism closer to the scientific endeavor because it involves offering the audience the same evidence that journalists had access to when writing the news stories. As explored by researchers such as Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) and O'Connor and Weatherall (2019), the public is rarely in a position where it can access, firsthand, the same evidence used in the practice of reporting.

In this respect, a quotation from O'Connor and Weatherall (2019, p. 44-45) is quite illustrative:

The reason to believe that there was a hole in the ozone layer was not because scientists said that there was such a hole; it was because multiple devices, designed and carefully calibrated to measure ozone levels in the upper atmosphere, located in different places and making their measurements in different ways, detected substantially reduced ozone levels [140]

Methodological transparency in journalism produces a similar effect: it shows readers how a given piece of evidence was measured or obtained. The journalistic authority that explains why this occurred is still valid – otherwise, even the presentation of transparency elements would make no sense – but the fact that the public can independently verify the validity of the evidence presented can be seen as an important factor in the relationship of trust.

The presentation of evidence evokes the senses of scientific reproducibility and replicability, to which Data-Driven Journalism approximates, and which has always been the objective of Precision Journalism. Both concepts are linked to transparency and work with the idea of open source. While replicability allows for the consultation of a code or narrative for new experiments, reproducibility seeks to guarantee the full reproduction of such procedures. "While publishing the code used in a computational analysis might provide a degree of transparency, the lack of underlying data, for instance, impedes reproducibility, since algorithmic transparency has various layers" [141] (TRIELLI; DIAKOPOULOS, 2019, p. 2).

Data-Driven Journalism approximates the perspective of open science through the proximity of principles and through methodological reproducibility. In response to what they call a "replicability crisis" in quantitative research, Dienlin _et al._ (2021) seek to implement an agenda of this type of research in the field of Communication, establishing some premises for the practice of open science: _1) publication of materials, data, and codes_, so that independent verification by other scientists is viable; _2) pre-registration of studies and submission of reports_, ensuring that research questions are registered before data collection, thereby seeking to avoid biases; _3) conducting replications_, i.e., carrying out new tests based on the methods adopted in prior research; _4) collaboration_, which presupposes greater exchange among researchers, including proactive sharing of data and study materials; _5) promotion of open science skills_, encouraging the registration of studies and the use of replicable data; _6) implementation of transparency and openness norms_ to guide authors and editors; and _7) incentive for open science practices_, which may be associated with promotion and professional-level funding.

Beyond the fear of exposing anonymous data used in studies involving the use of codes, and the concern that there may be a significant increase in workload for scholars – limitations already enumerated in the research by Dienlin _et al._ (2021) – the use of premises associated with open science must be considered within a broader context. From the perspective of implementing open science protocols in Latin America, Oliveira _et al._ (2021) problematize the parameters listed by Dienlin _et al._ (2021), arguing that aspects such as replicability, acceleration, and efficiency are associated with the standardization of science and include only the context that historically benefits the Global North, disregarding less privileged regions where the distribution of knowledge is asymmetric.

Thus, Oliveira _et al._ (2021) propose that open science be grounded in participation, social justice, and the democratization of knowledge. They therefore advocate for approximating this concept with the opening of scientific production, offering universal access to information and knowledge, which they understand as a basic right – if society finances science, it should have free access to it. Finally, the authors advocate for greater societal engagement in this type of practice, fostering a relationship of trust-building between the public and the researcher. This is related to what Fox _et al._ (2021) argue, for whom individuals, communities, and social groups should participate actively in the scientific process.

Although they recognize that replicability and methodological transparency are important principles of scientific practice, Oliveira _et al._ (2021) fear that the need to provide complementary material, the existence of new repositories and tools for publishing articles in _preprint_, may foster a kind of platformization of science, generating the most diverse costs, which would put underdeveloped countries at a disadvantage. It therefore seems to me that a contextual analysis of each situation is warranted for open science to be practiced.

Given the brief readings carried out to this point, it is possible to perceive that Data-Driven Journalism can approximate the premises of open science through methodological reproducibility. According to Trielli and Diakopoulos (2019), there are three main points for this to occur: 1) validation of findings; 2) prevention of errors; and 3) maximization of content circulation. The three axes are connected to the idea of verifiable truth with which journalism has its primary commitment, opening information to be verified by peers and the general public. Furthermore, the very fact of making data available potentially leads journalists to redouble their verification efforts before publication, avoiding the creation of new noise in the saturated digital space.

**7 FINAL CONSIDERATIONS**

The COVID-19 pandemic, which by the end of 2021 had caused the death of more than 600,000 Brazilians and 5.4 million people worldwide, is an ongoing global event. Consequently, a journalistic event of vast proportions. In addition to provoking social changes, I understand that the pandemic accelerated processes that were beginning to be rehearsed in journalism and that were until then manifesting primarily in the discourse of professionals, such as **transparency**.

As a value for journalism and with a view to the opening of reporting procedures and editorial decisions that manifest in the sphere of the product, transparency is a principle that makes sense primarily in initiatives that take science as methodological inspiration, as is the case of Data-Driven Journalism, which proposes to generate a type of explanatory and lasting knowledge – such as _news-as-relations_, as per Nielsen's (2017) classification. Even when based on a factual event, news linked to the DDJ mode of production differ regarding the ephemeral journalistic content routinely produced for digital journalism. They presuppose the fulfillment of a method that begins with a problem and culminates with the generation of a product whose results advocate for the opening and sharing of knowledge through the disclosure of methodology or adopted source code, maintaining the collaborative mode of operation (TRÄSEL, 2014). Transparency is also a fundamental part of practices such as _fact-checking_, being a normative value and a component of the reporting methodology (SEIBT, 2019).

Derived from Precision Journalism, DDJ is embedded in a context of abundant information and disinformation in the digital environment (BOCZKOWSKI, 2021), in which various types of content circulate primarily through platforms (BELL; OWEN, 2017), within a scenario of journalism in transformation (DEUZE; WITSCHGE, 2020) and networked journalism (HEINRICH, 2011). In Brazil, authors such as Mielniczuk (2003), Machado (2006), and Barbosa (2007) have been recording the transformations of digital journalism and the protagonism of data, which permeate everything from the production process to the circulation of news in this environment. In DDJ, databases are also viewed as sources (GEHRKE, 2018a), seeking to monitor and ensure the maintenance of transparency in public institutions in society and expanding the reporter's power in approaches to personal sources (MEYER, 2002). Even though public datasets are official, there are more conditions for them to be examined in advance, unlike what occurs with public discourse, which is generally analyzed _a posteriori_ in initiatives such as _fact-checking_.

Observing the development of Data-Driven Journalism since 2016, when I entered the master's program at UFRGS, I perceive an increase in demand for courses, disciplines, conferences, and prizes related to the field. One of the main expectations related to these pursuits had already been detected by Träsel (2014): in the early 2010s, DDJ was the response chosen by a group of Brazilian professionals to circumvent the identity and economic crisis permeating the profession. At the time, the researcher indicated the existence of an intersection between journalism and other fields of knowledge – the journalist's _ethos_, then, was composed of aspects also associated with what was observed in computing, such as cooperation and the pursuit of open data. This type of conduct, linked to the availability of open data and programming code, as well as to assistance and information exchange for problem-solving, indicated that transparency could develop in these spaces.

In addition to the pandemic itself, whose rapid development required the use of public data with greater speed and assertiveness in journalism, distrust of the press and the excess of disinformation in countries like Brazil sparked my interest in observing how Data-Driven Journalism, as a set of knowledge and practices applied to reporting and inspired by the social sciences, was addressing the issue. COVID-19, as a journalistic event, seemed to represent a favorable period for analyzing how DDJ behaved when faced with the possibility of adopting a more open posture toward the public – after all, especially at the beginning of the pandemic, there were limitations and uncertainties regarding data availability and even the stance of institutions, such as the Ministry of Health, in disclosing information. We therefore decided to investigate which transparency elements appeared in Brazilian press coverage of COVID-19 – it was definitively a moment of doubt for Data-Driven Journalism, but also of possibilities.

Since entering journalism research, I align myself with the strand that considers it a form of knowledge that reveals reality differently from other social practices (MEDITSCH, 1992; 1997), halfway between common sense and science (PARK, 1940). In previous work (GEHRKE, 2017), I drew on the theory constructed by Meyer (2002) on Precision Journalism to bring Data-Driven Journalism closer to a form of knowledge very close to science, which features method, systematization of results, and transparency. More recently, we revisited this definition to add discussion of post-truth and disinformation, arguing that, as a mode of informational journalism belonging to the category of _news-as-relations_ (NIELSEN, 2017), DDJ has the potential to generate knowledge from access to public data, and through new forms of engagement with the public (TRÄSEL; GEHRKE, 2021). To do so, it not only narrates the facts, but contextualizes them, exposing aspects of the phenomena with which it engages.

I argue in this thesis that DDJ is grounded on three fundamental pillars: **truth**, **objectivity**, and **transparency**. In summary, this means beginning from an approximation with the facts through structured techniques and procedures – this mode of access to reality corresponds to objectivity as method – and then presenting one's ways of thinking and doing, i.e., transparency. The verifiable and provisional truth with which journalism works, and which COVID-19 showed to be more provisional than ever, can be apprehended and openly communicated through discourse.

Investigating the theory associated with transparency led us to classify it along two main axes: as an **operator of an ethical practice** and as a **discursive resource for legitimation**. While the first is associated with a deontological stance on the part of the journalist, forming part of the list of basic principles to which the professional must commit in order to carry out their routine practices and maintain the trust covenant with the public, the second discusses transparency as an organizational strategy through which the organization _demonstrates_ that it is transparent, opening up part of its editorial policies and funding sources. In this case, of course, the journalistic organization shows only what it wishes to, selecting the information to which the public will have access and thereby producing an _effect of transparency_.

The theoretical contribution combined with the methodological journey of this thesis allowed me to confirm my hypothesis that, in light of truth and objectivity, **transparency becomes a fundamental value for the practice of Data-Driven Journalism in Brazil**. What was until then manifesting discursively in recent years (TRÄSEL, 2014; GEHRKE, 2020), in a sphere connected to professional _ethos_, could be verified in terms of product. COVID-19 coverage, in turn, served as a springboard for a process that was already underway. In my research, I observe that this acceleration took shape through journalists' initiative, and not necessarily that of the organizations. This is because, among other factors, competition between newspapers is still seen as a source of concern, especially among reference press publications. In other words, the collaboration constituted by the opening of information is not always in the interest of business, even though transparency is a value defended by data journalists.

In the methodological stage of this thesis, to identify the transparency elements employed in Data-Driven Journalism, I analyzed informational materials (news articles, reports, and projects) published from March 2020 to August 2021 by 11 reference and digital-native newspapers, using as methodology the combination of multiple case study (STAKE, 2006) and case study as illustration (MACHADO; PALACIOS, 2007) to access COVID-19 coverage. To study how the phenomenon of transparency manifests in the sphere of the product, I sought to incorporate a diversity of communication outlets and publication dates for the materials. This methodological strategy proved quite fruitful for obtaining a broad snapshot of Data-Driven Journalism coverage during the pandemic, when the knowledge encompassed by these practices was so greatly demanded.

As results of the thesis and fulfillment of the specific objectives, which involved discussion and theoretical deepening of transparency, as well as the mapping of its parameters, I present the 15 transparency elements detected in the empirical analysis, distributed across the **organizational and operational levels**, and here arranged in alphabetical order: **byline, profile and expertise**; **contact**; **error correction**; **publication date**; **documents**; **data sources**; **funding sources**; **degrees of uncertainty**; **traces of reporting**; **editorial guidelines and principles**; **methodology**; **diversity policy**; **privacy policy**; **content type**; **reproducibility and open source**.

Although Data-Driven Journalism has a significant methodological approach, it is not possible to separate its operational sphere from the organizational one. The presentation of transparent practices in news articles and reports presupposes an organizational structure that is at least minimally willing to share with readers how decision-making occurs within the organization – whether through presenting the editorial style guide or by establishing and demonstrating its concern with diversity. Ideally, one would expect the opening of information about its financial sustainability, allowing readers to evaluate any potential conflicts of interest, for example.

For this reason, the operational and organizational levels constitute the submerged part of the iceberg metaphor. **Figure 7** shows that normally only part of the information is available to the audience in news format, but the journalistic processes are more complex and profound than the product, being permeated by commercial and editorial decisions. Even when a reporter is responsible for reporting a piece of content – and therefore signs it, a basic requirement of transparent practices – the intricacies related to its production go far beyond the wishes of a single professional, involving a body of editors and directors.

The 15 aforementioned parameters serve as a guide for what I call the **Transparency Protocol in Data-Driven Journalism**, intended for the analysis of already-published journalistic products. As this is a first systematization and proposition, it seeks to assist researchers, journalists, and anyone else willing to reflect on the implementation or better use of the theme. To serve as a guide, I propose a series of questions for each item, so as to orient its use. Furthermore, some elements are structuring and may therefore carry greater weight in an evaluation. This kind of systematization, as argued by Karlsson (2022), brings transparency closer to fulfilling its main purpose – the approximation between newspaper and audience – since it allows for the implementation of tests and quantifications to understand which values are prioritized and can be implemented or improved.

In another result of the thesis, I offer the proposed **Method for Data-Driven Journalism with a focus on transparency and reproducibility** for implementation in data-driven journalistic initiatives. In this proposal, synthesized in **Figure 32**, the scientific method (vertical axis) is the guiding thread of the methodological step-by-step, while transparency (horizontal axis) accompanies the consolidation of two fundamental stages: open source and reproducibility, as well as supplementary documentation. Like the Protocol, the Proposed Method for DDJ activates aspects of transparency as **operator of an ethical practice** and as **discursive resource for legitimation**. The methodological proposal is comprised of ten points: **1) Problem**; **2) Hypothesis; 3) Research; 4) Context; 5) Collection; 6) Analysis; 7) Open source and reproducibility; 8) Product; 9) Replicability;** and **10) Supplementary documentation**. This proposition seeks to address a theoretical-experimental gap: as identified in the thesis, there are some typologies of methods that serve as guides for DDJ practice in newsrooms, but the existing formulas at this point – although they also generate contributions to the field – do not have the commitment or the proposal to be guided by the scientific method or by transparency. The formulation of hypotheses, for example, is openly advocated by few authors, such as Hunter _et al._ (2013) in a manual for elaborating investigative journalism projects.

I understand that the results of the thesis, structured by the theoretical and empirical contributions that materialized in the formulation of the Protocol and the Proposed Method, generate unprecedented and feasible contributions for the advancement of transparency in Data-Driven Journalism. By fulfilling premises such as those listed in both contributions, I understand that DDJ finally has solid conditions to approximate what Meyer advocated from the mid to late 1960s with Precision Journalism: a strong and meaningful approximation with the premises of science, using hypothesis, research, data analysis, critical thinking, and clear communication of results.

For this reason, I seek, at the end, to make one final theoretical contribution by way of approximating Data-Driven Journalism with open science studies. On one hand, the objectives of both sets of practices are aligned and depend on protocols for their implementation and monitoring of replicability or reproducibility. On the other hand, as cautioned by Oliveira _et al._ (2021), any type of standardization carries a risk of accentuating inequalities, since implementation depends not only on the conditions of the organization or the micro-scenario where it develops, but on the situation of countries or regions where access to knowledge or technology is limited. However, thinking about the Brazilian reality and that of many other developing countries, I arrive at my proposals with the awareness that, depending on the circumstances, their full reproduction will rarely be possible. And this is not a problem: the appropriation of theories and methodologies requires flexibility and adaptation. Their use and transformation is part of the knowledge construction inherent to science.

The existence of critiques of the transparency approach in journalism is undeniable, as discussed throughout the thesis. One of them, developed by Han (2017), concerns the dangers of the dual face of transparency/surveillance, in which people's privacy is at risk in a society that demands excessive transparency from its institutions. Another frequent critique is related to the technological dependency of journalistic practices, which is accentuated in Data-Driven Journalism. Karlsson (2022), for example, critiques the algorithmic opacity linked to the use of software about which little is known.

Data-Driven Journalism seeks to circumvent these issues by giving preference to the use of free software tools and open source code sharing. This is also reflected in research: Diakopoulos (2019) proposes a discussion on algorithmic transparency. However, precisely due to issues of unequal DDJ implementation, this is not always possible. DDJ created and maintained in Brazilian favelas (DE LIMA SANTOS; MESQUITA, 2021), for example, is made viable only because there are free and massively accessible tools, such as _Google_ online forms and the messaging application _WhatsApp_. Behind these practices lie major technology players. If they did not exist, however, entire communities would have no access to data about their own reality to understand their surroundings. While hyperlocal and local journalism seek financing models to ensure their sustainability, this seems to be a way to start producing data-driven reports.

Thus, I understand that opening up as much as possible to public scrutiny and presenting the context of production still seems to be a more viable alternative than simply not producing Data-Driven Journalism and arguing that, given all the limitations, it would become unviable. An essentially critical posture toward transparency cannot be a way of escaping its applicability, or of adopting a stance that, if it is not totally possible, it should not even be attempted. To some degree, there will always be a type of dependency – on software or on official sources – and Data-Driven Journalism is not free from this. Even if it is not possible for a newspaper to use programming language to create journalistic products, there are simple means of beginning to open its processes, such as presenting the methodology (in writing) of what was done and indicating, through external hyperlinks, the origin of the information consulted. Furthermore, the detail in error correction and the ease of communication on the part of readers tends to be a resource valued by the audience (KARLSSON, 2022), and can be implemented by newspapers to maintain trust and credibility. It is necessary to think about viable practices in daily life, since it is not only major journalistic investigations that gain public attention. It is in journalism practiced daily that the relationship of trust and honesty with readers is established.

As stated earlier, the contributions I make in this thesis are part of a pandemic scenario that opens precedents for the expectation and internalization of transparency processes in journalism. I was able to observe, empirically, that some digital natives are more advanced in this regard, grounding their practices on premises of methodological and organizational openness. For other outlets, however, the continuity of this process will depend on a change in mindset regarding digital journalism, its modes of narration and financing. My expectation is that both the Protocol and the Proposed Method can be explored in these spaces. I also hope that the contributions will be used in the classroom by professors who wish to critically discuss the modes of doing and presenting data-based analyses – which need not occur exclusively in specific courses on Data-Driven Journalism; it is possible to insert discussions about transparency and its elements into both theoretical and practical courses. I therefore hope that this thesis also generates contributions for journalism education. Based on my teaching experience developed during the master's, the doctorate, and more recently as a professor of a Data Journalism MBA program, I was able to observe that students feel curious and confident about addressing this theme and reflecting on their own professional processes.

The use of data through scientific pathways in journalism, in addition to contributing to better-informed citizens equipped with statistical information about their own reality and able to practice democracy in a more informed manner, serves as a refuge in times of disinformation. Transparency is the pursuit of honesty in times of all kinds of _fake_. Furthermore, I understand that the opening of methods and processes can reinforce the importance of journalistic verification, since some journalists check with low frequency – or even use uncritically – their numerical sources (LAWSON, 2021). The opening of data and additional information, in this way, can generate greater concern among professionals about the quality and accuracy of the content disclosed.

Naturally, my thesis does not cover all discussions about transparency. Since this principle aims to grant the audience access to information that is normally opaque, I understand that future studies may focus precisely on issues related to the audience's perception of the transparency elements mentioned in this thesis or even new ones that this work could not encompass. As observed by researchers such as Johnson and St. John (2021), some of these items tend to be more relevant for maintaining credibility as perceived by the public.

Finally, I understand that, in an atmosphere constituted by the excess of information and disinformation, it is necessary to recognize the existence of specialized forms of audiences – i.e., niche publics that constitute _audiences_, in the plural, including subscribers or people who contribute sporadically to the financing of a publication. I therefore consider that my thesis serves as the constitution and organization of foundational knowledge with the possibility of application for new research, especially related to the audiences that are part of digital journalism.

**APPENDIX A – STATE OF THE ART SURVEY**

The state of the art survey aims to map and detect theses and dissertations produced in Brazil with which this project is in dialogue. Searches conducted in the Brazilian Digital Library of Theses and Dissertations [142] of the Brazilian Institute of Information in Science and Technology (Ibict) show that a theoretical gap exists in studies that associate transparency with journalistic method.

Regarding the specificity of this thesis's object – Data-Driven Journalism – the results are limited. When combining the terms **transparency** and **data-driven journalism** in the search field, only one result is returned that approximates the proposal of this study. The thesis written by Brenol (2019) and defended at UFRGS brings important contributions on how journalists who routinely use public databases perceive their own work in monitoring the exercise of public power. The transparency of which the author speaks, however, is not related to the clarity of journalistic method, as in this work, but to digital governments and the information that public authorities provide as raw material for investigation.

The thesis by Brenol (2019) engages with the transparency portals of public bodies and information provided through the Freedom of Information Act (LAI). As a result of her research, the author listed four communicative modalities structured from public data: static, interactive, multimedia, and personalized, the last of which makes the greatest use of digital resources and provides a complete user experience, enabling the use of data for users to conduct their own queries. Although the journalists consulted in the research frequently use the LAI and its data request forms, the study indicates that the use of "leaked" documents continues to be a recurring practice.

142 The Brazilian Digital Library of Theses and Dissertations (BDTD) gathers complete texts of theses and dissertations defended at Brazilian educational and research institutions. The definition of search keywords and the initial consultations of this repository took place on 6 and 9 March 2020, at the time of the qualification project for this thesis, and were updated on 27 October 2021. On that date, the portal had a total of 708,800 documents (theses and dissertations). We are interested in observing the results of work produced in the last decade. The research in this database was conducted at the following address: http://bdtd.ibict.br/vufind/.

Since it is a word used in different contexts, even though it refers to the opening and clarity of procedures in opposition to the opacity and obscurity of processes, transparency appears in theses and dissertations in the communication field with different facets. When consulting the combination of the terms **journalism** and **transparency** in the Ibict library, I found 42 results. After an initial screening conducted by reading the abstracts, I selected 16 works that had a relationship with my object of study. The selection of these cases indicates the existence of two axes that stand out in the works: 1) transparency as a journalistic ideal, sometimes associated with the value of _accountability_, which calls for the opening of processes and error correction in order to restore credibility and establish a relationship of respect with the audience; 2) transparency as a characteristic of digital governments that generate raw material through the Freedom of Information Act (LAI) and, consequently, serve as sources for journalists.

I highlight, in the first axis, the investigation by Demeneck (2016). The author discusses the formation of unconventional cross-border newsrooms, which he calls transnational journalism, structured in a collaborative network format and constituting a set of practices adopted for the simultaneous analysis of leaked data and documents. By exemplifying such construction through cases such as _OffshoreLeaks_ and _SwissLeaks_, the researcher identifies elements of objectivity, transparency, and _hacker_ ethics, highly associated with the cooperation expected of the data journalism community. In this context, Data-Driven Journalism techniques serve not only as an operational basis, but renew expectations surrounding the social function of the press.

Still in relation to the first transparency topic, researchers such as Vieira (2014) and Santos (2017) show us that this value is associated with the error correction policy of journalistic outlets – starting from the premise that precision is at the core of this practice – for building credibility and is also related to the quality of the content produced. By monitoring 1,500 news items over a period of 100 days, Vieira (2014) discovered ten types of failures in demonstrating the correction of information, including a lack of transparency. Santos (2017), in turn, studied the reliability and plurality of reports published by _Revista Piauí_. To do so, she drew on the concept of _accountability_, from which the quality assessment [143] can be considered a parameter. The term, which has no faithful translation into Portuguese, is related to "the capacity to ascertain and demand responsibility and transparency in the fulfillment of certain obligations, whether legal or moral in nature" (SANTOS, 2017, p. 44). Also from the perspective of quality and _accountability_, Brandi (2018) examined _Jornal Nacional_'s environmental coverage, finding that factual content takes priority compared to coverage of broad and environmentally relevant topics such as urban planning and its impact.

In a similar context, Santos (2018) brings the methodological transparency of _fact-checking_ as an instrument for procedural clarity and combating disinformation. One of the points raised by the author concerns the transparency of sources – I consider this the main element of clarity that a journalist can offer readers – a prerequisite for the fact-checking practice that materializes through the online availability (via hyperlink) of spreadsheets and other documents providing access to the origin of information. Also fundamental is clarity about a news outlet's funding source – a factor cited by the author that ensures the disclosure of data about how the communication outlet maintains its financial sustainability, allowing the audience to assess whether a conflict of interest exists.

The second axis highlights public transparency practices and open data as sources for journalists, such as those accessible via the LAI. In her dissertation, Pereira (2019) investigated how reporters at two newspapers in Ceará incorporate transparency tools into their routines. In the universe investigated, the author found that two out of every ten reporters use public information for news production, mainly in the area of politics. Pereira does not explore the concepts of DDJ in depth, although she reports the existence of specialized units on the topic.

Other works mapped in the state of the art also draw on the Freedom of Information Act as a central theme, emphasizing the prominence of the legislation as a source for journalists (SOUZA, 2016). There is also an examination of similar legislation around the world (COSTA, 2017) and of the relationships between government transparency communication channels and dialogue with the community and journalists (SANTOS JÚNIOR, 2015; RAMOS, 2017; NOGUEIRA, 2017). The point regarding legislation, however, is of less relevance to this thesis.

When combining the terms **source** and **transparency** in the Ibict thesis and dissertation database search field, given that data can be treated as sources in journalism, 340 results are displayed. However, I selected only three related to the communication field and the topic studied here. These are the works by Pereira (2019), Santos Júnior (2015), and Souza (2016), mentioned earlier. The combination of terms becomes more relevant as the search becomes more specific. The terms **source** and **data-driven journalism**, when combined, return only two studies: my dissertation (GEHRKE, 2018a) and the research by Oliveira (2016). My work represented a first approximation of these two topics, which remain central to the thesis discussion, albeit under other aspects. At the time, I analyzed documentary sources used in 60 news items, classifying them into three types: documentary file, statistics, and reproduction. My aim was to look at the specificities of the sources used in DDJ, which go beyond public databases. The research by Oliveira (2016), in turn, investigates how the adoption of metadata contributes to the development of journalistic information in databases, bringing together the fields of Communication and Computer Science.

Replacing the term **data-driven journalism** with **data journalism** in combination with **source**, four other results are found. Of these four, only two correspond to the Communication field. In addition to the thesis by Souza (2016) on the Freedom of Information Act, there is the doctoral research by Gehlen (2016), who investigated how numbers are addressed in journalism. The author found that data journalists go beyond the everyday use of simple mathematical operations, seeking greater mathematical-quantitative reasoning in the face of database availability, which demands the use of statistics to generate knowledge.

When using only the term **data journalism** in the Ibict search engine, the portal displays 16 results. Even though the term was searched using quotation marks, not all results return the exact term. Only six works actually correspond to the topic, half of which have already been addressed in this state of the art. Two of them (ESTEVANIM, 2016; RODRIGUES, K., 2016) discuss infographics and visualization, and the research by Mesquita (2018) brings information on communicational strategies in newsrooms.

The term **data-driven journalism**, in turn, returns eight works, three of which have already been mentioned in this state of the art. Among the three remaining studies, two (FREITAS, 2018; LORENZI, 2019) have infographics and data visualization as their main topic, an area even more specific within Data-Driven Journalism studies and with no direct relation to the topic of this thesis. What matters here is the research by Träsel (2014), whose contribution is one of the pioneering works in Brazil regarding DDJ. In his thesis, the author identified characteristics of the data journalist's _ethos_ profile, as well as situating this practice as a response to the professional crisis and developed from methods that allow it to be brought closer to journalistic objectivity. Thus, this investigation connects with the topics of my research through aspects such as truth (provisional and verifiable), the objectivity of method, and transparency.

The combination of the terms **objectivity** and **journalism** produced 4,200 results, necessitating a narrowing of the object to better understand the theoretical gap into which this thesis fits. When the terms **objectivity** and **data-driven journalism** are combined – the variation "data journalism" does not produce consistent results in the same search – the theses by Träsel (2014) and Brenol (2019) appear, and only the first actually contains both keywords and addresses the discussion.
