**VI.3.1.2.3. Culture of Transparency**


As can be seen in Table 34, 75% of the sub-topic Culture of Transparency forms


part of the topic Insufficiency of the Law, and 12.5% of the topic Non-compliance with the Law. Only


two publications depart from these topics, both opinion articles: one published in _El_


_Mundo_ on December 19 under the title "Un voto para una España unida, plural y regenerada" [A Vote for a United, Plural, and Regenerated Spain]


and another published in _El País_ on December 14 titled "Ahora no pocos políticos comen el


menú del ministerio" [Now Quite a Few Politicians Eat from the Ministry Menu]. In both, the culture of transparency is used in very different ways.


While the _El Mundo_ editorial calls for a change in the culture of transparency


in Spain in the interest of democratic regeneration, in _El País_, José Luis


Barbería takes a positive view, assuring that the culture of transparency is changing in


Spain, due to the use of transparency in the political programs of political parties


ahead of the December 20 elections. For this reason, the latter article is included in the


minority topic Sufficiency of the Law.

Another notable feature of this sub-topic is that half the publications are


news articles and the other half are opinion pieces. If we remove the two opinion articles already


analyzed from _El Mundo_ and _El País_ in the preceding paragraph, the relationship between the


predominant topics of this sub-topic stands at 8 news articles and 6 opinion pieces. Among the


news articles, only 3 directly reproduce the self-interested voices of political parties: 1 from the PP, 1


from the PSOE, and 1 from Ciudadanos. Two other news articles reproduce the interests of one


private institution (Transparency International) and one public institution (the Transparency Council). In this


way, it becomes evident that the term "culture of transparency" is not widely used among


political parties, appearing in the few spaces where voice is given to


activist organizations such as Transparency International or independent public


institutions such as the Transparency Council. Both the news article that gives voice to the


Transparency Council on July 13 under the title "La cultura de la transparencia no existe


en España. Hay que cambiar la mentalidad" [The Culture of Transparency Does Not Exist in Spain. We Need to Change the Mindset], and the one published on December 10 under the


title "Transparencia Internacional: facturas de la corrupción las pagan los pobres" [Transparency International: The Bills of Corruption Are Paid by the Poor], give


an account of the law's insufficiency and non-compliance, and their interrelation through the culture of


transparency in Spain at that time.

**VI.3.1.2.5. Sanctioning Capacity**


The sub-topic Sanctioning Capacity is another example that shows the willingness of


newsrooms with data journalists to exert pressure on the politics and public policies of


transparency that affect journalism: all the publications in this sub-topic belong to the


topic Insufficiency of the Law and have named authorship, with a majority of reports (50% of


the total) and only 25% reproducing self-interested sources (two news articles). Furthermore, the


only outlet that published nothing in this sub-topic was _El País_, whose editorial line determined the non-publication of information favorable to Podemos and its allied formations, not even when


these governed by consensus together with the PSOE — a situation that neither _El Mundo_ nor


_Elconfidencial.com_ reached despite their editorial lines.

The following reports are examples that addressed the insufficiency of the transparency


law based on its lack of sanctioning capacity and its connection with aspects that


further reveal the law's insufficiency: 1- Alicia Avilés Pozo, titled "Castilla-La


Mancha multará a altos cargos que impidan el acceso a información pública" [Castilla-La Mancha Will Fine Senior Officials Who Block Access to Public Information] in _Eldiario.es_ on


November 10; 2- Marta Belber, under the title "Multas de entre 1.000 y 3.000 euros para los


concejales que no sean transparentes" [Fines of Between 1,000 and 3,000 Euros for Councillors Who Are Not Transparent] in _El Mundo_ on November 23; 3- Jesús Escudero


under the title "Madrid propone inhabilitar a quien no cumpla la ordenanza de transparencia" [Madrid Proposes Barring Those Who Fail to Comply with the Transparency By-Law]


in _Elconfidencial.com_ on November 23. In the case of the publications from


_Elconfidencial.com_ and _El Mundo_, both Escudero and Belber refer to the fines that the


Madrid City Council is prepared to impose on those councillors who meet with


lobby groups not registered in a registry, as well as the creation of a sanctioning regime


for public officeholders, contractors, subsidy recipients, and lobbyists. In the case of


the report by Alicia Avilés Pozo, what was highlighted was not a municipal regulation but a


regional law:

"The draft Transparency and Good Governance Law provides for sanctions of between

and in which Manuel Alcaraz, head of the Department of Transparency, Social Responsibility, Participation and Cooperation, was interviewed. This interview focused on various


topics, but the main idea revolved around the transparency law inherited from the PP and its

The only publications without reproduction of self-interested sources were a news article from September 13


under the title "Los consellers no verán su nombre en las placas de inauguraciones" [The Councillors Will Not See Their Names on Inauguration Plaques]


and a report from October 10 titled "El nuevo negocio de la transparencia" [The New Business of Transparency], both from _El_


_Mundo_. In the news article, H. San Juan reported on the Code of Ethics and Good Governance that


the Valencian Generalitat was preparing to set out the basic rules for all members

religious ceremonies in the institution's name, and the use of commemorative plaques to


extol political figures at works carried out with public money" (De la Torre, 2015).


This news item formed part of the topic Insufficiency of the Law and was based entirely on the draft


of this code, publishing it at the end of the article and serving as an example of news articles with

approved by new political formations.

In the case of the aforementioned report signed by Noa de la Torre, it exposes how the

Valencian Community from October 9 is being exploited by "associations, companies, and

shows how the agreement with Transparency International makes the national law something


insufficient, although it also implies non-compliance with the transparency law by highlighting


how various municipalities attempt to use it to generate business with private companies.

these opinion pieces is the editorial of September 18 in _Eldiario.es_ under the title "Las

perception of public management from a business perspective, highlighting the figure of the

practices in relation to the December 20 Elections from a conservative perspective that

Both articles belong to the topic Insufficiency of the Law and highlight the possible

Two of the news articles belong to the topic Insufficiency of the Law and reproduce self-interested voices


from institutions: one public, the monarchy, and one private, the Confederación Sindical de


Comisiones Obreras (CCOO). Both encourage greater transparency in private companies


to offset the lack of transparency in the public sector. These are two news articles that


contrast with the _El País_ report from November 7 titled "Hacia una nueva

of the audit committees of Gas Natural, Red Eléctrica, and BBVA, among other participants

regulation and consider the work of external auditors of large private companies to be sufficient. It is an article that hints at the sufficiency of the transparency law due to the


supposed improvement in corporate transparency of large private companies following the


crisis, backed by another _Elconfidencial.com_ news article from December 15 that reproduces


the self-interested voice of the National Securities Market Commission (CNMV) under the title


"La CNMV cree que España está en la vanguardia sobre gobierno corporativo" [The CNMV Believes Spain Is at the Forefront of Corporate Governance], showing


how many of the articles on Sufficiency of the Law form part of the communications campaign


of large companies and ideologically aligned public bodies affiliated with the governing party, with public spokespersons who have participated in the management of the business


world.

This sub-topic focuses on the appearance before the Finance and Public Administrations


Committee of the Lower House of the president of the Transparency Council, Esther


Arizmendi. Half of them belong to the topic Insufficiency of the Law and the other half to


Non-compliance with the Law, making it a perfect example of how the spokesmanship of


independent public bodies and experts demonstrates the insufficiency and non-compliance of the transparency law. Of the 6 publications in this sub-topic, 5 are news articles authored by agencies and


1 is a report authored by Irene Castro. It is notable that all publications


are from _Eldiario.es_, and of the 6, 5 were published on September 23 and 1 on the day of the


appearance, September 22. This indicates that the editorial line of _Eldiario.es_ prompted the reproduction of the same information in different ways over the course of 24 hours,


serving as a clear example of how an outlet attempts to position transparency and the opinion of the


Transparency Council in public opinion, despite being the outlet with the most


reproduction of self-interested voices from the PP.


**VI.3.1.2.9. Partido Popular (PP)**


The 7 publications in the sub-topic Partido Popular are all news articles reproducing self-interested voices


from this political formation, such as the then Minister of Justice Rafael Catalá, the


spokesperson on the Constitutional Committee, Pedro Gómez de la Serna, Vice-President Soraya


Sáez de Santa María, and the PP's spokesperson on the Madrid City Council, Esperanza Aguirre.


These news articles projected a PP that complied with the transparency law and was responsible for


Spain having a law of this kind. Only two news articles departed from this


pattern: the one from November 17 titled "Ayllón asegura que España está a medio camino en


las reformas que necesita" [Ayllón Asserts That Spain Is Halfway Through the Reforms It Needs] in _Eldiario.es_, and the one published in this same outlet on December 7


under the title "Soria presume de que el PP ha luchado contra la corrupción y Rosell


acusa al Gobierno de alimentarla" [Soria Boasts That the PP Has Fought Against Corruption While Rosell Accuses the Government of Fuelling It].

The first case forms part of the topic Insufficiency of the Law and hints at shortcomings of the


transparency law in order to promote new reforms that the PP wanted to pass at that


time. In the second case, it belongs to the topic Non-compliance with the Law given that the


then Minister of Industry, Energy and Tourism, José Manuel Soria, as the PP's candidate to


Congress for the province of Las Palmas, defended in a televised debate with the other political opponents the PP's reforms as part of the


fight against corruption, citing the transparency law passed during the PP's legislative term


as an example of this claim, causing other political party spokespersons to


demonstrate the PP's non-compliance with the law as part of their rebuttals.


**VI.3.1.2.10. Hepatitis C**


The 5 publications in this sub-topic belong entirely to the topic Insufficiency of the Law,


of which 3 are news articles from the EFE agency published in _Eldiario.es_ and


_Elconfidencial.com_, and two are reports by Elena G. Sevillano published in _El País_. As


already noted in the analysis by journalistic genre, the EFE news articles in this


sub-topic reproduced the complaints of the Platform of People Affected by Hepatitis C and the


Organización Médico Colegial, highlighting the lack of funding, discrimination, and


non-compliance with the plan drawn up by the Ministry of Health to treat this disease.


Sevillano's reports exposed how the Ministry of Health concealed the conflicts of


interest of the committee that drafted the National Strategic Plan for the Approach to Hepatitis


C and the parliamentary confrontations over this concealment:

"The Socialist Parliamentary Group has requested the appearance of the Minister of Health,


Alfonso Alonso, to report on 'the conditions under which the independence of the members of the committee that drafted the National Strategic Plan


for the Approach to Hepatitis C is guaranteed.' This request comes after the


Transparency Council censured the Ministry of Health's concealment of the


declarations of conflicts of interest of the experts it appointed, and urged it, in a


resolution, to hand over that information." (Sevillano, 2015b).

As this report by Elena Sevillano from July 22 under the title "El PSOE


pide que Alonso informe sobre los expertos de la hepatitis C" [The PSOE Asks Alonso to Report on the Hepatitis C Experts] has made clear,


the topic Hepatitis C has laid bare the insufficiency of the transparency law through the opacity with which


the government handled this matter, and the need for a resolution from the Transparency Council


to attempt to remedy the situation — which in turn revealed the law's non-compliance in failing to comply with the resolution, and once again the law's insufficiency in showing


how the sanctioning capacity's lack in this body perpetuates the opacity of


public institutions when they do not wish to provide requested information. This situation is


clearly reflected in the rest of the articles in this sub-topic.


**VI.3.1.2.11. Non-compliance with the Law**


The 4 news articles and 1 report belonging to this sub-topic are among those that


clearly reflect the insufficiency and non-compliance of the transparency law, although 3


belong to the topic Insufficiency of the Law and 2 to Sufficiency of the Law. The news articles in the topic


Insufficiency of the Law with the headlines "El Parlamento pide medidas para acabar con el


'clientelismo político'" [Parliament Requests Measures to Put an End to 'Political Clientelism'] and "La Diputación de Bizkaia mantiene en su cargo al asesor


imputado" [The Bizkaia Provincial Council Keeps the Indicted Advisor in His Post], both from _El Mundo_, dated October 15 and November 23 respectively,


show how self-interested political voices use the evident insufficiency of the law and its


non-compliance as a political weapon.

In the first case, the PNV and PP speak of the "future Transparency, Citizen Participation,


and Good Governance Law of the Basque Public Sector" with measures to fight against


political clientelism and conflicts of interest from a regional perspective, hinting at


the insufficiency of the national law on these points. In the second case, the Podemos representative in the Basque Country, Asun Merinero, uses the indictment of the then advisor to the Bizkaia


Provincial Council, Marcelino Elorza, to show examples of revolving doors in the


provincial government and failures of transparency and good governance by keeping an


indicted person in their post. In this case, the lack of sanctioning capacity in the law and the fact that an


indicted person is not immediately removed from their post was what led to placing this


article under Insufficiency of the Law.

The two articles in this sub-topic that fall under the topic Sufficiency of the Law used


self-interested political voices from the PP: the then Minister of Finance, Cristóbal Montoro, and


Vice-President Soraya Sáenz de Santa María. Both articles deal with the case of the


commissions charged by the former Spanish Ambassador to India, Gustavo de Arístegui, to


secure contracts for Spanish companies. In these cases, the articles fall under the topic


Sufficiency of the Law due to the supposedly swift action of the Office of Conflicts of


Interest, which was investigating this case, and which was used by both spokespersons as an example of the law's sufficiency, even though they made clear the law's non-compliance by failing to


show the incompatibility of the former ambassador's position.


**VI.3.1.2.12. Sub-topics with Fewer than 5 Publications**


In the three sub-topics with 4 publications — namely, Valencia Transparency Council, Castilla-La Mancha Transparency Law, and RTVCM (Castilla-La Mancha Radio Television) —


the topic to which these publications belong is predominantly Insufficiency of the Law, followed


by Non-compliance with the Law. The creation of the Valencia Transparency Council based on


the regional law demonstrates the insufficiency of the national law by granting the regional body


powers that the national Transparency Council does not have. Very similar to what occurred


with the sub-topic Castilla-La Mancha Transparency Law and RTVCM, which lays bare


the national law's insufficiencies that the regional law attempts to offset. Non-compliance with the


law was reflected in the failure to publish asset declarations by senior officials


of the regional government led by María Dolores de Cospedal, as required by the


national law.

In the 6 sub-topics with only 3 publications each (Graph 33), there is also a predominance


of Insufficiency of the Law as the topic, followed by Non-compliance with the Law, and only one


article among the 18 forming part of the topic Sufficiency of the Law. In these sub-topics, the


entry into force of the national transparency law in the regional governments exposed the


non-compliance with the transparency law in the majority of them, highlighting the lack of technical capacity to address its compliance at the level of municipalities and regional


governments. The insufficiency of the national law was reflected both in the lack of a regional transparency law in Asturias and in the approval of regional and


local measures seeking to offset the national law's shortcomings, such as the regulation of


political party financing, the regulation of lobbying, and the open accounts law in


Valencia.

In the 28 articles with two publications in each sub-topic, 12 belonged to the topic


Insufficiency of the Law, 6 to December 20 Elections, 5 to Compliance with the Law, 4 to Non-compliance with


the Law, and 1 to Sufficiency of the Law. In the 12 publications belonging to Insufficiency of


the Law, the opacity in public universities, regional governments, local governments, and


sports served to demonstrate these shortcomings. Those belonging to the topic December 20


Elections exposed these same insufficiencies through party programs and through


many political representatives who pointed to corruption and the need to reform the
transparency law among other related issues. As for those belonging to Compliance with the Law, the majority did not depart from the tendency linking this supposed compliance with the same self-interested political voices, even when these voices contradict compliance at the very moment they use the lack of transparency as a weapon against their adversaries. Of the 4 articles forming part of Non-compliance with the Law, 2


address the sufficiency of the law — on the one hand, in the case of the indictment of the PP deputy for


Segovia, Pedro Gómez, whose electoral allocation aid was withdrawn — although he was not removed from the PP's list for the December 20 elections; while the other 2 address


non-compliance through the sub-topic Caso Pujol, which drove one of the most highly regarded regional transparency laws, even though it was not complied with due to the


political entanglements this case involved in the Catalan parliament. Finally, the article


belonging to Sufficiency of the Law is nothing more than the reproduction of a self-interested source


from the PP — the then Secretary of State for Parliamentary Relations, José Luis

The remaining articles, with one publication in each sub-topic, will not be analyzed in


depth individually, as this would lengthen the results section without necessarily

**VI.4. Analytical Summary of Results**


The first anniversary of the transparency law's entry into force and the last day


of the 2015 general election campaign — December 10 and 18 respectively —


were the days with the most publications in the entire sample. This fact alone already leads us


to consider the importance that the political options that fought electorally at the end of that year, as well as


the media outlets that echoed their statements, placed on transparency


in the eyes of public opinion. Starting from this important finding, we will proceed to break down the most notable results of this


content analysis and their implications for our study, reflected in the following


sub-sections.


**VI.4.1. The News Article and the Self-interested Reproduction of Sources Predominate**


The news article was the journalistic genre with the greatest number of publications, accounting for 71% of


the total sample. Of these publications, more than 80% reproduced self-interested information


from sources, of which 60% were spokespersons for a political party. An


important finding was the fact that the majority of authorships belonged to news


agencies, making it apparent that these had a very large influence across almost all the outlets


analyzed, and showing that transparency in political discourse was considered key by


parties campaigning that year. On the one hand, by directly reproducing information from


self-interested sources, agencies published what political parties and their representatives


wanted to project about themselves — and in many cases about their political adversaries —

**CONCLUSIONS**


**VII.1. Big Data Is a Technological and Sociocultural Phenomenon That Differentiates Data Journalism from Other Types of Journalism That Use Databases and Large Datasets**


Data journalism differs from a technological and sociocultural standpoint from


previous forms of journalism that worked with databases and large datasets —


such as precision journalism and computer-assisted reporting — due to the context in which it


develops: _big data_. In this regard, the following **interconnections** were identified between the different cultural actors who form part of data journalism teams and _big data_ as a technological and sociocultural phenomenon:


**1.** **The open data movement and the culture of transparency linked to the**


**open data philosophy.**


This has been reflected in greater access to data thanks to freedom of


information and transparency laws, which now include


the digitization of large quantities of public information and the active and reactive publication of data by public administrations. This


point has also been demonstrated by the journalist's publication of the raw data


from their stories, as well as by the publication of the


process through which they acquired and analyzed the data that make up their journalistic piece.


**2.** **The free software movement and the collaborative working practices implicit in this**


**movement.**


This point is proven through the democratization of technology that


forms part of data journalism by the hacktivist or


activist hacker community, which has enabled journalists to use computing tools that did not


exist in the era of precision journalism and computer-assisted reporting. Another demonstration of this


movement and its collaborative working practices in data journalism


has been the detection of the hacker work ethic that promotes the reuse of data and technological tools by actors other than


their creators, as well as the incorporation of other cultural actors into the


news production process, such as programmers, web developers, and


digital product designers — infographics specialists, graphic designers,


video producers, etc.


**3.** **Mythologies particular to big data as a phenomenon.**


These have reinforced traditional journalism values in data journalism


practices, such as rigor, objectivity, and accuracy, leading data


journalists to associate these values more closely with journalistic practices that


involve the handling of large quantities of data. In this regard, the


proliferation of verification techniques adapted to the digital era, such as


_fact-checking_, demonstrates the incorporation of mythologies particular to _big data_


as a phenomenon into data journalism.


**VII.2. The** _**Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno**_ **Has Boosted Data Journalism in Spain Despite Its Insufficiency and Non-compliance**


The _Ley 19/2013 de transparencia, acceso a la información pública y buen gobierno_ [Law 19/2013 on Transparency, Access to Public Information, and Good Governance] has


driven the creation of data journalism teams in Spain because it has provided journalists, activists, and citizens in general with mechanisms of access to information that


did not previously exist. This has been used as a platform to demand greater transparency and for it


to permeate political discourse. However, the law and its portal are clearly


insufficient for the proper development of data journalism in Spain, to which is added


the fact that the public administration does not comply with the law.


**VII.3. The Insufficiency of the Transparency Law Has Left Data Journalism in a Still Incipient State**


Despite the boom it has experienced from 2015 onwards, the state of data journalism


remains incipient due to the insufficiency of the Transparency Law, whose


main causes are its legal standing and its lack of sanctioning capacity. Its legal standing


does not place it on a par with the Organic Data Protection Law (LOPD), [14] meaning that


it will always be subordinate to this law, even in requests for public information related


to public officials and elected officeholders. In this regard, the refusals to provide


public information requested through the transparency portal related to the LOPD, as well as the


double administrative silence that deems an unanswered request within the deadline to have been refused, are


aspects that have meant this law has not prospered in the spirit for which it was created: improving


access to public information by citizens and thereby encouraging more


transparent governance and a more active citizenry. Added to all this is the fact that in the event


that any institution refuses to publish or provide public information and the applicant


turns to the Transparency Council to appeal the refusal, the resolutions of the Transparency Council urge — but do not compel — the provision of the requested information.

This means that the law lacks sanctioning mechanisms that would compel its


compliance in the event of refusals by public bodies, meaning that the citizen or

journalist would have to go to court to attempt to obtain the denied information. This professional sector, already so economically hard-hit in recent times, also cannot afford


to go to court for public information that may no longer be relevant by the time a

The lack of a lobbying register is another reason why the Transparency Law


is insufficient. Without such a register, it is impossible to reveal information about sectors


that influence political decision-making affecting all citizens. In this


regard, without a lobbying register, one of the most important pillars of journalism

that from the Spanish public administration, backed by the judicial framework, action is being taken


against the spirit of the law so that it does not become effective, which is a very serious threat


to the democratic aspirations of Spanish society.

**VII.4. The sector of data journalism has influenced the political agenda by exposing the**


**insufficiencies of the Transparency Law to public opinion**


All of the insufficiencies of the Transparency Law noted above —with the exception of the veto by the


Constitutional Court of 1 November 2018— were exposed by media outlets


with data journalism teams ahead of the general elections of 20


December 2015. Furthermore, they were taken up and even used by some political


spokespersons, so the insufficiency of the law as projected by media with data journalism


teams influenced the political agenda that year. This also means that there is


a coincidence between the values associated with the culture of transparency expressed by


data journalists in the in-depth interviews and those intrinsic to


the informational treatment of transparency in the media.

**VII.5. Non-compliance with the Transparency Law is due to a lack of transparency culture in the Spanish public administration**


Non-compliance with the Transparency Law has been a constant since its entry into


force. The delay in responding to requests for public information is one example of this,


since according to the law the deadline for responding to a request for


public information cannot exceed two weeks. Furthermore, the agreement between the Agencia Española de Protección de Datos and


the Consejo de Transparencia regarding the personal data of a person holding a


public office (CI/004/2015) is not being complied with. This non-compliance with the law evidences the lack


of a transparency culture in the Spanish public administration, which has multiplied the


use of _crowdsourcing_ and the leaking of data that ought to be public in order to produce


data journalism pieces. Furthermore, the fact that the highest judicial


institution in Spain is vetoing the initiatives created by the autonomous communities in an attempt to address the insufficiency and


non-compliance of the law is another clear example of how the public administration and the


judiciary lack a transparency culture consonant with the spirit of the law.

**VII.6. Debate and proposals regarding the obstacles to the exercise of DJ in Spain**


A first step to resolving all these obstacles to the exercise of DJ in Spain would be to grant


sanctioning capacity to the law and, by extension, to the Consejo de Transparencia. In this way,


both civil servants and institutions that fail to comply with the


resolutions of the council, the established timelines for responding to requests for


information, and the subordination of the personal data of a senior public official to the Transparency


Law would be penalized. A second step would be to bring the law into line with its Anglo-Saxon counterparts. In the


British case, and despite the recent Brexit, it was the first country to follow the EU directive on


Extractive Industries, which requires a detailed description of a series of payments


made to foreign governments by these companies. Spain should


implement this directive, which affects public and private companies alike, to mention only


one example of how the law could be improved even beyond Nordic countries such as


Finland, which, despite its high degree of transparency culture in the public


administration, has had problems of access to public information due to the outsourcing of


public services to private companies.

**VII.7. Proposals for future research related to our study**


From the academy we should deepen the study of the mythologies of _big_


_data_, since the resignification in DJ of traditional journalism values —such as


objectivity and veracity— is closely related to the rise of _fake news_ and the problem


of bias in the algorithms used in automated journalism. It is precisely at


this point that the University could provide both journalists and activists with


tools to advance the struggle for transparency and accountability in


contemporary democracies. In this sense, the study of the relations between _big data_,


artificial intelligence, and journalism are research niches that would advance in the


direction in which this thesis was conceived.

citizenship.


Another aspect that shows the insufficiency of this law in Spain is the use of the strategic


litigation as a fundamental tool in both the activism for transparency and the exercise of data


journalism, since as has been pointed out, the expenditure of taking a negative response to a


request of public information to courts is very high in terms of money and time. Finally, it is


important to highlight that the deficiency is such that even some of the measures created by


some autonomous communities to overcome the insufficiency of the law are being vetoed by


the Constitutional Court, as in the positive official silence contained in the Autonomic Law of


the government of Aragon. This means that the public administration of Spain, backed by the


judicial framework, is acting against the spirit of the law so that it does not become effective,


which is a very serious threat for the democratic aspirations of the Spanish society.


**VII.4. The sector of data journalism has influenced the political agenda by exposing the**


**deficiencies of the Act of Transparency to the public opinion**


All of the mentioned insufficiencies of the law -excepting the veto of the Constitutional Court


on 1 [st] November, 2018- were exposed by the media with data journalism teams on the verge


of the general elections of 20 [th] December, 2015. What is more, these shortcomings were


assumed and even used by some political spokespersons, meaning that the insufficiency of the


law that were projected by the media with data journalism teams influenced the political


agenda of that year. This also means that there is a coincidence in the values associated with


the transparency culture expressed by data journalists in the in-depth interviews, with those


intrinsic in the treatment of information about transparency in the media.


**VII.5. The breach of the Transparency Law is due to a lack of transparency culture in**


**the Spanish public administration**


The breach of the Transparency Law has been a constant factor since it came into force. The


delay in responding to the petitions of public information is an example of this, since


according to the law the deadline for responding to a petition of public information is two


weeks. Besides, the agreement between the 'Agencia Española de Protección de Datos'


(Spanish Agency for Data Protection) and the Board of Transparency regarding the personal


data of a person that is in public office (CI/004/2015) are not being met. This failure to


comply the law shows the lack of a transparency culture in the Spanish public administration,


which has multiplied the use of crowd-sourcing and the leakage of data that ought to be


public to elaborate pieces of data journalism. In addition to this, the fact that the highest


judicial institution in Spain is vetoing the initiatives created by the autonomous regions in an


attempt to solve the insufficiencies and breach of the law is another clear example of how the


public administration and the judiciary lack of a transparency culture that is consonant with


the spirit of the Law.


**VII.6. Debate and proposals regarding the obstacles of exercising DJ in Spain**


A first step to solve all these obstacles to the exercise of DJ in Spain would be to give to the


Transparency Law sanction capacity and, therefore, to the Board of Transparency. This way


both officers and institutions that do not comply with the board resolutions would be penalize,


as well as the timelines established to respond to the petitions of public information and the


subordination of the personal data of a high ranked officer to the Transparency Law. A second


step would be to match the law to its Anglo-Saxon counterparts. In the British case and


despite of the Brexit, this was the first country that has followed the UE directive about


Extractive Industries, that requires a detail description of serial payments made by these


industries to foreign governments. Spain should apply this directive that affects public and


private companies equally, jut to mention an example of how the law could be improve even


beyond the Nordic countries as Finland, that in spite of its high grade of transparency culture


in the public sector, has had problems to access public information due to the outsourcing of


public services to private companies.


**VII.7. Proposals to futures researches related to our study**


From the academy we should deepen the study of the mythologies of Big Data, since the


resignification of traditional journalism values in DJ -as objectivity and veracity- are well


related to the rise of fake news and the problem of bias in the algorithms used in automated


journalism. It is precisely in this point where the University could provide both to journalists


and activists the tools to intensify the fight for transparency and accountability in


contemporary democracies. In this sense, the study of the relations between Big Data,


artificial intelligence and journalism are research niches that will move towards the direction


that this thesis was conceived.
