**Abstract**
With emergent subspecialties like data journalism bringing new skillsets and
job roles, professionals and journalism educators find it difficult to imbibe the
fast-changing industry demands. Such challenges in some countries and media
industries put journalism educators in an advantageous position, offering them an
agency to actively shape the contours of industry practice than getting shaped by
it. From this perspective, the present study tries to understand data journalism
practices in India and suggests certain insights to integrate data journalism training
in programmes offered by Indian journalism education. By probing insights from
the literature on data journalism education and by examining existing data
journalism practices in India, the study calls for intervention with a pedagogic
strategy to impart better data-sourcing practices, coding skills and critical data
literacy among the students as an antidote to the prevalent DIY culture and
overdependence on data aggregates. The pedagogic strategy should convey the
importance of audience centrality and ethics in data journalism practice. It argues
that such an approach can, in effect, improve industry practices as well as the
quality of journalism education in India.


**Keywords**
Data journalism, data literacy, data visualization, journalism education, pedagogy

**Introduction**


The quantitative turn of journalism (Coddington, 2015) and the emergence of
subspecialties like interactive journalism or algorithmic journalism (Usher, 2016)
is bringing about new job roles and skillsets in news production. Redrawing the
boundaries of journalism, these changes raise challenges for media organizations,
journalists, and journalism educators in terms of acquiring new expertise.
Academic research about data journalism education is in the form of case
studies carried out by journalism academicians about programmes or curriculum
offered by institutions in the USA, UK, Australia and Hong Kong. The present
study takes a different approach by examining the industry practice to develop
pedagogic approaches in data journalism training in India. It argues that the
changes brought by new practices like data journalism are an opportunity for
journalism academia to take up a proactive role in shaping industry practices than
getting shaped by it.
To prepare the ground, the academic literature on data journalism education
was surveyed and recurring challenges were identified. Then, data journalism
practice in Indian media was examined through a content analysis of the data
stories to identify the skillsets being used and the common practices involved.
Putting together these insights, the study suggests certain pedagogic approaches
for data journalism education in India.


**Review of Literature**


_Data Journalism Education: The Global Experience_


Past studies about teaching data journalism rely on case studies of specific
programmes (Davies, 2018; Green, 2018; Treadwell et al., 2016), surveys or
interviews with academicians who offer data-journalism-related courses (Davies
& Cullen, 2016; Leask, 2017), analyzing course documents or curriculum
(Hewett, 2016; Splendore et al., 2016) or developing normative and reflective
approaches to teaching data journalism (Bradshaw, 2018; Burns & Mathew,
2018).
The challenges of teaching data journalism are manifold. Students’ aversion to
math is a major worry reflected in case studies from different countries (Weiss &
Retis, 2018; Zhu & Du, 2018). This is a usual challenge in imparting numeracy
skills to journalism students (Dunwoody & Griffin, 2013). Some studies document
this disinclination as journalism students purported fear of math (e.g., Weiss &
Retis, 2018) or their reluctance to learn computer-science-related subjects (Plaue
& Cook, 2015; Yang & Du, 2016). The unwillingness of the educators towards
acquiring big data-related skills or the associated technical up-skilling (Splendore
et al., 2016) is also another issue.
Small-cohorts are better for imparting data journalism skills (Hannis, 2018;
Heravi, 2019; Hewett, 2016; Splendore et al., 2016). Similarly, project-based
teaching of data journalism is a commonly followed approach (Graham, 2015;


_Kashyap and Bhaskaran_ 3


Green, 2018; Hannis, 2018). As a ‘guerrilla method’, it helps to overcome
students’ data-phobia (Graham, 2018) and also to focus on skill-building and selfimprovement in low-resource conditions (Treadwell et al., 2016). Despite being
the most popular approach, it has certain pitfalls as well. Any mistakes in the
earlier stages of the project planning would affect the validity of the stories later
(Treadwell et al., 2016). The skills being imparted will be restricted by the nature
of the project (Graham, 2018). This approach, especially in a networked atmosphere, may not provide a level playing field. Graham (2015) notes that in such
cases the ‘heavy-lifting’ (like analysis and visual design) is done by selected
members only, limiting the skill acquisition across the cohort. The author also
points out that resource demands of a project-based approach for a large cohort
will be difficult to meet in a university environment.
Integrating data journalism training across the general journalism curriculum
is another approach. For instance, Wises and Retis (2018) advocate inclusion of
data skills at high school journalism education. Green (2018) called for integrating
data journalism into the core units of journalism programmes at the undergraduate
level, instead of focusing on a single course.
In both these approaches, collaboration is an integral element at the institutional
level (Treadwell et al., 2016). Green (2018) and Graham (2018) emphasize the
importance of inter-organizational collaborations such as summer schools, student
exchanges and inter-departmental collaborations to meet the resource gap in terms
of trained faculty. Several scholars emphasize the need for developing the data
journalism curriculum in collaboration with professionals (Hannis, 2018; Hewett,
2016; Plaue & Cook, 2015; Treadwell et al., 2016).
However, non-journalists teaching journalism students as part of collaborative
initiatives is raised as a serious issue (Green, 2018). Bradshaw (2018) opposes
such interdisciplinary collaboration, especially in terms of outsourcing journalism teaching to other departments. However, this concern is absent in case of
collaboration with the community of practice. Graham (2015) argues that a
project-based approach which has collaborative, industry-linked interface, can
effectively impart skills needed for investigative data journalism.


_Data Journalism as an Opportunity for Academia_


Generally, journalism education is shaped up by industry needs and is focused on
developing professionals who can contribute to newsrooms (Becker, 2003,
Dickson, 2000). This approach has been noted as outdated and ineffective because
it relies on an industry which is failing on its own globally (Mensing, 2010).
Industry-based approach in data journalism education is reflected in the
tendency of journalism educators to look forward to industry output and practices
as examples to emulate in the academia (Hannis, 2018). Rather than critically
examining industry practice to develop pedagogic approaches to refine it, the
literature on data journalism education excessively focuses on the curriculum.
Deuze (2006) argues that an obsessive desire to focus only on the curriculum
reflects the general lack of contextualized approach in journalism education.
Similarly, other scholars point out the need to involve stakeholders while bringing

reforms to journalism education (Picard, 2015), considering the larger context in
which journalism training is situated (Cohen, 2002). The literature on teaching
data journalism, as of now, considers industry professionals as potential students
for the academia, trying to understand their ‘customer’ needs (Heravi, 2019).
Academia has certain advantages for the industry in case of emerging
specialities. For instance, Graham (2018) argues that a data journalism classroom
project could formulate a workforce which has temporal and resource advantages
in terms of undivided attention which could make any editor with a resourcestricken newsroom, envious. She also notes that many students are at a level
playing field with working journalists when it comes to data journalism skills,
since it is an emerging subspecialty. However, academic attempts to develop data
journalism programmes ignore this advantage. In this backdrop, the study seeks to
suggest an approach to teaching data journalism in India by examining the
shortcomings of the current industry practice. In order to put this attempt in the
right context, a brief discussion on the status of journalism education in India is
penned in the next section.


_Journalism Education in India_


While industry-orientation is a general trend in global journalism education,
Indian journalism academia, especially the university-based system, is largely
disconnected from the industry. Behera (1994) raised concerns about the noticeable
gap between media education, training and the industry requirements. Muppidi
(2008) observed that journalism education in India does not focus on developing
analytical thinking which makes classroom teaching a different reality than the
professional one. He suggested collaboration between academics and industry
professionals to improve the situation. This apparent imbalance between the
media industry requirements and academic curriculum has resulted in the mushrooming of media-backed institutions. This has not led to an improvement. While
the university-based journalism educators lack practical skills, their techno-sound
counterparts in the media-backed institutions lack in-depth understanding and
critical thinking (Bharthur, 2017). In this context, many scholars called for an
approach where journalism educators impart skills as per industry needs and also
suggested the need for the journalism faculty to acquire industry experience
(Behera, 1994; Murthy, 2011; Sanjay, 2012; Ullah, 2014).
This approach can be seen as a reflection of industry orientation of the
US-based journalism education. Indian journalism education has been highly
influenced by curricular models from the West, especially from the USA (Bharthur,
2017; Eapen, 1991; Murthy, 2011; Ullah, 2014). Indian journalism education, on
the one hand, is struggling to provide quality education to develop critical and
analytical thinking in students and to meet industry needs (Tere, 2012), and on
the other, it is crippled by its industry disconnect and Western influence. The
challenges put forward by disruptive changes in journalism as a profession are the
latest entrant to this mix (Bhaskaran et al., 2019).
Indian journalism academicians rarely take stakeholders into confidence to
develop a roadmap for academia. Some recent studies tried developing approaches

considering stakeholders other than just academicians (Bhaskaran et al., 2019).
The present study also attempts to take a similar approach by triangulating insights

insights generated by closely examining data journalism practices in India.


_Data Journalism Practice in India_


Many Indian media organizations started acknowledging data journalism practice
by assigning ‘data’ sections in their websites. New entrants outside the mainstream,
legacy media also were part of this change. For instance, _IndiaSpend_ which claims
to be the first dedicated data journalism initiative in India started in 2011. Similar
data initiatives like How India Lives, though not producing conventional news
products, also started around 2012 onwards, mainly based on the availability and
facilitation of open data in India. Despite such changes, data journalism practice
in India has not attracted much academic attention yet.
Due to the lack of academic literature on data journalism practice in India, this
study examined data stories produced by _The Hindu_ and _IndiaSpend_, two Indian
news organizations which adopted data journalism practices. The former is a
legacy organization which has a specific section for data stories in its website,
while the latter is a non-profit, digitally-native, start-up which is an exclusive data
journalism organization. _The Hindu_ represents large, legacy media organizations
with a profit-oriented revenue model banking on print tradition in India, and
_IndiaSpend_ is a representative of the new, non-profit, digital media start-ups
mushrooming in India.
With the primary intention to assess the status of data journalism practice in
Indian media industry, the study examined data stories produced by these
organizations. Through a content analysis of the data stories, the study examined
interactive visualizations, tools used for their production, data sources used and
data-sourcing practices. In this regard, the following research questions were
formulated:


**RQ1** : What is the status of data journalism practice in India in terms of skill
sets and tools used for producing data stories?
**RQ2** : What are the present data-sourcing practices among Indian data
journalists?
**RQ3** : What is the nature of data visualizations in terms of interactivity in data
stories produced by the organizations in the sample?


Based on the findings of the aforementioned research questions, the study seeks
to answer a fourth research question:


**RQ4** : What are the specific pedagogic approaches needed in journalism education in terms of improving the quality of data journalism practice in
India?

**Methods**


To understand aspects of current data journalism practice in India such as the
skillsets involved, tools used and data-sourcing practices, the study carried out
a content analysis of data stories published by _The Hindu_ and _IndiaSpend_
between 2017 and 2018 ( _N_ = 428). Based on the research questions, relevant
variables from past studies about data journalism practice such as _nature of data_
_sources_, _nature of maps_, _nature of graphs_, _visualization interactivity_ (Knight,
2015; Loosen et al., 2017; Stalph, 2017; Tandoc & Oh, 2017; Young et al.,
2017), _forms of data visualization interactivity_ (Boy et al., 2015; Yi et al., 2007)
were identified and operationalized. Other specific variables such as _tools used_
_for visualization_ and _method of data acquirement_ which are relevant to the
research questions were also operationalized. Operationalization of terms
helped in developing measures and generating a code-sheet (Neuendorf, 2016).
For testing inter-coder reliability, Krippendorff’s alpha (α) was calculated
(Table 1) after 50 stories (11 percent of the total sample, 25 stories from each
organization) were coded by two coders.

_Skillsets and Tools_


Results show that _IndiaSpend_ used visualizations in more stories (93 percent)
than _The Hindu_ (63 percent). Among the stories in _IndiaSpend_ which used data
visualizations, 65 percent used dynamic graphs, 6 percent used static graphs and
3percent sported both. In _The Hindu_, 34 percent stories (percentage of only those
stories which used visualizations) used dynamic graphs and 2 percent stories used
static graphs. Comparatively, maps were used more by _IndiaSpend_ (20 percent)
than _The Hindu_ (7 percent). In _IndiaSpend_, 11 percent data stories used dynamic
maps, 8 percent used static maps and 1 percent had both. In _The Hindu_, 5 percent
data stories had dynamic maps and 3 percent stories had static maps. Collectively,
the data about the use of visualizations show that both organizations use dynamic
graphs and maps (which offer interactive choices to the user, unlike their static
counterparts) more. This is an indication that they follow the global trends of data
journalism practice. However, to put this in context, especially in terms of the
skillsets involved, the tools used for their production and the levels of interactivity
offered shall be analyzed.
The use of bespoke tools or coding languages for creating visualizations was
less as compared to the use of freely available visualization tools. In _IndiaSpend_,
29 percent data stories used Datawrapper, 14 percent used Tableau Public, 12
percent Google Charts and 14 percent of the news stories used multiple tools. In
_The Hindu_, Google Charts (23 percent of the stories with a data visualisation) was
the most common tool for creating visualizations, followed by Datawrapper
(2percent) and several other tools like Infogram (13percent). It was noted that few
stories also used JavaScript libraries and R libraries for creating visualizations.
Barring these few cases of exception, the data show that the DIY culture of
visualization production in data journalism (Young et al., 2017, p. 127) is prevalent
in the Indian scenario also.

_Visualization Interactivity_


Visualizing datasets into interactive interfaces which allow users to explore them
to generate insights is a common practice in data journalism (Coddington, 2015).
This study categorized visualization interactivity into three forms based on the
taxonomy of semantic operations posited by Yi et al. (2007) and Boy et al. (2015).
The first form of interactivity depicts the lower level of interaction with the
content allowing readers to ‘inspect’ (show the specifics of data) and ‘narrate’
(show a different section). The second form of interactivity is a higher form where
readers can ‘connect’ (show related items), ‘select’ (mark something as interesting)
and ‘abstract/elaborate’ (show more or less detail). The third form of interactivity
offers readers the opportunity to interact more by allowing to ‘filter’ (show
something conditionally), ‘explore’ (show something else) or ‘reconfigure’ (show
a different arrangement). The first form of interactivity was the most common
form in the stories analyzed. In _IndiaSpend_, 48 percent visualizations had the first
form of interactivity, 4 percent had the second form of interactivity and 21 percent
had the third form of interactivity. In _The Hindu_, 29 percent stories had the first
form of interactivity, 3 percent had the second form of interactivity and 8 percent
stories had the third form of interactivity.


**Discussion**


The results reveal that Indian data journalists are heavily dependent on data
aggregates provided by the government, like elsewhere (Young et al., 2017). It
can be due to the inaccessibility of raw data or journalists’ unwillingness to
explore ways to access it. The widespread use of single data sources and data
aggregates also points out to the lack of better data-sourcing practices and the
absence of critical approach to using data aggregates. Data journalists do not
proactively use RTI requests to access raw data or rely on methods like crowd
sourcing. These inferences point to an aspect of the present data journalism
practice in India which journalism academia may address to improve.
The present state of data journalism practice in India involves less advanced
skillsets. Though dynamic visualizations including maps are used in several
stories, most of them are produced with free tools and offer minimal interactivity.
While considering the advanced forms of the practice globally, the presence of
bespoke tools and the use of coding languages instead of DIY tools is a major
difference. Moreover, the use of diverse and larger datasets may lead to more
interactive choices offered to the audience, resulting in richer engagement.
The absence of these aspects in the stories analyzed could be due to several
reasons. When the underlying datasets are thin, only minimal skills are required
to transpose, clean and analyze it. Advanced storytelling with higher interactivity
visualizations, which offers explorative choices to the user, is not viable with such
datasets. The widespread use of data aggregates and single datasets thus requires
minimal expertise in production but limits user experience. The presence of DIY
tools is another element worth discussing. Compared to coding languages, the

free tools are very restrictive in terms of the visualizations and interactivity
options they offer. They are meant for users with no coding expertise to make
visualizations. Their widespread use indicates the lack of coding skills among
Indian data journalists. Scholars argue that these tools function as external entities
which shape up journalistic narratives in data journalism because the journalists
are not exercising editorial authority for optimal storytelling with available data
but what can be done for free (Young et al., 2017).
The over-reliance on thin data aggregates, DIY tools and minimal interactivity
may be also due to the preference for the fast-paced daily production as opposed
to bigger, long-term projects. The absence of policy initiatives from media
organizations to invest in training to develop better data teams might also be a
reason for the lack of coding and visualization skills. The less advanced form of
the practice might be the reason why there are only fewer entries and winners
from Indian media organizations for data journalism awards, a benchmark event
for advanced forms of the practice.


_Pedagogic Measures_


The less advanced form of data journalism practices in Indian media puts up a
strong case for journalism academicians to exercise their agency by adopting
novel approaches in journalism education to effectively shape up the emerging
data journalism practice. According to Gray and Bounegru (2019), the progression
of data journalism practice shall focus on critical data practice where the reflexive
critique of the entities (such as datasets, tools data sources or data infrastructures)
is as important as the craft of data journalism practice. The superficial approach
of Indian data journalists with their dependence on data aggregates and single
sources indicates the absence of such reflexive critique. Academia shall address
this issue by formulating pedagogic approaches to develop reflexive critique, in
the form of critical data literacy, among students of journalism.
Currently, the curriculum of many Indian journalism schools aims to develop
critical thinking abilities in students, though the effects are skewed (Bharthur,
2017). However, critical data literacy is not just about developing critical thinking
abilities. According to Gray et al. (2018), data literacy shall not be limited to
numerical and technical competencies to work with datasets but shall include
understanding about the socio-technical infrastructures which generate and store
data. Literacy about data infrastructures shall enable journalists to hold such
structures socially accountable. Attempts to develop critical data literacy inside
classroom shall help students to question datasets efficiently to bring out socially
relevant storytelling and to be aware that datasets need not be neutral always. In
the Indian context, such attempts shall also include developing the skills and
strategies needed to derive raw data through different means, than just depending
on data aggregates to produce data stories.
For critical data literacy, a pedagogic approach like interrogating data using
open datasets (Burns & Mathews, 2018) is effective. In this approach, classroom
activities using a raw dataset are introduced to students. They are urged to

interview the dataset by asking critical questions and explore it to find those
answers. When applying this pedagogic approach in the Indian context, it may
require specific measures. For instance, students shall be encouraged to adopt
better practices (such as the use of diverse data sources and preference for raw
data) through incentives in assessment. In the Indian scenario, special emphasis
on using RTI requests to get hold of raw data should be part of assessment
activities. Journalism educators shall collaborate with RTI activists to train
students pursuing journalism regarding the strategies to overcome complex
bureaucratic hurdles to access data.
The presence of minimal interactive forms in data stories indicates the lack of
acceptance of audience centrality among Indian data journalists. In an industry
where the legacy of a dominant print media culture is still influential, it is vital to
sensitise journalism students about the importance of audience centrality in news
production and consumption. Doing this through pedagogic means is challenging.
Critical interaction with select data stories from industry outlets where students
explore them from a user perspective to critique the relevance of interactive
choices and transparency practices (such as sharing methodology, scripts and
datasets) could be effective in this regard. It shall also address the emerging
ethical issues like privacy concerns in using crowd-sourced and scrapped data.
Small-scale, community-based, crowd-sourced projects are a potential pedagogic tool which can impart better understanding of critical data practices and
audience centrality. Crowd-sourcing data from marginalized communities about
pressing issues (like gender violence or caste discrimination in India) help students
to comprehend audience and community centrality in data journalism practice.
The data also help them understand the potential challenges (like privacy violations, low response rates or the iterative nature of a crowd-sourced project)
associated with such methods. This approach shall ensure that students generate
interactive visualizations and exercise transparency measures while telling a
coherent story about the community. Developing a workflow chart which ensures
every visualization created has basic interactive options and transparency will be
effective, especially in a project-based, team effort.
The over-reliance on free tools for data journalism production, as reflected in
the content analysis data, calls for coding lessons and computational thinking
skills to be incorporated in the curriculum. Advanced data journalism programmes in Indian journalism schools shall include coding lessons in R, Python or
JavaScript libraries and also in database management through Ruby on Rails or
MySQL.
While the abovementioned suggestions are based on the inferences drawn
from the content analysis of data stories produced by Indian media organizations,
implementing them in the Indian journalism education scenario is a daunting task.
The present scenario of journalism training in India is very diverse, with more
than 495 institutions offering courses in communication or journalism. Among
them, roughly 59 are funded by the central government, 256 are funded by states
and 177 are by the private sector (Dahiya, 2018). The private-sector institutions include journalism or communication departments under private universities
and also journalism training institutes run by media organizations. Journalism

educators in these institutions have diverse backgrounds in terms of their educational qualifications, industry experience and access to resources. Many of these
educators lack any industry experience and often work in very resource-stricken
conditions where media production as part of journalism training is not viable. In
this scenario, putting forward a one-stop solution for data journalism training and
for Indian journalism education would be very unrealistic. So the suggestions put
forward by this study shall not be mistaken for a normative framework which is
applicable in every Indian journalism school. It rather constitutes a reflexive and
iterative approach which shall be adopted based on organizational realities.
Taking cue from the global experience, this study suggests that a combined
approach would be beneficial in imparting data journalism training, where basic
skills are integrated to general journalism education and specialized courses are
introduced for advanced leaners. Basic critical data literacy skills can be integrated
into general journalism education as a means to overcome the tight academic
schedule. Considering the diverse nature of journalism curricula being taught in
India by different organizations, formulation of specific ways to integrate basic
data journalism training to existing journalism curricula is not viable. However,
the curriculum for University Grants Commission’s National Eligibility Test for
Lectureship is considered a benchmark in journalism and mass communication
education across the country. In the present form, it does not include any aspects
specific to data journalism (University Grants Commission [UGC], 2019).
However, attempts to integrate data journalism training in Indian journalism
education shall consider including concepts such as critical data literacy and
audience centrality in the units of the curriculum which deal with the digital media
production processes and concepts. While such an inclusion would ideally impart
the conceptual clarity needed in practising data journalism, it might not be
impactful enough to intervene and improve the present data journalism practices in Indian media, since mostly the focus of the curriculum is in terms of
understanding concepts, not practicing them.
While the abovementioned integration is an essential intervention needed,
institutions which focus on advanced skill-building in media production shall
introduce a specialization meant for small cohorts to impart specific, advanced
skills in data journalism. This specialization programme shall have a projectbased segment, which involves industry collaboration with a possibility of publishing the outcome. Interventions like integrating new concepts into the general
journalism curricula and the employment of specific pedagogic approaches shall
help journalism academia to take a prominent role in shaping and improving an
emerging practice like data journalism in the Indian media industry.


**Conclusion**


This study sought to look at the emergence of data journalism as an opportunity
for the academia to be an active agent to shape the contours of journalism practice
in a national industry, rather than getting shaped by it. In this attempt, it put
forward certain pedagogic approaches for data journalism education in India.

As per the content analysis results, superficial reportage of openly available data
aggregates and reliance on DIY practices to produce low-interactive data
visualizations are widespread in Indian data journalism practice.
In this context, this study recommends pedagogic approaches to improve
critical data literacy in students and help them understand the importance of
audience centrality and ethical data journalism practices. It suggests a combined
approach where basic skills needed for data journalism practice like critical data
literacy are integrated to general journalism education, while an advanced
specialization programme is offered for advanced learners. According to the
suggestions of the study, such a programme shall use crowd-sourced projects
associated to local communities as a pedagogic tool and impart coding lessons to
enhance critical data exploration and to produce better interactive choices for
users.
However, just weaving in suggestions would be a very romantic approach, if
it does not take care of the realities of Indian journalism education. The global
concern about technically skilled and data-literate educators is real in Indian
scenario too. An active collaboration between university departments and industry
bodies and non-profit players in the country can improve the situation. However,
such collaborations shall be limited to the level of teaching and updating of skills
for journalism educators, than outsourcing journalism teaching to computer
science or statistics departments or to industry practitioners.
The insights of the study are generated from an emerging corpus of the
academic literature and from a limited sample of data stories from a large and
diverse media industry where different forms of the practice exist. This, to an
extent, restricts the findings of the study. Certain suggestions may get temporally
irrelevant in the near future due to its focus on specific tools and technology
(Bhaskaran et al., 2019, p. 165). Due to these limitations, the insights shared by
this study shall not be considered normative but iterative, and shall be improved
by future studies.

**Authors’ bio-sketch**


**Geeta Kashyap** is an Assistant Professor with the Department of Journalism and
Mass Communication at DAV University, Jalandhar. She has been a contributor to
leading Indian dailies like _The Hindustan Times_, _Times of India_ before joining
academics. Apart from academic research, Geeta Kashyap has also published data
stories with organizations like IndiaSpend, Focusing on Wildlife and her own
web portal. She was a recipient of Data Journalism Fellowship for training by
IndiaSpend and Global Investigative Journalism Network.


**Harikrishnan Bhaskaran** is an Assistant Professor at the Department of
Journalism and Mass Communication, Central University of Himachal Pradesh,
India. He had a stint with _The New Indian Express_, Kerala, as a Staff Correspondent
before shifting to academics. His research interests include Journalism Studies,
Fake News and Science Communication.
