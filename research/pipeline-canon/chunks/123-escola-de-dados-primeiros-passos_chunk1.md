# First Steps in Data Journalism

For journalists, working with data can seem daunting at first glance. Statistics, programming, information design, and other skills rarely developed in journalism move to the foreground, discouraging some people from going deep into this field. However, those who are just starting out can benefit from tips and shortcuts from people who also started from scratch and are today considered references in the field.

At the IV Conference on Data Journalism and Digital Methods (Coda.Br 2019), Natália Mazotte and Sérgio Spagnuolo presented the session "Data Journalism 101: learn where to begin." This tutorial presents the main tips shared at that session and also in the post "How to Get Started in Data Journalism" by Natália Mazotte.

## Personal Trajectories

Mazotte came into contact with Computer-Assisted Reporting (CAR) in the US in 2008. In 2010, she carried out an investigative data journalism lab at UFRJ. "There is an essential role for experimentation within universities." She drew closer to Transparência Hacker and Open Knowledge Brasil, becoming a local volunteer for School of Data and bringing Escola de Dados to Brazil. "There was no space in newsrooms for me to do what I wanted, so I started creating my own work."

Sérgio Spagnuolo had a more accidental trajectory. He worked at Reuters. "Between 2010 and 2012, I did data analysis in my master's dissertation without even knowing what I was doing." He became interested in data journalism through the Snowden case in 2013. He took a course at Escola de Dados in 2014 with Mazotte and Marco Túlio Pires. "In 2015, I started pushing harder on my programming studies."

## What Is (and Is Not) Data Journalism?

Characteristics of data journalism:
- Uses statistical analysis applied to journalism
- Produces stories, visualizations, and apps
- Exploratory (may lead to nothing)
- Patient: good analyses take time

Data journalism is NOT:
- A service desk for other departments
- A miracle worker: "I need the analysis of everything by today"
- Restrictive: confined to mere number-crunching

## General Guidance

Reference to the Data Journalism Manual for Newsrooms (Volt Data Lab). The importance of having curiosity as a driving force. Choosing an area you like to cover for data analysis. Numbers alone are not relevant — you need to answer "so what?": what do these numbers affect, who do they affect, and how?

It is fundamental to read the notes on data-recording methodologies. The date a piece of data was recorded may differ from the date the recorded event actually occurred.

## Six Tips to Kick Things Off

1. Find an introductory course to call your own
2. Turn to books and online tutorials
3. Be part of a community (or several)
4. Unlock your computational thinking
5. Get inspired by other journalists in the field
6. Develop your own projects
