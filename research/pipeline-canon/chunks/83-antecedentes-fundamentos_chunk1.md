1. Introduction


Access to the Internet and the vast number of information sources in cyberspace have produced an exponential development of journalism on the Web. In 2005, digital tools for processing and using large volumes of internet data began to be developed, giving rise to a new discipline: data journalism.

Data journalism consists of the collection and analysis of large amounts of information, which journalists make comprehensible to the public through articles, visualizations and web applications. Access to information and the advance of computing applications for data processing have caused this journalistic discipline to become established in the media, beginning in the USA and reaching Spain recently. A new way of obtaining and telling the news to which more and more journalists are joining every day, seeking specialization and a new professional market niche. For the advancement of this journalistic field in Spain, the backing of Spanish universities is needed for training in data journalism in schools of information sciences, with the corresponding adaptation of curricula to Information and Communication Technologies (ICT).

2.1. Origin of Data Journalism


Data journalism emerges as a new method for obtaining information hidden in large databases. Through computational processing, data is extracted and then displayed in different types of news articles, features, visualizations or web applications, among others. A discipline with its origins in precision journalism and in CAR (Computer Assisted Reporting), or what is known as Computer-Assisted Journalism; innovative currents in working with information that appeared thanks to investigative journalism.


2.2 Background of Data Journalism


2.2.1. Precision Journalism


Precision journalism is considered an evolution of investigative journalism in which news is obtained through sociological and social observation methods. A definition of precision journalism is offered by Philip Meyer, considered the father of this discipline:

"It is the application of scientific methods of social and behavioral research to the practice of journalism" (Dader, 1997:20)

At the beginning of the 1960s, the challenge facing Meyer was how to work with large amounts of information. This journalist believed that a processing stage was necessary — therein lay the key to precision journalism.

"Raw data is never sufficient by itself. For data to be useful and intelligible it must be processed, conceptualized, integrated into some kind of framework. The raw data must be inserted into some mental frame of reference that aids its interpretation and comprehensibility"

(Meyer, 1993: 33)

Regarding the need for teamwork to achieve major news stories, in 1960 Philip Meyer already believed that media organizations should create new specialized departments so that social scientists and journalists could share tasks. He showed the need to create mixed teams that could achieve a new type of journalistic information, that could discover stories that could only be obtained through extensive analysis. But what are the objectives of this analysis?

- To locate new social facts of great journalistic relevance that allow knowledge previously unnoticed to be brought to light.

- To use methods developed in other disciplines in related fields to produce social knowledge.

- To apply a scientific perspective to newsworthy facts and phenomena.

- To locate new methods for identifying news stories.

- To build investigative models from those already developed in related disciplines.

The originator of this journalistic current, Philip Meyer, carried out the first precision journalism piece in 1959. This reporter for the Miami Herald revealed waste in the contracting of fire and hurricane insurance for schools in Dade County (Florida). Through rigorous investigation he compared insurance costs for different public real estate values in the same county. He also cross-referenced the names of insurance company executives who had contributed to school board campaigns with the school board directors, concluding that:

"Insurance professionals proved to be major contributors to the campaigns of the five school board members who had stood in the last elections" (Meyer, 2003:41)

This report was carried out with just a set of index cards and through comparison he concluded that there was political patronage between school board members and the beneficiary agents of school insurance. In 1967, Philip Meyer worked on another story at the Detroit Free Press about racial disturbances in Detroit. In collaboration with several colleagues at the newspaper, they demonstrated through house-by-house sampling in various Detroit districts that the journalistic coverage given to the events showed a completely distorted reality and that vandalism was not linked to people of lower educational levels, nor to African Americans from the South.

Through this empirical study they discovered that young people with higher levels of education had participated in the riots in similar proportions to those with lower educational levels. They also discredited the negative image the media conveyed of southern immigrants as those responsible for the street disturbances. Using precision journalism they concluded that the racial riots were misrepresented. Work that earned them the Pulitzer Prize a year later. This fact laid the foundations of precision journalism. In Spain, the pioneer of precision journalism was José Luis Dader, in 1992, who defines data journalism as:

"journalistic information that, on any subject of social relevance, systematically applies or analyzes empirical methods of scientific research, whether numerical or non-numerical, with a particular inclination toward the social sciences" (Dader, 1997: 22)

Although precision journalism bears some relationship with investigative journalism, José Luis Dader explains that both journalistic currents are not the same; because not everything considered investigative journalism uses methodological applications or instrumental knowledge of scientific research. On the other hand, he notes that precision journalism is not only applied in the field of communication. Reaching this point, we ask: What is the profile of a precision journalist?

According to Professor José Luis Dader, the communicator needs to:

2.2.2. Computer-Assisted Journalism


In 1969, precision journalism evolved into computer-assisted journalism or CAR. A journalistic discipline that introduced the use of spreadsheets to process information, something never done before.

Another journalist at the Miami Herald, Clarence Jones, leaving pencil and paper behind, used a computer to demonstrate irregularities in the judicial system of criminal sentencing in Dade County (Florida).

For his investigation Jones used punch cards and a programming language called COBOL, with which he analyzed 13,000 punch cards of processed data. His article "A Scientific Look at Crime in Dade" became the first example of computer-assisted journalism by analyzing government reports that normally remained hidden and of no interest. A method that would later be called 'public service journalism'.

In the mid-1980s, Elliot Jaspin, a reporter at the Providence Journal, adopted this methodology. He carried out an investigation into alleged corruption in local government. Jaspin analyzed data from 35,000 mortgages supposedly intended for middle and low incomes in Rhode Island. After the analysis, using a magnetic tape of all the computerized local government information, he discovered that the children of senior state officials had the lowest interest rates and the largest loans. He also uncovered the diversion of a large portion of these public funds to a hidden bank account.

Another example of computer-assisted journalism can be found in May 1988. A team of journalists from the Atlanta Journal-Constitution, including Bill Dedman,

## 11 Isbn 978-84-617-6853-0


discovered that the banks and savings institutions in Atlanta were discriminating against Black citizens. Banks were granting more mortgage loans to white citizens than to Black citizens of the same income level. For every Black citizen who was granted a mortgage loan, there were five white citizens who obtained the same credit. A story titled "The Color of Money" that earned them the Pulitzer Prize for Investigative Journalism in 1988. An award that demonstrated the potential of this new journalistic methodology.


2.3 Foundations of Data Journalism


Computer-assisted journalism continued evolving and developing into data journalism, also called "Data Journalism". Paul Bradshaw, a professor at the University of Birmingham, and Adrian Holovaty, an American journalist, were the great driving forces behind this journalistic discipline.

In 2005, Adrian Holovaty created "Chicagocrime.org". A project consisting of a virtual map or mashup of crimes occurring in Chicago. Holovaty, with his crime blog, managed to create a platform that collected all local information from Chicago, New York and San Francisco through a database open to everyone. Users could search by street name or zip code for all the news happening in these cities. In 2007 the Knight Foundation donated one million dollars to Adrian Holovaty's project, which evolved into "EveryBlock", an information portal that allowed users to find out what was happening in their district.

Two years later, in 2009 the American newspaper St. Petersburg Times received the Pulitzer Prize for the "PolitiFact" project. A website that analyzed the statements of American politicians. Although it was not until a year later, in 2010, that data journalism became known worldwide thanks to the Wikileaks case.

"Julian Assange himself (2012) will define Wikileaks as 'an organization that occupies cyberspace and is expert in moving its information around the underlying immersions, perhaps we are a post-state organization due to the lack of geographic control'"

In the summer of 2010 more than 90,000 confidential documents about the war in Afghanistan came to light. From that moment many journalists realized the importance of learning to decipher and interpret the information provided by databases. That year Wikileaks received Amnesty International's 'New Media' award for revealing a massacre in Kenya.

A year earlier, in 2009, the development of data journalism was spreading to other countries thanks to the "Hacks and Hackers" movement promoted by three journalists: Aron Pilhofer, of The New York Times, Rich Gordon, of Northwestern University, and Burton Herman, former AP correspondent. The goal was to establish a meeting point between computer scientists (Hackers) and journalists (Hacks), to exchange information about digital tools and analyze the contribution of programmers in areas such as handling large volumes of data, visualizations, etc. Since then, data journalism has been spreading across different media outlets.


2.4. Definition of Data Journalism


There are many definitions of data journalism to be found, without any consensus being reached among journalists and researchers. Paul Bradshaw, professor at the University of Birmingham and proponent of data journalism, explains that it is a discipline in which the power of computers is used to find, cross-check, visualize and combine information from various sources. Sandra Crucianelli, a data journalist and member of Knight International Journalism Fellows (ICFJ), defines it as:

"it is the same old investigative journalism (including methods of in-depth, precision, analytical journalism and what is known as 'computer-assisted'), to which some essential components must be added: Work is done with large volumes of open data, many of which come from closed formats, so in these cases a prior task of opening the data must be undertaken. It is team work: together with a data analyst, a programmer and an interactive visualization designer" (Crucianelli, 2012)

"talking about data journalism means referring to new techniques in the creation of journalistic information that requires specific knowledge and skills in the use of databases and the presentation (visualization) of information in new interfaces." (Flores, 2014:2)


2.5. The Inverted Pyramid of Data Journalism


Paul Bradshaw, in addition to offering a definition, was a pioneer in developing a new method for structuring information in data journalism, which he called the inverted pyramid. This procedure consists of five steps that go from greater to lesser amounts of information and importance. According to Bradshaw, data journalism involves the following steps: compile, clean, contextualize, combine and communicate the information. This process begins with a large amount of information that is gradually reduced throughout the process, leaving finally the information that we want to communicate or use.

Source: Bradshaw (2012) and own elaboration


A. Data Collection:

The information sources in data journalism are large databases. Many bytes of information available for analysis by the journalist. That

data found in bulk can be divided into two categories: structured (traditional data) and unstructured (Big Data). Structured data are those that have fixed fields, while unstructured data are those that do not follow fixed fields and are more difficult for the user to understand.

Who provides that information? A journalist can obtain their database from different sources:

1. Databases obtained directly from a public or private company. Different organizations can offer us their data for thorough analysis in a reusable format. A great source of information today are public bodies, thanks to the Law of Transparency and Open Government, which legislates public access to information for all citizens. If a citizen wants to know some data that is not available on the different transparency portals of public entities, they can request the information through that platform.

2. Databases by browsing different Internet portals. Many websites offer information online that is susceptible to analysis. An example of this would be the National Institute of Statistics, which daily offers databases in reusable formats.

3. Databases created through scraping. New technologies offer tools for extracting information from web pages in reusable format. This technique called scraping or in Spanish 'rascar' (to scrape) is carried out using applications such as: Kimono Labs, Scraper Wiki and Outwit Hub, which automatically extract information from an Internet portal in ".xml" format for subsequent analysis.

4. Databases from documents. Using a document, whether ".pdf" or ".doc" format, with conversion tools it is possible to obtain that information in a reusable format for subsequent processing.

B. Data Cleaning

Once we have the data, we need to trust it. The information in the database must be correct. To achieve this, human errors must be corrected, which sometimes arise when transferring data to a reusable format. In addition, we can eliminate data that is not useful for our investigation. Tools like Excel perform these cleaning tasks. Steps to carry out in data cleaning:

- Find and eliminate unwanted data in spreadsheets.

- Format the data appropriately according to the tools to be used for subsequent processing.

- Handle inconsistent data.

C. Contextualizing Data:

At this step we must interrogate the source and know how to interpret all the data we have. Questions to ask:

- Who collected this information?

- When was the data collected?

- For what purposes was it collected?

Many times we will have fields in our database whose meaning we will not know at first glance. We must look up their meaning and know how to interpret them, to gain a broader view of the information we are seeking. Sometimes this step will lead us to collect more information, in order to better understand the data.


D. Combining the Data

In order to display the information, combining two datasets is necessary. Although the information may be found in one source, it is necessary to combine it with another template, such as a map template to provide spatial visualization.


E. Communicating the Information

This is the final point of a major effort to select and filter information. At this point the results are visualized through an infographic, a visualization or through a web application.

4. Results


After a brief overview of data journalism and its background, I define this discipline as:

"A new way of telling the news based on the analysis of large volumes of data using computational tools, expressed through features, visualizations or web applications"

Data journalism must perform its public service function of providing information by reporting the news objectively and based on data. This journalistic modality makes use of mathematics and some journalists go so far as to consider it a science, since given the same sources, another person must reach the same conclusion as the journalist. It offers journalistic rigor and professionalism that many news stories based on a press release cannot provide.
