Information will always be important. We will need people who tell stories and contextualize data and events. The key lies in knowing whether the professionalization that began in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — in 1912 the Columbia University School of Journalism was founded — is the only option, or whether others exist. Everything points to the fact that this future can exist, because journalistic genres — especially the chronicle and the feature report — are far more attractive for achieving large audiences or goals of persuasion of public opinion than simple analyses or opinion columns by experts. _The Guardian_, _The New York Times_, _El País_, and _El Mundo_ will always have far greater audience and influence than an aggregator of academic opinions. And, furthermore, it is becoming ever more necessary to distance oneself from trashy television and infotainment (Elías, 2004). Many studies indicate that journalism on the internet is losing quality. According to María Teresa Sandoval, senior lecturer in Journalism at the Universidad Carlos III, digital journalism establishes "the primacy of immediacy over reflection and interpretation and, very especially, to the detriment of quality" (Sandoval Martín, 2005). Sandoval also draws attention to plagiarism and the pirating of content. On the other hand, young people — the readers of the present and the future — prefer the internet to the print press, which they never buy and barely read. According to a study designed by Miguel Túñez, senior lecturer in Journalism at the University of Santiago de Compostela, for young people "the press is the most credible medium, the internet the most independent. Young people see the newspaper as the medium closest to power and the internet as the one closest to individuals" (Túñez, 2009: 522). The best thing about twentieth-century journalism was that there was a sector of the population that could make a living from it without having to work in another profession. However, the internet has changed the environment. To start a blog one does not need the same effort — economic or personal — as to found a print newspaper or a radio or television station. If a blog offers public information, this means that, in a certain sense, it allows anyone to practice journalism. It could be an advantage, although one would need to analyze why someone, _motu proprio_ and without pay, begins to publish information. On the other hand, it is clear that it will no longer be as easy to earn a living solely from the journalism profession, as in the twentieth century. Perhaps the editors-in-chief of media outlets will be able to do so, but the bulk of information will be written by interns from the ever-growing number of Communication schools and by experts — the blog of Nobel Prize-winning economist Paul Krugman has more audience and is more respected than many traditional media outlets — or by science communicators — the blogs of NASA scientists are replacing traditional journalism — (Elías, 2010). These bloggers — journalists? — inform the public for free because they have other means of survival (the university, research centers, companies, banking, cinema...). That is, they will be dilettantes with respect to journalism. This phenomenon will need to be analyzed in depth: do these journalism dilettantes write as a social service or to promote their personal brand? Traditional journalism has made mistakes, particularly in countries with a weak democratic tradition. José Zepeda, director of the Latin American Department of _Radio Nederland_, considers that, for example, traditional media have done a great deal of harm to Latin American politics and society: "Pressure on the public agenda, improper use of facts to politically attack undesirable rulers, undisguised support for coup attempts, silence about successful activities or information intended to strip all relevance from positive government actions. Manipulation to exaggerate flaws, personal and collective smear campaigns" (Zepeda, 2011). And he adds: "The media feel themselves to be holders of an authority that greatly exceeds their functions. Their main defense is freedom of the press, a right transformed into the panacea that cures various ills." Something similar happens in Mexico. Traditional media, above all the duopoly of Televisa and Televisión Azteca, hold so much power that it is the media (and not society) who negotiate which candidate they select and support for the presidency of the Mexican Republic. In fact, workers from these traditional media are put forward to be elected as members of parliament. Mexico is the clearest case of a country where traditional media do not merely exercise counter-power: they are themselves the only power, above all Televisa and Televisión Azteca (Orta Vélez, 2012). The Mexican parliament has members of parliament who are chosen by the media (what they call the "telebancada"). This provokes a very strong reaction on social networks against traditional media or the journalists who work in them. It remains to be seen how the new cybernetic environment evolves. A significant part of society would welcome traditional media not having as much power as in the twentieth century. They demand that civil society, and above all experts, be the ones who analyze what is happening in the world and why. Everything points to economists, scientists, filmmakers, writers, historians, philosophers, sociologists, and political scientists sharpening their teeth to regain control of mass media — both in print (the few prestigious ones that survive) and on the internet. Experts are cheap (their salary is paid elsewhere) and highly specialized. Some have adopted the forms of communication that journalists have developed over decades (and many unemployed journalists have retrained as communication technique trainers for those experts). However, an economist — or a physicist or a sociologist — may be able to write a good economic — or scientific — analysis that is comprehensible to the general public, but it would be altogether ridiculous for them to publish a report in which they interview colleagues in the hallway or question themselves. It would be very difficult for them to write a vibrant chronicle of how a budget is debated in a parliament or how a new exoplanet is discovered. And not because they lack intellectual capacity, but because journalism — defined in the words of Italian journalist Eugenio Scalfari as "telling people what is happening to people" — is, above all, a craft: and writing a good report requires having written many not-so-good ones over a long career. Years of exclusive dedication are needed — accumulated experience, as in all crafts — to achieve mastery. If journalism wants to survive, it will have to take in young people and offer them good working conditions so that they can train in peace. But also, if it wants to survive, it cannot do without veteran journalists, who are the only ones capable of transmitting to the young the idiosyncrasies of the craft. Nor should one forget that journalistic genres (above all, the chronicle and the feature report — that is, journalism as a great literary genre —) have proven to be one of the most effective elements, not only for informing adequately, but for attracting mass audiences. The same is true of photography and infographics. The best weapon of mass persuasion is beauty; and journalists have always tried to use art — whether literary or visual art through the image — not only to recount and publish the truth, but to bring it closer to the segment of the public most reluctant to become aware of real problems. There is hope. It is true that _The New York Times_ and _The Guardian_ — or, in Spain, _El País_ and _El Mundo_ — have financial losses, but the digital environment has exponentially multiplied their influence and prestige. They have never been as influential as they are now (certainly far more so than when they were profitable). And, both before and now, their dominance in all respects — except the economic one — is infinitely greater than any that _The Conversation_ or any content farm could possess. On the other hand, a physicist or an economist working at a university does not contemplate denouncing corruption, uncovering a Watergate or a GAL scandal; rather, they will use the media to proselytize their point of view and sell more books, have more cited articles, or attract more funding for their research. It is not necessarily a social service, any more than the magazine published by an energy multinational or a tobacco company need be. Communication and journalism must not be confused. A society that does not want to know the truth — and that prefers reality television or fiction — does not deserve a future, and will not have one. If a society wants to survive with dignity, it will have to support and bet on quality journalism and on journalists committed to the truth.


**6.10. Proposed exercises**

1. Do you think the crisis of the traditional press has to do with the economic crisis or with a crisis of traditional journalistic formats?
2. What would your ideal media outlet look like? You can draw on the models analyzed in this chapter.
3. What differences do you observe between the (print) press and media that exist only digitally?
4. Analyze some types of reports that appear in _The Economist_. Do you think they would find a place in the media of your country or region?
5. Produce a report with classmates who are doing internships, examining whether communication companies are using student interns to replace workers or whether, on the contrary, veteran journalists are devoting time to teaching young students.
6. Differences and similarities between the _The Conversation_ model and that of _The Guardian_. Which do you prefer? Argue your answer. Which do you think is more influential?
7. Differences and similarities between _Demand Media_ and _The Huffington Post_. Which do you prefer? Argue your answer. Which do you think is more influential?
8. Do you agree with the notion of "digital feudalism" described in this chapter? What measures would you recommend to avoid it, if you do agree?
