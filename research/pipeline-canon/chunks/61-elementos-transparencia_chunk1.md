**ABSTRACT** [Portuguese]


This thesis investigates transparency in Data-Driven Journalism (DDJ), a body of
knowledge and practices linked to digital and networked journalism, situated within a scenario of
information overload and disinformation. It is grounded in theories of journalism
as a form (PARK, 1940; MEDITSCH, 1992; 1997) and forms (NIELSEN, 2017) of knowledge. Intertwined with fundamental values such as truth and objectivity, transparency (KARLSSON, 2010; 2020; 2022) concerns the openness of editorial processes and decisions,
allowing the audience access to information that is normally asymmetric in the relationship between the news outlet and the public. Thus, I present transparency in two
broad dimensions: as an **operator of ethical practice** and as a **discursive resource for**
**legitimation**. To examine how this value appears in journalistic products, I combine multiple case study methodology (STAKE, 2006) and the case study as illustration (MACHADO;
PALACIOS, 2006), observing the coverage of 11 reference and digital-native Brazilian newspapers in relation to the Covid-19 pandemic. As a result, I systematize 15 elements of transparency, distributed across **organizational** and **operational** levels, detected
in coverage produced within DDJ and presented here in alphabetical order: **byline, profile**
**and expertise**; **contact**; **error correction**; **publication date**; **documents**; **data**
**sources**; **funding sources**; **degrees of uncertainty**; **reporting markers**; **editorial**
**manual and principles**; **methodology**; **diversity policy**; **privacy**
**policy**; **content type**; **reproducibility and open-source code**. These parameters
serve as a guide for what I call the **Data-Driven Journalism Transparency**
**Protocol**, designed for the analysis of already-published journalistic products. Finally,
detecting a theoretical-experimental gap, I produce the proposal of a **Method for Data-**
**Driven Journalism with a focus on transparency and reproducibility** for
implementation in data-driven journalistic initiatives. I argue that the pandemic has accelerated the process of incorporating transparency as a fundamental value for

- DDJ, previously demonstrated in the discourse of professionals and now verified in the sphere of the product. The tendency is for these changes to endure and improve, reaffirming DDJ's commitment to approaching science via method in order to generate
a specific type of knowledge, more contextual and lasting.

**Keywords**: digital journalism; Data-Driven Journalism; Data Journalism;
transparency; Covid-19.


**ABSTRACT**


This doctoral dissertation investigates transparency in data journalism. Data journalism,
or data-driven journalism, is a set of practices and knowledge connected to digital and
network journalism. It is based on journalism theories that discuss the news as a form
(PARK, 1940; MEDITSCH, 1992; 1997) or forms (NIELSEN, 2017) of knowledge. Transparency (KARLSSON, 2010; 2020; 2022) is also connected to journalism fundamentals
such as truth and objectivity, so it comprehends openness of processes and editorial decisions, giving the audience access to information typically only used by journalists. In this
regard, I present transparency in two axes: as a **guide to ethical practices** and a **re-**
**source to legitimize news organizations**. To examine how this value appears in terms
of journalistic product, I combine the methodologies of multiple case studies (STAKE,
2006) and case studies as illustration (MACHADO; PALACIOS, 2006) to observe the news
coverage conducted by 11 news outlets (legacy media and native digital) during the
Covid-19 pandemic. As a result, I systematize 15 elements of transparency in two levels:
**organizational** and **operational**, here alphabetically organized: **byline, profile, and ex-**
**pertise**; **contact**; **content type**; **correction**; **data sources**; **diversity policy**; **docu-**
**ments**; **financing sources**; **manual of style and editorial principles**; **methodology**;
**open-source code and reproducibility**; **privacy policy**; **publication date; uncertainty**
**degrees**; **verification tactics**. These are the parameters for the **Data Journalism' Trans-**
**parency Protocol**, which is destined to analyze journalistic products that were already
published. In addition, observing a theoretical and experimental gap, I propose the **Data**
**Journalism Method focused on transparency and reproducibility** to be implemented
by news organizations in investigations using data. I argue that the pandemic has accelerated the presence of transparency as an essential value for data journalism. It was previously demonstrated by studies that included professionals' discourses, and now it is
possible to recognize it in the published content. The tendency is that those changes will
be maintained and improved, consolidating the commitment of data journalism to be
practiced towards science through the method and, thus, generate a specific type of
knowledge, which is contextual and has a longer duration.

**Keywords**: digital Journalism, Data-Driven Journalism, Data journalism, transparency,
Covid-19.

**1 INTRODUCTION**


In March 2020, when the Covid-19 pandemic began to spread across the

world, open data activists immediately identified a problem: the precariousness

in the recording of cases and deaths from the disease. In Brazil, where information

is initially recorded at the municipal and state level, it falls to the Ministry

of Health to systematize and compile the numbers. For a series of reasons that will be explained

throughout the thesis, including the incompetence of the federal government, it became clear that

one could not count on the government for precise, localized records on the

pandemic, since it was releasing only coarsely granular information by state of the

federation.

The limitations in information and the absence of any prospect of improvement prompted

a partially independent count of Covid-19 cases and deaths —

partially because there was still a base of knowledge tied to the state

health agencies — by civil society initiatives, such as _Brasil.io_ [1],

still in March 2020; and by the consortium of Brazilian press outlets [2], formed in

early June of that year due to a "data blackout" registered at the Ministry

of Health. Other targeted initiatives, such as _Open Knowledge Brasil_ (OKBr), created ways

to monitor the quality of public information. OKBr, for example, founded the _Covid-19_

_Transparency Index_ [3], whose aim was to classify the level of openness of

state data and subsequently of capital city data.

In a country of continental dimensions like Brazil, where there is heterogeneity

between municipalities and regions, it was crucial from the outset that there be specific

and detailed data on the situation of the disease, so as to guide the government in

making conscious, evidence-based decisions. As could be seen in the country,

some mayors and governors took the situation seriously, suggesting containment

measures to slow the spread of the virus, such as social distancing and mask use. President

Jair Bolsonaro, in turn, chose to attack science and journalism, which he repeatedly

accused of spreading _fake news_, and opted to encourage the use of medications without

efficacy. As we found in a survey conducted at the beginning of the pandemic

From the start of the pandemic, I was very close to the numbers related to

Covid-19. In addition to acting locally as a member of the Open Knowledge Brasil Ambassador Network

since 2019, organizing annual events such as Open Data Day,

I was one of the volunteers in the state of Rio Grande do Sul for _Brasil.io_. For six months,

two other colleagues and I collected daily the data on cases and deaths released

by the State Health Secretariat (SES/RS); to obtain the numbers at the national level,

around 40 volunteers worked daily throughout the pandemic. In the case of RS, we

even noticed advances in data openness: while the first bulletins were

the possibility of downloading spreadsheets in an open, non-proprietary

format (.csv). Until that point, one could say that the data were _available_, but not

_accessible_ because they were not offered in an open format that allowed for the reuse of

information and the production of new analyses. _Brasil.io_, in turn, was committed to

openness from the start, additionally explaining the collection methodology [4] in public

databases.

One of the first and most important lessons I drew from my experience with open

data and the Covid-19 pandemic was precisely the precariousness in the collection and opening

of information — in the first year, the problems were confined to case and death

numbers; in the second, the issues extended to vaccination records. As a

qualified mediator of reality, it would fall to journalism to explain to people what was

happening and how to interpret the whole situation. That is, journalists were required to

look skeptically at the statistics, understanding their contexts of production

and availability. More than explaining concepts like the moving average, which received

so much attention,

putting the numbers in context required journalists to move closer to

science coverage. Science, in this case, in two senses: science communication, since

the pandemic is linked to the health field, and method, since journalists had to

understand the provisional nature of science and the cumulative character of this type of

knowledge, without there being a definitive and sudden answer to everything.

Faced with the provisional nature of the situation, I began to observe that, when drawing on

public databases to write reports, journalists needed, for example,

to expose the limitations of those records. They needed to explain that the numbers were not

detailed or that there could be delays in entering information into public

systems, which in turn would generate a temporary distortion in analyses. In some cases,

when certain statistics could not be used, data journalists would indicate,

in the stories, why they decided to exclude one or another state from the analysis,

or why such information should be observed with caution by the reader. They were,

then, explaining their working method, informing what sources were consulted,

how reliable those sources were, and how one or another result was reached.

In doing so, they were offering the public a kind of guide to reading their

work — put another way, they were offering **transparency**. Journalism, which so

often limits itself to demanding openness from the government, was finally

disclosing information — also of public interest — that had to do with

its method. It is likely that the pandemic accelerated the process of methodological

transparency in journalism, or that this happened specifically with Data-

Driven Journalism because, as we shall see, this set of practices

and knowledge is historically constituted on the basis of a scientific approach.

Given the complexity of the pandemic, which includes both information overload and

disinformation, there is a sense of content overload in the digital environment (BO
CZKOWSKI, 2021), which at times makes it difficult to know where trust should be placed. According to the _Digital News Report_ (NEWMAN _et al._ 2021), trust in news

rose three percentage points in Brazil in 2021, the second year of the pandemic, compared

to 2020. This means that 36% of people from a representative sample of the Brazilian

population declared trust in journalism — the highest index had been recorded in

2018, before the presidential elections, at 41%. It should be noted that during the presidential inauguration, in January 2019, supporters of Bolsonaro chanted "_Facebook_" and

"_WhatsApp_" [5] as a provocation to journalists covering the event on-site, as if the

platforms were neutral and receiving information gained more quality

without journalistic mediation.

Journalists and researchers, then, seek to look at transparency as a

fundamental value of journalism that can strengthen or even re-establish the link between

the public and the professionals of the field. Experienced and award-winning reporter at _Folha de_

_S.Paulo_, Patrícia Campos Mello (2020, n.p.) compares transparency to a journalistic version of "visit our kitchen," in which readers are invited to understand the ins and outs of production.

Karlsson (2022) identifies three waves of transparency in journalism, with the

current and third — beginning in 2016 — related to scrutiny and questioning

of established social institutions, which fits the scenario of disinformation and

public distrust. Thus, transparency in journalism would be a response to this

hesitation. The author, however, demonstrates a certain skepticism about the capacity of journalism

to open new information to readers, since the scenario of news production and distribution

is currently permeated by digital platforms and constant algorithmic

interference, making integral openness of a broader process unfeasible.

To address this issue, authors speak of algorithmic transparency

(DIAKOPOULOS, 2019), that is, the possibility of verifying computationally structured

information. As part of the scope of digital journalism, Data-Driven Journalism

presents characteristics that allow it to allocate human and technological resources toward transparency, such as the architecture of the Web, structured

through hyperlinks (MIELNICZUK, 2003). Thus, targeted solutions, such as the availability

of original sources, could be implemented without major technical difficulty.

In summary, transparency in journalism carries the meaning of openness,

of "strategically managed visibility" (KARLSSON, 2022, p. 78) and of reduction of information

asymmetry (KARLSSON, 2010; 2020; 2022), since the journalist has

first-hand access to data that the audience does not possess; thus, authors such as

Kovach and Rosenstiel (2011; 2014) advocate that ways be found for journalism to compensate for this

aspect, by revealing information about sources and reporting methods.

In Data-Driven Journalism, this includes, for example, providing hyperlinks to

This thesis presents elements of transparency found in informative content

of Data-Driven Journalism (DDJ). To do so, it draws on a theoretical foundation that understands

journalism as a form (PARK, 1940; MEDITSCH, 1992; 1997) or forms (NIELSEN,

2017) of knowledge, and DDJ as a body of knowledge and practices that approaches

science via method (GEHRKE, 2017; TRÄSEL; GEHRKE, 2021), the basic premise of

its oldest predecessor, Precision Journalism (MEYER, 2002).

My **hypothesis** is that, connected to truth and objectivity, transparency

becomes a fundamental value for the growing practice of Data-Driven Journalism,

which materializes in elements whose demonstration intensifies during periods of

significant events for humanity, such as the Covid-19 pandemic. Following from this hypothesis, my **main objective** is to identify and analyze the elements

of transparency activated in informative content (news stories, reports, and projects)

of DDJ published by Brazilian reference newspapers [6] and digital-native outlets [7]. The **specific objectives** are: a) to discuss Data-Driven Journalism as a form capable of generating

specific knowledge, linked to science; b) to understand how transparency

manifests in this type of journalism, relating it to journalistic ethics and

as a legitimation strategy; c) to systematize the transparency parameters verified

at the organizational level and at the operational level; and d) to develop a protocol for identifying transparency and a methodological proposal for implementation in DDJ. To test

my hypothesis and fulfill the objectives related to it, I employ a combination

of methodologies useful for the field of digital journalism and its innovative

characteristics: multiple case studies (STAKE, 2006) and the case study as illustration (MA
CHADO; PALACIOS, 2007).

**2.1 The complex scenario of disinformation and its variations**


Around the time of the US presidential race, on December 4, 2016, Edgar Maddison Welch left his hometown, Salisbury, and drove to Washington,

D.C. to verify, with his own eyes, the location attributed to a supposed pedophilia network connected to candidate Hillary Clinton. With the intention of saving the children who

supposedly were victims of abuse at the site — and who were never found — he opened

fire on the pizzeria _Comet Ping Pong_. Employees managed to flee and no one was

injured. Interviewed [16] by a journalist from _The New York Times_ after surrendering to police,

Welch, the father of two girls, said that, from reading a series of articles

and a "combination of sources," he had found evidence that something nefarious was occurring at the

pizzeria and decided to go to the location to check for himself. A few days before, he had installed an internet

service at his home.

The chain of disinformation that led Welch to take a drastic action

is known as _Pizzagate_, a conspiracy theory created from the

email correspondence of the pizzeria owner regarding a fundraising dinner for

Clinton's campaign. These messages, from campaign chairman John Podesta,

were leaked by _WikiLeaks_ [17] and, at some point, people began to associate the use of

terms such as _cheese pizza_ with _child pornography_.

The _Pizzagate_ case illustrates with precision the chaotic scenario of today, in which

diverse types of information and disinformation coexist and can be accessed through

search engines and digital social networks. Conspiracy theories, also present

in this environment, add a dangerous ingredient to this scenario. Like

other types of disinformation, they draw on themes that provoke outrage and indignation.

Welch declared having been motivated by the impulse to defend children.

but also by Donald Trump's recurrent use of the term "_fake news_" to

attack the credibility of the press. After him, other far-right politicians began

to adopt similar discourses and strategies. Research shows that false content

generally has greater propagation potential than truthful content. In a recent scenario,

As stated earlier, in this thesis I use the term **disinformation** to designate

misleading content that circulates with the purpose of deceiving and undermining reputations. Among

other factors relating to the association of journalism with the falsehoods circulating on the Web,

I agree with Wardle and Derakhshan (2017), for whom the term _fake news_ is essentially

too shallow to describe a complex phenomenon that encompasses information pollution.

Thus, the authors hold that three terms can describe more precisely what

occurs in this scenario. They are: _misinformation_ — when false content is shared

without the intention to deceive; _disinformation_ — when content circulates to cause

harm; _malinformation_ — when truthful information is shared to

cause harm, normally associated with something that should have remained in the private

sphere. In broad, generic terms, all three can be rendered as "desinformação" in Portuguese.

Commonly invoked by politicians, the term _fake news_ can be understood as

a subtype of informational fraud that seeks to mimic news (TANDOC JR;

LIM; LING, 2018). Dourado (2020, p. 54) defines it as "[...] a type of false or distorted information that simulates a news item — or novelty — in order to narrate political facts and

thus gain greater visibility in transit across social media platforms." In

her view, the urgent character of journalism is fundamental in this case, since the

so-called _fake news_ mimics the idea of novelty and alleged public interest. Thus,

disinformation mimics the format of news — often including supposed

sources — making use of headlines, photos, and texts. This type of content also commonly presents

phrases like "urgent" and "share with your contacts." The process

of fabricating disinformation makes the intent to deceive even more evident. These are signs

of this deliberate confusion, according to Dourado (2020): the use of _clickbait_ in headlines, video

or image montages, and other graphic pieces.

Authors such as Egelhofer _et al._ (2020) observe a hollowing out of meaning

in relation to the term _fake news_. It is notable that the term is being used excessively, normally

referring to lies and inaccuracies in general, which occurs, including, in

publications released by the press itself. For the researchers, one of the problems with the term

circulating freely within journalism itself is its use by political actors, who feel

at ease to employ it in attacking the legitimacy and credibility of the press

as a democratic institution.

If for theorists the term _fake news_ can identify a type of disinformation,

among most people there are no clear signals. Through questionnaires and focus

groups, Nielsen and Graves (2017) observed that the public notably associates

low-quality journalism (sensationalism, for example), propaganda, and some types

of advertising with the term _fake news_, even though, at first, this type of

content is not produced with the intent to disinform. Thus, the researchers observed

that the public associates the term _fake news_ not only with false content, but also

with a broader (dis)informational [18] scenario that includes the press, politicians, and

the platforms.

There are also other subdivisions within the breadth of disinformation or _fake_

_news_. Broadly speaking, Wardle and Derakhshan (2017) and Wardle (2019) classify disinformation into: fabricated content, manipulated content, imposter content, false context, misleading content, false connection, and satire or parody. Tandoc Jr., Lim and Ling (2018),

in turn, observe the following typology: satire, parody, fabrication, manipulation, advertising, and propaganda. Fabrication, the authors state, is what could most

closely be classified as what is known as _fake news_.

Regarding the type of content most widely circulated during the pandemic, we identified

(GEHRKE; BENETTI, 2021) that, of the 300 qualitatively analyzed cases, 145 (48.43%)

employ false context, that is, they make use of a true fact or genuine image, but

remove the materials from context to create a falsehood. The second most commonly

invoked content type is fabricated content, with 86 (28.67%) occurrences. This is content created

to deceive, including the use of research institutions and fictitious names in an attempt to

attribute credibility to what was written. The results align with what was detected

by Dourado (2020), who observed that the use of photos and videos functions as a

sort of guarantee or evidence that the story being told is plausible.

Covid-19 is not the first scientific event to be linked to

disinformation. As observed by Machado _et al._ (2020b), there is not necessarily

a questioning of science or its methods, but similar strategies

are used to spread disinformation, such as uncertainty, which is inherent to science as

there is a systematic production of knowledge and the validity of theories

until they are replaced by others that better account for reality.

**2.2 Top-down: the influence of politicians and public figures**


Throughout 2020, according to data from the fact-checking agency _Aos Fatos_ [21], President

Jair Bolsonaro made 915 false or imprecise statements about the Covid-19

pandemic. In his memoirs, former Health Minister Luiz Henrique Mandetta, who was

in office when the disease began to spread through Brazil from January 25 onward, said that, still in early March, the Palácio do Planalto was operating as if the new coronavirus did not exist. According to Mandetta (2020), Bolsonaro never agreed to analyze charts

and confront the reality of the disease that the government would need to face. This was reflected

in the president's actions: throughout the pandemic, he minimized the virus, criticized social

distancing, and encouraged people to take ineffective medications.

Political actors play a fundamental role in networks and, therefore, in the propagation

of information or disinformation. Although there are strategies and mechanisms of propagation, studies suggest that an individual's influence can be decisive

in the spread of false content. In the research by Machado _et al._ (2020a), carried out

through network analysis and lexical analysis, people with some degree of social authority, even those distant from science, drew on their hierarchical positions within

their sphere of action to challenge the guidelines proposed by expert systems such as

the press, universities, and international organizations, thereby supporting conspiracy

theories.

In her thesis, Dourado (2020) found that personal profiles are the most relevant

in the propagation of disinformation, showing that this phenomenon spreads far more

through organic than automated channels. To support this, the study showed the prevalence

of personal profiles among the principal disseminators in general and among those coded

as the most relevant propagators. At the same time, she also discovered

that, among the most relevant personal profiles, those that did not appear to be bots (that is,

automated accounts) were nearly five times more frequent than those that

appeared to be bots. This means that, however much the climate of informational fraud is

Wardle and Derakhshan (2017) propose examining the elements of disinformation

separately, namely: the agent, the message, and the interpreters. The agent,

for example, can operate in different instances, such as fabrication and distribution. And this is precisely

one of the main challenges in disinformation studies: understanding what

the motivations of people are. As we observed in our research (GEHRKE; BENETTI,

2021), it is not possible to assert that the propagators of disinformation are also the creators

of the rumors; but it is a fact that these people have a high degree of influence in

networks. Regarding Covid-19, federal deputy Osmar Terra and members of the Bolsonaro

family, including the president himself, are among those who most spread disinformation,

according to our survey.

In research examining disinformation about Covid-19 on _YouTube_, Ma
chado _et al._ (2020a) identified two main narratives related to the disease: one

of a geopolitical character, blaming China for the supposed production of the virus as a biological weapon and using it for economic purposes; and another of a religious character, seeking to frame

the pandemic within a context of biblical symbolism. In the case of the religious narrative,

one of the prominent figures in the spread of disinformation is pastor Silas Mala
faia, leader of the neopentecostal church Assembleia de Deus and a supporter of President Jair Bolsonaro. In this narrative, data and scientific aspects are distortedly used to

associate the pandemic with a divine plague. Another person who appeared in this research as

a significant agent in the diffusion of disinformation was journalist Alexandre Garcia, former

host at Rede Globo. In one of the videos published on _YouTube_, he questions the

circulation and persistence of the virus in tropical environments like Brazil. The common thread

among the campaigns identified by Machado _et al._ (2020a) was the minimization of the

pandemic. The authors assess that, when convenient, the discourse of disinformation distorts

science and decontextualizes points from technical notes.

Other studies (MACHADO _et al._, 2020b; SOARES; RECUERO, 2020) have also demonstrated

the influence of figures like Bolsonaro in the Brazilian disinformation scenario. By analyzing a dataset composed of 8,600 posts verified

by 70 fact-checking agencies in 134 countries, Machado _et al._ (2020b) found that

disinformation about Covid-19 follows regional patterns, involving cultural,

geographic, and political factors. In this scenario, countries like Brazil and India find

themselves more isolated than the rest of the world. In Brazil's case, the main reason is Bolsonaro's insistence on the use of measures without scientific proof, such as the drugs

hydroxychloroquine, azithromycin, and ivermectin.

In his memoirs, Mandetta (2020, p. 133) clarifies Bolsonaro's insistence

on chloroquine: for the president, it would be enough to offer a remedy for workers

to return to activity and to get the economy moving. "For Bolsonaro the solution was

always simple: his project for combating the pandemic was to say that the government has the

medicine and that whoever takes the medicine will be fine. Only those who were going to die

anyway will die."

Research such as that of Rossini and Kalogeropoulos (2021) suggests that trust in the

federal government is higher among Brazilians who have right-wing political preferences,

that is, those who are aligned with the government. In general, respondents said they trust

scientists most for obtaining information about the pandemic — percentages varied

from 70% (right-wing) to 90% (left-wing). In second place, participants said they trust

the information provided by the World Health Organization (WHO), reaching a

percentage of 86% among left-wing people, with the exception of right-wing participants,

who say they trust the Ministry of Health more (63%) and the federal government (63%).

We have seen, in this section, that political actors and influencers play an important

role in the diffusion of information or disinformation, given that they play a decisive role

in the way networks are organized and because of their reach in the network.

Below, we shall examine what the main measures are to combat disinformation.


**2.3 The effectiveness of disinformation and strategies to combat it**


Researchers from different areas of knowledge seek strategies to combat

disinformation. In this scenario, one of the greatest challenges is understanding the

intentions that motivate the agent creating or disseminating false content, a practice generally

motivated by ideological or economic concerns (TANDOC JR; LIM; LING; 2018).

Moreover, this type of content alters the way reality is perceived and confronted

by people, since the image of institutions is affected by this type of discourse

(CARNIELLI; EPSTEIN, 2019). Since there is confusion between beliefs, opinions, and arguments, discourses that seek to disinform stimulate an emotional

response to events in opposition to rationality.

Carnielli and Epstein (2019) define it as follows: a **belief** is a conviction

that someone accepts as true, while an **opinion** is a manifestation of that

belief. Neither are arguments. A **fact**, in turn, is something that can be proven

(for example, through repeated scientific studies, as is the case with global warming).

Facts guarantee the truth of premises, which form the basis of the **argument**.

Thus, invoking the maxim that "this is my opinion" is a disloyal conduct

completely removed from facts and argumentation.

Furthermore, on the platforms, algorithms are adapted to favor interpersonal communication, amplifying interactivity among users and the reinforcement of beliefs already established

users are led to interact with each other and consume information" (DOURADO, 2020, p.

Various cognitive biases are used to perpetuate disinformation,

such as confirmation bias — the acceptance of information with which one

already agrees (CARNIELLI; EPSTEIN, 2019; COSENZA, 2016). In an attempt to uncover

how beliefs are entrenched and how they spread in society, researchers

O'Connor and Weatherall (2019) seek explanations by examining social networks — not necessarily

digital ones. One of the network models worked by the authors concerns the

predisposition of a group to trust someone who shares the same perceptions as them

in some respect. For example, if a person holds conservative values (in favor of the traditional family and against abortion, by way of illustration) and sees that the president

shares the same premise, the tendency is to trust him with other decisions.

Other cognitive biases explored by Carnielli and Epstein (2019) are the bandwagon effect,

in which people believe something because many others believe it; the repetition cascade,

in which content becomes plausible due to repetitions in public

discourse; and the third-person effect, the belief that messages have more effect on others

than on oneself. One of the most common examples, explain Capella, Ophir and Sutton (2018),

involves cigarettes: a smoker knows that smoking is bad, but tends to think that it may

be harmful to most people who smoke, but not to him. Thus, the authors

understand that, to correct disinformation effectively, one must first measure

people's knowledge about a topic, stimulating different approaches to understand,

also, how much someone sees themselves as part of the problem.

One limitation, however, is that we learn from others. According to O'Connor

and Weatherall (2019, p. 8), most people acquire false beliefs in the same

places where they acquire reliable beliefs, and for this reason there is the risk of ending up learning both.

This is what makes _homo sapiens_ the winner of human evolution. "The ability to

share information and influence one another's beliefs is part of what makes

humans special." [22] Not infrequently, as Kakutani (2018) shows, populist politicians draw

on a "us versus them" discourse, attacking what is external to a small group and

reinforcing feelings of nationalism, tribalism, and hatred of foreigners.

There is also complexity regarding the reasons why people spread

disinformation — and do not necessarily believe it. The persistence of some stories

circulating, say O'Connor and Weatherall (2019), is related to the fact that some

people believe it to be something funny or unbelievable, and therefore share it

out of irony or sarcasm. Additionally, there are those who share

content because the meme or other digital piece is capable of reflecting their feelings

about a topic.

Specifically about Covid-19, Posetti and Bontcheva (2020) understand that there

are some strategies to correct and curb disinformation. For the authors, responses

to this public health problem can be structured around four fronts: monitoring

and investigation (fact-checking and _debunking_ — publicly rebutting rumors —

for example); State policies and legislation; curation, technology, and economic response; and

an ethical, educational, and credibility-related perspective.

For Carnielli and Epstein (2019), the evaluation of premises through critical

thinking is a way of combating disinformation. In this sense, the authors encourage

people to identify who benefits from the news or post, what its central

point and conclusions are, detection of fallacies in reasoning, differentiation between facts and

the interpretation of facts, quality of evidence, source credibility, comparison

with other sources, whether the story is too interesting or impressive, whether someone signs

the piece, among other measures.

Regarding Covid-19, two recent studies presented by Pennycook _et al._

(2020) involving more than 1,700 adults in the United States show that

people share disinformation in part because they did not sufficiently reflect

on the accuracy of the content they read and passed on. Thus, the authors showed that,

to stimulate critical thinking, one effective measure can be the practice of _nudge_,

that is, offering small stimuli for people to reflect before sharing

content. By analyzing _WhatsApp_ and _Facebook_ as environments where

One of the best-known practices within journalism as a form of combating

moves between the political and academic spheres, since it involves public figures and is a practice

with a specific method of verification. As we shall see below, transparency,

journalism (SEIBT, 2019), similar to what occurs in Data-Driven Journalism

**2.5 The Covid-19 pandemic as a journalistic event**


Monitoring the progress of the Covid-19 pandemic and reporting on its developments

has been the primary duty of the press since the disease became known to the world at the end of 2019, and especially after the declaration of its global proportions by the WHO

in March of the following year. More than ever, journalism has a social role to fulfill,

which must be accomplished through the publication of truthful,

precise, qualified, and verified information.

As of October 26, 2021, according to the _Johns Hopkins University_ tracker,

there had been 244.3 million [33] infection cases worldwide, and a total of 4.9 million

deaths. The United States was, at that point, the country with the highest number. Alone, it

tallied approximately 45.5 million infected, in addition to 737,600 deaths. Since the beginning of

the pandemic, Brazil has been following a similar path, with 21.7 million cases and 605,000

deaths. The scenario began to improve with the advance of vaccination across countries. According to

this same tracker, 6.8 billion doses were administered. Covid-19

is likely the main public health phenomenon recorded in this century and, consequently,

the largest journalistic event of the period.

Unpredictable and unlikely, the event is what departs from the normalcy of routine.

In summary: "[...] the event is unpredictable, it erupts accidentally on the epidermal

surface of bodies as an unexpected reflex, as an effect without cause, as a pure attribute"

(RODRIGUES, A. 2016, p. 53). Also from Rodrigues (2016, p. 51) comes the classic definition

that "An event is everything that erupts on the smooth surface of history [...]."

The temporal perspective is intimately tied to the backdrop of the event. For Quéré (2005, p. 61), the event is a beginning and a rupture, since it creates actions and

has an inaugural character. "The event introduces a discontinuity, only perceptible

against a background of continuity." In this case, we are not speaking of artificial

and manufactured events (pseudo-events) with the idea of novelty, as occurs with

press conferences, nor of media events (such as the

Olympics and similar events), which depend on press coverage to take

shape (PONTES; SILVA, 2010).

To understand where the event is situated, it is necessary to return to the idea of

a shared map of meanings, that is, of values shared in society. Journalism plays a fundamental role in communicating events, since it is capable

of legitimating already-established frames of reference. In a text that proposes thinking of journalism as an event, Benetti (2010, p. 147) says that what can be questioned is

the supposed homogeneity between the maps of meanings belonging to different groups.

"It is a circular movement that begins with the interests of institutionalized speech of power

and returns to those same interests, always mediated by the technical procedures that

legitimate the discursive practice of journalism." By invoking concepts repeatedly, journalism becomes an event. "Journalism is, thus, _an event_ when

I can take it as a record of the hegemonic values of a society in an era"

(BENETTI, 2010, p. 162).

It is important to recognize the Covid-19 pandemic as an event because its

coverage escapes the conventional and requires a different type of journalistic treatment. Because it is a serial concatenation (QUÉRÉ, 2005), it demands interpretation and explanation

from journalism, which can be made possible by the context of the facts. All of this

occurs in a scenario of cyber-events (HENN, 2013, p. 32), in which it is not possible

to disentangle facts from the contemporary culture inherent to digital networks, where

public and private spheres intermingle. The author understands the cyber-event as "[...] a new

modality in the process of occurring" in which journalism loses its centrality to the detriment

of the characteristics of the network.

Rodrigues (2016) identifies three main categories of registration for determining

the referential event: 1) excess — involving massacres, drunkenness, and others; 2) failure — encompassing natural disasters, floods, earthquakes, and other cases that

demonstrate a failure in the functioning of some institution; 3) inversion — comprising

the opposite of what is expected in normal situations (example: a thief who steals a car

and then returns it to the owner). Beyond referential events, the author proposes

the category of meta-events: those that only exist if they are enunciated, which

are directly conditioned by discourse. By reporting the facts, journalism,

as a discursive genre (BENETTI, 2008), produces a new event. The meta-event is the event that allows going beyond its own dimensions, transcending the

singularity of the reported fact (BENETTI; STORCH; FINATTO, 2011).

The coverage of the Covid-19 pandemic activates the _referential journalistic event_

marked by _failure_ and _excess_ and the _meta-event_. The spread of an initially unknown

virus at a global level is capable of, at first, putting into question consolidated institutions

such as science itself. Questions immediately arise

about the predictability of events like this, as well as possible immunization against

the virus through a vaccine. Science is pressed to have all the answers: what virus is this

and how did it spread? What are its characteristics and symptoms? How was no one capable

of tracking it in advance? What is the treatment? Is there a cure? And, as the number

of infected and dead advances, the functioning of other institutions is equally

placed under suspicion. Politics, materialized by its leaders and the essential services

offered to the population, is targeted by questions as the precariousness in the structures of public healthcare is laid bare.

With the advance of the event and the changes it causes in daily life, another type

of journalistic coverage is required. Thus, the news is no longer about the emergence of the

virus itself, but, for example, about how governments in various parts of the world

handle the case in their health systems and regarding mandatory social

isolation. In this way, the meta-event arises: a narrativity that occurs in its own

time. In cases like this, the event being narrated is no longer the first event,

but constitutes a narrative built upon another narrative (PONTES; SILVA, 2010).

The axis of signification of a meta-event is long (BENETTI; STORCH; FINATTO,

2011), which gives it the potential for various thematic approaches. As Genro

Filho (2012) says, events must be understood as incomplete processes

narrated by journalists with the necessary openness of meaning so that the dialectical

relationships between the categories singular, particular, and universal may be established.

The journalistic event occurs insofar as this professional practice is capable

of treating phenomena that generate shared experience, organizes the temporal experience

of contemporary human beings, and produces supposed consensuses (BENETTI, 2010). Sometimes disoriented by the unforeseen, people turn to journalism to seek explanations

and to insert the fact into a temporal sequence. In this way, the idea of context is fundamental

for journalism, so that a causal explanation for the facts is sought. Thus, journalistic coverage,

even if concentrated in the present, draws on elements from the past to explain

the progress of events. The event, therefore, gives rise to a dimension of the past. When covering Covid-19,

for example, journalism draws on other epidemics or pandemics faced around the world.

Looking back, in this case, gives the sense of having elements with which to understand what is occurring

at the moment. "There are things that happen, and that we thought impossible to happen, because

they exceeded the thinkable or our sense of the possible. When they happen, we are forced to

acknowledge that there were possibilities, potentialities, or eventualities" (QUÉRÉ, 2005,

p. 63).

Events with the dimensions of this pandemic work, above all, with values

dear to society. Given that birth and death are among the limit-accidents in

relation to occurrences (RODRIGUES, A. 2016), there is a return to ritualistic processes

(BENETTI, 2010, p. 155) in this type of coverage. "The movements of life bring us back

to great existential questions: death, violence, love, chance, the

imponderables."

In journalism, the less predictable an event is, the greater the probability of its

becoming news and integrating journalistic discourse (RODRIGUES, 2005). In this

case, Benetti's (2010) criticism of journalistic practice is the naturalization of persistent

problems, such as hunger, inequalities, and social justice issues — because

they are treated as something that simply occurs in society, they are rarely addressed

by news outlets. "Stated bluntly, these are facts cynically perceived

as ordinary and commonplace and which, for this reason, fail to meet the requirements that would allow

them to attain the status of journalistic event" (BENETTI, 2010, p. 146).

It is likely that an event of the dimensions of Covid-19 is especially

impactful in the order of lived experiences. If the event is generally elevated

to the character of a fact (QUÉRÉ, 2005), in high-impact situations it operates in the order of experience. Given that the event is a phenomenon that happens to someone, people

are usually deeply marked by the actions it generates. If, as a rule,

people remember what they were doing on September 11, 2001, when the

twin towers of the _World Trade Center_ were targeted by a terrorist attack in New York,

it is very likely that, in a few years, people will record their own memories and

experiences from the time the pandemic struck humanity. Human experience

allows the event to be memorized, recalled, and reconstructed.

Memory, in turn, depends on social contexts and how such recollections are constructed

and experienced in social groups (BABO-LANÇA, 2011).

From my point of view, treating the coverage of the Covid-19 pandemic as a journalistic

event is fundamental to understanding what role this professional practice

plays in extreme situations whose unfolding introduces new interpretive possibilities

that dialogue with the present, past, and future (QUÉRÉ, 2005). Journalism is sometimes

accused of having a short memory and of not conducting systematic reviews and coverage

of the same topic, concerning itself only and essentially with the

small events that arise each day. Covid-19 coverage tends to be,

therefore, surrounded by lessons and learnings across the world. A pandemic is a precise

example of an occurrence that overflows both spatially and temporally, as Quéré notes.

The event gains social meaning above all by dialoguing with other events

(BABO-LANÇA, 2011). Furthermore, the pandemic evokes a case that brings journalistic

and scientific facts into dialogue (ARQUEMBOURG, 2011). By describing scientific studies —

which derive from observation, description, measurement, and comparison related to an

object — journalists organize such information into a narrative plan with the description

of facts. When Arquembourg (2011) studied the earthquake that struck and devastated Lisbon in 1755,

she observed that the newspapers and gazettes of the time conducted a continuous

coverage of the event, making connections between journalism and science

to communicate the facts to a lay and an erudite public.

To understand and explain to the public the objects of their investigation, journalists who

covered Covid-19 were compelled to approach the specialized coverage that

science and public health demand, learning to read and understand scientific studies,

as well as to interpret data on virus infections and deaths. In Data-Driven Journalism,

a collaborative process in the collection and provision of numbers was observed, which in coverage

were transformed into infographics and maps, re
resources that help unravel the facts.

However, even anchored in a verifiable truth and under the impetus to inform in a qualified manner (REGINATO, 2019), journalism can do a disservice to the population when it reproduces discourses uncritically or prioritizes commercial aspects over qualified information. One year into the pandemic and breaking records of deaths, a group calling itself _Médicos pela Vida – Covid-19_ published in _Folha de S.Paulo_ and in other newspapers across the country, on February 23, 2021, a half-page advertisement defending the supposed early treatment promoted by the president, using drugs such as chloroquine, ivermectin, zinc, and vitamin D. Despite having published numerous journalistic texts debunking the existence of early treatment, _Folha_ agreed to run the advertisement. As a kind of mea culpa, it also published a report [34] debunking it, informing that the supposed early treatment is not supported by science. The publication of the advertisement [35] was also the subject of _Folha_'s _Ombudsman_ column, at the time written by Flavia Lima, who criticized the newspaper's dual stance.

In circumstances such as this, where public health is at stake and the way it is portrayed by the press must be considered, what Henn (2013, p. 44) says about the cyber-event applies: "Journalism falters in its traditional modes and in the business ties that underpin much of its framing." Although it might seem like a simple decision at first — separating journalistic content from the newspaper's advertising — journalism struggles to encompass the complexity required by a narrative like Covid-19.

A study by Soares and Recuero (2021, n.p.) shows that professional journalism also contributed to some extent to the construction of disinformation narratives during the pandemic. By using declaratory headlines, journalistic productions were employed to corroborate misleading content on Facebook. This occurred especially through the association of journalistic URLs with low-quality titles or ones with accuracy problems. "Consequently, journalists ought to take extra care when producing news, especially headlines, which will be the most visible part of the stories on social media." [36]

Journalists also need to pay attention to false equivalences and to the type of sources they use. Just as in the case of tobacco, explained in this chapter, there are some circumstances where there are not two sides to be considered — think, for example, about global warming, in which climate change deniers are routinely interviewed to give voice "to the other side." Green and Donahue (2018) draw attention to the difficulty of correcting information that has already spread, including when the errors come from scientific studies themselves — when a mistaken approach is disseminated, the correction ends up being ineffective because it often does not reach the same proportions as the first reported fact.

Although editorial decisions typically do not even pass through reporting teams, journalists seem to be somewhat aware of the challenges that the era of information — but also of disinformation — poses to the profession. In a study of 405 journalists in the United States, Vu and Saldaña (2021) concluded that, since 2016, the time of President Donald Trump's election, reporters have been more inclined to adopt transparency and accountability strategies in their reporting to help combat disinformation, such as cross-checking sources, using recent technologies in fact-checking, and limiting the use of anonymous sources. Journalists who believe that disinformation is a threat to democracy tend to open their processes more, betting on methodological transparency. As we will see in the following chapters, Data-Driven Journalism, situated within the scope of digital journalism, is one such form.

**3 DIGITAL AND NETWORKED: THE PLACE OF DATA-DRIVEN JOURNALISM**

Data-Driven Journalism, which will be studied throughout this thesis and defined in this chapter, is a body of knowledge and practices applied to reporting and inspired by the social sciences, grounded in truth, objectivity, and transparency by offering a contextual and enduring approach in its productions. Because of this reference, it stands out for its proximity to the scientific method, which is made viable through a series of procedures beginning with hypothesis testing, moving through data search and analysis, and culminating in methodological openness through transparency. The epistemological foundations of DDJ were developed mainly in the United States, from the second half of the 1960s, with Precision Journalism and Computer-Assisted Reporting (CAR).

In this section, I seek to situate this form of journalism in the modern context of the network society (CASTELLS, 2016) and networked journalism (HEINRICH, 2011), within which digital journalism also develops. I understand Data-Driven Journalism as part of digital journalism, being constantly affected by decentralized flows and interactions that occur through networks. Such networks are mediated by platforms (BELL; OWEN, 2017) and characterize a scenario of journalism in transformation (DEUZE; WITSCHGE, 2020) and post-industrial journalism (ANDERSON; BELL; SHIRKY, 2013).

Networked journalism names the phenomenon that identifies significant changes in the search for and dissemination of information, showing that there are new patterns in the journalistic production system that are no longer embedded in a fixed physical context, but in flows organized through the internet. Heinrich (2011) understands that these transformations are grounded mainly in two processes: globalization — understood as the increase in connections in a network — and technological advances through digitization. The horizontality that replaces verticality in content production and distribution flows generates what Barbosa (2013, p. 33) calls a "dynamic multimedia continuum" for journalistic products made available on the Web. In this way, new arrangements take shape, and professional challenges include learning to incorporate user-generated content into journalistic practice, or measuring the influence of platforms in the process of producing and distributing news (BELL; OWEN, 2017).

At this point, it is worth noting that the development of digital journalism is neither stagnant nor uniform, and therefore does not occur in a single, homogeneous way around the globe. Countries of the Global North, whose societies generally have faster access to technology than other communities, tend to have access to a wide range of technologies and possibilities. Other countries, in turn, adapt and reinterpret the initial guidelines based on their own tools and possibilities. Aware of this scenario, I bring, in this chapter, aspects of the development of digital journalism as a theoretical field and briefly present its development in Brazil before moving on to Data-Driven Journalism. Furthermore, it is necessary to consider that the informational and networked context generates transformations prior to journalistic practice itself. It is related, for example, to the working modes of professionals — the newsroom is no longer the only viable configuration (DEUZE; WITSCHGE, 2020) — since decentralized work arrangements — such as networked investigations — are also established.

Deuze and Witschge (2020) argue that, as it is a process, journalism is in constant transformation. For the authors, current practices in the field carry three main markers: the way digital technology affects the field, including the role of platforms, aggregators, and distributors beyond the reference press; the integration of editorial and marketing areas, bringing new reflections to the professional field and to the very definition of the journalist's role; and the adherence of professionals not only to newsrooms but their insertion in commercial and non-commercial entities. Similarly, Waisbord (2019) identifies changes in the functioning and arrangement of journalistic organizations. Although the reference press remains relevant, there are other actors in this network society, in multidirectional communication flows and amid the abundance of information in digital media, altering the routines and perceptions of the audience (BOCZKOWSKI, 2021), which is naturally exhausted by the excess of information.

In this thesis, I draw on studies that situate journalism as a social institution (FRANCISCATO, 2005; PICKARD, 2020), an agent in the social construction of reality (TRAQUINA, 2005; TUCHMAN, 1978), an expert system (MIGUEL, 1999) and a form or forms of knowledge, which was initially addressed in a seminal text by Park (1940) and subsequently discussed by authors such as Genro Filho (2012 [1987]), Meditsch (1992; 1997), Lisboa and Benetti (2015) and, more recently, updated by Nielsen (2017). The pursuit of knowledge generation is made viable by the practice of informing in a qualified manner (REGINATO, 2019) based on verifiable truth that takes facts as its starting point (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014). Naturally, technological development cuts across journalism and, therefore, its impact on journalistic practices is undeniable. Even so, technology does not replace journalism, which is the transformative agent. I agree with Zelizer (2019, p. 343) when she says that "[...] it is journalism that gives technology purpose, shape, perspective, meaning and significance, not the other way around." [37]

To name the scenario of journalistic production in which the Web is the main medium of production and distribution, I use the term _digital journalism_ and align with authors such as Waisbord (2019, p. 352), for whom the digital refers "[...] to the rise and consolidation of networked social action, facilitated by technological innovations" [38]. Journalism and news, in this case, are associated with institutional and structural transformations, made viable mainly through the internet and its information and technology attributes. Combining the two terms, the definition of digital journalism means "[...] the networked production, distribution, and consumption of news and information about public affairs" [39] (WAISBORD, 2019, p. 352). In Portuguese, nomenclatures such as ciberjornalismo and webjornalismo are also used, and have their own specificities (MIELNICZUK, 2003). Since this is not the main discussion of the thesis, I understand there is no disadvantage in using the term digital journalism as a conceptual framework to situate Data-Driven Journalism.

Waisbord (2019) understands that journalism, even in its industrial mold, was already characterized by a type of network — but the connection, in this case, was formed by journalism and other actors, such as governments, corporations, advertisers, public relations professionals, and others; and all of this influenced characteristics such as content, funding forms, style, format, and editorial positions. In digital journalism, these networks become more complex and open, promoting the relationship of multiple interests and different ways of doing reporting.

**3.1 The potential for generating specific knowledge**

Journalism [42] is an institution structured on the principle of truth that has specific purposes to fulfill in democratic societies, such as scrutinizing public power and circulating accurate, qualified, and relevant information (REGINATO, 2019). The news, the most basic form of informative journalism, has the potential to generate knowledge as it presents verified facts and presupposes the contextualization of phenomena.

In an attempt to advance along a theoretical path trodden by various researchers, I seek to situate Data-Driven Journalism as a **set of practices and knowledge whose procedures allow it to approach scientific knowledge through method**. To do so, I draw on earlier reflections (GEHRKE, 2016a; TRÄSEL; GEHRKE, 2021) and on studies by contemporary researchers (NIELSEN, 2017; WIHBEY, 2019) who update the thinking of seminal authors such as Robert Park. Thus, I understand that DDJ presents three pillars that allow it to approach systematic knowledge: **truth**, **objectivity**, and **transparency**, characteristics explored in this thesis.

In situating Data-Driven Journalism, I do not intend to minimize other journalistic practices or forms of knowledge; I merely seek to contribute by pointing out its specificities. DDJ is usually perceived as an essentially practical field. However, when its origins are examined, it reveals itself as a form grounded in methodological objectivity, approaching what science prescribes, as we will see below. It is a place where there is explanatory and enduring content, different from other digital forms existing today (NIELSEN, 2017).

Why, after all, is it important to situate knowledge generation in close proximity to DDJ? For Wihbey (2019), only from this condition can journalism adopt better practices. By establishing knowledge-based processes, journalists avoid, for example, low-quality raw material in content production (such as the use of simple declarations and press release copies), since they consider their content a qualified composition that can guide the public in decision-making. Sponholz (2009), in turn, identifies journalism as a form of knowledge because through it one can know reality and process what is experienced in society.

In general terms, knowledge can be understood as the result of systematic information to be scientifically validated, independent of the judgment of individuals or social groups (WIHBEY, 2019). It is also known that most scientific advances occur through a slow accumulation of knowledge (O'CONNOR; WEATHERALL, 2019). In the context of journalism, it concerns the elaboration of news, which reflects representative patterns of the world. On this, it is possible to think of at least three processes in the news production chain: the **journalist's knowledge** (DONSBACH, 2014; GUERRA, 2008; KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014; PATTERSON, 2013); **shared social knowledge** (BERGER; LUCKMANN, 2014 [1966]; TRAQUINA, 2005; TUCHMAN, 1978), which dialogues with content production and shared social values; and **knowledge to be produced** (LISBOA; BENETTI, 2015; GENRO FILHO, 2012 [1987]; MEDITSCH, 1992; 1997; PARK, 1940) by journalism.

For authors such as Donsbach (2014), Guerra (2008), Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) and Patterson (2013), the **journalist's knowledge** serves as a starting point for how events are apprehended and communicated to the audience. Donsbach (2014, p. 666) describes the journalist as an individual belonging to a knowledge profession, since he performs his functions (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014; REGINATO, 2019) in a society based on the most diverse types of knowledge, indicating how things work and sharing socially expected parameters of behavior in society. By collecting and translating information to the community, the professional is able to generate knowledge. For this to actually occur, the journalist must, according to the author: know historical and current affairs issues, as well as exercise analytical thinking; be an expert in coverage topics; have scientific knowledge about communication processes; possess journalistic skills; and practice the profession in accordance with ethical norms.

For Donsbach, the journalist, with the aim of identifying the singularity of events and the elements that stand out in each newsworthy fact, must present intellectual characteristics that allow seeing the phenomenon as a whole. In the course of reporting, professionals must apply analytical knowledge to test the validity of information from sources, for example. The role of the journalist, for Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014), lies in generating meaning — that is, placing events in context and thereby transforming information into knowledge.

One can say, then, that the journalist's knowledge is fundamental because it is the journalist who carries out the process of interpreting events. To do this, journalists are guided by "cognitive techniques," that is, their knowledge base, to investigate and analyze a fact within an "interpretive path" (GUERRA, 2008, p. 189). This interpretation presupposes a retrieval of previously activated concepts that are socially shared, as we will see below. Guerra (2008) structures the interpretive path into four stages: the question, the framing, the selection, and the systematization.

However, to achieve the generation of knowledge, the journalistic narrative cannot be merely an interpretation. Although every fact is mediated by one, it must have an explanatory character (LISBOA; BENETTI, 2015), with a clear and thorough narrative (GANDOUR, 2020). In addition to critical thinking, Data-Driven Journalism activates other types of skills — these competencies sometimes depend on the establishment of a multidisciplinary team.

Wihbey (2019) lists six professional competencies for the journalism/knowledge relationship to be effective, all of which are strongly associated with DDJ: 1) Understanding basic statistics and possessing quantitative skills, including how a study is developed, sampling issues, etc.; 2) Understanding how new hypotheses are tested; 3) Having the ability to manipulate data in tabular forms; 4) Mapping discourses and being able to establish the state of knowledge on a given subject; 5) Being fluent with public records, understanding procedures related to legislation; 6) Knowing Web techniques to acquire and present information.

Under the perspective of knowledge-based journalism, Patterson (2013) understands that the explanation of facts should be the protagonist in journalistic texts. The author establishes two main forms for this premise to be made viable: content knowledge, linked to a specific topic and requiring a degree of specialization among professionals who cover an area; and process knowledge, related to the application and communication of reporting methods — in other words, **methodological transparency**.

For the journalist's knowledge to be useful to society, its members must share common values. This leads us to the second topic of journalism as a form of knowledge: **shared social knowledge**. The reality portrayed in news represents facts already apprehended and objectified through language (BERGER; LUCKMANN, 2014 [1966]) and based on the processes of production, selection, and transformation of events (TRAQUINA, 2005). Journalism deals with a world of socially shared meanings, even though each individual lives and interacts in their own surroundings.

To produce social meaning, journalism makes use of typifications, that is, the attribution of characteristics to specific individuals or groups. Such typifications are activated in the news and contribute to the production of meaning, since individuals share the same social stock of knowledge. "Social structure is the sum total of these typifications and of the recurrent patterns of interaction established by means of them. As such, social structure is an essential element of the reality of everyday life" (BERGER; LUCKMANN, 2014, p. 51). Along the same lines, Tuchman (1978, p. 2) admits that journalism is not the only means capable of promoting everyday understanding of the world, but shows itself to be fundamental in helping contextualize phenomena. "By seeking to disseminate information that people want, need, and should know, news organizations both circulate and shape knowledge." [43]

At the same time as the socially shared repertoire is essential for the operationalization of news and the understanding of meanings, it opens a precedent for the reproduction of stereotypes and commonplace. It is necessary to consider, therefore, that news must be observed as a social construction that reflects aspects of the reality of an event, and includes the organizational constraints of where they are produced, the journalists' narratives, their work routines, news values, and the information sources consulted in news production (TRAQUINA, 2005). Added to this are the various forms in which mediated communication (COULDRY; HEPP, 2017) currently presents itself, that is, across platforms and devices.

Under the axis of shared social knowledge, journalism can also be thought of as a social institution or public good. Drawing on economic theories, Pickard (2020, p. 64) proposes that journalism be understood not as a commodity for purchase and sale, but as a public good that society needs. "Thus, journalism's value to society transcends the revenue that it generates." [44] In this case, the author argues that it generates value not only for those who directly consume its products, but for the entire population, which remains informed.

Many public goods — clean air, open space, artificial light, and knowledge, to name a few — produce tremendous positive externalities [...]. Society requires these goods, but individuals typically undervalue them because they are unable or unwilling to pay for their full costs, which leads to the market under-producing them (PICKARD, 2020, p. 64) [45].

By treating journalism and news production as a public good, the author elevates the importance of news and places it on another level, recognizing that it plays an essential role. The main issue, from this point of view, seems to be the monetization and inclusion of journalism in limited or unconventional spaces. In a democratic society, the author considers that universal access to journalistic content should be a priority, but admits that the market generally fails to support the adequate infrastructure for news consumption. One should therefore think about a public media system that ensures the insertion of journalism in local and regional communities.

The implementation of Pickard's idea would depend on a joint effort among organizations, since journalism is also categorized as a social institution [46]. Tuchman (1978) presents three reasons for this classification: 1) news constitutes an institutional method of making information available to consumers; 2) journalism is allied with legitimized institutions — in general, politicians and other figures can access the press as sources; 3) journalism is produced by professionals who work in organizations and therefore have institutional practices.

Up to this point, we understand that considering journalism a form of knowledge implies admitting that the journalist possesses individual knowledge that will be used in professional practice, and we also recognize that there is a shared social knowledge that serves as the starting point for news. It also implies accepting that journalism is a social institution bounded by time and identified as credible and with sufficient authority to, while depending on a series of processes, orient life in society.

In this regard, it is worth mentioning the perception of Miguel (1999), who classifies journalism as an expert system. That is, a system that presents specialized competence and receives the trust of the public. Let us take some examples: one travels by airplane because there is trust in a (often quite abstract) safe air travel system, in which accidents are rare; one goes to the doctor because the assumption is that, having studied for it, the professional has the knowledge to diagnose the problem. For journalism to be perceived as an expert system, the audience must trust the journalists' choices regarding the veracity of information, the selection of elements pertaining to the fact, and the selection and hierarchy of relevant news; moreover, it reverberates the knowledge produced by other social institutions.

Starting from the premise that journalism brings together qualified professionals capable of living up to the production of knowledge, who carry out their practice in a shared scenario of knowledge and values, it is necessary to understand how, ultimately, **knowledge to be produced** functions. Lisboa and Benetti (2015, p. 16) help us understand, in short, how this happens: "Journalism becomes knowledge when it constructs itself as a verisimilar testimony of reality, basing its news production on methods and processes that attempt to reduce error and false accounts."

One of the pioneering works in journalism studies specifically to problematize the type of knowledge generated by this social practice comes from sociologist Robert Park (1940). In a widely cited study in the field, he establishes a _continuum_ between formal and systematic _knowledge about_ and _acquaintance with_, linked to common sense. For the author, news generates conversation in society, has the character of a public document, and is capable of generating immediate changes.

The _acquaintance with_, stated by Park (1940, p. 674), characterizes an intuitive knowledge, the product of personal experience and experimentation. It is something ephemeral, with which we have contact, but do not delve into. _Knowledge about_, in turn, is formal, rational, and systematic — it presupposes confirmed and verified facts. Unlike _acquaintance with_, which exists in the order of perception, _knowledge about_ can be tested, experimented, and communicated, as occurs in science. "Knowledge about is not merely accumulated experience but the result of systematic investigation of nature." [47]

Park's categorization still captures the essential aspects of journalism and the circulation of information today, but does not encompass all types of content currently published. Thinking of a digital news scenario, Nielsen (2017) updates Park's theories and creates three ideal types to encompass types of digital content. What the author calls _news-as-impressions_ corresponds to the fragments of information circulating in news alerts, social network sites, and messaging apps. It corresponds to what Park (1940) would classify as _acquaintance with_. The second ideal type is what Nielsen calls _news-as-items_, which are newspaper, website, or radio and television articles with the idea of currency and referring to the last 24 hours. These are the traditional news forms of the late 20th century, with classic journalistic characteristics. Finally, the author presents _news-about-relations_ — the equivalent of _knowledge about_ — with explanatory and contextual content, such as longform reporting and Data-Driven Journalism.

The three categories created by Nielsen encompass perceptions of ephemeral content — the idea of flow, of fast information circulating through the feed — and of stock, that is, durable content that can be consulted regardless of the date. The fast forms of news, which circulate in flow, integrate the news ecosystem of which Wihbey (2019) speaks. In a decentralized network scenario, the emergence of social facts [48] is recurrent and sometimes replaces the true facts that give rise to and sustain the news. At the time Park published the inaugural text on news as a form of knowledge, in the first half of the 20th century, the media landscape was very different: most journalistic content was distributed through newspapers and radio stations, and journalism was essentially regional. Today, reference newspapers, television channels, and online news distribution stand out, in a market with national and international reach (NIELSEN, 2017).

There is a long trajectory of argumentation about the knowledge generated by journalism. While some theorists ignore the existence of the knowledge produced, other researchers place it in a distinct space, such as Robert Park (1940) himself, Genro Filho (2012 [1987]), Meditsch (1992; 1997), and Franciscato (2006). For authors such as Meditsch (1992; 1997) and Franciscato (2006), journalism presents some methodological fragility and, therefore, some distance from science — because, when working with story pitches and not necessarily with hypotheses, it develops its themes from uncontrolled observations. As we will see ahead, Data-Driven Journalism seeks to address this aspect.

DDJ, I believe, has the potential to produce knowledge from durable and explanatory content, or stock content, as Nielsen (2017) states. In summary, I have argued, up to this point, that journalism as a whole can be characterized as a form capable of generating specific social knowledge, and that this is structured around three main factors: the **journalist's knowledge**, **shared social knowledge**, and **knowledge to be produced**. For this, its practices must be anchored in **truth**, **objectivity**, and **transparency**. Just as in science, this allows for methodological reproducibility and peer review. In this regard, DDJ is strongly influenced by one of its precursors, Precision Journalism, created by journalist Philip Meyer.

**3.2 The scientific basis of Precision Journalism**

The term Data-Driven Journalism began to be adopted in the first decade of the 2000s and has as its landmark the text written by American programmer Adrian
Holovaty (2006) about the need to structure and reuse information collected in the day-to-day work of reporting. This text is complemented by the expansion of public transparency policies around the world and the consequent increase in the availability of open data, which prepared the ground for the expansion of DDJ.

The development of DDJ and similar practices, however, did not occur uniformly at a global level, since the social, cultural, and technological landscape varies from region to region. Its origins trace mainly to the United States, where the emergence of journalistic forms that would come to give rise to it was recorded: Precision Journalism, created by reporter Philip Meyer in the second half of the 1960s, with a book of the same name published for the first time in 1973; and Computer-Assisted Reporting (CAR), considered by researchers to be an updated version of its precursor, but with a strong technological bias. In a recent scenario, one of the main landmarks of Data-Driven Journalism is _The Guardian Data Blog_ [49], founded in 2009 and led by Simon Rogers.

In this thesis, the objective is not to recapitulate in depth the first uses of data in the history of modern journalism, which go back to the 19th century and are linked to the publication of tables and infographics (ROGERS, 2013). For now, considering the subject of this work, I briefly recover the closeness between Data-Driven Journalism and how this practice allowed journalism to have more knowledge about the epidemics that have affected societies throughout history.

One of the starting points for the use of data for changes in public sanitation policies, and an inspiration for DDJ, is the map [50] drawn by physician John Snow as a solution to the cholera epidemic that struck the metropolis of London in 1854. At the time, the prevailing theory was that the disease spread through the air. Through systematic observation and microscopic analysis, Snow was the one who developed the thesis that transmission occurred through the consumption of contaminated water. By locating the deaths on a map, he identified a pattern: the cholera deaths occurred in the vicinity of the Broad Street water pump. Armed with the city's statistical information, then systematized in the map, Snow identified that, among 83 deaths, 73 were of people who lived near the pump in question (JOHNSON, 2008). London had already experienced other crises of contamination by the bacterium that causes cholera. It was discovered that the 1854 epidemic was caused by contact of water with the feces of a sick infant: the water (contaminated, as discovered later) used to wash the diapers was poured into the house's sewage, which came into contact with the well's supply system and, consequently, contaminated the residents who consumed the water from the location. At that period, the English capital was experiencing its expansion as a metropolis, with about 2.5 million inhabitants, many of whom lived crammed into residences, contributing to the rapid spread of diseases at a time of precariousness in the water supply and sewage systems.

Returning to the present day, it is difficult not to draw a parallel with the Covid-19 pandemic: data are once again capable of establishing patterns in a quite abstract scenario of contamination by the Sars-Cov-2 virus. With increased mobility between countries, an epidemic gains pandemic proportions and spreads rapidly. To observe the contamination scenario and prepare alternatives, it is necessary to collect and analyze the data and put them in context. In 2021, the _No Epicentro_ [51] project (developed by _Agência Lupa_ and _Google News Lab_), which seeks to locate the number of Covid-19 deaths on an interactive map, was one of the winners at the international competition called _Sigma Awards_ [52].

Precision Journalism, established by Meyer (2002) as the application of scientific methods from social and behavioral research to journalistic practice, emerged as a scientific response and is considered one of the precursors of DDJ. He believed that only in this way would it be possible to reduce the risk of manipulation suffered by reporters in their dialogue with official sources. Meyer's proposal was not immediately well received or even fully understood, and his book took some years to be published (MEYER, 2012). In an interview with Gehrke and Mielniczuk (2017), Meyer said that his main learning in the process was understanding that scientific research was practicable in the field of journalism, going beyond merely observing and reporting a fact. Having spent a sabbatical year at Harvard, where he learned about social science methods and became acquainted with statistics, Meyer opened his eyes to the universe and power of data analysis. Thus, he believes that his main contribution was demonstrating the value of the scientific method for the search for facts and the analysis of data in journalism.

The end of Meyer's study period at Harvard, where he had contact with computers, coincided with a series of social disturbances that occurred in Detroit, United States, in 1967. The first action that led to the social collapse in the city was a police raid on a clandestine bar on July 23. The confrontation that took place there spread, and five days later there were 43 dead, 7,000 detained, and blocks destroyed (TRÄSEL, 2014). The initial suspicion was that the collapse had been caused by members of socially excluded strata. Assigned to work with the _Detroit Free Press_ team to cover the events, Meyer proposed a survey in the affected area with the aim of discovering the motivations for the confrontation with the police and the subsequent actions, such as the destruction of the city. Based on hypothesis testing, as is customary in science, the team discovered that the riot had been caused mainly by police brutality, poor housing conditions, and lack of employment (TRÄSEL, 2014). Contrary to expectations, immigrants were not the main actors responsible for the actions in Detroit; the majority were residents of the city itself. Meyer and his colleagues' research also allowed for a generalization: contrary to initial thinking, Black people who participated in the protests were a minority, and their actions did not represent the sentiment of the community as a whole. The team then confirmed the theory of relative deprivation: the social collapse had been caused by frustration. The closer one got to a goal, the greater the frustration at not achieving it. It was as if a portion of the population had been abandoned and could not advance socially — and this was the key reason for the disturbances.

Because of these movements, Meyer was able to put into practice the lessons learned at Harvard and, finally, to test his own theory by applying social science methods to his reporting work. In 1968, the publication of a research report developed by the _Detroit Free Press_ team won in the local reporting category of the _Pulitzer Prize_, the most important journalism prize in the world. Meyer became known at the time as a "computer reporter," since the application of social science methods seemed to be a difficult premise to grasp.

In practical terms, the reporter who adopted Precision Journalism methods would not wait, for example, for the results of a voting intention poll — he would go out into the streets and conduct the research himself. Despite Precision Journalism being frequently associated with the combination of opinion polls and reporting practice (MEYER, 1999), its creator clarifies that this professional model envisioned various applicable forms of scientific method, including other types of field experiments, developed not only by him as a reporter but also by his undergraduate students at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Meyer, as a university professor, recounts having sent students to measure the politeness of drivers, the racial prejudice of pedestrians, and the sale of alcohol to minors.

Delineating the scope to which each term linked to the origins of Data-Driven Journalism belongs is not exactly a matter of consensus. Some researchers consider CAR a revamped version of Precision Journalism from the time computers began to be used more frequently in newsrooms, which in the United States happened in the mid-1980s — before that, in the early 1950s, the computer had been used sporadically in calculations and predictive models. While LaFleur (1999, p. 27) defines CAR as an initiative aimed at "[...] analyzing data to do stories that reporters couldn't do any other way" [53], Meyer states that in Precision Journalism the use of the computer is not essential. On this point, he criticizes the use of the word "computer" in the formation of the term CAR, advocating the adoption of a new name with an emphasis on science, and admitting that Precision Journalism was dated.

Whatever the new term, let's give up on CAR. Computers are used in so many different ways, even in the newsroom, that it no longer defines us, if ever did. The time has come to declare CAR victorious and move on to a fresher, more ambitious concept (MEYER, 1999, p. 5) [54].

Data-Driven Journalism seems to have taken on the conceptual role of which Meyer (1999) speaks, since the term has become widely diffused today. Around the world, it is common to see its association with computational journalism [55] — Precision Journalism, CAR, data journalism, and computational journalism integrate what Coddington calls the quantitative turn in journalism. In general, journalism uses specific names to mark a given time or to meet a specific demand. To give an idea, the _X Journalism_ project, developed by Loosen _et al._ (2019), mapped 130 terms used to designate types of journalism.

For Anderson (2018), Precision Journalism concretizes the interpretive and explanatory turn that journalism was experiencing since the 1930s in the United States. At the time, just when journalism sought to explain the reasons behind events, thinking of a contextualization process, it was the target of criticism from scientists, who considered it distant from science because it was primarily guided by public interest issues [56] rather than formulating abstract models of social life. It can be inferred, therefore, that the origins of DDJ have as their backdrop a contextual journalism, also known as explanatory or interpretive [57].

Like Precision Journalism and CAR, Data-Driven Journalism has technological, political, and cultural dimensions. Relevant to its own era of operation, each type places a certain emphasis on how to obtain data. **3.3 Characteristics of Data-Driven Journalism today**

Data-Driven Journalism is defined by Träsel (2014, p. 119) as "[...] the application of computing and social science knowledge in the collection, processing, interpretation, and presentation of data, with the aim of expanding journalism's function as a defender of the public interest." Commonly associated with the pragmatism of reporting, I reiterate the definition that it is a **set of practices and knowledge whose origins are associated with scientific knowledge, through truth and methodological objectivity, capable of offering high degrees of transparency and collaboration, producing enduring content**. With due consideration, of course, for the specificities of the social contexts in which it is employed.

It is also a set of practices situated in the context of digital and networked journalism and investigative journalism (HAHN; STALPH, 2019), but with a markedly collaborative bias from the beginning of the process, in opposition to the stereotype of the lone wolf of investigative journalism. First, because a journalist will rarely have all the necessary skills to develop, from start to finish, a data-driven story (BRADSHAW, 2013); at the same time, there is a proximity between the ethos of the investigative and data journalist in identifying systemic problems or patterns and commonly the act of shedding light on hidden information; second, in a scenario of internet connectivity beyond borders, it is possible to expand local investigations to other parts of the planet, enabling networked investigations adopted by journalists and newsrooms.

Data-Driven Journalism became a relatively stable field at the beginning of the 21st century, according to Anderson (2018). In this scenario, there is an abundance of open data in new digital domains. It is as if Meyer's dream and that of so many other scholars had finally come true: journalists use databases and computers to communicate social context to their audiences, occasionally using social science techniques to do so. "Journalism can now deploy professional certainty and mobilize the public for democratic purposes by drawing on the evidentiary standards of social science [59]" (ANDERSON, 2018, p. 137). When assessing the current state of Data-Driven Journalism, Meyer reported, in an interview with Gehrke and Mielniczuk (2017), that the concept still seems to be in development. For him, the scope of DDJ may include data analysis and reporting, but also a kind of data curation — that is, offering a user-friendly interface to audiences with little familiarity with data and spreadsheets.

The community of data journalists has been gaining importance in the world. Ironically, it is a challenge to obtain numbers on how many practitioners there are. Even so, the search for certain types of courses gives us clues about how the practice has been spreading and generating global interest. Offered in 2019 by the _Knight Center for Journalism in the Americas_, the free course on Data Journalism and Visualization with Free Tools had enrollments in several countries, as shown in a released map [60]. The largest portion of participants is in Brazil (2,297 enrolled), followed by the United States (1,756) and Mexico (775). Although this center prioritizes training professionals in the Americas, there were also participants registered from countries such as Spain (684 participants), India (284), and the United Kingdom (237).

Gradually, researchers from Latin America are gaining space to describe specific situations of DDJ development in their countries, since access to practices from the Global North is more common. In a hyperlocal approach to Data-Driven Journalism — newsrooms in Brazilian favelas — De Lima Santos and Mesquita (2021) recognize particular characteristics of this type of practice in the Brazilian context, including the difficulties in news production and the audience's participation in that process. It is estimated that, in the country, DDJ is practiced in 52 institutions (DE LIMA SANTOS, 2019) — even though the exact number of practitioners is not known —, especially in the Southeast, which accounts for close to 70% of the press organizations in the survey.

Still in an empirical and not very systematic way, I perceived, over nearly six years of periodic observation of products launched by DDJ teams, that the practice is very people-centered — that is, centered on professionals with high technical knowledge — and not necessarily on the identity of newsrooms. In general, it is these technical and knowledge-related issues that represent a barrier to the broader diffusion of DDJ. **3.3.1 Ways of working: method and workflows**

Data-Driven Journalism has as one of its differentiators its proximity to the scientific method. Therefore it bets, for example, on the use of hypotheses (HUNTER _et al._, 2013) in place of the conventional construction of a story pitch. This hypothesis will serve as the starting point for the investigation, which has its own method. In this section, I clarify some points of intersection between journalism and science and present examples of workflows used as a method for DDJ.

In science, in general terms, the method is understood as a "[...] set of basic rules for developing an investigation aimed at producing new knowledge or correcting and integrating existing knowledge" (GIL, 2019b, p. 9). By making use of methodological procedures that can be described and tested by peers, the scientist — or journalist, in the case of DDJ — uses resources so that subjectivity does not compromise the step-by-step procedures adopted.

The journalistic method is different from the scientific one, although it also functions as a structured search for answers. And, just as in science — which departs from the accumulation of knowledge made viable through method — journalism acts as a discoverer of facts in constant unfolding. Since journalism is made viable through description and argumentation, it strives to show that the truth is not merely a subjective condition of its own, but works objectively and methodically to dispel the risks of deception or error (GOMES, 2009).

Following the discussions in this chapter, it is worth noting, once more, that the knowledge produced by science and journalism are distinct, even though both are social forms of knowledge (MEDITSCH, 1992). In practical terms, there are some markedly different characteristics between these social fields of knowledge production. The first, and perhaps most significant, is time: while the journalist works with tight deadlines and little reporting time, the scientist has the potential to test their hypotheses over a longer period. To these factors, Franciscato (2006) adds other significant points, including the fact that scientists and journalists belong to different communities, each with their own rules, norms, and values.

The author also points out some specificities of the journalistic method:

In journalism, we can refer to the existence of a method as a set of procedural rules for investigating a fact, based on values and conceptions about the role and forms of journalistic action in order to achieve a certain end (producing news) about facts and opinions considered to be of public interest or for entertainment. Therefore, a journalistic reporting method means a set of practices (skills and techniques) carried out by journalists, such as the observation of unfolding events, interviews with information sources, reading of documents and other data of a technical nature, etc. (FRANCISCATO, 2006, p. 5)

Furthermore, the journalist's work occurs within an organizational structure, and is therefore subject to framing. Another characteristic concerns the framing of the object: the journalist looks at the social fact without being an expert in the topic in which they are immersed; the scientist, in turn, works with the accumulation of knowledge through a small contribution to a very specific area.

Today, with the ease of access to public data and programming languages, Data-Driven Journalism seeks to consolidate itself as a branch of journalism that proposes to collect information that can be contextualized through hypothesis testing, the application of an objective method, and the presentation of results — in this case, the open methodology or source code, as we will see in the chapter on transparency elements — as a way of approaching what is done in science, through peer review, as observed by Träsel (2014).

Researchers and practitioners of Data-Driven Journalism present different perspectives on what they consider the ideal set of procedures. In general terms, outlets and authors agree on a basic structure: everything starts with a statement or question that needs to be tested based on publicly available data on a topic of collective interest (BRADSHAW, 2013; BELISÁRIO _et al._, 2020). The scientific roots of Data-Driven Journalism push it in the direction of investigation based on hypotheses — the first step is to establish a guiding question and then test it with open sources, understanding whether there is a possibility of exploring a given topic. In previous works (GEHRKE; FREITAS, 2021), I argued that the use of hypotheses also helps in the search for the context of information, allowing for deeper exploration of a topic.

In the following pages, I present some of the main methodological proposals used in the field and, in the final chapter of the thesis, I elaborate a proposal for an approach to bring the DDJ method closer to science, focusing on transparency and reproducibility. I emphasize that the systematization of information presented in this section is based on the digital journalism model and, therefore, some stages cannot be fully carried out in other media, such as radio and television — although much of the reporting process is repeated regardless of the final publication platform.

Proposals for data workflows have been established since Precision Journalism. Meyer (2002), for example, suggests the following stages for journalism to approach science: _collection_ — data gathering through scientific method; _storage_ — comprising the organization and forms of data storage; _retrieval_ — presupposes consulting data made available by third parties and offering ways of consultation (usability and personalization); _analysis_ — combining statistical tests and other ways of investigating patterns in relation to a topic; _reduction_ — selection of what is maintained in the analysis and what will not be addressed; _communication_ — data presentation, which in DDJ usually occurs through the use of charts, also called visualizations [63].

The Escola de Dados (School of Data), one of the main organizations that trains journalists in Brazil and worldwide, developed its own methodology — the so-called _Data Pipeline_ (BELISÁRIO _et al._, 2020) — to organize the stages that form a method: _ask, find, obtain, verify, clean, analyze_, and _present_. The main difference in relation to Meyer's proposal concerns the inclusion of a question as the starting point for obtaining data related to the topic to be addressed. In line with this logic, a model previously discussed by Professor Paul Bradshaw (2013) also emphasizes the existence of a guiding question for the investigation.

**4 TRANSPARENCY AS THE OPENING OF METHOD AND EDITORIAL DECISIONS**

Data-Driven Journalism is, above all, journalism. And for journalism to fulfill its fundamental principles — such as informing in a qualified manner and, from there, generating knowledge — it must commit to three guiding principles: **truth**, **objectivity**, and **transparency**. In this chapter, each of these elements will be discussed individually, although all are interconnected.

Truth — functional, provisional, and verifiable — is the foundation of everything; it is what guarantees journalism its grounding in facts and reality. Objectivity, in turn, concerns the method that journalism uses to access and report to the public the information of the real world. Transparency, then, is the opening and communication of conduct and procedures. At the organizational level, it presupposes the disclosure of funding sources and error-correction policies; at the operational level, it explains, for example, how a news piece was produced, why the topic matters, what sources were consulted, what the results of the analysis are, and the existence of any limitations.

Transparency, the central focus of this thesis, can be compared to the visible part of an _iceberg_: it is what the journalist or media outlet decides to make visible; it is what emerges from normally routine decisions, made within journalistic organizations and not open to the public. In a scenario of demands for institutional transparency and accountability, it is expected that journalism not only points to social problems, but knows how to communicate its own choices and processes.

**4.1 Truth**

The practice of journalism is grounded on the paradigm [67] of truth (BROERSMA, 2013). This is a functional truth (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014), provisional and non-philosophical. It is supported by a correspondence with reality, since the journalist reports — or should report — facts that truly occurred, bringing the journalistic account closer to the fact itself (LISBOA; BENETTI, 2015). To observe and tell the truth, the journalist occupies a position of exteriority in relation to the facts, observing them from outside and thus being able to provide a verified and credible account (CORNU, 1994). In their routine of description and argumentation related to the facts, journalism strives to show that truth is not merely a subjective condition, and therefore works objectively and methodically to distance itself from deception (GOMES, 2009).

Truth is the first obligation of journalists (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014) and the primary condition for qualified information (CORNU, 1994); its communication is linked to the idea of the field's authority — if something has been published in the news, then it is real, and the reader will be able to find true and verified information in this space. As a social mediator, journalism has the real fact as its object (GUERRA, 2008).

It is a foundational normative principle of journalism, which operates based on elements that specify and delimit its practice. For Franciscato (2005), journalism presupposes the existence of an idea of truth that can be apprehended in its main aspects, so as to become news through journalistic techniques. Fidelity in reporting occurrences is also seen as a fundamental aspect by the author, who advocates the publication of content that helps readers understand the present. Lisboa and Benetti (2015) state that truth is the first condition for journalism to become a justified true belief, which ultimately enables the reader's trust in the journalistic institution. If trust in institutions is associated with the need to take risks to some extent, transparency, as we will see in this chapter, is a way of minimizing that risk — or at least making the risk seem smaller (KARLSSON, 2020).

Truth as a starting point permeates journalistic procedures in the search for information; it is a principle that should guide the discipline of verification. "News that is not oriented toward truth — even if that truth is partial and provisional, and aware of being so — will not be information" (CORNU, 1994, p. 394). In general terms, the truth that journalism deals with can be understood as "[...] a statement of what is most probable in proportion to the evidence available at the time" [68] (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2011, p. 32). Thus, the unfolding of an event brings new evidence as time passes. Admitting that truth has a provisional character implies considering the existence of fragments about the knowledge of an object. "Truth presupposes a search, a revelation of hidden facts, a relationship between these facts, which allows one to highlight an image (because it will always be an image!) of reality" (CORNU, 1994, p. 75).

Factual truth is the basis for journalistic objectivity, as we will see below, being associated with an ethical dimension, according to Cornu (1994). In summary, what is reported is taken as truth by the public insofar as there is trust in this ethics and in the professional dimension of the reporter. "The pact of mediation demands from journalism obligations proportional to its importance. Truth, honesty, correction, loyalty, respect, balance, fairness, impartiality are all the values and principles that should guide a journalism ethics [...]" (GOMES, 2009, p. 87). Such honesty, says Cornu (1994), cannot be defined broadly and vaguely; the reader must trust what is being communicated because there is a work method to investigate information; and such method is presented when there is transparency in journalism.

Among the 12 journalistic purposes proposed by Reginato (2019), I understand that two have the greatest relationship with the pursuit of truth. They are: informing in a qualified manner, which corresponds to verified, relevant, contextualized, plural, and engaging information; and verifying the truthfulness of information, that is, investigating data and checking information to deliver it to the public precisely and in consonance with the truth of the facts. As Cornu (1994) says, information reflects aspects of reality, but is not the truth itself. Thus, there is a reality that can be observed, apprehended, and communicated.

The defense of publishing true facts is also associated with the professionalization of journalism to avoid increasing public criticism. At the beginning of the 20th century, according to Picardo (2020), there was concern about the imminent loss of revenue if there were a lack of trust in journalism. To avoid damage to credibility, values such as balance and objectivity were adopted. Thus, it was up to professionals to offer fact-based information. Before that, in Europe, the structuring of information with truth as a starting point helped the first newspapers to position themselves in the information market, differentiating themselves from other forms of communication (BROERSMA, 2017).

In 1939, at the time of World War II, _The New York Times_ explained to its readers why they could and should trust the war news the newspaper published, which included facts that extended beyond the territory of the United States. According to Kovach and Rosenstiel (2011), the outlet circulated numerous advertisements in its international coverage pages explaining how information from outside the country was received and edited. In this way, the audience would be familiar with journalistic practices and even with the idea of the truth of facts that permeated that coverage. In this manner, the newspaper offered, above all, information about the nature of evidence and how it had been evaluated, demonstrating its concern with truth and communicating its methods to the reader.

It is worth noting that journalistic truth is always an approximation, and every journalistic account will be mediated by an interpretation (LISBOA; BENETTI, 2015); subjectivity, thus, is the condition for objectivity. For the authors, there are some strategies so that the reader is able to identify elements that help measure the degree of fidelity between the fact itself and the account derived from it. Among the cited examples are elements such as photography, access to specialized sources, and the detailing of events. In citing the interpretive path taken in journalism during the knowledge and investigation of a fact, thinking about the cognitive techniques it demands, Guerra (2008) considers that truth and relevance are performance parameters for journalists and quality parameters for news construction. The interpretation and presentation of facts impact the effectiveness of journalistic discourse, which, according to Broersma (2013), lies in the capacity to describe and produce social phenomena simultaneously.

That said, it is necessary to recognize that the journalist acts as an interpreter of reality based on fragments of the facts. As Cornu (1994, p. 332-333) states:

[The journalist] Has no direct access to the core of this present reality. They cannot apprehend it in its deep truth, which escapes them. They must try to decipher it through the lived or reported fragments, minutes, speeches. The complexity, the contradictions, the incoherences, the gaps of current events — this presents itself as a tattered fabric — are evident.

This point raised by Cornu relates to what Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) say about journalistic truth, functional and practicable in day-to-day work. For it to be possible to put it into practice, two concepts are fundamental: accuracy, referring to the exactness of information, and verification, linked to the fact-checking process. Accuracy, according to the authors, is the defining point for other fundamental elements of news: context, interpretation, debate, and public communication. For them, the discipline of fact verification, made viable through methodological objectivity, is what differentiates journalism from other practices. Thus, it lies at the core of its knowledge production.

Journalism that has verification at its core aims to answer questions about the facts and place them in a context where they can be understood. In an interview with Gehrke and Mielniczuk (2017, p. 11), Philip Meyer stated that readers deserve not just the facts, but the truth of the facts: to guarantee the reliability of information, journalists need to use "belt and suspenders," making an analogy to the need for double-checking.

Through in-depth interviews with Canadian journalists, a study by Shapiro _et al._ (2013) shows that, for professionals, verification is a circular process that occurs throughout the entire process of news production; it would not then be a work stage or a markedly separate process, but inherent to the pursuit of the truth of the facts. In general terms, the authors found that official documents tend to be the target of greater trust among professionals. For this and other reasons, the process of information triangulation — which involves the comparison of independent sources — does not function the same way in journalism as in the social sciences. According to Shapiro _et al._ (2013), journalists usually verify details so as not to compromise the accuracy of the text (names, geographic references, etc.), but do not check broader claims or facts, unless it is a case of defamation.

Authors such as Broersma (2013) point out that the journalistic paradigm may be fractured by the arrival of new agents in the chain of information production and diffusion. In this context, journalism has its authority weakened, and the public must agree that the norms and values of this social practice still make sense in the reality that journalism comprehends and communicates. In any case, the audience seeks news from reliable sources because it assumes that truth is a value dear to journalism, and that there is an obligation of truthfulness. On this point, Gomes (2009, p. 11) emphasizes that "[...] only a verbal act that carries a claim to be true is news" and, in doing so, journalism assumes the commitment to seek the truth and find ways to avoid errors.

Understanding what is meant by journalistic truth seems fundamental in this scenario of disinformation and distrust in relation to facts reported by the press. Truth is the fundamental principle of journalism, and is necessarily linked to its raw material, since it corresponds to the foundation of informative journalistic products. Investigation, accuracy, and verification in relation to the facts is what allows journalistic interpretation and the approximation between the reality of the facts and the journalistic account. This is possible through journalistic objectivity, that is, via method.

**4.2 Objectivity**

Journalistic objectivity corresponds to the methodological procedures adopted in the practice of reporting. It encompasses the search for and collection of information on a topic, as well as the verification processes related to it. Journalistic verification and accuracy are associated with the trust that the audience places in journalism. As Cornu (1994, p. 391) says: "A bond that the journalist must defend so that it never breaks." Objectivity allows the journalist to access and apprehend verifiable truth, that is, to achieve the approximation with the real from the account; it is, according to Cornu (1994, p. 394), "[...] a method of orientation toward truth." Thus, objectivity is the means found by journalists to generate knowledge in society (CORNU, 1994).

Mistakenly, the concept of objectivity was rejected over the years by thinkers who spoke of the impossibility of practicing it due to human subjectivity. However, subjectivity is the condition for journalistic objectivity to be made viable, since every interpretation — a fundamental characteristic of mediation — contains subjective aspects. I align my argument with those of Cornu (1994), Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014), Sponholz (2009), and many other authors for whom objectivity lies in the method, not in the journalist.

According to Sponholz (2009), the method can be described as a series of planned and adopted actions to achieve a given objective. In the case of journalism, this objective is to know reality and mediate the information obtained through the investigation of the facts, which subsequently become a product. In other words, objectivity ensures that the discourse produced in journalism is faithful to the facts (GUERRA, 2008).

For Cornu (1994, p. 391), it is not enough for the journalist to define, broadly and vaguely, the honesty they plan to maintain with the reader in communicating the facts. The trust that truth is a fundamental part of the investigation lies in the rigor of the journalistic method — that is, in the objectivity of which we are speaking. "Rigor is going to the bottom of the materially achievable investigations, in the time given for this research. It is the collection of all confirmed available facts. It is everything that opposes falsification, distortion, and lying."

Among the journalistic procedures related to methodological objectivity are a series of decision-making processes, such as the choice of topic, framing, and the selection of sources. In this way, observation and the use of documents and interviews can be regarded as objects of evidence that journalism employs (ANDERSON, 2018). Journalistic objectivity is grounded in an ethical dimension that has verifiable truth as its basis. Thus, given the difficulties of apprehending the characteristics of an event, the journalist must value the correct description of the facts, publishing only information whose origin they know (CORNU, 1994).

Sponholz (2009) notes that news production also derives from processes arising from journalists' production routines, which includes aspects of the organization for which they work. In a classic text in which she identifies the perception of objectivity by professional journalists as a strategic ritual, Tuchman (1972) acknowledges that factors such as form — the way in which news elements are presented —, organizational relationships, and the content produced, that is, what results from the apprehension of social reality, all exert influence on journalistic objectivity. The strategic rituals the author speaks of in the text are routine procedures that journalists point to as providers of objectivity. In a recent study, McNair (2013) says he understands objectivity as an extension of strategic rituals sustained by the perception of truth and trust of readers in journalism.

The unity of method was already advocated by Lippmann (1920) in the early decades of the 20th century, in a scenario of professionalization of journalistic practice. For the author, good reporting work depended on an exercise in scientific virtues. Knowledge and access to truth were within the scope of freedom he judged to be essential. Thus:

There is but one kind of unity possible in a world as diverse as ours. It is the unity of method [...] the unity of the disciplined experiment. There is but one bond of peace that is both permanent and enriching: the increasing knowledge of the world in which experiment occurs. With a common intellectual method and a common area of valid fact, differences may become a form of cooperation and cease to be an irreconcilable antagonism (LIPPMANN, 1920, p. 67) [69]

According to Sponholz (2009, p. 153), what differentiates science and journalism from common sense is the use of professional rules for the apprehension and communication of reality. Both, in their own way, process and structure what is experienced using techniques that confer reliability to the process. "[...] the sources and the method used must be transparent so that other journalists or experts can repeat the same procedure under the same conditions. They must also arrive at the same result."

Under this perspective, journalists and scientists share an ideal of objectivity made viable through method. The purposes of use, however, are different. For scientists, as Galison (2015) notes, what matters is performing a mode of encoding shared knowledge to access objects in their field of research, while journalists seek a means of reaching most of their audience and their advertisers, formalizing the method in a very specific text format — the journalistic one — in which the element of novelty is prioritized and arranged in non-sensationalist text.

Anderson (2018) observes in his research that, since the beginning of the 20th century, journalism's response to moments of epistemological crisis has been a commitment to objectivity, embracing professional certainty in uncertain times. However, objectivity has not always been well regarded. While journalists use it as a response to criticism, the argument of those who do not believe in its effectiveness is that objectivity is supposedly an unattainable ideal. Maras (2013) notes that the concept of journalistic objectivity responds to a series of commercial, cultural, and organizational conditions in a society and, for this reason, changes over time.

From the 1830s [70] onward, journalism began to emphasize the collection of facts and the separation of opinion from information. Before this period, according to Schudson (2010), newspapers were expected to present a political or partisan point of view [71]. The idea of a non-partisan news item arose alongside the first North American news agency, the Associated Press, in 1846, shortly after the invention of the telegraph and the possibility of communicating with other parts of the world.

Objectivity began to establish itself as a value in the final decades of the 19th century, a time when the North American university, professional associations, and admiration for science were emerging. According to Schudson (2010), reporters at the time saw themselves as scientists uncovering the political and economic facts of industrial life. At the time, objectivity was understood as consensually validated statements about the world, with a radical separation between facts and values.

After World War I, and driven mainly by war propaganda and the emergence of Public Relations, journalists lost confidence in the facts, subjecting them to a series of norms and procedures. Objectivity as a consistent method for testing information appeared in the 1920s in the United States (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2014). This approach, the authors state, would allow for transparency regarding evidence, preventing possible personal and cultural biases from compromising the accuracy of reporters' work.

In the 1930s, interpretive reporting developed, driven by readers' demand for depth and interpretation in the face of a complex world (SCHUDSON, 2010). The American Society of Newspaper Editors decided, in 1933, that there should be greater space and dedication to interpretive and explanatory journalism with the presentation of behind-the-scenes information so that readers could understand the importance of the events of the time (BARNHURST, 2014). In that decade, journalistic practice was becoming more visual and contextual (ANDERSON, 2018). Despite the increase in interpretive news from 1930 to 1950, in the 1960s objectivity became a widely used term and something supposedly unattainable. This occurs in a scenario of generalized institutional crisis in journalism and at the time of the development of _New Journalism_, with the idea of a journalism close to literature. Since facts and events are the objects of journalism (GUERRA, 2008; SPONHOLZ, 2009), objectivity has two main ethical implications, according to Guerra: it becomes a parameter to be pursued and a reference for professionals, and it creates elements of measurement that support public trust in the journalistic institution.

It thus becomes an unquestionable given that the press has as its object a fact, which initiates the news and guides the work of professionals both with regard to the demand and credibility that comes from the public — after all, it attests the validity of the imperative — and with regard to the object — the occurrence or the "plot" — around which the institution is organized. **To deny this would be to deny the existence of the activity** (GUERRA, 2008, p. 102, emphasis in original).

Therefore, the verifiable truth, previously discussed in this chapter, is fundamental to understanding objectivity. The absence of objectivity, says Cornu (1994, p. 393), opens the way for "anything goes" in journalism, generating a journalism of "approximation" and "laziness." To refute the existence of objectivity corresponds to a fallacious, fragile, and unproductive argument insofar as reality can only be apprehended through language (CORNU, 1998) and every apprehension of the facts will require some type of interpretation, which must be justified within the journalistic discourse itself (LISBOA; BENETTI, 2015).

Sponholz (2009) proposes that objectivity be understood as a representation of reality, originating from the relationship between social (primary) and media (secondary) realities, emphasizing that its epistemological conception is structured in truth as the correspondence of facts. In this sense, the element of fact verification is added, which serves as the basis for journalism. Only through the checking of information do facts become a journalistic product.

In general, the term "objectivity" still seems to generate doubt among readers. A report written by Toff _et al._ (2021) suggests that values such as objectivity, impartiality, and balance are normally invoked by readers when talking about trust in news. However, these terms generate confusion among themselves. For some readers, being objective means keeping opinions out of the news; for others, it has to do with expanding transparency in journalistic processes when dealing with opinions. Objectivity must, above all, be associated with method and the verification of information.

Furthermore, the use of numbers is frequently associated with supposed objectivity and an openness to trust. Authors such as Bulla (2015) discuss that there is a predisposition to accept the number as objective reality both in terms of the production and reception of news. By applying Discourse Analysis to publications in the newspaper _O Estado de S. Paulo_ and interviewing reporters, Bulla identified that there is an appropriation of numbers for the production of arguments and construction of meanings as suits the journalistic organization. Thus, the author concluded that the use of numbers enhances the value of news through the quantification of information. Quantification in news, according to Lawson (2021), simultaneously generates a sense of magnitude, proportionality, and specificity — an effect that only numbers are capable of generating, since it is often not possible to achieve the same result through words alone.

As a general rule, readers are not able to experience firsthand the events that give rise to news, so they turn to journalism to access this data about reality (McNAIR, 2013). There is trust that journalism will present investigated and verified information to the public. Thus, the discipline of verification, about which Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) speak, is fundamental for objectivity as a method to be fulfilled. Verification, according to the authors, resides in journalism as a central role — its demonstration occurs via transparency.

In consonance with what Kovach and Rosenstiel argue, Cornu (1994) understands that journalism must value the maintenance of verification as a central ethical care in journalistic practice, as something that should not be abandoned. For the author, the ephemerality and rapidity of the unfolding of events must be the target of attention so that objectivity is not ruptured by speed and haste; objectivity, accompanied by methodological rigor, is the most important thing the journalist presents to the reader.

Verification in times of networked journalism and the speed inherent to it is a matter of concern for authors such as Cornu (1994) and Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014). In consonance with the discussion about the event, which occurs and is resignified as journalism provides new elements about its developments, one can reflect on the condition of the pseudo-environment of which Lippmann (1920) speaks. Since (in most cases) readers do not have access to primary information, the perception of reality
concrete reality is constructed and apprehended by the press.

p. 160) points to the path of Data-Driven Journalism in this trajectory. After tracing the history of Precision Journalism and the development of similar quantitative practices

**4.3 Transparency**


Transparency in journalism corresponds, broadly speaking, to the process of **opening**

**up** information from the journalistic organization and the processes it develops.


Under this aspect, it can be considered to encompass procedures preceding the


reportage, but still inherent to the _modus operandi_ of a journalistic organization, such as its


sources of funding, diversity policies, and error correction. In operational terms, it mainly covers the clarity of the method adopted and the sources used, allowing the reader to follow what has been published and to be able to verify and eventually reproduce the step-by-step of the report.


I present, in this section, the argument that transparency is divided into two


main categories: as an **operator of an ethical practice** and as a **discursive resource of**


**legitimation**. Through it, the aim is to promote a relationship of respect with the audience, reinforcing bonds of trust and amplifying the perception of credibility (JOHNSON;


ST JOHN, 2021; KARLSSON, 2020; ZELIZER, 2019). If trust stems from a process


in which one of the parties presents sufficient credibility to stimulate this sensation


in those who perceive it, transparency in journalism is also a way of minimizing


risk, since every trust relationship implies dealing with uncertainty to some degree


(KARLSSON, 2020).

In digital journalism, transparency has been studied for about 20 years,


driven by the publication of content on the Web. Karlsson (2020; 2022) identifies three


waves of transparency in journalism. The first, which the author calls "foundational," corresponds to the early 2000s and is an adaptation of broader institutional contexts on this topic, such as the demand for openness in processes of public institutions and


how this could be reflected in journalism. The second wave, called "consolidation"


and developed from 2010 to 2016, inserts the concept of transparency alongside digital social networks, updating its application in that landscape. It also undergoes a process of institutionalization as it appears in codes of ethics. From 2016 onward, Karlsson


(2022) perceives a third wave — currently underway — which he associates with the terms "hope" and "scrutiny," in which transparency is seen as a way of allowing


the public to evaluate socially established institutions. This scenario is a reflection of distrust motivated in part by the excess of disinformation available in the digital landscape


and mobilized by politicians and other public figures, expanding the trust relationship of


part of the population in traditionally established institutions.

Journalistic transparency can be seen from different angles. Explained broadly,


it consists of making public the usually private information that influences the process of news production (ALLEN, 2008). It has also been viewed


as a new norm or a new guise for objectivity (WEINBERGER, 2009) and


seen in the sense of openness (_openness_) of reporting processes (KARLSSON,


2010), ensuring the fulfillment of objective procedures that lead to the truth of


facts and allow audiences to be capable of understanding such evidence as true.


Although the sense of openness has prevailed in transparency studies


mainly from Karlsson (2010) onward, the author recently changed his approach,


arguing that it is more useful to present it as a "strategically managed visibility"


(KARLSSON, 2022, p. 78), since it serves the purposes of a social actor (the journalistic organization, in this case). In this way, the author updates the perception that


transparency would serve to correct an asymmetry, given that journalists have access to the information they use to produce content, which is not fully revealed to the public.


It encompasses an ethical dimension of journalism (KOLISKA; CHADHA, 2018; PLAISANCE, 2007), with _accountability_ initiatives associated with legitimacy (ALLEN, 2008)


and credibility (ZELIZER, 2019). Being also a way of differentiating journalistic discourse from other discourses, transparency can be seen as a normative innovation


(GADE _et al_., 2018), a practice that aims to delimit the role of journalism in relation to other areas (ALLEN, 2008), and an effect to be demonstrated to the public (LISBOA;


FRANZONI, 2018). Transparency presents itself as a normative principle (SEIBT; FONSECA, 2019) for the practice of journalistic genres such as fact-checking,


in which the journalist confronts public discourses with facts, assigning degrees of veracity to content using an objective method (GRAVES, 2016). Transparency


is defended by Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) as the key aspect of the discipline of verification.


As has been observed in digital journalistic products, transparency can


be considered a strategy for obtaining public engagement, broadening shared knowledge,


and demonstrating journalistic values, reinventing practices and conduct that


are normally associated with conventional and analog newsrooms, such as the figure of the


_ombudsman_, whose work is to bridge the gap between the outlet and the audience (MEIER, 2009).

In guiding society about what to expect regarding the consumption of information, Kovach and Rosenstiel (2011) enumerate six points to be observed. The audience


must therefore ask itself about the following topics: 1) what type of content it is


encountering (journalism or advertising); 2) the completeness of the information; 3) journalistic


sources and their credibility; 4) the presentation of evidence and the possibility of


testing it; 5) the existence of alternative explanations for the reported fact; 6) learning


information about the reported fact.


Once — ideally, based on their professional deontology — journalists seek the truth of the facts and communicate it to their audience while respecting the rules


of objectivity, the pursuit of transparency would guarantee the audience access to the same


resources (or a large part of them) that journalists used in producing the content. In practice, it means revealing information about the methods and sources adopted. What Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) call the "Spirit of Transparency" draws its inspiration from


the scientific method: explaining what is known about a fact and how it is known.

On the relationship between audiences and journalism, Karlsson (2022, p. 8) emphasizes


that it is necessary to find common ground between the two. In this way, an


alignment of expectations can occur between what journalism is capable of offering and what the


audience expects to receive. "If transparency is not translated into performable rituals


that are acceptable to the public, it would be impossible to decide whether a piece of journalism is or is not


transparent and whether or not there are implications for the perceived credibility from the perspective of the


public." [74] He thus understands that there must be a kind of guide that orients journalistic practices and makes transparency viable.


In reflecting on the relevance of the information to which the public should have access,


Kovach and Rosenstiel (2014) argue that journalists should include, in their texts, answers


to three questions: what does the audience need to know to evaluate this information? Is


there any aspect of how the information was handled that requires explanation? What are the

capture and communicate even the existence of what is not known (WIHBEY, 2019).

Contributions such as those of Karlsson (2010) and Meier (2009) are fundamental as


a starting point for creating operational concepts of transparency. Karlsson


(2010), for example, classifies transparency into two main strands: _disclosure transparency_, which encompasses explanations of how news is selected and produced, including making it clear to the public how much constant production, seven days a


week, can affect content quality; and _participatory transparency_, which involves the


participation of the audience at different stages of the news production process and


includes elements of interactivity. Years later, the author added the broad concept


of _ambient transparency_, which seeks to bring the audience closer to content production through interactive tools and techniques that add context to the content offered

Meier (2009), in turn, argues that transparency in journalism can take


two forms: external (_outside transparency_) and internal (_self-transparency_). While the external strand concerns traditional journalistic practices — insofar as it reports facts about the economy, politics, and science, journalism brings transparency to society —, the internal strand encompasses a kind of peer review and self-regulation,


in which the journalistic community takes it upon itself to evaluate what is produced by the media.


The external participation linked to the production and verification of content produced by


journalism has been particularly valued by audiences, according to a study conducted


by Uth, Badura, and Blöbaum (2021). When asking 1,029 German readers about what makes them trust an outlet, they found that audiences consider it important to participate


actively in the creation of content, whether by suggesting coverage topics or contributing


to the research process related to reporting. At the content level, practices such as


_fact-checking_ are also valued by the public. "Since transparency enables better judgment of journalistic products and processes, recipients are in a better position both to judge the trustworthiness of journalism and the risks


associated with using journalistic information conveyed by journalism as the basis of their actions" [75] (UTH; BADURA;


BLÖBAUM, 2021, p. 77).

In the wake of offering clarity to the reader, the discussion of transparency as


the operationalization of objectivity revisits the possibility of contextualization on the Web,


whose architecture allows for the use of hyperlinks. As explained earlier in this thesis,


hypertext, the mode of writing for the Web, enables layers of information, allows for deepening and contextualization at different levels, and also presents the original references that point to the provenance of information, allowing readers themselves to verify where it was extracted from. "What we used to believe because we thought the author was objective we now believe because we can see through the author's writings to the sources and values that brought her to that position" [76] (WEINBERGER, 2009, n.p.).


In a previous text (GEHRKE, 2018b), I defended the creation of three essential parameters for


the fulfillment of transparency as a value in journalism: clarity in sources; openness


of research, tests, and analyses; and error correction and updating.

For Allen (2008), transparency is a way for journalists to open up their processes without departing from the objective method of reporting. Although the two concepts operate in


consonance, the author believes that transparency is capable of modifying objectivity


in different ways. While objectivity is linked to the truth that must be discovered, transparency recognizes that journalism works with a verifiable,


provisional, and complex truth. Through transparency, journalism shows its proofs of


truth — that is, it presents evidence such as documents and other records that allow


the public to perceive that the information is valid and has undergone a verification process.

**4.3.1 Operator of an ethical practice**


Transparency emerges in contemporary journalism as an ethical principle


(PLAISANCE, 2007) to be upheld by journalists — that is, as part of their professional deontology. In operational terms, it is included in the list of basic fundamentals with which


the journalist must commit, alongside honesty, independence, verification,


and responsiveness in correcting errors (RUSBRIDGER, 2020).


According to Bucci (2000, p. 25), journalistic ethics represents, above all, "[...] a


pact of trust between the institution of journalism and the public, in an environment where democratic institutions are solid." For this reason, while journalistic ethics is a requirement linked to professional practice, it is also the result of a democratic society grounded in press freedom. In O'Neill's (2002) view,


transparency functions only when there is a predisposition on the part of the public to trust


a given institution — in this case, journalism.

As we noted earlier, journalism is guided by the provisional and verifiable truth of facts, which can be accessed and measured through working methods.


For this reason, the journalistic enterprise is not merely another company that produces and commercializes a type of product — in this case, information. The press, according to Bucci


(2000), exists because there is a trust relationship between the parties, a fundamental bond


for the public's perception of credibility. The author thus understands that


transparency is a fundamental condition for journalistic companies to deal with their


limitations. Along the same lines, Wihbey (2019) argues that the connection between journalistic ethics and transparency implies that professionals admit their work is an unfinished product, an ongoing process. Journalists would thus be able to communicate


to audiences aspects of uncertainty and possible failures.


Although it is not entirely possible to dissociate the individual journalist's practice


from the collective conduct expected of newsrooms, there are some differences in the handling of information. Cornu (1998) distinguishes between the responsibilities of journalists and media


outlets. According to the author, the professional is personally responsible to their sources and the public, to professional norms, and to their own convictions.


Within the media outlets where they work, however, there is shared responsibility


over the information disseminated and communicated to interest groups, the State,


and society as a whole.


Regarding the journalist's conduct, one can reflect on their relationships with personal


sources and issues of privacy (CHRISTOFOLETTI, 2019). According to Bucci (2000, p.


12), ethics in journalism presupposes institutional premises, but deals with the field of


individual decisions made by professionals. At the same time, the author reinforces, it encompasses values that only make sense when followed by both employees and employers, and in such a way that the public can serve as a watchdog. To this end, journalistic ethics has as its fundamental premise press freedom, a non-negotiable principle "for the benefit of democratic society in its civil and public dimension."

Transparency is at the center of the 2014 version of the Code of Ethics of the Society


of Professional Journalists of the United States. One section of the code is dedicated exclusively to this matter. According to the text, ethical journalism presupposes taking responsibility


for one's acts and explaining to the public the decisions made. Thus, from a normative standpoint, the code indicates that professionals should: explain ethical choices to the audience,


encouraging civil debate; respond quickly to questions about accuracy and clarity; identify and correct errors immediately, making the corrections made clear; and expose


any conduct that falls short of ethical standards in journalism (SOCIETY OF PROFESSIONAL JOURNALISTS, 2014). In the Code of Ethics of Brazilian Journalists [79],

2007, there is no mention of transparency.

Some editorial guidelines and conduct manuals in journalism are outdated and do not

be conducted concludes that transparency is a value widely invoked by professionals. However, this keyword is not cited by the audience — the public prioritizes familiarity with the brand and the reputation of the media outlet, also valuing


the way in which a news story is presented. In this study, focus groups were conducted


with people in different countries — including Brazil —, but it should be noted

criticize journalistic mistakes — which in principle should not occur, being prevented

they admit they were wrong. Thus, it is possible to think that a trust relationship


is built between audiences and media outlets; like any interpersonal relationship,


trust presents itself as a fundamental element. "Even if transparency is not always a sufficient condition for more


ethical behavior, its absence is a prerequisite for deception [...]" [80] (PLAISANCE, 2007, p. 193). If


transparency is not a complete antidote to failures, its absence becomes a risk.


Since journalism's purpose is to inform society, it is to this public


that it must show itself to be honest, bound to ethical principles, and accountable. "If a fact


was reported in a partial or inaccurate manner, if an article or broadcast disclosed some


information containing material errors, the facts must be clarified and the errors corrected" (CORNU, 1998, p. 68-69). Ethical conduct, even if it seems centered on the practice of reporting, must extend to editorial decision-making that guides the entire


conduct of a journalistic company. This involves, for example, making clear who finances the newspaper, since financial matters tend to raise questions about


possible conflicts of interest.

any limits that may arise in its productions, journalism seeks to maintain its role of authority and ensure the legitimacy needed to continue disseminating only


what suits it (BENETTI, 2008). The constructed narrative thus aims to promote an


effect of truth and ensure the credibility of the one who speaks (in this case, journalism itself). In this way, transparency can also be thought of as a discursive


strategy of legitimation associated with an _effect_ — even if the public does not reproduce the methods detailed in the news, the mere fact of having this option available confers


an effect of transparency (LISBOA, FRANZONI, 2018). And, even though it is difficult to measure real


gains in credibility, the demonstration of transparency can serve to allow journalism to simply maintain the _status quo_ of qualified mediator of reality on


topics of public interest, avoiding drastic changes in the way the public perceives it


(KARLSSON, 2022).


When discussing journalism as a justified true belief, Lisboa and


Benetti (2015) understand that the transparency effect falls within the scope of justification. Thus, the journalistic account presents clues and proofs that it speaks the truth, sustaining the credibility of the discourse. In this case, as Weinberger (2009, n.p.) says, the evidence can be even more easily accessed by the public, becoming something tangible: "[...] transparency mirrors the nature of the Web: Content is linked, public, discussed and always subject to dispute and revision." **4.3.3 Transparency in Data-Driven Journalism**


In order to execute the science-inspired method and, consequently, generate a type


of specialized knowledge (TRÄSEL; GEHRKE, 2021), Data-Driven Journalism


must submit its work to public scrutiny, offering audiences information


about its practices (operator of an ethical practice) and editorial principles (discursive resource of legitimation). Transparency thus functions as a reflection of truth and objectivity.


By operating primarily on the Web, DDJ inherits a tradition developed since


the time of its emergence in the 2000s: transparency through the use of hyperlinks.


At the time, blog authors used this openness resource to ensure the credibility of their publications (MEIER, 2009), just as they viewed their own work as


a construction, subject to frequent updates and therefore corresponding to a


provisional truth (PHILLIPS, 2010). Offering audiences the possibility of retracing the


steps adopted means acknowledging that the public has the right to access the same


information as journalists. "While reporters have to rely on second and third-hand knowledge, the public itself is even


more poorly equipped to assess the truthfulness of news" [88] (KOVACH; ROSENSTIEL, 2011, p. 33).

Creator of Precision Journalism, the main precursor of DDJ, Meyer (2002) understands that journalists and scientists share similar premises in carrying out their


work. Both categories of professionals, according to the author, adopt in their work: skepticism, by questioning the information received; openness, by explaining the investigation procedures through documents; an instinct for operationalization, since


they seek to test a hypothesis in the real world; a sense of obtaining truth, taking


into account that truth is provisional; and parsimony, since they remain attentive


to selecting theories that truly have the potential to explain a phenomenon.

By working with spreadsheets, mathematical models, statistical analysis, script formulations, and more — all under the hacker ethic of sharing —, it is expected


that journalists exchange information among themselves and are able to resolve doubts and advance knowledge as a community. The daily routine of newsrooms, however, seems
