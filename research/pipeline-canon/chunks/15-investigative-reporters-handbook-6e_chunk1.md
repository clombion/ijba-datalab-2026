# Preface

In the decade since the last edition of "The Investigative Reporter's Handbook," journalism has continued to undergo seismic changes. Many of those have been horrendous to the business and the communities journalists serve. But investigative reporting has remained essential and has continued to evolve, in many cases expanding through nonprofit newsrooms to fill the void left by the severe cuts to traditional news organizations.

Newspapers especially have been hit hard. More than 2,000 newspapers have closed, and more than half the editorial jobs have been cut in the past decade. At the same time, the rise of nonprofit newsrooms from a few to hundreds since 2009 has brought more emphasis and momentum to investigative journalism, as those newsrooms have quickly adopted new technologies and techniques.

However, it remains essential to uphold the principles and methods that support all good reporting --- and especially all good *investigative* reporting.

This book emphasizes those fundamental guidelines --- accuracy, multisourcing, documentation, fairness and a relentless pursuit of the truth --- and serves as a road map for any journalist, student or citizen who wants to practice investigative journalism. Its chapters repeatedly show how obtaining authentic documents and data and conducting numerous interviews lead to excellent stories that serve the public while holding the powerful accountable. This book also explains how to embrace new techniques, approaches and technologies without abandoning the hard-earned knowledge of working journalists.


## What Is Investigative Journalism?

Journalists and scholars have for many years debated the specific meaning of the term "investigative journalism." IRE defines it as "the reporting, through one's own initiative and work product, of matters of importance to readers, viewers or listeners. In many cases, the subjects of the reporting wish the matters under scrutiny to remain undisclosed." Over the years, journalists have expanded the definition to include analyzing and revealing the breakdown of social or justice systems and documenting the consequences.

Others have proposed a broader definition, suggesting that every journalist is an investigative journalist because they all ask questions and read documents, but this broader definition ignores the more rigorous requirements of credible investigative journalism. Every journalist *can* be an investigator, even if only for one story, if the journalist is willing to put in the time. Investigative journalists ask more questions, go through more documents and data, and spend more time thinking about and producing a story than daily reporters do. Investigative journalism also demands the curiosity and desire to know the story behind the story --- that is, to understand the circumstances of a situation and the real goals of a politician or businessperson.

Investigative journalists also spend more thought and time on deciding what is worthy of coverage. Instead of simply attending a city council meeting and filing a brief deadline story, an investigative reporter will often read council and staff reports, audits and city contracts, and minutes of previous meetings, and collect and analyze city databases to find out if there is favoritism or misdeeds in how the city is being run.

An investigative journalist wants to know how the world works --- or fails to work --- and takes the time to study it closely. But persistence and study must always be accompanied by a healthy skepticism, stopping short of cynicism. Investigative reporting must be accompanied by an outrage that expresses itself through the journalists' creed of comforting the afflicted and afflicting the comfortable. Those traits of a good investigative reporter --- curiosity, persistence, skepticism, a sense of outrage --- lead to important exposés not because of luck but because chance favors those who have done the deeper research and interviews. If a reporter has these traits, then much of the rest can be learned.

## About IRE

IRE, a grassroots nonprofit organization dedicated to improving the quality of investigative reporting, was formed in 1975 by leading investigative journalists in the United States. Its goal was to provide a network for those seeking to do investigative reporting and editing, to provide training at seminars and conferences, and to provide resources and guidelines for the profession. It was incorporated in Indianapolis by founding reporters Myrta Pulliam and Harley Bierce and attorney Edward O. DeLaney.

The first IRE conference in June 1976 drew more than 300 journalists who shared tips and techniques. But the conference had an additional focus: Just days before, investigative journalist and IRE member Don Bolles was fatally injured by a car bomb while working on a story in Phoenix. In response, IRE formed a team of editors and reporters from around the country, known as the Arizona Project, to go to Phoenix to expand on Bolles' work on government corruption and organized crime and to create an "insurance policy" for investigative journalists. IRE wanted to show that no one could kill a story by killing the reporter because dozens of other journalists would appear to continue the work.

The Arizona Project resulted in a 23-part series that was published nationwide. Although the series was criticized by some mainstream press leaders as presumptuous "gang-bang" journalism and IRE was unsuccessfully sued by some subjects of the investigation, the Arizona Project received a major national award and raised the profile of IRE as an organization that is crucial to the future of watchdog journalism. It also demonstrated the power of collaboration and cooperation among newsrooms to produce much stronger investigative reporting and has served as a model for the widespread collaboration of investigative newsrooms today.

Despite the cost of fighting the lawsuits, IRE survived and established its headquarters at the University of Missouri School of Journalism in 1978. The first full-time executive director was John Ullmann, who was a co-author of the first and second editions of this book. Those editions were published by Macmillan Learning. The third edition was written by Steve Weinberg for IRE, who followed Ullmann as executive director. The fourth edition was written for IRE by Brant Houston (then executive director of IRE), Len Bruzzese (then IRE deputy director) and Weinberg, who had continued as faculty member at Missouri School of Journalism. The fifth edition was written by Houston with research support from IRE.


## About This Book

Since 1983, IRE has produced "The Reporter's Handbook," now "The Investigative Reporter's Handbook," to provide both journalism students and professionals with a comprehensive guide to investigative journalism. In each edition, it has updated many of the story examples, recognized new and relevant topics, and been on the forefront of reviewing new investigative digital techniques and data analysis. With this book, users can plan and carry out investigations on beats and topics of their choosing while building on the knowledge and stories of other reporters. In addition, the book is more closely tied to the Resource Center at IRE, where useful tip sheets and compendiums of government and business websites are updated regularly.

"The Investigative Reporter's Handbook" not only guides journalists to thousands of documents and databases, it also helps them find and cultivate human sources. It is both a primer of investigative techniques and a reference for investigations into particular topics; it is designed to be both a textbook and a practical guide for working journalists. In this edition, the book begins with an overview of the skills and knowledge needed for any investigative story. Then it focuses on particular topics and beats, covering agencies and issues in Part Two and business in Part Three.

Although you can use the index or table of contents to find specific information, we suggest that you concentrate on the chapters that are most relevant to the story you are working on.

Investigative journalism is a critical part of creating an informed and democratic society. It helps citizens make knowledgeable decisions. It reins in the excesses of the powerful and holds government and business organizations accountable for their actions. It exposes the exploitation of the poor and weak, and it brings about significant changes for the better.

To perform that service, investigative journalists must produce credible, original and illuminating stories that are thoroughly researched through documents and data, probing and in-depth interviews and sometimes dangerous excursions into the field.

Most journalists feel comfortable doing interviews and going out into communities, though many feel challenged when it comes to delving into documents or databases. But the broader a journalist's range of sources and the sharper their data skills, the greater the results can be. Consider these examples:

- The Atlanta Journal-Constitution built a database by downloading doctor discipline records from throughout the U.S. and used it to expose a system that allowed doctors to get away with continued sexual abuse of patients.
- A whistleblower leaked a massive database of offshore bank accounts to journalists, and the analysis of that data resulted in the Panama Papers, one of the largest collaborative reporting projects in history, crossing international borders and exposing tax evasion by criminals and politicians around the world.
- In a third exemplary series, The Associated Press followed a trail of documents and data to a small island in Indonesia where they uncovered an operation using slave labor to process seafood sold in U.S. grocery stores. The investigation, which incorporated strong interviewing skills and basic shoeleather reporting, resulted in freeing hundreds of enslaved workers.

James Steele and Donald Barlett, a longtime investigative duo, emphasized decades ago that investigative journalists should have a "documents state of mind" if they want to produce compelling projects. Steele and Barlett define a "documents state of mind" as knowing that documents exist somewhere that can explore, contradict or confirm each point of an investigation.

In addition, the best investigative journalists sometimes use a technique that can be best described as "working from the outside in." Rendered graphically, the approach would resemble concentric circles.

Investigative stories can be daunting to the inexperienced journalist because of the amount of research and interviews, not to mention the persistence and patience, required for an investigation. But the only way for journalists to discover whether they have the temperament and talent for investigations is to try.


## The Investigative Process

Beginning an investigative story can feel intimidating, but by keeping the investigative process in mind, it can be broken down piece by piece.

### Choosing an Investigation

Before tackling an investigation, a journalist must choose an appropriate subject, although often it seems the subject has chosen the journalist. That is, a major news event, a series of complaints or tips, an exploration or analysis of a database, or a natural follow-up to a daily story may steer the journalist into an investigation.

But if a journalist has the ability to think about what he or she might investigate, there are a number of topics that always present themselves: government corruption, unsafe workplaces, shoddy construction, medical malpractice, environmental hazards, homelessness, exploitation of women, and abuse of children or the elderly.

An investigative journalist could begin a story with a tip from a longtime source or from a stranger. A news story done on general assignment or a regular beat might call for an investigation to determine why a program isn't working. Sorting or grouping of information in a database might reveal a trend or an unusual case, or provide new context for an old problem. Sometimes curiosity about why something is the way it is spurs a reporter to dig deeper. Sometimes basic daily observations can lead the way to a story, too.

Daily reporting provides many investigative ideas. For example, the famous investigative reporter Bob Woodward started his career at a weekly newspaper in the Maryland suburbs of Washington, D.C. By using routine assignments to start building investigative projects, he worked his way to The Washington Post. There he combined ideas from his beat with his penchant for digging. Leonard Downie Jr., in his book "The New Muckrakers," describes how Woodward "assigned himself" to uncover "small-to-middling scandals," such as supermarkets selling fatty meat and pharmacies dispensing outdated drugs and mislabeled prescriptions. Those stories prepared Woodward for the Watergate exposé, which at first sight appeared to be no more than a minor burglary.

### The Hypothesis

Beginning with a hypothesis is not the same as starting with an angle or idea to prove. The best investigative journalists collect both supporting and contradictory evidence for a story. They then weigh carefully what the evidence shows. For instance, because of several tips, a journalist might hypothesize that the elderly in a chain of nursing homes are being neglected and abused. But if inspection reports and interviews with relatives of the elderly demonstrate otherwise, then the journalist would reconsider the story.

Newspaper editor Tom Honig wrote in The IRE Journal that "most good investigations come down to one of two things --- either a process did not work or people did not follow the guidelines ... If a baby dies, was it one Child Protective Service worker who screwed up, or is the whole system failing?"

The film "Spotlight" highlighted the need to focus on the system as it recounted The Boston Globe's investigation into abuse of children by Catholic priests. Marty Baron, who was the editor of The Globe at the time and later editor of The Washington Post, said in an interview: "I think ultimately, you have to make a judgment on what's the most important, what will hold powerful interests accountable, what is the systemic problem, as opposed to an issue that is less substantial."

Thus, the possible questions to ask on the way to launching a project are:

- How is the system supposed to work?
- How well is the system working?
- Who is benefiting and who is suffering because of the way the system works?

### Secondary Sources

When a journalist is working from the outside in, information already published or broadcast can serve as a starting point for answering basic questions. For Baron, a news column in The Globe itself on a Catholic priest's abuse of children led him to push reporters to see how widespread the problem was. Such information is secondary source material because it's often using information from primary documents such as court records. If independently verified, it can then turn into a road map or a tip.

Secondary source material isn't difficult to come by. Depending on the issue or topic you're investigating, you can choose from a number of mediums and formats. News organizations have online versions of their stories, and other organizations aggregate news articles from around the world (see [Chapter 2](#hou_9781319102845_ch02_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_m4qJC2nJr9)). Secondary source materials from television and radio are also often available online.

### From Secondary Documents to Primary Documents

Secondary sources, as noted, are most useful when they lead to primary documents or databases. A legislative hearing transcript is a primary document, as are a real estate deed, board minutes, a political candidate's electronic campaign finance report, a lawsuit filing, an insurance policy and a discharge certificate from the military. As you can see, primary documents are original information, not the articles or books citing them in research or stories. Documents and databases can lie and err just like human sources can, because, after all, documents and databases are prepared by humans. However, unlike humans, documents do not claim to have been misquoted.

The best journalists assume that somewhere, a document or documents exist on any topic. And primary documents are more readily available than many journalists realize. With the expansion and prevalence of the internet, documents and databases on anything have become much more accessible and have presented investigative journalists the challenge of having an overwhelming amount of information.

If the "documents state of mind" is difficult to imagine, a journalist need only consider his or her own life. From birth, the journalist is followed by a document trail, including a birth certificate and hospital admission and discharge records. School records, from report cards to yearbook entries, are next in the trail. Then a driver's license and most likely the title and registration for a car in the journalist's name. The journalist goes to college and receives student loans and college scholarships. Many college students work part-time jobs and may get documentation such as a food-server license or a cosmetic license. After they graduate, there are updated résumés and completed job applications.

The credit card charges that begin in college continue. Hobbies like fishing or hunting require licenses. A journalist has a pet and the pet has a license with information about its owner. The journalist pays personal property taxes on a car and eventually may be able to afford a house and then pay real estate taxes. The journalist registers to vote and may marry, have a child and divorce. Every one of these stages results in a record. The journalist also would have left a trail on social media with reports on activities, comments on news events and an increasing list of what interests the journalist.

Institutions --- whether government agencies, private corporations, nonprofit charities or some hybrid --- leave document trails much as individuals do. For example, the articles of incorporation for a business can be likened to an individual's birth certificate, its annual reports to records of daily life and its dissolution to a death certificate. ([Chapter 3](#hou_9781319102845_ch03_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_2xJ4HgTOBS) contains a fuller discussion of primary documents and databases.)

### Human Sources

Documents and databases serve many purposes, one of which is helping locate human sources. Too many investigators contact only the obvious sources --- those in current positions (such as current spouse and current neighbors) --- while ignoring "formers" (the former spouse, former professional colleague, former accountant, former neighbor, former minister and so on). The "formers" often have scores to settle or outdated knowledge, but they can be candid, have had time to reflect and may have kept relevant documents.

Human sources are crucial because documents take on new meaning when explained or discussed by insiders or experts. This is one reason to refrain from key interviews until the document trail has been firmly established. Information in documents often serves as the basis for questions during the interview; because a journalist might get only one interview with a source, it is best to wait until most documents are in hand. When it comes to interviewing the main subject of the investigation, documents serve as a reality check, helping to determine if the subject is lying or is ignorant about certain matters.

At the same time, it can help to show documents to nonadversarial sources because they can correct errors in the documents or can identify other documents to gather. (See [Chapter 5](#hou_9781319102845_ch05_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_8t361b8Ko5) for more information about people trails.)

### Research Techniques

During the information-gathering stage, longtime journalist Dennis King merged paper and people trails through what he called parallel backgrounding and indirect backgrounding. Other top investigative journalists work the same way, but few have explained it as well as King in his classic book "Get the Facts on Anyone."

About parallel backgrounding, King says that when the subject is connected to an institution, the organization will leave its own trails. By following those trails, investigators might locate new information about the subject. For example, personal documents about Mr. Smith may contain no negative information, but city public works files on his contracting firm might include information suggesting bid rigging.

"The same principle also works in reverse," King says. "If your main target is a business enterprise or nonprofit organization, you may gain startling insights by examining the personal backgrounds of its principals or officers. That seemingly innocuous annual report of your local community development corporation may appear in a different light once you learn that the city has padlocked two buildings owned by the executive director because of illegal gambling on the premises."

King defines indirect backgrounding as "parallel backgrounding on a grand scale." An investigative journalist might find that the subject is tied to economic or political interests. The way to understand the relationships is to analyze the larger environment. King's research on a West African nation visited often by a New York businessman helped identify the man's possible Libyan connections. In another case, King's journey into the history of the Teamsters Union and organized-crime families led to a better understanding of politician Lyndon LaRouche, the subject of a biography by King.

### Organizing the Information

As a journalist collects primary documents and human sources, the information gathered can become overwhelming if it is left to stack up in a seemingly endless set of computer files or papers on a desk. It's best to prepare digital files and folders to catch the information as it comes in and then to evaluate the information often (perhaps weekly) and make preliminary decisions about what is valuable.

The potentially valuable material should be entered into a chronology, whether of a person's life, an institution's history or an issue's progression. Creating a chronology does not mean writing the story chronologically. Rather, the chronology is meant to organize large amounts of information in a linear way to help illuminate links among factors that might otherwise seem unrelated. It also helps you to step back and see the bigger view when you feel you are getting lost in the details.

For example, suppose a city council member tells you that he never participated in a closed-door session 18 months earlier about rezoning land owned by a real estate group that his wife is on. The council member tells you he was out of town at the time. You enter that detail in the chronology as a matter of good record keeping. Then, three months later, you read minutes of a long-past meeting held by a neighborhood association concerned about the rezoning. As you enter details from those minutes into the chronology, you read that the city council member spoke at the meeting --- during the period he claimed to be out of town. You investigate further, discover that the council member lied, and you're able to write that the council member did in fact participate in the rezoning meeting. Without the chronology, you might never have discovered the lie.

One caveat about chronologies: Information excluded from the chronology early in an investigation may become relevant later. As a result, all information collected must be reevaluated from time to time during the reporting stage.

### Questioning Conventional Wisdom

Sound information-gathering, organizing and writing techniques mean little if the underlying logic of the investigation is faulty. Investigative journalists should always question the conventional wisdom about an individual, institution or issue.

Nikole Hannah-Jones has investigated issues of inequality for ProPublica and The New York Times. Hannah-Jones takes a different fundamental approach than many journalists, stressing the need to focus on outcomes, not intentions. Hannah-Jones stresses that reporters often try to prove that an official or elected body acted with prejudicial intent in creating education systems or housing programs that result in disparities among racial groups. But it can be difficult or impossible to prove what someone was thinking. If a journalist uses documents, data and interviews to prove that the resulting system is inequitable and that people are suffering as a result, proving intent becomes unnecessary. The outcome of people suffering is the point to focus on.

Journalists should look for answers about how and why things are as they are. Mary Kay Blakely, while writing for the Los Angeles Times Magazine, practiced that kind of thinking as she considered the problem of homelessness in an affluent society. The homeless "were not dropped on our streets by a deus ex machina," she wrote. "They arrived through human actions and social choices," which included government urban renewal that destroyed low-income housing but failed to replace it with something desirable and affordable; elimination of unskilled jobs that supported families; and deinstitutionalization of the mentally ill along with the simultaneous reduction in funding for community-based mental health programs.

When the Star Tribune of Minneapolis decided to investigate whether rapists are punished effectively, the reporters and editors looked at past research projects on the topic. The team determined that many of the previous projects had been flawed in their proceedings. John Ullmann, in his book "Investigative Reporting: Advanced Methods and Techniques," explains why:

> Short follow-up periods. Many studies followed sex criminals for only a year or two after their release, not enough time to draw meaningful conclusions. Faulty study groups. Much of the research has focused on only a handful of sex criminals --- too few to draw conclusions. Others have lumped all types of sex criminals together --- rapists with incest offenders, for example --- even though their patterns are as different as apples and oranges. Poor counting techniques. Many studies noted only subsequent convictions or returns to prison, failing to count such reliable indicators as arrests. Erroneous time calculations. Many studies were sloppy in calculating the time frames in which sex criminals committed later offenses; for example, many didn't deduct the time a rapist was in prison and therefore unable to commit another rape.

The Star Tribune team grounded its investigation in accepted principles of sound social science research, such as studying a sample of adequate size.

All these approaches serve to prove that journalists must confront what they think and become aware of certain assumptions they have brought to their subject. During an investigation, everything should be questioned.



## Storyboarding

Storyboarding is a critical process to make time for when planning an investigative story. While there are variations on how this process can be accomplished, journalists should keep a basic structure in mind to ensure that they're hitting all the key points of their investigation. Paul N. Williams, an investigative journalist from Omaha and a theorist of the craft at Ohio State University until his death in 1976, set forth a landmark idealized description of investigative reporting. Williams proposed that investigative journalists follow a procedure of 11 major steps (and numerous substeps). Since then, journalists have incorporated storyboarding --- that is, visualizing the steps of an investigation --- into the investigative process.

### The Basics of Storyboarding

Time spent working on an investigative story will be all the more successful if you make a plan ahead of time. Storyboarding can allow you to walk through your potential story and get a sense of the feasibility of what you're trying to accomplish.

### The Paul Williams Way to Storyboarding

Paul Williams conceived an 11-step procedure for journalists to ensure that they pull everything in their investigation together. This chapter only covers an in-depth look at a few of the steps, but of those not mentioned, Williams also encourages journalists to focus on go/no-go decision (deciding based on feasibility if an investigation will yield at least a minimum story), base building (the process in which the journalist familiarizes themselves with the norms of the individual, institution or issue), planning, original research, reevaluation, filling the gaps, final evaluation, writing and rewriting and publication or airing of the story and follow-up stories.

#### Conceiving the Investigation

Williams lists conception as Step 1 in his storyboarding process, and in order to conceive or create an investigation, a journalist must always be informed. Today, the best journalists read local and national news articles every day, check websites and blogs, watch television news and listen to radio stations and podcasts, look at a range of magazines, read books, monitor social media and place their names on discussion groups. Veteran investigative reporters consistently respond to the question of what one activity they would suggest to aspiring journalists: "Read."

In addition, the best journalists develop regular sources who are independent of each other. The range of sources includes officials, advocates, clients and researchers. Journalists need to leave the office, meet sources face-to-face, listen to conversations in elevators and hallways and cover government meetings that no other journalists attend. Every person a journalist meets knows something the journalist does not and therefore is a potential source.

#### Considering the Feasibility of the Investigation

After conceiving of an investigation, a journalist has to make a realistic assessment of what it will take to get the story. That includes accessibility of records and sources, access to buildings and sites involved in the story, and the time it will take.

The journalist will have to take into account the possibility of public attacks and the threats of lawsuits. These should not necessarily dissuade a journalist from an investigation, but a journalist should have a plan for dealing with such possibilities, especially those in small newsrooms. In addition, considerations about computer security and physical security may need to be assessed.

Given the limited resources many newsrooms have, a journalist should also consider who might be a good collaborator on a story, whether it is other journalists or other newsrooms. Once a decision is made to go forward, lists of documents and data needed, people to interview and places to visit or observe should be made. As many investigative journalists have noted, the lists will lengthen as the investigation goes on, and decisions will need to be made on when to stop and run with what's been accumulated.

Among the tasks to plan at the beginning of the investigation are:

- Obtaining documents and databases, through Freedom of Information Act requests if necessary.
- Finding and interviewing human sources.
- Transcribing interviews.
- Filing manually and electronically.
- Writing.
- Copy editing.
- Photography.
- Videography.
- Graphics.
- Accuracy checking.
- Checking for possible libel --- or, any defamatory or unjustly unfair or untrue statement made against someone or some organization in an effort to expose them or damage their reputation.

These tasks ensure that the journalist has an adequate picture of what's to come.

#### Assessing the Investigation

Periodically, an assessment of how the investigation is going should be done. Decisions on whether to stop, postpone or publish should be calmly considered. During those assessments, it will become clear what major gaps remain in the reporting. The investigative journalist should ensure that all those accused have had --- or will have --- a chance to respond. This is not only about fairness, but some journalists have received valuable information during those interviews that have changed the course of the investigation. An assessment also allows the journalists to once again look for inconsistencies in interviews, documents or data.

Journalists should begin writing parts of the story as early as possible. It will mean that some of the basic facts about the individual or institution have been catalogued and verified, saving time during the overall writing of the story.

Also, it is through writing and editing that gaps in reporting are found earlier rather than at the end of the investigation.

Remember that some of the best investigative stories may be the follow-up stories. The first story has a way of prying out more evidence and new stories. As in the film "Spotlight," when a story is published or aired, it is crucial that reporters be available to get new tips and comments that may come in at that time. In addition, journalists often have follow-up stories already developing as they finish the first investigation.

#### Examples of the Paul Williams Way

Some projects serve as timeless models of the Paul Williams Way in action. The first example that follows, by Pat Stith, who worked for The (Raleigh) News & Observer, relies on thought patterns and research techniques that any investigator reading this far ought to grasp. As you'll see, Stith begins with conceiving the investigation based on an exchange of money that stuck out to him. He considered how feasible this investigation would be to investigate and ultimately followed a document trail to begin building and planning his investigation.

Earlier that year, Stith and a colleague had started looking into a \$100,000 annual bonus paid by the North Carolina Agriculture Department, which runs the state fair, to the carnival company. Stith already knew that the carnival company had benefited from a lucrative contract for many years, a contract awarded with no competition. Stith also knew that the carnival company had entertained North Carolina agriculture officials at a convention in Las Vegas.

Stith could not help wondering: How far back did this cozy relationship extend? Thanks to a "documents state of mind," Stith assumed that information could be extracted from a document or an interview. Documents first: "State officials save the darndest things, so I went to the records warehouse looking for proof of an old, friendly relationship between agriculture officials and \[the company\]. The copy of the 20-year-old note I found thanked the carnival owner for his hospitality in Las Vegas --- for meeting the North Carolina officials at the airport, for the dinners, the 'free booze' and the chauffeur." For his work on the project Stith prompted governmental reforms and won a Pulitzer Prize.

In another example, Walt Bogdanich, a longtime investigative reporter at The New York Times who has won several Pulitzer Prizes for his work, recounted in a video interview how he went from a curious remark by a corporate official to exposing dangerous railroad crossings and corporate cover-ups throughout the U.S.

Bogdanich attended a meeting in which officials from the pharmaceutical company Bayer visited with The New York Times reporters. During the meeting one official said a big issue in Washington, D.C. facing the company was "railroads." Bogdanich thought that was odd, and he proceeded to carry out the investigative methods cited in this chapter. When he placed a follow-up phone call to the company to get more information on why this was an issue, the company official said railroad companies were taking advantage of the company by charging high rates.

Bogdanich made the go/no-go decision to follow this lead. He started reading past newspaper articles on railroads. While doing that, he discovered a different trail to follow when he came across reports of a fatal accident at a crossing in California. He called a railroad regulator who said that accident was not in the agency's database. With the help of a data analyst at The Times, he discovered numerous accidents that had been reported and, by comparing the data to news articles, he also discovered those that were not reported. By interviewing plaintiffs' attorneys and victims and victims' families to fill in the gaps, he collected accusations and evidence of accident sites being tampered with by railroad companies to cover up the evidence at the scenes. Thus, a key paragraph in the award-winning story, "Death on the Tracks," reads:

> Over the last eight years, railroads have also broken federal rules by failing to promptly report hundreds of fatal accidents, 71 of them last year, denying the federal authorities the chance to investigate when evidence is fresh and still available, according to a computer analysis of federal data by The Times. Enforcement of these rules is so lax that federal officials said they were not even aware of the reporting problems.

# Chapter 2 Secondary Sources: From the Web to the Page


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_W19UK5qpck" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Identify and mine already published information to find story ideas and add depth to existing investigations.
- Conduct better online searches to find secondary sources.
- Mine articles and books to find experts, studies and more.

</aside>

South Carolina had the highest rate of women being killed by men in the nation, and the staff of the Charleston Post (S.C.) and Courier wanted to figure out why. The problem was, no single organization had investigated instances of domestic violence or kept track of all the cases. So the journalists at the Post and Courier had to do it themselves.

The result of more than 12 months of work was the Pulitzer Prize--winning "Till Death Do Us Part" project. To get hard data on the details of the murders they wanted to investigate, the journalists couldn't rely on government source documents alone; they had to rely on secondary sources as well. In addition to gathering police and judicial records from courts throughout the state, they compiled newspaper articles and published obituaries from online archives. All of the information went into a database built by the journalists, ultimately allowing them to write a powerful and specific lead: "More than 300 women were shot, stabbed, strangled, beaten, bludgeoned or burned to death over the past decade by men in South Carolina, dying at a rate of one every 12 days while the state does little to stem the carnage from domestic abuse."

The journalists' collection of archival material continues a long tradition of this kind of work. In the past, journalists have searched both hard copy and digital news articles to identify patterns in occurrences like these. This might include trends in murders conducted by serial killers, farm accidents, police misconduct and patterns of discrimination.

Secondary source material --- that is, already published information --- is routinely used by investigative journalists to generate investigative pieces. Such sources, from newspapers, magazines and books, to online databases and analysis, provide leads to investigative stories and extensive background on any issue, institution or individual.

But journalists should independently verify all secondary source information. A journalist must check later issues of the publication for corrections, comments on stories, letters to the editor and clarifying follow-up articles, and then confirm the accuracy of the information by seeking out the original sources, if possible. In general, web resources must be vetted for accuracy and thoroughness. But secondary sources still can be an invaluable starting point and sometimes the core of a good investigative story.



## Navigating the Web

The internet offers limitless possibilities to a researcher, but it is equal parts potential and peril. Nonprofit organizations, nongovernmental organizations and various interest groups publish studies, research and data that can be invaluable for journalists. That said, it is absolutely essential to vet the accuracy and objectivity of all information found online, just as with any other sources.

One of the first places to go on any organization's website is the "About" section, to look for a statement of purpose and to evaluate the people behind the site. If it is a nonprofit, look for a list of financial supporters. Also check to see if the site is part of a larger organization and, if so, look into that group. (You can use the skills discussed in [Chapter 5](#hou_9781319102845_ch05_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_8t361b8Ko5) to dig into any of the principles listed on the website to learn more about their backgrounds and potential conflicts.)

Another important resource that can help journalists uncover information is a search engine. That said, journalists should remember that no web search can find all the information on the internet. In fact, a large amount of information --- known as the Invisible or Deep Web --- resides in layers below the information that traditional search engines like Google see. That "hidden" information on the Invisible Web includes databases, scholarly papers and many other resources. Google and other traditional search engines mine what is known as the "surface web." They operate with lightning speed, but in most cases do not pull results from many of the databases available on individual websites. Using a traditional search engine like Google is essential, but it is important to supplement your search by going to specific sites.

Margot Williams, a veteran research librarian whose work forms the spine of dozens of major investigations, has written many tip sheets listing dozens of websites that are available in the IRE Resource Center. Relying on resources collected by an expert like Williams is one good way to help ensure that the sites you are visiting are legitimate. Another expert who uncovers and vets web resources is Paul Myers, a BBC researcher, who shares his knowledge on the Research Clinic website --- a site he's run for many years.

While Google has its limitations, using the Google Advanced Search can yield powerful results. Doing so allows a journalist to, for example, point all of the power of Google toward one specific web domain. This often finds many more results than using a specific website's own search engine. And Google's Advanced Search allows a searcher to specify the type of information sought, from specific types of data files to PDFs, PowerPoints and text files. This is a great way to find reports and databases.

Journalists who cover a beat should routinely use the Google Advanced Search to scour key websites on that beat (such as cities and school districts). Any investigative reporter researching a topic can use the advanced search to mine websites for reports, databases and even presentations prepared by group leaders that can provide invaluable insights.

When using any online search engine, it's important to understand Boolean Logic Search, which uses operating words like "and," "or" and "not" to help narrow down or expand a search. In most real-world cases, using "and" in conversation widens the subject being discussed. (When you tell the waiter "I'll have pie and ice cream," your dessert has just gotten bigger.) Not so when using Boolean Logic. Instead, using "or" in a search makes it broader. Searching for "federal or state regulations" on a topic would mean that the results include either federal OR state laws, or both. Using "and" narrows the search because both federal AND state regulations must be present to return results.

It is also important to know whether a search engine like Google has its own syntax. For example, doing an "inurl" search in Google looks for words included in the web address, as opposed to only in the text of the webpage.

Similarly, choosing the right key terms can make all the difference in narrowing your search. Your key terms should be words that appear in your investigation question. Think about what question it is you're investigating and pull words from that question. From there, you can also search synonyms or related subjects that may provide an additional pathway.

A novice searcher who cannot rely on an experienced librarian for help should look for training either in person or through online instruction, particularly through IRE and the National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting. Tip sheets from the IRE Resource Center will help any journalist get a jump start with conducting online research. (The tip sheets can be found at ire.org/resource-center.)

Because of the extensive information and databases on the web, a journalist can track everything from the service history of an aircraft that crashed to the history of supporters of a political candidate. And if it isn't online, then journalists should see if there is information, particularly decades-old information, available at libraries or through commercial vendors.

It's also possible that old information can still be obtained online. The best resource for this is archive.org, better known as the Wayback Machine. The site has been methodically collecting and saving webpages for decades, and often documents that were posted years ago are still obtainable through old versions of an organization's website. For example, one of the earliest versions of the IRE website on the Wayback Machine still has a working link to a news release about the resignation of a former executive director, a document that is nowhere to be found on the current website.


## Newspapers and Newsrooms

As the work at the Post and Courier in South Carolina shows, articles and other information published by newspapers can be great resources for investigative reporters. Almost any project should begin with a review of what's already been reported; there's no point in devoting time to a story if new ground can't be broken.

Previously reported stories can offer a goldmine of information unavailable elsewhere. For example, comments a politician made several years ago can become vitally important in a new context and can provide examples of hypocrisy or a change in belief. Reviewing previously reported stories is also a great way to find experts, skeptics, victims and advocates.

There has been a tremendous growth in the number of news sites that serve specific communities, from neighborhoods and workplaces to specific ethnic, cultural or religious groups. These can be found using free online search tools. It is also worth checking the public library in your community, or a university library, to see if it subscribes to one of several databases that collect news articles from various publications. For current articles, search engine options like Google News can be especially helpful.

Although many published articles are accessible through online services, older articles may have to be obtained through commercial databases, from behind the newsroom's own firewall or by reading hard copy or microfiche. In addition, many news organizations have changed their operating systems, making their online searches largely useless because stories from before the date the system changed are no longer accessible. In such cases, contact the news organization and ask for assistance.

Members of Investigative Reporters and Editors or other journalism organizations can use the membership directory to look for contacts at specific news organizations whose stories do not appear on databases. Another alternative is the distant local public library, where sometimes the staff clips the hometown newspaper, and then files the clippings under topic headings or digitizes them.

### Local News Sources

For many stories, local news organizations (newspapers and news websites) are the best place to begin your search for secondary source information. Often, only local news providers contain specific information about local banks, businesses and government leaders. Only local news sites have investigations that uncover misdeeds by businesspeople or public officials who are powerful in those communities.

In addition, stories in local newspapers have aided reporters in other states and at national newspapers in their investigations into, for instance, stories about government subsidies to farmers, workers' compensation and international con men bankrupting financial institutions nationwide.

Besides news stories, local papers sometimes publish routine information, such as wedding and funeral announcements, that can help journalists. (This is especially true for archival information, as some local newspapers no longer provide such services.) Birth and marriage notices can help determine relationships that might not surface otherwise. The birth notices tell of new parents with different last names: Carla D. Crane and Michael R. Storm are the parents of a 7-pound girl born March 2 at Regional Hospital. Marriage license listings serve a similar purpose, connecting Leslie Anne Logan, 30, of Columbia, with George Jacob Tice, 33, of Columbia.

Some organized-crime reporters have used newspaper announcements to establish undisclosed relationships within crime families. Journalists also use society columns telling who was seen with whom (such associations may prove helpful, as when the subject of an investigation denies knowing someone), rewritten news releases with details about individuals that give details of their job history or education, and advertisements placed by businesses and professionals that turn out to be false claims.

The local newsroom contains many other resources. As noted earlier, obituaries can be checked for names of survivors. Those names can reveal connections among people who previously seemed unconnected. Journalists covering city, county and state government can methodically collect information that shows relationships revealing nepotism and patronage. Information from obituaries is especially helpful when blood relatives have different last names. If the deceased is Harold P. Burnside, a powerful low-profile businessman, a journalist might learn from the obituary that Gayle O'Connor is his sister. That in turn might lead to a story about how O'Connor, as a zoning board member, cast the deciding vote on a rezoning request from her brother's development company without revealing her conflict of interest.

Increasingly, online-only news organizations serve local communities in addition to, or in place of, a local newspaper. While these sites may not have the comprehensive resources and announcements that a newspaper once did, they can provide unique local coverage.

### Legal Notices, Police Reports and Classified Ads

The paid legal notices in the back of a newspaper or on its website are all tips for possible investigations. These include notices on wills, divorces, child custody disputes, personal name changes, foreclosures and auctions of property, and government requests for bids on products or services. In addition, government reporters should read the legal advertisements every day. Often, they will find bids, purchases and projects they have never heard about. Reports about seized property and unclaimed property can also lead to stories.

Journalists should always check lists of alleged and actual lawbreakers. With knowledge of the community and a good filing system, journalists might recognize that the person arrested for shoplifting is the financial officer for a community college. Or that the person arrested for drunk driving is a school bus driver.

Classified advertisements also should be checked. Quite often, get-rich-quick schemes or escort services are listed. In fact, the classified ads of most publications --- hard copy or online --- can provide a road map to the illegal sex trade in any community, including telephone numbers to call.



## Additional Secondary Sources to Investigate

Outside of sourcing from news stories in an investigation, it can also help to look in places such as broadcast and cable, industry-specific publications, dissertations and theses and books.

### Broadcast and Cable Sources

The subjects of some broadcast news programs are sometimes indexed and available in databases, as well as on the web. One option is the Television News Index and Abstracts, run by Vanderbilt University's Television News Archive. For those interested in history, the Library of Congress has a collection that includes some news broadcasts.

It is often possible to get direct quotes from public officials or business leaders by watching already broadcast stories. In such cases, those sources may make promises or contradict current statements. Having that evidence on video can be powerful.

### Magazines and Newsletters

In addition to broadcast and cable sources, magazines and newsletters can also be helpful secondary sources. Periodicals come in many forms: general-interest magazines, newsletters, newspapers, trade journals, in-house publications, association newsletters, and local-, state- and federal-government-generated publications and websites. Journalists mining secondary sources should search listings of periodicals to get an idea of the range of publications that cover a specific investigation subject. Sometimes free access can be obtained through a university.

Editors of subject-specific magazines, newsletters and websites will have years of experience overseeing coverage of a topic. Editors of such specialized publications and sites are often generous with their knowledge, sharing industry norms as well as specific tips. There are thousands of newsletters, some so specific that they cover just one law or one agency --- focusing on everything from mortgage finance to mining to medical devices.

In her book "Poison Penmanship: The Gentle Art of Muckraking," which was republished in 2010, Jessica Mitford emphasizes the importance of specialized publications but distinguishes between those intended for broad public consumption (such as the Journal of the American Medical Association) and those not (such as Medical Economics), about which Mitford says, "In its glossy pages, you will find many a crass and wonderfully quotable appeal to the avarice of the practitioners of the healing arts." Discussing her investigation of the funeral industry, Mitford points out the gap between funeral directors' public in advertisements aimed at customers and their trade publications: "Here were undertakers ... talking to each other, in the secure belief that no prying outsider would ever have access to their inner councils."

Furthermore, sometimes industry newsletters are a secondary source for information as well. In some states, law enforcement newsletters contain officer discipline data. Journalists can regularly check such newsletters for officers from any local agencies, and these newsletters can be a useful source to build a database of disciplinary cases.

### Dissertations and Theses

University libraries house doctoral dissertations and master's theses, each of which are part secondary source, part primary source. Doctoral and master's students often receive unparalleled access to institutions because they are seen as nonthreatening. This means they may obtain access to records or sources that working journalists may not. A journalist should assume a thesis or dissertation has been composed on the project at hand, and then find it through the online versions of Dissertations and Theses Global published by ProQuest. Many universities also offer free searches of theses and dissertations, so if you're interested in a certain topic, look for schools that specialize in that kind of research and then look at what they make available.

### Books

Sometimes the key for a journalist is a good book because some of the best maps to good sources are in their appendices, bibliographies and endnotes.

Scouting through a library can work well only if the journalist understands that books on the same topic are scattered throughout the building. For example, books about drug abuse have a home base in the HV5800 classification (social pathology) of the Library of Congress system. But drug abuse books also appear in AS35 (societies), BV4470 (pastoral theology), LB1044 (teaching), QP37 (human physiology), several H classes (social sciences) besides HV, several KF areas (U.S. law), several R classes (medical) and under Z (bibliographies about drug abuse).

But perhaps a better way to find books illuminating an individual, institution or issue is to figure out the best search terms by using controlled vocabulary subject headings. A journalist investigating the death penalty will miss uncountable sources if he or she simply searches under "death penalty." The most useful material might be found under "capital punishment," "legal execution" or some other phrase.

Once a researcher has found some useful books, the reverse side of the title page can be checked for something called a "tracing," the subject headings assigned to that title. It can be used to search for other books that might be equally or even more useful. For example, the subject headings assigned to the book "Home Town," Tracy Kidder's profile of Northampton, Mass., include "Northampton (Mass.) --- Social conditions," "Northampton (Mass.) --- History" and "City and town life --- United States --- Case studies." The most systematic way of finding the right subject heading is to consult the five volumes of the Library of Congress Subject Headings, found in any major library, which contain more than 280 headings.

Entries for each term also include narrower terms. For example, the heading "divorce" yields a narrower heading "children of divorced parents." The heading "suicide" refers journalists to "youth --- suicidal behavior." The holdings of many libraries can be searched simultaneously through online public access catalogs, such as the OPAC Directory, published by Mecklermedia. (Database searches are covered later in this chapter and in [Chapter 4](#hou_9781319102845_ch04_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_U91XOgrhpc).)

To increase your chances of locating the most recent book on your subject, look beyond a library catalog search because it may take some time for a new book to be cataloged. A variety of websites list new and upcoming books, from large sellers like Amazon and Barnes and Noble to individual publishing companies.

When a journalist locates a useful book, it is wise to contact the author to request any relevant information that may not have found its way between covers and resides unpublished in a file drawer.

### Libraries

The Library of Congress in Washington, D.C. is the ultimate repository for secondary source materials. From the library's various reading rooms, it is possible to request a large percentage of books and periodicals printed in English and other languages.

The library allows online searches of its database, which guides access to secondary sources as well as many primary documents. It offers a vast selection of resources on a huge range of topics. The library's information office handles requests for interviews of subject-matter experts at the library. The library also offers an online search for reports from the Congressional Research Service.

The Library of Congress is not the only national library useful to journalists. The U.S. National Library of Medicine operated by the National Institutes of Health, and the National Agricultural Library operated by the U.S. Department of Agriculture, house collections and experts rarely consulted by journalists, with many resources available online.

Most federal agencies operate subject-specific libraries, which contain a mix of secondary and primary source information, in their Washington-area headquarters. In an Environmental Protection Agency library, for example, a journalist might find previously unknown, privately published periodicals, as well as obscure EPA-generated information about hazardous waste sites. Federal agency libraries are listed in the Library of Congress' online directory of government libraries. Specialized libraries can also be located through Information Today's American Library Directory.

No matter what library you visit, check with the reference librarian. Their expertise can help you find sources and leads you would likely otherwise miss.


## Secondary Sources for Finding People

Some of the resources listed above, such as obituaries, wedding and birth announcements can be great tools for tracking people down. Survivors are often listed along with spouses and hometowns, for example. If a subject has been listed over a period of time, it is useful to compare the most recent information with older versions. The older entries might list previous addresses or former spouses dropped from recent editions or might show a gap of time.

Biographical listings that are less obvious than a Who's Who list include local, regional and national social registers for the socially prominent; government handbooks, as well as privately published versions, with biographies of elected and appointed officials and their top aides; college reunion directories; and regional, statewide and national listings of members sharing a trade or profession, such as those kept by medical organizations and their parent, the American Medical Association. School yearbooks can also be a valuable resource.

What appears to be an uncontroversial listing might contain a key tidbit. Dates of birth are valuable information that can lead you to other public records and help confirm a person's identity. A name of a parent or former spouse can lead to a divorce or will.

Reference books that are directories can be located through Directories in Print, which may be available at a local library. One example is the Private Fleet Directory, covering companies that transport their own freight. Journalists can use such directories to locate competitors of a company and to learn about the industry.

Journalists should also check the American Reference Books Annual, published by Libraries Unlimited, which also has a searchable website that requires a subscription.



## Helpful Resources for Searching

In addition to the many available secondary sources that can build an investigation, there are also resources that may be less obvious, like searching bibliographies and secondary source databases. These resources can be helpful for taking information from one source and using it to expand your search to new avenues of investigation.

### Bibliographies

Searching published bibliographies can also be revealing. They list hundreds of relevant published works that probably would take months for a journalist to compile. After finding the Library of Congress subject heading, a journalist can plug it into these two formats in the library's main catalog: "SUBJECT HEADING --- Bibliography" (for example, "Education --- Bibliography") or "SUBJECT HEADING --- Geographic subdivision --- Bibliography" (for example, "Homeless persons --- United States --- Bibliography"). This process can open up many more source possibilities than a journalist might see at first glance.

### Secondary Source Databases

Thousands of U.S. and international daily newspapers are searchable in full text through one or more commercial computer databases and through newspapers' own websites. Database vendors that allow you to search for newspaper stories offer other resources as well.

Database superstores, including LexisNexis, NewsBank and Factiva, provide access to databases produced by thousands of organizations. Each superstore offers access to some of the same resources, but each has traditionally enjoyed exclusive access to other resources. Full-text databases are usually preferable to those providing citations or abstracts only. Full text means that a search will look for your search term anywhere in the document, not just in the citations or abstract. A passing reference to your investigative subject might even hold the key to the investigation. A journalist could mine full-text databases for references to the subject of an investigation, finding his name in footnotes to judges' opinions and near the bottom of a feature story; similar references in footnotes and bottom paragraphs have become starting points for entire new sections of investigative projects. One reference work, Fulltext Sources Online, helps sort out which resources are available online in full text.

The conventional wisdom that lots of information is free on the web is not a myth; it is just overstated. There are lots of websites with free versions of a few publications in full text, but the majority of periodicals still cost money to access a full text online. Using an electronic version of a print publication can be problematic. Portions of the print text (such as sidebars and graphics) are routinely omitted online, and sometimes entire years of coverage are missing.

Whatever the time or cost involved, learning about online databases is vital. They are often superior to print indexes, not only because of broader coverage and full text, but also because the searcher can combine terms, such as "organized crime" and "waste haulers," capturing only items mentioning those terms together, and thereby offering more focused results. Increasingly, information available from databases cannot be found in print indexes. Furthermore, these resources are updated more frequently than in print. That is true, not only for some secondary sources, such as privately published newsletters, but also for certain primary sources from government agencies.

# Chapter 3 Primary Documents


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_vbbI08uSU0" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Identify original records, known as primary documents.
- Search for and find primary documents.
- Use primary documents as a foundation for investigative stories.

</aside>

Authentic primary documents are the building blocks for high-impact, credible investigative reporting. With them comes the sense of discovery and unfiltered truth. Some examples of primary documents include the following:

- Memos
- Emails
- Letters
- An official social media account
- City council meeting minutes or recordings
- Transcripts
- Licenses
- Certificates
- Property deeds
- Loans
- Databases
- Video or audio recordings

Pieced together like a jigsaw puzzle, primary documents incrementally create a full portrait of an individual, an institution or an issue, or all three. In fact, one primary document may serve to reveal information on an individual's background, an institution's practices and the crux of an issue. For example, a tax lien (a government claim on a property due to a person or business's failure to pay taxes on it) might show increasing debt, reveal a business about to go under or provide key information on the issue of deteriorating finances of a community during a recession.

Throughout the following chapters, this handbook will list a wide variety of primary documents, but no reference book lists every document and database available at every government agency and in every library. Good reporters, however, will find primary documents pertinent to any investigative topic, obtain those documents and make sure they have an overview and details of a person or an institution --- both of which illuminate the issue being investigated --- before the first formal interview. In fact, the best interviewers often compose a dossier --- or a collection of documents about a particular person, event or subject --- on the subject or topic before they set up the interview.

But which documents or databases a journalist seeks first depends on whether the story, as noted, is mostly about an individual, institution or issue that requires defining the issue or target of the investigation.

Starting in the 1990s, the ability to background an individual or institution through primary documents and original databases rose dramatically. But it would be a mistake to assume that everything gathered through research is complete and accurate. Nor should investigative journalists assume they can forego checking for hard-copy documents that existed before computers or the web --- and still do exist instead of digital records. In addition, it is important to remember a database may be composed of only parts of primary documents because they often are a summary of all the information actually filed electronically or in hard copy.



## Document Guides

One starting point for pursuing a search of primary documents is through guides. Document guides are resources or organizations that file requests for information, organize and collate information that's already been requested and provide a repository of government documents. Investigative journalists anywhere might find a guide to local documents, especially in a newsroom or at a library.

One of the best sources for finding public documents throughout the U.S. comes from the organization MuckRock. This organization provides free summaries and links to public records guides throughout the U.S. It also collates Freedom of Information requests and the documents resulting from those requests. In addition, it has DocumentCloud, where thousands of journalists have posted millions of searchable public documents. MuckRock and DocumentCloud are an ongoing tutorial in the effective use of open-records laws and primary documents.

For more on how to utilize open-records laws in the U.S., a journalist can check the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, the National Security Archives and the National Freedom of Information Coalition. The IRE Resource Center also has many tip sheets on seeking primary documents through open-records laws. Internationally, the Global Investigative Journalism Network has a global guide to freedom of information laws.


## Primary Documents on People

Many types of primary documents can help you investigate different types of people. In most cases, paper trails and records can teach you a great deal about where these people have been and where they might be going. Lou Rose, author of "How to Investigate Your Friends and Enemies," said in his classic book:

> There are people I've constructed who I've never met. I can tell them, totally from the public record, when and where they were born, the color of their eyes, how many cars they own, what they paid for them, where they borrowed the money for them, how often they trade cars, whether they have been divorced, whether they've gotten traffic tickets, how long they've owned their home, the whole history of their real property, what their credit reports are.

During an investigation of an individual, a journalist should try to examine every public record concerning the individual, including property records, loans, property taxes, licenses, voter's registration, liens and lawsuits, including any divorce or probate court proceedings.

### Identification Numbers

There was a time when getting someone's U.S. Social Security number was key because it is a unique, quasi-national identifier. Growing concerns of identity theft and invasion of privacy have made Social Security numbers and similarly unique identifiers much harder to obtain, but an overall lesson learned from understanding Social Security numbers is that often numbers can help locate or identify people and entities. For example, states or cities may have unique employee numbers that are not Social Security numbers. In Connecticut, journalists at The Hartford Courant used employee identification numbers to link databases about regular salaries, workers' compensation overtime, time off and pension payouts to do a series of stories about an out-of-control system. They found employees receiving pay from as many as seven different departments, corrections officers filing for almost unrealistic amounts of overtime, state university coaches actually receiving salaries 80% higher than stated through special funds, and workers' compensation claimants receiving 120% of their regular salaries while out with minor injuries for years.

### Vital Records

Vital records include birth and death certificates, marriage licenses and divorce decrees. In the U.S., vital records are kept at the county level and in the state capital. Access varies from state to state, and because of identity theft, some states have severely limited access to these records. However, once obtained, these documents can provide a sturdy entry point into investigating an individual or relationships between individuals.

The U.S. Center for Disease Control and Prevention publishes a guide to birth and death record locations in the states. Private companies gather vital statistics in ways that make searching more efficient, and nonprofits also gather records. For example, the National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting periodically acquires death records from Social Security master files. Although NICAR is massive and flawed, it can help when a state will not release its death records. For searches on a few individuals at a time, an easy-to-use website for Social Security death records is Ancestry.com.

In some cases, journalists will have to use online database pay services, such as Intellius, for information on individuals, but a one-time search is generally inexpensive. If a newsroom has a subscription to a document-and-data gathering service like Accurint that can speed the process. Vital records also can be included in legal documents such as civil lawsuits.

As with all other documents, the information contained in vital records might be misleading. After analyzing death certificates, a reporter from Harper's Magazine gave an example: The doctor for the famous entertainer and pianist Liberace had listed subacute encephalopathy, a brain disease, as the cause of death, but the coroner, citing autopsy findings of a form of pneumonia found in patients with AIDS, rejected the certificate; the coroner said Liberace's doctor had known the truth all along. The lesson: Investigative journalists must try to verify all information in primary documents.

### Licenses

To practice a profession or open a business, an individual often has to get a license. This license typically contains an individual's address and vital record information. There are licenses to drive, to be a doctor, to sell property, to be a hairdresser and for many other occupations. Students in Columbia, Mo., once discovered how five massage parlors were fronts for a network of Seoul businessmen because the owners served as references for one another on business applications.

Furthermore, when a person buys equipment or land, there are often deeds listing not only the seller and buyer but also names of lawyers and administrative assistants who witnessed the purchase or deeds being signed. A network of businesses can be traced by seeing that the same lawyer and assistants notarized numerous deeds and loan documents.

### Confirming Document Accuracy

Be aware that although Social Security numbers and other primary documents are usually valid, sometimes they are falsified or stolen. At KCBS-TV, Los Angeles, Joel Grover and Sylvia Teague uncovered a criminal network established to sell legitimate California driver's licenses to recipients who then used them illegally to obtain government benefits, board commercial airliners and perpetrate illegal activities tied to the licenses' role as de facto identification cards.

Also, military veterans who want to enhance their records and nonveterans who want to pose as wartime heroes have found ways to obtain or create falsified service records. Glenna Whitley of D Magazine in Dallas and B.G. Burkett, a Vietnam veteran upset at the widespread fraud and how often those frauds fooled journalists, collaborated on a book to expose the situation, entitled "Stolen Valor: How the Vietnam Generation Was Robbed of Its Heroes and Its History."

It is frequently difficult for investigative journalists to establish the validity of what seem to be legitimate primary documents. But the illicit nature of such documents is something to which investigative journalists must always be alert. In past years, both CBS and the Los Angeles Times had major stories retracted because of unauthentic documents or questions about authenticity.



## Primary Documents for Businesses and Institutions

There are some similarities between primary documents for people and primary documents for businesses and institutions. For instance, a major gateway to tracking businesses in the U.S. is an EIN (employer identification number), which is issued by the IRS, similarly to an SSN. EIN numbers can be used to track not only individuals but companies and their dealings with governments.

Another primary document to track businesses is a license that can serve several purposes. A professional license for a physician and accompanying disciplinary records can reflect on the hospital where he or she is employed. In the massage parlor story noted earlier, primary documents not only established links between individuals but also the parlors themselves.

In addition, property records are useful not only for individuals but also for businesses and institutions. Many stories have been done on property owned by hospitals in neighborhoods around the hospital that have substandard or slum housing while the hospital waits to expand its own buildings.

### The Uniform Commercial Code

One valuable primary document that is not used enough for business is the Uniform Commercial Code filing. When an individual or company borrows money and promises to repay on schedule, a lender that wants legal protection should file a Uniform Commercial Code financing statement. UCC recordings are to personal property --- such as computers, yachts and automobiles --- what mortgages are to real property like land and buildings. They are called "uniform" because they are used by every state. The statement, filed under the borrower's name, is now kept at the secretary of state's office in each state and can often be found on their websites. UCCs filed in the past might also be found at the offices of the county recorder of deeds.

Journalists can use UCC statements to document who is doing business with whom. For example, while checking a rumor that a local politician voted to shift the city's money from one local bank to another in exchange for favorable personal loans, journalist Steve Weinberg found bank officials invoking confidentiality. Looking at UCC filings under the name of the politician turned out to be a first step in establishing a borrower-lender relationship.

UCC forms have revealed the address of a borrower unlisted in telephone directories; the names of a borrower's spouse or business partners; dates of transactions plugged into a chronology about a subject's rise or fall; and property owned by the borrower, listed as collateral.

UCC filings have also been used to track airplanes or yachts as collateral, and that can lead to property or registration documents. Journalists have tracked art fraud and inside deals among art dealers when paintings are used as collateral and have uncovered misuse of government funds such as loans from the Small Business Administration.

### Liens and Tax Documents

Borrowers --- whether they be individuals, businesses or institutions --- sometimes end up with liens placed against their property by the IRS or a state agency due to unpaid taxes. The lien is generally filed at the county or city recorder's office against a person who owes a debt, although in some states, federal tax liens are filed in one office and state tax liens in another.

Some counties have records of tax liens online or a tax lien database that can be obtained inexpensively, used often and updated routinely as a basic newsroom reference source.

When longtime investigative journalists Donald Barlett and James Steele examined 20,000 tax liens during an investigation of the IRS, they found a physician who owed more than \$900,000 in income taxes and an insurance executive who owed more than \$2 million.

Debtors often end up paying far less than they owe. Settlements for reduced payments are filed at an IRS regional office or IRS headquarters. Settlements, liens and UCC filings can lead investigative journalists to tax cases that end up in court where many more documents can be found. (U.S. Tax Court, tax cases in district courts and bankruptcy courts are treated more fully in [Chapter 10](#hou_9781319102845_ch10_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_P9YaXL0GUi).)

If a borrower fails to pay local property taxes, the property can be foreclosed on and sold at auction. Every week in local newspapers and on websites, unpaid property auction notices appear, listing the borrower and the lender. Sometimes the debtor is prominent in the community. In addition to providing leads to stories on individuals and businesses, the notices also can indicate whether foreclosures are on the rise in the community.


## Primary Documents From Databases and Other Sources

Finding primary documents can feel daunting or overwhelming. Many journalists may not know exactly where to start. Luckily, a number of databases that organize information on primary documents can provide a starting point for research.

### Institution Records

A journalist investigating an institution can examine records from government agencies, such as the National Labor Relations Board and the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, if the institution is a major business in town. For example, filings from the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission and records from congressional and state legislative hearings would be a good place to start when investigating the issue of nuclear waste disposal.

A journalist should consider which level of government records (federal, state, county, city or special district) to examine first. Having done that, an investigator should search all branches of that level. Every level has three branches: legislative, executive and judiciary.

### Using Government Databases and Documents

Understanding how to use government databases to locate primary sources can be the key to beginning or completing research. When a Midwest reporter received a tip from a college student that an immigration lawyer had left town with her money, he checked county records online. By doing so, he learned that the lawyer had recently purchased a \$305,000 home with a \$275,000 mortgage and more recently had taken out a \$40,000 second mortgage. He had also bought a \$40,000 automobile. It seemed he spent more than he could afford because a federal tax lien of \$36,825 had been placed on his home and a lien of \$22,504 on his business. Creditors had sued him; the names of the lawyer's own attorneys showed up in the online county records. Because the county's database only summarized court records, the reporter telephoned the court clerk, gave her the case numbers and arrived at the courthouse later that day to pick up the complete records. The reporter was then able to start reporting how the lawyer had stolen his client's money.

### Commercial Databases

Commercial database vendors like LexisNexis and Westlaw offer many primary documents --- from the legislative, executive and judicial branches.

Reporters who once faced the daunting prospect of traveling to records repositories in county after county, state after state, now find that much of what they seek is online. Journalists searching for documents from a distance because of time or money constraints can do just as well as if they were actually at the government agency.

To get an idea of what's online, reporters can go to sites like publicrecords.searchsystems.net/, which indexes all the public records it can find online. Journalists may still have to travel to examine the complete files, but online searches of both commercial databases and government databases provide enough information for them to make intelligent decisions about which trips to schedule.

Database vendors publish state-by-state lists of documents available online. Westlaw, for example, offers primary documents from Missouri that include Missouri Supreme Court rulings and cases, appellate court cases and attorney general opinions, public utilities reports, required filings by corporations and partnerships, insurance regulation cases, all state statutes, the status of proposed legislation while the legislature is in session, and current Uniform Commercial Code filings, which show some of the loans made to businesses. Even when it comes to the federal government, database services can offer many of these same documents on a higher level.

LexisNexis and its competitor, Westlaw, are known for their online state and federal judges' opinions. These include opinions not found in hard copy. In one year, the 6th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals issued 550 opinions in hard copy and digitally, but made 2,250 opinions available by computer only.

### Depository Libraries

Federal and state depository libraries allow journalists to find lots of primary documents in one location. The Hathi Trust Digital Library, for instance, lists computerized record sets from federal agencies, divided into dozens of topics. It makes available online "A Directory of U.S. Government Depository Libraries," which lists about 1,400 libraries. Depository libraries decide which documents to order from the government; those designated as regional depositories must carry certain items and provide interlibrary loan and reference services.

Every federal depository carries the federal budget, the census of housing and population, the Congressional Record, the U.S. Government Manual, the Federal Register, a monthly catalog of the Government Printing Office, subject bibliographies, the Weekly Compilation of Presidential Documents, U.S. Supreme Court decisions, the U.S. Code (laws divided by subject matter into 50 titles) and the Code of Federal Regulations (regulations meant to implement the laws).

Congressional hearings and publications available at depository libraries are especially valuable for journalists --- and not just those working in Washington, D.C. A hearing record might contain valuable information about a local program, for example. Depository libraries carry congressional committee hearings and reports in hard copy or on microform. A superb guide is the Congressional Information Service Index available through ProQuest. It allows investigative journalists to search by topic, congressional committee, institutions mentioned prominently or individuals testifying.

### Technical and Scientific Sources

Government technical reports are intended for experts but can be useful to journalists. Most are sponsored by three agencies: the U.S. Department of Defense, the U.S. Department of Energy and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration.

The National Technical Information Service offers millions of reports at any given time. The reports are divided into subject categories (such as health care and transportation), which in turn are divided into hundreds of topics. Many are derived from overseas research. Reports are available from the sponsoring agency, the funding recipient or from NTIS, at the U.S. Department of Commerce.

Journalists should also check the proceedings of technical and scientific conferences around the world; many proceedings break new ground. The Institute for Scientific Information publishes the Index to Scientific and Technical Proceedings online, covering thousands of meetings annually. Here is an example from the "Forestry" category: "International Symposium on Fire and the Environment --- Ecological and Cultural Perspectives." The specific presentations listed would excite any journalist interested in covering natural resources. In addition, ISI publishes Current Contents, which reproduces contents pages from hundreds of scientific journals.

### The National Archives System

Besides the Library of Congress and its satellite depositories, a government repository filled with primary documents is maintained by the National Archives and Records Administration, or NARA. Many of the records are recent and relevant for contemporary investigations and can be found at its website. The Guide to Federal Records in the National Archives of the U.S., from the Government Printing Office, can help speed a journalist's research.

While researching a biography, for example, journalist Steve Weinberg found documents showing how the subject --- then still alive and therefore not "historical" --- had sought favorable government rulings for his business ventures. One of those documents was a passport application that helped verify a crucial point made by a human source. The ins and outs of obtaining passport applications and other documents are explained in "Prologue," the archive's publication.

Longtime journalist Tim Weiner has produced revelations in books on the CIA, FBI and former President Nixon. On Nixon in particular, Weiner examined files from the Nixon White House, the CIA and FBI --- among many agencies that were made public in 2013 and 2014. Weiner sifted through tens of thousands of newly unsealed files from NARA, minutes of White House committees, transcripts of grand jury testimony by Nixon and many hours of recordings to paint a portrait of the former president that didn't have to rely on unnamed sources.

NARA also administers military and some civilian personnel records centers, separately from regional records centers, in St. Louis. When reporter Joanna Kakissis wrote to the National Personnel Records Center to obtain as much historical information as possible about a career military officer killed by a terrorist bomb in Greece in 1988, she received detailed records, including date of birth, dates of military service, names and birthdates of his children, name of his wife, educational achievements, military decorations and a photograph. If a journalist wants to explore records like these, a good place to start is "How to Locate Anyone Who Is or Has Been in the Military: Armed Forces Locator Guide," by Richard S. Johnson, an Army lieutenant colonel.



## Putting It All Together

Collecting primary documents is only the start of building an investigation. Skilled journalists also understand how to piece those documents together. During this process, the foundation of the investigation starts to take form and, as noted, the focus on the issue --- whether systemic failure of an agency, individual wrongdoing or social justice --- takes place. Understanding how to search for and use primary documents can be foundational to conducting your investigation.

In a real-life example of using primary documents, a classic investigation came about because journalist Alan Green volunteered at the National Zoo in Washington, D.C. When a zoo insider wanted to complain to somebody about questionable animal-handling practices at Reston Animal Park in suburban Virginia, that insider contacted Green. Curious, Green started poking around, eventually finding a dozen boxes of court records derived from the divorce proceedings of the animal park's former owner. Mixed in with all sorts of documents, Green found receipts showing that the National Zoo had sent surplus animals to this little-known roadside menagerie. So had other big zoos, such as Lincoln Park Zoo in Chicago, Illinois. Intrigued, Green decided to learn more about whether this discarding of animals was standard operating practice.

Green decided to maintain his focus on Reston Animal Park, in particular a pair of bear cubs housed there. He had located documents at the Virginia Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services showing that a Wisconsin animal dealer actually owned the bears. That dealer, Green discovered, had pleaded guilty to the federal offense of supplying bears to an exotic meat broker.

Suspecting the bears would be relocating after the Reston zoo closed for the winter, Green did some detective work. Learning the bears' departure time from an inside source, he followed the truck about 500 miles to northern Ohio. When Green looked for paperwork later about the bears' move, he experienced a defining moment in the project: There were no records about the move. Federal and state regulators knew nothing of the animals' whereabouts. Green wondered how many other exotic animals were disappearing from regulators' sight. For another three years, he pieced together the answers.

As Green's investigation expanded, he created his own database from two years' worth of advertisements in Animal Finders' Guide, a trade publication for the private exotic animal trade. But despite all the documentation, Green knew he would be missing the big picture without lots of help from human sources. State veterinarians told Green that, at most, 15% of exotic animals moving across state lines did so with the required paperwork. With backing from the Center for Public Integrity, Green turned his research into a book, "Animal Underworld: Inside America's Black Market for Rare and Exotic Species."

Additionally, in a more recent example of following primary documents, reporters Jaimi Dowdell and Kelly Carr did an investigation into the secret ownership of planes in the U.S. that allowed drug trafficking, movement of money by corrupt politicians and use of planes by terrorists.

The investigation included analyzing an aircraft registry database and tracing ownership of planes through primary documents of corporations' registration and documents on trusts that hid the true ownership of planes. In one case, the reporters found more than 1,000 planes registered to one P.O. box in a small town in Texas that did not have an airport.

From the primary documents they did numerous interviews to reveal that lax regulation and major legal loopholes allowed money laundering and drug running using planes. Dowdell said data was used to look for overall trends and to track changes over time. She said that when they found examples of planes that might be involved with "bad actors," they requested more detailed records.

Among the other records that Dowdell and Carr gathered were:

- Court records (indictments and complaints).
- Corporate filings in the U.S. and other countries.
- Business filings and public comments posted at regulations.gov, which helped locate key companies and players.
- County property records.
- General Accounting Office reports and department inspector general reports.
- Transcripts from congressional hearings.

The result was an extensive collection and records that formed a detailed document trail that revealed the deceitful web of companies and transactions. The following graphic shows what a possible document trail can look like using both primary and secondary sources.

# Chapter 4 Data Analysis for Investigations


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_sGx6aqygRH" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Find relevant databases for investigations.
- Apply the basic tools of computer-assisted reporting for short and long investigations.
- Keep up with the latest in more advanced tools, especially for unstructured data such as text and video.

</aside>

Whether the story is about transportation safety, campaign contributions, government contracts or international corporate corruption, journalists throughout the world are using databases for both quick and long-term investigative stories. Many of these are stories archived in the Investigative Reporters and Editors Resource Center and involve database analysis as an integral part of the reporting. Consider the range of stories:

- The New York Times analyzed databases extensively while investigating what would become Pulitzer Prize--winning stories on dangerous and sometimes fatal conditions for workers in the U.S. and for stories on railway crossing accidents.
- Several local newsrooms used the U.S. database on dams to show that old and potentially dangerous dams were subjected to little or no inspection.
- Reporters at Bayerischer Rundfunk and Der Spiegel in Germany found discrimination against foreigners, particularly with Arab and Turkish names, by surveying 8,000 landlords.
- The Atlanta Journal-Constitution used sophisticated techniques to download and analyze data from 50 states on disciplined doctors and found that doctors in every state had abused patients and, even when caught, still went unpunished.
- The Tampa Bay Times used statistical analysis and linear regression on data derived from dozens of records requests from databases to document how steady resegregation of Pinellas County schools left black children to fail at higher rates than anywhere else in Florida.
- Local broadcast stations and newspapers used databases in reporting on the ineffective placement of sirens that were supposed to warn of tornadoes.
- Analyzing over 2.6 terabytes of data in 11.5 million files from databases with over 400 journalists, the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists revealed in its series of stories, the Panama Papers, the extensive network of offshore companies that provided tax havens and laundered money for the powerful rich throughout the world.

Reporters now routinely use databases for tips, context or to provide more depth for an investigation. Database analysis --- also known as computer-assisted reporting (CAR) or data journalism --- is an integral part of investigative reporting and has improved the accuracy, credibility and breadth of journalism. Data analysis takes the reporting to a much higher level, although investigative journalists must still use other primary methods of reporting --- like interviewing and getting out into the field --- to judge how accurate the data are and to bring the human stories into their reporting.

Indeed, more journalists each year seek to learn the skills and techniques of data analysis. The National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting, or NICAR, a joint program of Investigative Reporters and Editors and the University of Missouri School of Journalism, offers a broad range of training at the university, in on-the-road seminars and at the IRE website.

Training in Europe is offered by institutes and journalism associations there. And the Global Investigative Journalism Network provides training at conferences and online.



## Obtaining Databases

Journalists today often acquire databases from government agencies by simply downloading the data in an easily usable format from government portals such as data.gov in the U.S. or from data collections such as those at the World Health Organization or the United Nations.

But if the online data is not easily available, or is offered in only a batch of a few records at a time, journalists have become adept at "scraping" the data --- that is, running a program that downloads all of the data and automatically puts the information into a database according to specific parameters.

In other situations, journalists consult a Freedom of Information Act or an Open Records Act, a law that requires the government to release the information in digital format. However, when no database yet exists, reporters can build databases themselves by getting paper or digital records and entering the information manually into a spreadsheet or other software.

To learn more about these tools, see "Data for Journalists: A Practical Guide to Computer-Assisted Reporting" by Brant Houston (Routledge, 2018).

### The Tools of Computer-Assisted Reporting and Data Journalism

There are three basic tools of CAR and an ever-increasing number of advanced tools. The first basic tool is finding and downloading data. The ability to search efficiently for databases pertinent to a particular story is a skill that can involve conducting searches using combinations of key words, web domains such as .gov, and file types such as xls (Microsoft Excel).

The second basic and most often used tool is the spreadsheet, such as Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets. Although often used for databases with numbers, such as budgets or salaries, spreadsheets are adept at filtering and summarizing data, such as discovering where the most traffic accidents are in a community or determining where the highest rate of accidents is.

The third basic tool is a database manager --- such as Microsoft Access, DB Browser SQLite or MySQL --- which allows a journalist to sort, filter, summarize and, most important, compare hundreds of thousands of records quickly. Database managers are absolutely necessary if a data set is composed of two or more files that are meant to be joined based on some kind of common ID. They also are needed to join data sets not necessarily meant to be joined. For example, criminal records have been joined to data sets of government employees --- based on name and date of birth --- to find criminals who have evaded agency screening procedures.

While those are the three basic tools, data visualization --- charts and maps, for example --- toggles the line between the fourth basic tool and the first advanced tool. Because data sets have become so large and visualization tools have become easier to use, journalists often turn columns and rows into visual representations. For example, a journalist can create a map of votes that better describes the geographic distribution of support for candidates in an election or, as in the tornado siren stories, show the poor placement of the sirens. To do these visualizations, journalists may use some form of Google Maps, the software Tableau or another software, ArcGIS Online.

A second advanced tool is statistical software --- such as the more advanced uses of spreadsheets or the free software R --- which helps journalists look for significant patterns in large data sets and for possible causes of events as well as to create visualizations. For instance, statistical software has been widely used to examine racial disparity in the denial of home loans.

A third advanced tool is social network analysis software --- such as NodeXL --- which helps journalists visualize connections and lines of influence among people or entities. It has been used to look at overlapping boards of directors of major corporations, charities associated with terrorists and the flow of money from political committees to candidates.

A fourth advanced tool is coding, which involves creating programs and algorithms to download data and place the data into a database, to clean data (eliminate errors), and to restructure, analyze and visualize data. A popular open source coding software used by journalists is Python.

### Finding Databases and Deciding Which to Use

Because governments have been using data for decades, there are databases waiting to be analyzed for almost any story that comes to mind. Here is a quick rundown of how to find out if a relevant government database exists for your story:

- Check the IRE Resource Center story index with the key words "computer-assisted reporting" or "data analysis" and the topic of your story to see if other news organizations have found useful databases.
- Check with the NICAR database library, which collects government databases for distribution to journalists.
- Check local, state and federal audit reports. Auditors frequently analyze an agency's databases as part of their audit and cite which databases they reviewed in the appendix of the audit.
- Check the weekly data updates on the Global Investigative Journalism Network, which cite data sets that can be applied to local or regional stories.
- Check the website of a government agency, particularly its area on data or statistics. This section often refers to databases and sometimes offers databases to download that might be denied to a reporter in person or via a request made under a Freedom of Information Act or Open Records Act.
- Check to see if there has been a recent inventory of the databases that a government administrative agency maintains. One inventory may be an agency's record-retention schedule. Agencies must keep track of the information they keep because laws stipulate how long an agency must wait before throwing out records.
- Check tip sheets at the IRE Resource Center on specific topics. They often list useful databases for covering a topic.

Another important tool in using databases and searching them is learning to use Google's advanced search tool. With this, you can specify the data type as "xls" or "xlsx" when you search for your key words. This will find Microsoft Excel spreadsheets associated with the key words. To find Microsoft Access databases, you can type the key word in the regular search box followed by "filetype:mdb." For example, to look for databases associated with hospitals, you would type "hospital filetype:mdb." The same approach applies to other software --- just use the correct extension.

### Other Key Information

After a journalist ascertains that a certain database exists, there are several steps to follow before acquiring it or while acquiring it.

First, the journalist should ask the agency who runs the database for the record layout, the code sheet and either the electronic or the hard-copy form that is filled out for each record. The record layout shows what columns (that is, categories) of information are included in the database. A campaign contribution database, for example, would have columns for names of contributors, their addresses, amounts of contributions, kinds of contributions, dates of contributions and the names of those receiving the contributions. The code sheet translates any codes used in the database. The actual form filled out for the database is useful for checking whether all the information was entered into the database.

Second, the journalist should ask to see the actual data on a computer screen or on a printout. Just because there are columns listed in the record layout doesn't mean the data was actually entered. The database will be worthless if the data needed for a story isn't there.

Third, the journalist should determine what format the data is in and the size of the database. Format is important because it tells the journalist how difficult it will be for a spreadsheet or database manager software to read or use the file. The size of the file allows a journalist to determine how large and fast a computer is needed to deal with the data analysis and also serves as a check on whether records are missing. If the database is supposed to be 10 megabytes in size and only nine megabytes are received, that is an indication records are missing.

After receiving the database and beginning the analysis, it's always important to do an "outer integrity" check: Compare the electronic records against a sample of hard-copy records or other studies using the data, and compare the totals of amounts in the database to totals in other reports. Also, other journalists or experts who have worked with the same database can explain its potential flaws or misleading data. Remember that every database is incomplete and imperfect --- just like human sources. One newsroom once received a database of U.S. defense contracts that totaled \$6.4 billion. But a government report summarizing the data had a sum of \$6.2 billion. When the reporters asked the government agency distributing the data about the difference, the agency said that some amounts had to be multiplied by −1 according to a code in another part of the database because the contract had been withdrawn. But the agency never told the reporters that until asked.

### Keeping Databases on Hand

Many news organizations have set up internal networked sites, or used Google Drive or cloud accounts, that make data available to the entire newsroom or to those trained to handle data. One challenge is making sure to warn reporters of limitations of a database, like whether it is incomplete or includes erroneous data. Another challenge is keeping the data sets updated. As an alternative to continuously maintaining databases, some newsrooms are setting up systems to provide databases on an as-needed basis for stories. When a newsroom does want to accumulate databases, it's difficult to choose among the huge electronic stacks that are available. Here are some general principles for managing databases:

- Community databases come from more than just local agencies. A multitude of regional, state, federal and commercial databases can be easily sliced to the local level.
- Whenever possible, a database should not be used for just one story. It will remain valuable for years, so it should be archived, be part of a reference library and, when appropriate, be distributed on the newsroom intranet.
- There should be a plan for the periodic updating of databases to keep them current and to provide the opportunity for trend stories.
- Journalists need to keep up with databases available online that can augment or update in-house databases. Make a schedule for downloading that data.
- When a database doesn't exist, it's reasonable for a newsroom to consider building its own from hard copy if the database can produce a minimum story --- that is, a story worthy of being published, aired or posted --- and be maintained in an archive library for other stories.

Some of the newsroom databases that have been built from scratch include information on homicide victims, shootings, police reports and government contracts and bids. If reporters do build their own, they should carefully consider what information is needed for the story and do test analyses as the database is being constructed. You don't want to spend hours entering data into a database and then discover you left out a crucial column of information for the story.

### Gaining Access

Elliot Jaspin, who taught many reporters how to use databases and did groundbreaking work while at The Providence Journal, developed a strategy for gaining access to databases that was adopted by those he oversaw. It consists of:

- Getting a hard-copy record or digital form for a record so you know it exists and what information it contains.
- Requesting and obtaining one electronic record.
- Then requesting a copy of the database of all the records since the release of one record has established it is public information.

Obtaining a paper record first is not always possible. A Houston Chronicle series about access told of a local police department that entered crime reports into computers with no paper backup. In the past, citizens could read (or in the parlance of public records "inspect) reports at no charge. But citizens now had to pay, sight unseen, for computer printouts. Another police department no longer separated public information from private in computer printouts. Each had to be reviewed by a police lawyer.

In addition, sometimes cost can still be factor. Requesting a breakdown of the costs of obtaining the information can help break through an agency's reluctance to release records. Costs involved now generally are just the labor (employee time) to retrieve and copy the records and legal review time. If the charges are too high that can be a story in itself. (The Department of Justice once asked IRE to pay more than \$2 billion for a database.) But remember most open-records laws provide an exemption for a journalist using the data in the public interest. So always request a cost waiver.


## Local Data Sources

Much of the information in this chapter is simply instructions on how to obtain in electronic form what reporters formerly acquired on paper. What complicates this process is the availability of the local data, which is sometimes withheld because of concerns about security or privacy.

States and many countries now have their open-records laws on the web, but a helpful overview guide is also available through the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press. (A world list is available on several sites including that of the Global Investigative Journalism Network.) The committee's website also has the complete Electronic Freedom of Information Act for the U.S. and sample requests for electronic information. The IRE website (ire.org) also has sample freedom-of-information requests, and the site MuckRock (see [Chapter 3](#hou_9781319102845_ch03_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_2xJ4HgTOBS)) is devoted to archiving requests and information from those requests.

Journalists and newsrooms can now break down basic local databases into different categories based on a beat or topic.

### Politics

A voter registration database is an excellent tool for finding and identifying people in the community. The tabulations of votes in previous elections are often kept in electronic form.

Campaign finance reports are critical. Make sure to get federal campaign contributions reports for local House and Senate seats in the U.S., which are available from both the Federal Election Commission and the nonprofit site OpenSecrets. For state legislators and municipal candidates, it's well worth collecting databases of contributions and expenditures. These databases help to trace the lines of influence and the players in a community, and they provide yet another "people finder."

Most communities require some officeholders to file disclosures on their business and personal finances to prevent conflicts of interest. Sometimes these are digital, but if not, a newsroom can build a database of these records.

### Business and Economy

International websites and federal and state governments in the U.S. provide an overview of economic data, but the only place that a local reporter can get the nitty-gritty data is at local offices. For a profile of businesses, a journalist or newsroom can begin with the database of business licenses issued by the city or county. Those records should have the names of the operators of the businesses and the addresses and types of businesses in the community.

To see loans obtained by businesses for equipment or other assets, look for the Uniform Commercial Code records, which are generally digital and at a state level (see [Chapter 3](#hou_9781319102845_ch03_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_2xJ4HgTOBS)). A database of building permits also helps gauge a community's economic health.

### Property and Tax Records

A database of property-assessment records can reveal whether the taxes are fair for each property owner. This database is critical for looking at the equity of the property tax system because it shows the value of a piece of property as assessed by government officials. Taxes then are based on that assessed value. If two similar houses have very different assessed values, then one taxpayer is being unfairly taxed.

The Chicago Tribune and the nonprofit ProPublica did a project on unfair property assessments in Chicago that had high impact and led to reforms and to the election loss of the county assessor.

Tax delinquency records should be checked twice a year. The tax collector's office keeps these records, which frequently forecast business or personal bankruptcies. They can be checked against public officials' names, too.

If a county maintains a database of tax liens, a newsroom should obtain it. Tax liens, filed by the IRS or a state against businesses or individuals, also forecast business or personal financial problems and offer numerous tips for stories.

### Crime and Guns

The FBI's Uniform Crime Reports is a standard database to consult and can be downloaded from the FBI site or other criminal justice websites. It should be supplemented by Bureau of Justice Statistics surveys and any local crime data sets that are available.

Other valuable data sets are incident (or call-for-service) reports that show from where police receive the most requests and what kind of requests, and also arrest records. The small nonprofit CitizenAccess-CU in Champaign, Illinois, used those data sets to show that although half the calls for service within the county involved domestic violence, few of those incidents led to arrests.

Many communities have a database of gun permits issued, and the federal Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms maintains a database of registered gun dealers. These databases can be cross-referenced with incidents of gun violence and with estimates of unlicensed gun activity.

### Education

Test score databases are available from both local and state agencies and are standard databases to have in the newsroom. School enrollment databases and school disciplinary reports are good for stories that show how a community is changing or stories on racial disparity. Personnel information about school employees (name, title, salary, date of hire and other information) can be coupled with their time and attendance records or with criminal records.

### Courts and Jails

Many courts have digital civil and criminal dockets. A database of dockets for the preceding year can show patterns of litigation. The personnel database for courts is another must-have. Databases on bail and sentencing can provide comparisons in how judges treat different ethnicities.

Local jails keep a database of prisoners and usually have data on race and ethnicity and whether individual inmates are awaiting trial or have been convicted.

### City and County Government

Every newsroom should have a database on municipal employees that includes name, title, gender, race, department, salary and date of hire. This database should be updated every six months.

A vendor database --- listing companies and individuals supplying goods and services to the municipality and revealing how much each of them was paid --- should be updated twice a year. It can be compared to databases on campaign finance or compared with lists of politicians' family members.

Databases on building and restaurant inspections should also be accessed or downloaded, along with actions and fines.

### Infrastructure

Two valuable databases available from the federal government are the National Bridge Inventory and the Dam Inventory. NICAR has these databases and can inexpensively slice and distribute local data from them. Annually, journalists and newsrooms acquire these inventories and create state or local subsets of these databases for stories in their areas.

In Missouri, a student journalist found by analyzing the dam inventory database that 1,228 dams in that state should have had Emergency Action Plans to help with the evacuation of people living downstream if the dams should fail. According to the state's own records, only 33 did. When the student tried to verify that at least those 33 actually did have the plans on file, only three were found.

The Associated Press has often used the National Bridge Inventory to identify potentially dangerous bridges and to look at the history of bridges that have been closed because of defects or even collapsed.



## State and Federal Sources

Moving from local to state and federal sources is actually a much easier transition than it may seem. There is a fair amount of crossover between areas of investigating that can occur on a local level but may also be covered on a state or federal level. For such cases, it's important to consider the scope of your investigation to determine the best way to search for data.

### Accidents

If there is a collision, journalists want to report on it. Both large and small accidents and collisions will frequently make the local news. For local stories on accidents, local broadcasting stations and newspapers can be examined for data. However, there are also several good federal databases to check for possible stories and to keep as references:

- *Highways.* Fatality Analysis Reporting System is the federal database giving the location of accidents and the number of fatalities nationwide. State highway patrols generally have more detailed information on both fatal and nonfatal accidents.
- *Aircraft.* The Federal Aviation Administration, the National Transportation Safety Board and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration all have reports on maintenance problems, airport dangers and snafus, and in-flight difficulties for specific aircraft.
- *Railroads.* The Federal Railroad Administration offers downloadable databases on its website that show where railway accidents occur.
- *Boating.* The Coast Guard and states keep a database with detailed information on boating accidents --- both in the ocean and on inland lakes and rivers in the U.S.

### The Environment

There are numerous environmental databases, but a good start is the Toxic Release Inventory kept by both state agencies and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency. The database offers information on estimated pollution by manufacturers. Journalists should assume there is a database --- and probably more than one --- for any potential story relating to the environment.

### State Data Sources

Even before looking for locally kept data, reporters should tap into key state agencies for online data and databases that can be brought in-house. Furthermore, not every state has opened all these records, but most of these databases with local information are available.

A number of state agencies have not only valuable infrastructure data but also databases on environmental data, corporations, loans, wages and medical facilities.

The Houston Chronicle has used Texas state environmental data and other databases to document, aggregate and map dangerous chemical spills throughout the region, especially after Hurricane Harvey, and to reveal deep flaws in the regulatory system.

### Demographic Data

Census data is a key component for a database library for community reporting and exists on a larger scale. The census serves not only as a way to better understand the composition and change in a community but also as a template for investigative stories.

The U.S. Census Bureau has a yearly survey to supplement its 10-year census report. The American Community Survey is a nationwide survey to provide communities with "a fresh look" at how they are changing. The survey collects updated information that includes age, race, income and home value, and that information can be critical in looking at social injustice.

Census data generally comes out in several releases, with more detail with each release, so it is important to understand what each release offers. For more detailed information on the census, go to the U.S. Census Bureau website (census.gov).

Journalists can use demographic information to gain greater understanding of information in databases on other topics. Reporters have matched lottery ticket sales to income data to show how the poor are gambling the most, house mortgage denials against racial data to show racial disparity, toxic waste sites against income data to show how toxic chemicals are being dumped in poor areas of town, and disallowed voter ballots against racial data to show that the oldest election technology is used in minority areas. To supplement federal census data, journalists should make contacts with state data centers and local demographers.

For profiling community demographics, the Census Bureau website provides other useful data sets that can be downloaded, as well as links to other sites.

### Other Federal Data Sources

There are many federal databases that can help journalists track the impact of federal money on the local economy. The Consolidated Federal Funds Report shows how much federal money is flowing into an area through expenditures, grants and loans. The Small Business Administration offers databases that show which businesses in a community have received loans guaranteed by the government and which businesses or individuals have received loans after a disaster. The Federal Emergency Management Agency, however, provides comprehensive disaster information. And the U.S. Department of Agriculture has many databases often overlooked by journalists.


## Unstructured Data (Text, Audio and Video)

While journalists have been working with structured databases (columns and rows) for decades, the analysis of unstructured data, such as text and video, is becoming more routine as the tools for working with it have become simpler.

A quick way to become familiar with the usefulness of this analysis is to:

- Spend time browsing DocumentCloud.org. This site allows journalists to upload public and business documents and run them through software that identifies patterns and key entities --- people, places and things --- that are repeated throughout the documents.
- Create your own Word Clouds, which show the frequency of words and phrases in speeches and transcripts.
- Study The Guardian's analysis and mapping of media messages during riots in the U.K.

To see how the turning of unstructured data into investigations is developing, a journalist can follow the investigative site Bellingcat or sites by Malachy Browne at The New York Times. Longtime investigative journalist Eric Lipton has said that Adobe Acrobat Pro DC is useful for optical character recognition tools, which index the words on each page, allowing journalists to search for key words. He also has tried services used by law firms for document discovery work that are useful for analyzing and annotating numerous types of documents. In addition, he finds DocumentCloud extremely valuable.

# Chapter 5 People Trails


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_jCic68yR95" title="Chapter Objectives">


## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Find sources through contacts, documents and social media.
- Evaluate the credibility of sources.
- Conduct better cultivation of sources.
- Interview more effectively.
- Deal with difficult and "off-the-record" sources.

</aside>

When people think about investigative reporting, they often picture a journalist surrounded by piles of documents or crunching data for hours on end. While all of that can be true, the document trail alone is rarely adequate. As many investigative editors and reporters have noted, it is crucial to call the people who maintain the records to determine what they really mean.

The same is true of data. A database can provide certain answers but often also raises more questions. Great journalism pairs documents and data with people who can illuminate the words and numbers, explain the context, provide anecdotes and fill in the blanks. Because interviewing is tied up with understanding human nature, there is always more to learn.


## Identifying and Evaluating Sources

A journalist can begin to identify sources in a number of ways. It's important to look at the people surrounding an individual or business, both presently and in the past. Once you find them, learning to evaluate these sources will begin to give structure to your investigation.

### "Currents" and "Formers"

Journalists frequently get advice and training on finding documents, but they don't get the training they need on finding sources. When beginning an investigation, a journalist can take one quick organizational step by dividing potential sources into "currents" and "formers."

"Currents" are those people currently dealing with an organization: current employees, current contractors, current consultants and so on. They are usually easy to find with online directories, phone calls, email and through social media. "Formers" are those who used to belong to or do business with the organization.

Experienced investigative journalists work to keep up with where "formers" go in an agency or business they are covering. They know that a "former" is much more likely to talk freely about their previous place of work, teacher, neighbor or partner. Looking for people who have had personal relationships with subjects of an investigation, such as former spouses, can also be very helpful. But finding "formers" can be difficult when you are starting an investigation of an agency or business you haven't covered before.

In each investigation, it's good to make a list of potential interviewees, both current and former. Let's say a journalist is looking into alleged misconduct by a company president. Among the potential current and former interviewees would be:

- Board directors of the company.
- Employees who have had routine contact with the president.
- Employees at past places of employment.
- Executives at competing companies.
- Labor union leaders, if there has been union activity.
- In-house and outside lawyers, accountants, stockbrokers and so on.
- Licensed professionals providing services to the company.
- Neighbors.
- Leaders at the subject's place of worship.
- Colleagues from clubs or lodges.
- Social friends.
- Spouses.
- Family members, including children, siblings, cousins, aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews and their families.

We will go into more depth later in the chapter for finding "formers," but good starting points are checking newspaper archives for articles that deal with the comings and goings of people in government and business, or government paper trails that can reveal things like past spouses or former religious groups. Newspaper business sections often are packed with such items. Check press releases and the websites from related agencies or businesses. Check industry magazines and accessible government records.

Social media can also provide many avenues for finding "formers." Job-focused networks like LinkedIn can be mined for finding former employees, and people often list their work experience in their biographies on social networking sites. Doing basic online searches for the name of a company or organization in question can turn up "formers," who often list past employment in current workplace bios. And of course check with "currents" on where the "formers" have gone.

### Whistleblowers

Whistleblowers are "currents" or "formers" who seek to bring attention to a problem or find themselves unwillingly in the spotlight because they know of wrongdoing. Their importance in exposing wrongdoing is recognized by a federal law, the Whistleblower Protection Act, and by some state laws. Many state legislatures have approved whistleblower laws protecting public employees; some states also provide protections for private-sector employees. The federal government and states have public records not only about the individual whistleblower but also about the alleged corporate or government transgressions that led to the whistleblowing.

Many whistleblowers are sincere and correct in their assertions, having become whistleblowers reluctantly because they could not change the organization through regular channels. Some are correct but are seeking either publicity or payback because they are disgruntled. Others are sincere but ultimately incorrect. Some may simply want money; whistleblowers can receive monetary awards under the federal False Claims Act if the government collects a penalty from the company.

If a journalist is not contacted directly by a whistleblower, he or she can find whistleblowers through other people or documents. If the employer is unionized, union representatives can serve as a contact between the journalist and the whistleblower. If the whistleblower has worked for the federal government, the websites of the federal Office of Special Counsel and the federal Merit Systems Protection Board are excellent sources to track the whistleblower down. An investigative journalist also can contact watchdog groups such as the Government Accountability Project or go to the organization's website.

Online chatrooms or other forums such as Facebook or LinkedIn can also be good places to find potential whistleblowers. Groups sometimes comprise disgruntled current and former employees or other individuals whose lives have intersected with the organization being investigated.

### Outside Experts

Journalists can benefit by interviewing outside experts who, unlike whistleblowers, do not have to worry about recognition leading to disciplinary action. Experts are especially prevalent at universities, which often publish directories of faculty members who are willing to talk to journalists. They often are what longtime investigative reporter Mike Berens calls "the detached source."

It is important to background outside experts to see if their knowledge is up-to-date and if they are respected in the field. Being an expert can be big business for some individuals. A journalist should check the résumés of and articles on these sources before interviewing them. The experts' postings on social media should be checked.

Several journalists have shared their indexes of reliable experts on the web, and members of IRE frequently share their experiences with experts with other members. Doing a simple online search can also help determine an expert's credibility and expose potential biases. LinkedIn can provide basic résumé material, while comment-heavy sites like Twitter or Facebook can help determine whether an expert has strong political leanings or other prejudicial beliefs.

When looking for experts, sources to consult include the Washington Information Directory from Congressional Quarterly Press and the Encyclopedia of Associations, covering about 23,000 groups, from Gale Research. Specialized groups have their own organizations, too, such as the American Society of Association Executives. PR Newswire runs ProfNet, a web service that is a network of organizations with experts. When a journalist posts a query to the system, it goes to thousands of universities, corporations, nonprofits and public relations firms. The service is free to journalists.

Most organizations of significant size have public information officers with their own specialties. Sometimes journalists get what they need from these spokespeople. But it is not always useful to go to the publicists or their supervisors because they and their bosses are frequently unaware of what is happening in the ranks.

Think tanks are other valuable places to locate experts, although think tanks can have an ideological bias, and that bias must be identified in any story. Many think tanks are located in Washington, D.C., including the American Enterprise Institute, the Heritage Foundation, and the Brookings Institution. A second geographic locale filled with think tanks is California; the Hoover Institution at Stanford University and the RAND Corporation in Santa Monica are examples. Some experts who reply to journalists' queries may have financial or ideological ties that journalists will need to evaluate. When experts are used from these think tanks, it is critical that the journalist identify the think tank's ideology or politics.



## Locating Sources

Once identified, sources must be located. When a source's whereabouts are not readily available, there are many resources that can help journalists in tracking him or her down.

### Online Tools

Nora Paul and Kathleen A. Hansen, authors of "Computer-Assisted Research: Information Strategies and Tools for Journalists," explain how much easier it is to locate a source, thanks to the internet.

- *Email*. They advise that finding an email address for a source or subject might actually be easier than finding an unlisted telephone number. Mining social media and online search engines can result in email addresses and other contact information for sources. It is also often possible to use a little guesswork to figure out what someone's email might be. Many people use their first and last name with popular email services like Gmail, for example. Create such an email, search for it online, and see what comes up. Additionally, workplaces tend to use the same format for emails (for example, first initial and last name@company name or first name.last name@company name). In addition, online search tools, both free and pay-as-you-go, typically retrieve email addresses and often phone numbers. One such tool is PIPL.
- *Discussion groups or listservs.* There is an email discussion list (or listserv) for just about every topic. If you cover a specific beat, subscribe to at least one listserv or discussion group on the topic. This is a great way to keep up with what experts are talking about, solicit information, advice or contacts from them and to generally tap into a broad expert base. You can find listservs at CataList, an online catalogue.
- *Personal and professional websites or blogs.* A journalist should check whether the source or subject has an individual webpage. More contact information might appear there. A journalist could start by simply typing the individual's name into a web search engine such as Google or the like.

### Directories

Journalists should always consult online phone directories. Phone directories are effective for finding many "formers," who leave jobs but stay in the same geographic area.

Because so many people use only cellphones, which are much harder to track, journalists must also frequently go beyond telephone directories to locate sources.

Online searches can often pull up cellphone numbers because people often list them on blogs or social media sites like Facebook.

Online "reverse directories" can also be invaluable. In addition to the traditional alphabetical listing, these directories list the names of adults at the same address, plus their occupations; telephone numbers in numerical order; and street-by-street, block-by-block listings. Some sites can even show the name and phone number of a potential source's neighbor. A Sports Illustrated reporter found a former neighbor of a subject through this kind of directory. The building manager could not help and tenant records went back only five years, but the former neighbor knew where the subject had gone.

### Workplace Directories

One of the most useful directories is the workplace directory. Whenever a journalist begins an investigation, he or she should get the in-house directory, whether it's for a business, government agency, church, college or social organization. Alumni directories are particularly useful.

Many, if not most, internal directories are in databases, and many are on the organization's website. In addition, it is worth using an advanced search function on a search engine in which you search for the place of work, the word "directory" and use the file type for a spreadsheet such as xls or xlsx. It is startling how many internal directories of agencies or businesses are posted in a spreadsheet that is publicly accessible.

### Personal Habits

Some seemingly hard-to-find individuals can be located through a combination of detective work and common sense. If somebody is known to fish or hunt regularly, for example, an address might show up on an application for a fishing or hunting license.

Most people live according to patterns. What are the potential source's habits? Does he frequent a diner at breakfast because of its famous biscuits? Does she normally have lunch downtown once a week with a business group like the Rotary Club? Can she be found at a particular church for Sunday services? What about the Tuesday night bowling league that his former supervisor mentioned? Maybe calling the labor union she belongs to will yield a workplace shift.

### Useful Documents and Records

Voter registration records can lead to a local address, as can pilot's licenses. Driver's license records used to be especially efficient for this purpose; driver's license databases are statewide (voter registration, in contrast, is county by county), and the majority of Americans age 16 or older have a license. The records are kept in the state capital; the agency in most states is named the Department of Motor Vehicles or something similar. However, in recent years some states have made the information in driver's licenses private.

Still, there are many potential alternatives. Pet licenses often contain otherwise unlisted numbers. When people are trying to escape detection, they sometimes leave clues by adopting a variation of their name, a nickname or their mother's maiden name. A Boston detective found a suspected murderer 25 years after the killing by starting with his given name, his nickname (Stacey) and his mother's maiden name (Griffin). The name the suspect was using? Stacey Griffin.

Combine the elements of the information you already know. No matter how common the name of the person you are searching for, you always have at least some information that can help set him apart, or you wouldn't be interested in him in the first place. Why are you looking for her? Because of the kind of work she does? Because she once lived in a certain town? Because she attended a certain school? Combining each piece of information can help quickly narrow a search.

Keep a list of potential leads as you find them, whether you know they are accurate or not. Find a name that may be someone's sister? Write it down. A possible club the person of interest belonged to? Write it down. You can later combine his or her name with each of these elements in new searches; some will bear fruit, some won't.

### Mapping Power Structures

Whether investigating an agency, business or institution, it is often clear who has ultimate authority in the organization. But sometimes titles and organization charts can be deceptive. In some cities, the mayor is a figurehead, with major employers calling the shots from their closed weekly luncheons. In some universities, the chancellor is a rubber stamp for the board of regents. A journalist should be able to discern and then penetrate power structures. To this purpose, some journalists have used social network analysis --- an accepted method of social science --- to diagram networks of power.

In fact, as long ago as 1976, IRE's Arizona Project got the assistance of a university professor to diagram the informal power structure of Phoenix, Arizona. Members of the Arizona Project team were reporters who came to Arizona to finish the work of journalist Don Bolles, who had been murdered while reporting on public corruption. The power structure of Phoenix actually called themselves the "Phoenix 40," since they were 40 movers and shakers in business and government who often kept a low profile. The project resulted in a 23-part series that exposed an extensive web of organized crime, public corruption and land fraud in Arizona.

An investigative journalist might compose profiles of individuals in the power structure as a way of educating readers, listeners or viewers. Reuters created an ambitious version of such a project when it created an interactive social media network graphic showing ties between China's ruling party and key business leaders. The project offered great insight into key ties in the country --- so much so that online access was blocked in China.

In each of these cases, analysis can be a great tool for reporting because it provides a map of sources to seek while at the same time pointing out their connections and place in the organization or network.

### Marginalized Communities

Finding and cultivating sources in marginalized communities can take extra thought and effort, but some of the most important and overlooked stories in any city can be found there.

If a group has been largely absent from the media, or only covered in certain cases (crime, controversy), its residents will be rightly suspicious of journalists. Maria Polletta, while working as a diversity and demographics reporter at The Arizona Republic, put together a series of recommendations for IRE.

Among her tips of what *not* to do:

- Practice "parachute journalism," when reporters show up out of the blue and expect people to open up to them.
- Act like you have nothing to learn from the community you are covering.
- Look for people to fit an ideal narrative.
- Ignore community members' efforts to pitch stories and share their concerns.

So what *should* you do? According to Polletta:

- Go to lunches, meetings, rallies --- any you are invited to attend.
- Consider doing smaller stories to show that you value what is important to the community.
- Seek out solutions-based stories, so that the reporting done is not always focused on problems without hope.
- Ask community leaders "Who else should I know?" Ask residents "Who in the community is important?" and "Who do you look up to?" and crucially, "Who doesn't represent you?"

Another useful way to identify these community leaders is to check to see if there is a neighborhood association or a Facebook page. These groups can help put you in contact with leaders and members of the community you're trying to find.


## Interviewing Sources

Once a journalist has located potential sources, the next steps are learning about the subjects, scheduling interviews in a logical order (if the schedules of those involved allow), and eliciting information on the record and condensing it fairly and accurately, all without burning bridges for future investigations. The more effort you put into building relationships and lists of sources, the easier your reporting will become.

When veteran investigative editor Manny Garcia was a new city hall reporter in Miami, he built sources by getting to know the rank-and-file people who worked at city hall. He asked about their families and told them about his own, he remembered key dates in their lives --- he treated them like people. As a result, some became trusted sources.

Garcia, in an IRE presentation, offers several tips for building that trust:

- Invest the time necessary to get to know a beat and potential sources.
- Treat folks well.
- Respect culture and nuance.
- Be polite. Be clear.
- Do not lose your cool.

### Researching the Source

An interview might focus on an issue, the person being interviewed or an institution or organization or a topic about which the person has special knowledge. Whatever the emphasis is, the interviewer needs to know as much as possible going into the interview. Reporter Topher Sanders discusses the importance of doing advanced research, to allow a reporter to make a personal connection with a source. "The goal is to discover interesting and important insights about your potential source and the place they work or worked," Sanders says in an IRE tip sheet. If possible, Sanders says, look for points of commonality you have with a source: a school or church connection, following the same sports teams, or other similar interests. Sander also says it's key to be genuine. "Folks smell fake a mile away," he says. He describes his own approach: "Respectful, courteous, inquisitive about people's lives, passions and pain."

Interviewers should know how to speak the language of their potential sources. If an outsider wanted to chronicle the work of journalists, it would be necessary to understand terms like "scoop," "lead," "managing editor" and "field producer." Similarly, an interviewer speaking to a physicist should understand quarks; one looking into airline safety should understand stress fractures before asking questions of an engineer. Good preparation leads to productive interviews.

When preparing for interviews, an investigative journalist should look not only for lies but also for neutral and positive information. Some information can be used as an icebreaker, to show something common between the reporter's life and the interviewee's. Knowing the interviewee collects stamps is an icebreaker, especially if the interviewer is also a collector. Or perhaps the connection will be that the subject's daughter is attending the same college the journalist attended two decades earlier. A journalist knowledgeable about the interviewee's employer or specialty (such as nuclear waste disposal) will impress the source, whose prior experience with journalists may have left the impression that they tend to be inadequately prepared.

The positive information might relate to the interviewee's accomplishments. An interviewer should work into the conversation the interviewee's receipt of a local award or authorship of a book. Compliments can be a door opener; furthermore, mentioning positive information is one more way to demonstrate preparation.

Documents discovered during preparation can come in handy during the interview. In John Ullmann's book "Investigative Reporting: Advanced Methods and Techniques," the now-deceased journalist Jerry Uhrhammer said that reluctant witnesses are more willing to talk if they are being asked about documents and what those documents mean. This approach has an added advantage: If you get good documentation, it often obviates the need for a human source, named or unnamed, in the story. This can appeal to someone who may have key knowledge of what's happened but doesn't want to be publicly associated with the story.

### Looking for Credentials Fraud in Résumés

An investigative journalist should check a source's résumé line by line for exaggerations or falsifications. Journalists have over the years found inflated or false résumés by school superintendents, academics, politicians and businesspeople. After Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans, reporters discovered that the head of the federal disaster agency had no real qualifications for his job and had even misstated his job title and duties when working for a city in Oklahoma.

Many assertions on a résumé can be checked easily. College degrees can be verified with a call to the campus registrar. If a subject mentions military service, a journalist should check the local courthouse for military discharge Form DD 214 when publicly available.

For example, consider an example in which a business reporter was covering the naming of a new CEO to run an international corporation. A tipster called to say that the man had lied about having a college degree many years ago. After checking with the university (inconclusive, as many years had passed), the reporter tracked down the professor who the man had long described as a mentor. The CEO was a good man, said the mentor, but no, he had never graduated. The story published, the corporation fired him and was thrown into turmoil, all because a journalist checked what its many human resources officers had not. The final kicker --- the man had claimed to have a divinity degree.

### Getting in the Door

When time permits, some journalists try the old-fashioned way to set up an interview: They write a letter. Often, though, it is a phone call, email or direct message via social media that begins the process. The timing of phone calls must be considered. Knowing a person's work hours allows a journalist to call at the start of the workday; if the person is known to be a workaholic, the journalist can call after hours when there is no assistant to intercept the call.

But even when a journalist gets through by telephone, it still may be an inconvenient time for a source. A journalist should quickly arrange another time to call that is convenient for the source or should arrange a meeting away from the source's workplace.

When using email, the subject heading needs to be precise so it won't get blocked as spam or ignored. It is wise to follow up the email with a phone call.

Writing letters is one way to overcome the perils of phone and email. Most sources see mail addressed to them. Furthermore, most people read their mail when they have time; letters are not as intrusive as telephone calls. They can reread the letter, too, mulling it over.

The wording of the letter, email or any written correspondence is crucial. It is important to be consistent because anything you put in writing can be shared with others or the public, via email or social media. Be sure that if anyone compares what you've said about your work, they will see that you're being straightforward.

While formulating your wording, journalist and author Steve Weinberg suggests avoiding words like "interview" and "investigation." "Instead," he says, "I use phrases such as, 'I would like to talk with you to fill gaps in my research.' When I have reason to believe sources are reluctant or hostile, I explain why it is in their interest to talk. That involves first guessing why a source would talk to any journalist, then figuring out why you."

On the matter of why a source would talk to any journalist, investigator Don Ray says, "She has to, it's her job; she believes strongly in her cause; she wants to defend herself against rumors; she hopes the exposure will help her career." People have many reasons to talk to journalists, sometimes just because they are curious about what the journalist is working on. On the matter of why a source would talk to a particular journalist, Ray lists these reasons: "She believes you really understand and care; she is impressed with your previous work; she believes you have information to share."

The timing and order of interviews is often important. In difficult stories, always assume every question and statement you make in an interview might be repeated to the next person you interview. If a source refuses to talk, then it's time to appear at the person's home or office --- depending on which is appropriate --- and politely explain why it is important that the journalist speak with the source. If a source is reluctant to talk, a journalist can use a third party to vouch for his or her integrity.

When contacted, some people may want to talk but feel they cannot for job-related or legal reasons. In those cases, an investigative journalist could ask where else information might be obtained. The source might reveal a specific person to talk to or the precise location of relevant files. The classic investigative book "All the President's Men," by Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein, has many examples of this kind of technique.

In any case, a positive mental attitude might tip the balance. Reporter Eric Nalder says, "As a warm-up, maybe during your morning shower, imagine a successful interview. Reporters who don't believe they will get the interview ... usually fail. As far as I'm concerned, no one should ever refuse to talk to me. It works."

The time and place of a potentially difficult interview are crucial. Meeting the source at home makes sense if the journalist knows that contacting the source at his or her workplace could be damaging to the source's career. But the office may make more sense if the source will be more comfortable there. If the journalist works in a newsroom, that might be the best place because a source won't be seen by chance in public with the reporter.

If complete confidentiality is an issue, the journalist should be careful to prevent being seen by others. It also means the journalist should be careful not to leave an electronic trace. A journalist should turn her cellphone off --- or better yet, not bring it --- when going to a confidential interview, if the subject would be placed at risk should the existence of the meeting become known. Cellphones leave geographic traces and these can be pinpointed, even when no call is made.

### Understanding the Source's Motivations

It is the journalist's responsibility to try to understand the motivation of an interviewee, whether a "current" or a "former." The journalist should also know what defects or shortcomings the "current" or "former" has, since any source may come under attack or criticism. After all, a "current" can be just as unreliable as a "former" who holds a grudge.

Mike Berens has put together a description of various kinds of interviewees and their motivations. Here is a summary of his list:

- The socially conscious source who wants to correct injustices and change the world.
- The disgruntled employee who wants to get even with the boss.
- The ignored source who can't get anyone's attention at his or her place of work and gets no respect from his or her colleagues.
- The stealth source who enjoys secretly brokering information to cause trouble at his or her place of work or in the hopes of gaining some kind of control over the reporter.
- The investment source, usually highly placed in an organization, who gives the reporter information in hopes that later the reporter will not do critical stories about the source.
- The detached source who is highly intelligent and generally looks down on the media but will talk to reporters who do their "homework" on the topic before the interview.
- The circumstantial source who is a victim or witness that a reporter needs to talk to in order to get the details of a story. This source may have little motivation to talk to a reporter except for the need to talk about the event or to be mentioned in a story.
- The belligerent source who hates reporters and has something to hide or believes the story may hurt someone or some entity that the source wants to protect. This source may believe he or she can bully the reporter into not doing the story.
- The principled source who talks to the media but whose loyalty lies with the institution he or she is a part of. Often, this kind of source believes he or she can hide the facts or can try to guide the reporter in another direction.
- The unsure source who doesn't know if he or she can trust the media but who will throw a reporter a small bit of information to see how he or she handles it.

There are strategies for dealing with each one of these sources, and a journalist needs to think about a source's motivation for speaking to the media, especially if the source approaches the journalist first. After judging the source's motivation, the journalist then can decide on the best approach to the interview.

### Preparing Questions

The order in which the journalist asks questions can be crucial, and so some time should be spent before the interview carefully sequencing the questions. Sometimes a chronological account helps fill in holes in the source's story.

Many interviewers move from neutral questions to more specific and worrisome ones. Good interviewers ask sources what they were wearing at the time of a key event, what they remember about the meeting room, whether it was storming or sunny outside. The details might help construct a compelling narrative when it is time to write, and they also allow the journalist to compare the source's story with existing facts. Asking for specific details can also put a source back in a certain place and time, helping them to share a deeper, more detailed narrative. Because answers sometimes open up new avenues of inquiry, the journalist needs to stay flexible.

It's important to remember that even if the journalist has heard serious allegations of misconduct, he or she should be careful to maintain a balanced and neutral stance on whether the allegations are true --- especially if they come from only one reliable source.

Leading questions are risky, but like most other risks, they occasionally yield results. Much like "yes" or "no" questions, asking an interview subject directly about allegations against them can lead to a direct denial or confirmation. In fact, it's often an issue of fairness. If a story is going to include allegations, it's necessary to ask directly about those issues.

An investigative reporter should leave no easy out for the interviewee. Rather than ask whether the subject fired the whistleblower, ask why. That puts the subject in the position of explaining rather than issuing a simple, and perhaps disingenuous, denial.

Some of the best interviewers use silence to their advantage. Most people feel impelled to fill a silence. It may be hard for a hurried journalist to slow down and shut up, but the payoff is worth it.

It is important to figure out what is not being said, as well as what is. Reporter Bruce Selcraig noted in an IRE article: "It might be significant if the mayor speaks to you routinely about his family yet never mentions his wife, or a successful businessman talks for hours without crediting his partner." Selcraig tested for lies in confrontational interviews. He asked questions for which there is only one true answer.

When interviewing sources who have good reason to be concerned about his questions, the late reporter Mike McGraw uses a three-phase approach. Here's how he described it:

- Phase one is the "time to lie" interview. He would use the opportunity to tell his subject (and related sources) about the proposed story, then ask what appear to be softball questions like, "How do things work here?" and "Is there anything wrong with the way things work?" The answer was often to the effect that everything is just fine, though McGraw suspected otherwise. "Okay," McGraw would say, "I'll do some checking and get back to you."
- Phase two is the "interim" interview, when McGraw would return with some harder questions. By then, he had worked hard to adopt his subject's angle of vision, to see how things look from that side of the desk. McGraw had thought about ways his subject could gain power or money by abusing the system. McGraw might have appeared naive, hoping to receive detailed responses from someone who can't help explaining himself.
- Phase three would be the "come to Jesus" interview, in which he let his subject know the results of all the research. "We both know a lot now," McGraw would state. "Have you told me everything you want to say? Are you sure that's your final answer to this question?" That approach often leads to confessions and then to explanations of the confession. Respect is part of the process. As reporter Pat Stith puts it, "You can say 'please' and 'thank you' and still ask, 'Did you steal the money?'"

### Structuring the Interview

The first few minutes of the interview should be devoted to breaking the ice. This is the time to mention the small-world commonalities that were the fruit of preinterview research, to talk about the institution or issue at hand. It is also wise to explain where the investigation is headed and to go over any ground rules previously discussed. Such a review can minimize misunderstandings later.

Impressions created during the small talk are important. Eric Nalder says, "Never approach your subjects as though they seem menacing or likely to clam up. Appear innocent, friendly, unafraid and curious. If you are a hard-boiled, cynical reporter who talks out of the side of your mouth, you will need acting lessons." From the other side, the journalist should never be seduced or turned off by first impressions. First impressions, after all, can be misguided.

While the source is talking, good interviewers make eye contact. They nod their head. They get their eyes off their cellphone or notebook. They make reassuring noises such as "uh-huh" and "yes," and maybe they say "fascinating" or "how interesting." When appropriate, they ask, "How?" or "Why?" or "What do you mean by that?" Eventually, they ask for documentation in an indirect way, such as "How do you know that?" They hope such questions will produce a response along the lines of "I am going to tell you how I know."

When an interview becomes unproductive, the interviewer must know how to get back on track. Officials and others who are regularly interviewed can be masters at changing the subject in ways a journalist doesn't recognize until the interview is over. When the conversation begins to veer off track, gently move it back to your question or topic. One way to do this is to relate something the speaker is now talking about to your original issue.

To keep an interview moving productively, the structure of questions is vital. If the journalist wants full-blown answers, open-ended questions should be asked, and those that can be answered yes or no should be eliminated. If an open-ended question ("What are your plans after you retire from the Senate next year?") results in a useless response, the journalist can ask a second question that forces the source to focus ("What would it take for you to return to teaching at Harvard?"). To elicit feelings, the interviewer can ask, "Describe your state of mind when you decided to quit." To get details for local color, he or she might say, "Take me back to the day before the murder; walk me through the scene." And "yes/no" questions have their place. When it's important to be able to state in an article whether a source would admit to something, comment on something or deny something, a straightforward question is best.

Sources are frequently comfortable sharing what they consider anecdotal information. While recounting anecdotes, an interviewee may reveal previously unknown information --- such as an ex-wife or a hobby. The journalist can use that information to identify additional sources to interview or new document searches to conduct.

The best interviewers suggest that as the interview is ending, it is always good to ask "Is there anything I didn't ask that you want to discuss?" Journalists also should ask whether there is anybody else they should interview and any books, articles or reports they should read.

It's a good idea to bring with you to the interview a list of the key points you need to cover or key pieces of information you must have. It's easy to forget in the heat of the moment, and that can lead to a sinking feeling when, while driving away or after hanging up the phone, a journalist realizes what they failed to ask.

One final note: Some journalists rely on conducting interviews by email, text or direct message as opposed to actual one-on-one conversation. Except for the most basic pieces of information, this is a bad idea. It's difficult or impossible to ask follow-up questions, it's impossible to read body language or to seize a moment and take an interview in a new direction, and in the case of top officials, a journalist doesn't even really know who is typing the responses.

### Conducting the Interview

Canadian investigative broadcast journalist Julian Sher talks about the importance of focusing on the subject and their surroundings when conducting interviews. Sher says that showing interest can create "a safe space" where a source feels more comfortable sharing. At the same time, he and other experts talk about the importance of picking up on key details in an office, home or other place where an interview is taking place. What can you learn about your subjects from their environments?

Veteran investigative broadcast journalist Tisha Thompson says that when conducting key interviews, she doesn't bring the camera into the room right away. She first meets the source, makes a connection and discusses the general parameters of the interview. That way, the source has a chance to relax a bit and feel some semblance of control before the process begins. Barging in with notebook in hand and a photographer hauling equipment can put an interview subject on guard.

### Taking Notes and Following Up

A journalist who is a weak note taker or who wants proof of every word said should record the session. Some sources are nervous about that and they should be offered a copy of the recording or be encouraged to do their own recording. In circumstances where recording is impractical or likely to stifle candor, the journalist should devise a personal shorthand that he or she can read later.

If the note taking still fails to keep up with the subject's words, the interviewer can ask from time to time for the source to repeat what was said, perhaps by saying, "That last point was so fascinating I want to make sure I have it word for word." Another tactic is to ask a throwaway question, completing the note taking from the previous answer while the source rattles on about an irrelevant matter. Or an interviewer can simply ask for a moment while they finish writing. This isn't embarrassing or a sign of weakness: Sources appreciate efforts at accuracy. Slow note taking can also provide the opportunity to ask the question in a different way or get a source to repeat an answer that might be different the second time.

If the interviewer expects the interview to be on the record but the source says it's off the record, offers not-to-be-quoted background or suggests some other unacceptable condition during the session, there are ways to reply. It's always best to establish the rules of the interview at the outset, and to make sure everyone is on the same page. "Off the record" or "on background" may mean one thing to the journalist and another to the source.

One possibility is to listen respectfully, then later interject, "That information you shared earlier is fascinating. Is there any way I can quote you about that?" Another possibility is to find out from the source whether documents or other people might reveal the same information. A journalist can also stand on principle, telling the source that everything must be on the record because that was the original verbal or implied contract.

When a story is likely to continue for months after an interview, the journalist should contact sources periodically to let them know about the story's progress and to check to see if anything has changed. This sometimes leads sources to call back with additional information.

A journalist should not hesitate to contact sources after a critical story. It shows that the journalist is not worried or embarrassed about the story and sometimes can lead to more information being gathered while the source berates the reporter or after the source cools down. Such a call might keep the source talking in the future. Interviewing is about building bridges, maintaining them and sometimes repairing them --- all to get as much information as possible.

# Chapter 6 Writing the Compelling Investigation


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_w2hw44Uof2" title="Chapter Objectives">


## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Prepare to write the story from the beginning of the reporting process.
- Consider how to best reach an audience.
- Select the right structure, from writing strategies to interactive graphics.
- Include literary elements such as narrative and dialogue.

</aside>

Telling the story is every bit as important as reporting the story. Many investigative reporters sometimes forget this and spend most of their time gathering information, giving little thought to the ways that information will be presented to the audience. That's a huge mistake. There are countless examples of brilliantly reported work that has been largely ignored and had little impact because the writing was impenetrable and unengaging, with little effort made at connecting emotionally with the audience or clearly stating why a litany of facts matter. And this is true whether in print, broadcast, an interactive online project or a podcast.

Investigative stories must show the impact of the issue on people, must speak to the issue or injustice in a direct and understandable manner, and still must unfold in a way that draws the reader or viewer into the story, no matter how complex it might be. Just as you gain perspective and insight into investigative journalism through reading each chapter of this book, so, too, must your audience feel guided through each piece of your investigation.


## The Fundamentals

Newsroom managers tend to divide journalists into "reporters" and "writers." Some journalists spend so much time on the reporting that when it comes time to write the story, they are fatigued or lost in too many facts. In contrast, some journalists may become so focused on reaching the end of their story that they miss key insights during their reporting.

While it is true that some journalists excel at reporting and others at writing, it's best if a journalist works at being as strong as possible in both areas. A journalist's reporting will improve by knowing that attention to the details of a particular person's behavior and mannerisms may be key to telling a memorable story. Similarly, a journalist's writing will be much stronger if it's based on thorough research that involves extensive interviews and reviews of documents.

Over the years, a series of adages has evolved in investigative journalism when it comes to telling the story:

- Start writing as soon as the reporting begins, whether that means jotting down a weekly memo or composing small sections of the story.
- Constantly work on summarizing your story in as few words as possible. Be able to tell an editor or manager what the story is about in 25 words or less. (Broadcast journalists are often told to tell what the story is about in three words.)
- If you have worked primarily in print, start thinking visually about the story from the day you start investigating. Involve photographers, graphic artists and web staff at the beginning of your investigation to ensure you collect the necessary material for a strong visual presentation.
- Read colleagues' and other journalists' work --- and read books on writing.

### Writing an Effective Story

One of the best overviews on writing investigative stories is the classic article by Ron Meador, then projects editor at the Star Tribune in Minneapolis, entitled "Remembering the Outrage: How to Stop Drawing Indictments and Start Telling Stories." The article appeared years ago in The IRE Journal. Here is an excerpt:

> Suppose you catch your state-run vocational schools putting out phony placement statistics, luring job-hungry hopefuls to courses that tout 90 percent success but deliver less than 50.
>
> You prove this using the schools' own data, taken from computer tapes and analyzed with expert help. You find juicy examples of duplicated and wasteful programs that violate state efficiency rules but serve the pork-barrel interests of legislators and local school boards. You publish this in the paper with sidebars and pictures and color graphics in the very week the legislature is reviewing budgets for all college and vocational programs. And your story sinks like a set of car keys.
>
> Legislators ask the program chiefs a few questions and let the budgets go forward. Dozens of students write to thank you, and so do several academics, but nobody else pays much attention. The schools adopt a few internal reforms --- but also stop collecting the data you used to prove their misdeeds. Can you imagine how this might feel? I know because it happened at the Star Tribune, and it was my fault.
>
> By all the usual measures, the project was a very solid piece of work. The findings were important and presented authoritatively and were never challenged. The stories were clear and crisp even by the usual high standards of their author, David Peterson. Graphics and sidebars added depth and detail. Editors assumed that readers would flock to a series showing that a \$160 million-a-year, tax-financed educational system was ridden with waste and deception. But they didn't. Why? Perhaps because many of the stories ran very long, covering multiple topics, and included too little about individuals. The project took a tone of informing and educating readers rather than talking to them, provoking them, engaging them. The lesson of this experience? We must avoid the impulse to create stories that feel like indictments; we must tell stories.

#### Keep the Outrage in Sight

There was a time when the focus of investigative reporting centered on nailing the individual bad actor, but over time the emphasis of much of the best journalism shifted to dissecting failing systems. Sometimes we forget that real people are getting screwed, that other people are screwing them or letting it happen. We forget the outrage that first moved us, and we lose the aspect that can move our readers.

It is almost impossible to make readers, viewers or listeners care about an injustice without showing the impact of the misdeeds on an individual or group. When the Los Angeles Times documented the inappropriate actions of a University of Southern California gynecologist, the reporters documented his actions in great detail and also wove in the voices of women he victimized. The 2019 Pulitzer Prize--winning work had power both because of its specificity --- the reporting left little doubt about the doctor and his actions --- and because readers heard the voices of the students he violated. But the real power of the project was its documentation of the multiple ways that USC officials had been notified about the doctor's actions over time, yet had done nothing to stop him. The end result was work that nailed down the key points, sparked outrage among readers and focused attention on systemic failure.

#### Put People in the Foreground

Historically, there has been a tradition in project writing that put the issues and findings in the main pieces, and individual human stories in the sidebars. Editors' decision-making often reflected that thinking: This is a Numbers Story, this is the Policy Story, these are the People Stories over here. Numbers are essential, people are optional. When people show up in a Numbers Story or a Policy Story, it is usually as a three-paragraph anecdote leading off the piece or one of its subsections. The idea is to use a bit of pungent humanity to lure readers into the hard stuff. This sort of journalistic bait-and-switch is cheap and cynical. Readers deserve more respect. They care about issues but are impatient with abstraction. They want to see problems and conflicts in the flesh. They want to make a personal connection, to imagine how this outrage would feel to them.

Journalists must weave human experience throughout stories. The heavier and harder a story is, the more humanity it needs. Allen Short and Donna Halvorsen of the Star Tribune wrote a three-day series about predatory sex offenders and how the courts fail to protect us from them. The main findings were about numbers and policies --- high recidivism rates, lenient sentencing patterns, failed treatment programs --- but each day's presentation gave equal prominence to powerful portrayals of sex crimes, of the men who commit them, of the women and children whose lives have been shredded by rape. That approach required that each story have a unique identity. The goal was that readers would not lose sight, even for a moment, of the horrors being endured by women and children.

#### Telling Stories With Visuals

The role of visuals in journalism has grown exponentially. Initially seen as a way to enhance a story, graphics, photos and videos are now viewed as a way to *tell* investigative stories. Whole news websites are devoted to graphics and data, focusing on everything from politics to sports.

News organizations are creating interactive graphics that stand alone or carry a huge amount of the weight of an investigative project. Consider an award-winning project looking at hurricane deaths in Puerto Rico in 2018, a collaboration between The Associated Press, Centro de Periodismo Investigativo and Quartz that documents a much higher death count than the official government record.

A centerpiece of the project was a virtual, interactive "wall" with each brick containing the name of a victim and, when clicked, details of their death. The cumulative impact provides hard proof of the death toll and pulls off the difficult feat of providing both large scope and small humanizing details. The graphic achieves this impact in a way that narrative storytelling could not and exemplifies a case in which the best way to tell the story wasn't to tell the story at all.

The New York Times created a "visual investigations team" that incorporates photos, videos and graphics. Malachy Browne, a senior producer of the team, has led investigations that used video, social media posts and other material to build timelines that exposed lies and deceptions told by Syrian and Russian officials about the bombing of civilians with chemical weapons, the lies told by Saudi officials about the murder by an assassination team of a The Washington Post columnist, and the lies told by Nigerian officials that soldiers killed protesters in self-defense. This kind of investigation highlights the power of using timelines to uncover misleading and false statements.

While graphics and other visual elements can be difference makers, there are some caveats to consider. When graphics are good, they are very, very good. When they are bad, they say the same thing as the story, only not as clearly. Consider these two rules for graphics:

1.  Each one has to make its own point. The point must be sharp enough to be conveyed in a short declarative sentence. This sentence focuses the content and design of the graphic and often becomes the headline. Not "Patterns of Crime," but "Sex Criminals Commit Multiple Offenses." Not "Treatment and Recidivism Rates," but "Treatment Shows Little Benefit."
2.  Graphics must stand on their own, just like sidebars. They exist to make or develop their own point, which may have been mentioned in a story only briefly, or not at all. They are not there to say in pictures what has already been said in words.

#### Numbers: Less Is More

Data and statistics can be key elements of any news story, and the ability to be specific about a problem --- X cases of domestic violence were not prosecuted, exactly Y dollars were misspent by the city manager --- is often the difference between an interesting story and an investigation with lasting impact.

But numbers should be used judiciously, and it's crucial to use them correctly. Journalist Sarah Cohen, author of the definitive book "Numbers in the Newsroom" published by IRE, has produced tip sheets and other training materials to help journalists navigate numbers. Here is some of her advice:

- Readers want people, not numbers. When you can't report on people, at least keep the story short.
- Choose only the numbers that have meaning for your readers. The most effective writing comes from selection, not compression, of facts.
- Consider charting numbers instead of writing them. Removing them from text not only improves your story, it often makes a bigger impression on your audience.
- Pepper your story with just the right number in just the right place rather than cramming them all together. Use an anecdote, quote or observation to separate paragraphs with numbers.
- Recast as many numbers as possible in simple terms that remove their abstraction. Ratios, rates and rounding can help simplify numbers.
- When considering how to construct a sentence, remember that dates are also numbers. Compare these two examples: "Spending on redundancy research by the Office of Unessential Affairs rose from \$847 million in fiscal 2019 to \$1.26 billion this year, a 49 percent increase." (Four numbers with 12 digits.) Or: "Over the past fiscal year, the Office of Unessential Affairs increased spending on redundancy research by almost half, to \$1.3 billion." (One number with two digits.)

Using numbers in a story is much like using visuals: They must serve a purpose in adding substance or context, and they must be used in just the right amount.

#### At Every Step, Remember the Interested, Impatient Reader

Perhaps there was a day when we could throw our evidence of waste and fraud and wrongdoing before our readers like raw meat, then sit back and watch them eat. If so, it certainly is long gone. Readers are much more demanding now, and they're getting more sophisticated all the time. Readers are not losing interest in issues. Instead, they are losing patience with news organizations that force them to work too hard and make them care too little. Readers do not like dense, endless stories. They do not like graphics that read like income-tax forms. They do not like getting the same point over and over in a story, sidebar, graphic, cutline, blurb. They do not like to be lectured.

In addition, readers exist in a highly digital space. In a world of Twitter and Facebook, not only is it quicker and simpler for reporters and organizations to get their stories out on a large public platform, but it's also a lot easier for readers to fact-check and criticize.

There is no simple chart of knowing when to choose a specific narrative device for a project. The facts and the nature of the story should guide the form. One way to tell if you've made the right choice is to get feedback on a draft. Ask a half-dozen uninitiated readers to look it over and react. You can use people from around the newsroom or people from outside who can be trusted with the secrets. Odds are they will show you a lot of things that could be done better. This is not always welcome news in the end stages of getting a project ready. But if you are turning off your audience, it is better to know it before publication.

All of this advice may sound obvious, the kind of thing everybody already knows and everybody already does. But too often, journalists aren't following it. Ask anyone who judges investigative contests, and they will tell you horror stories of work that should have been great but was undone by boring writing, bad organization, a reliance on recitation of facts over emotional involvement or the tendency to assemble thousands of words when hundreds would have done.

Gathering the facts needed to nail down an investigation is hard work. Don't let all that effort be undone when it comes time to write.

### Getting the Details

An investigative story should answer the "so what?" as well as the who, what, when, where, why and how. The answers are usually in the details. The reporter and editor should think about collecting details, not only for their content, but also for how those details can illustrate the project's theme and make audience members angry, sad, relieved or more informed about a topic that touches their lives.

Throughout this book, investigative journalists are encouraged to notice details on their own, as well as ask questions of sources that will elicit details. Rather than ignoring the obvious, top investigative journalists are perpetually amazed by it. They collect details that nobody else does.

The famous police reporter Edna Buchanan of The Miami Herald once wrote about a man who had been murdered and whose body was dumped into the street by the driver of a pickup truck. It was only later, after publication, that Buchanan learned that the dead man was wearing a black taffeta cocktail dress and red high heels. When she questioned the detectives about why they never told her this, they replied, "You didn't ask." She started asking after that.

It's a simple fact, but one that bears repeating: Good writing comes from good reporting. If you are constantly observing --- what you see, what you hear, what you smell --- you will be able to provide the key small details that bring a story to life and put readers instantly in the scene you are setting.

Consider the use of detail in these introductory paragraphs by Ken Armstrong and T. Christian Miller in their gripping narrative "An Unbelievable Story of Rape," published by ProPublica. Neither writer witnessed the scene; the details come from in-depth interviews in which the reporters teased details out of their sources and visited the location:

> A little after 1 p.m. on a wintry day in January 2011, Detective Stacy Galbraith approached a long, anonymous row of apartment buildings that spilled up a low hill in a Denver suburb. Snow covered the ground in patches. It was blustery, and biting cold. She was there to investigate a report of rape.
>
> Galbraith spotted the victim standing in the thin sunlight outside her ground floor apartment. She was young, dressed in a brown, full-length coat. She clutched a bag of her belongings in one hand. She looked calm, unflustered. Galbraith introduced herself. Police technicians were swarming the apartment. Galbraith suggested that she and the victim escape the icy gusts in a nearby unmarked patrol car.

Details also help build trust with readers. Increasingly, journalists are linking specific documents to individual facts in their stories so that readers can see for themselves the material that backs up the reporting. Like The New York Times visual investigation team, some projects are constructed largely by piecing together these documents, building a case that is hard to refute.

Brant Houston, one of the authors of this book, calls it "investigation by annotation." A good example is an investigation by Lisa Chui when she was at the Chronicle of Philanthropy. Chui wrote an investigative story on the misleading statements that Project Veritas, a political organization posing as a nonpartisan nonprofit, made in its application for nonprofit status from the U.S. Internal Revenue Service. But she went further by publicly posting the documents she obtained under the Freedom of Information Act and highlighting the misleading or outdated information submitted in the application. She also annotated the document with comments about what was wrong and what the actual facts were. This approach told a second, more detailed story that allowed readers to follow along in the investigation.

### General Guiding Rules for Writing

Ken Armstrong offers a collection of great advice for writing the investigative story in a tip sheet he compiled for IRE. Before we dig into story structure, consider Armstrong's overall recommendations:

- **If circumstances allow, write a story, not an exposé.** Create suspense. Use dialogue. Shift perspective. Circle and loop. Heed Elmore Leonard's 10 Rules of Writing, particularly No. 10: "Try to leave out the part that readers tend to skip." If you're bored writing it, others will be bored reading it.
- **Pronouns can be good for you.** Lose your fear of first- and second-person pronouns (I'm talking to you). Sometimes you become part of the story --- or your news organization does. If that happens, shoot straight. Say "we." Pronouns are direct; to readers weary of institutional voice, they can be engaging and honest.
- **Choose wisely.** As a reporter, you have a choice. You can describe a debate, or you can inform it. Inform it. If you reach a bold conclusion, state it boldly. Don't tiptoe. At the same time, remember: You must earn the right, through your reporting, to speak with such authority.
- **Long live the nerd box.** Some readers want to know how you did what you did --- what databases you merged, which documents you consulted, how many queries you ran. Most don't. Put your sourcing and how-we-did-it elements in a "nerd box," displayed prominently. A nerd box provides authority and transparency without getting in the story's way.
- **Behind every dot on the map (or row on the Excel sheet), there's a person.** Don't let readers forget that.
- **Report for story.** Gather transcripts. (Remember: Dialogue.) Gather video and audio. (Everyone's recording everything these days, or so itseems.) Think in scenes --- and what you'll need to make those scenes pop.
- **Too few dimensions can make you a cartoon.** If you're serious about writing, don't limit yourself to investigative stories. Experiment. Write profiles, essays, hard-edged features, long-form narratives. This can help you avoid the trap of casting people in simplistic roles: victim, villain, whatever.

Keeping these recommendations in mind will help inform your writing and keep it strong.

### Writing From Chronologies and Outlines

As the details accumulate, an experienced journalist will find that building a chronology is an effective way to manage the information because it organizes information linearly and helps a reporter find connections that otherwise might be hidden. But a chronology is more than a way to keep track of information. It is also a writing tool, as The New York Times and others have shown. An investigative group called Bellingcat often relied on chronologies during its investigation into the downing of a Malaysian airliner over Ukraine.

No matter the investigation subject, an experienced journalist will start building a chronology from the first week. After each interview, the journalist decides what to include in the chronology and what to file away. A chronology should also include revealing direct quotations and relevant descriptions and other facts that might help make the story be compelling as well as accurate. Experienced journalists will use the chronology as a basic text as they write their first drafts to check the original thesis, identify gaps in reporting and look for new angles. This approach saves time by avoiding rereading massive files.

Writing a draft from a chronology or from memory, though, is no substitute for an outline for many writers. For some writers, outlining is an essential step before a single word of a story is written. After gathering notebooks full of interviews, piles of notes and thousands of rows of data, organizing can be overwhelming. Trying to write before putting all of that information into shape can be counterproductive.

One approach many long-form writers take is to go through all of that material and compile a master list of what they've got. It's even better to keep a running list as you report. Then, that list can be used to structure an outline. Group the parts that fit together, and then put those groups in an order that makes sense. Include key quotes, statistics, anecdotes, etc. Once done, the skeleton of the story exists, and the writing can proceed much more fluidly.

For other journalists, outlining can be an effective tool after writing a draft. Does each major point of the project deserve its own Roman numeral? If not, something is awry. Do the secondary points, designated by capital letters and grouped together under each Roman numeral, derive in a natural way from the major point? Do those secondary points fit together naturally in a grouping, or do they need to be separated, perhaps placed elsewhere in the outline?

T. Christian Miller of ProPublica is a big believer in chronology. He describes why in an IRE tip sheet:

> My favorite story organization structure is the chronology. If you can unspool a story in more or less chronological order, it helps the reader understand what's happening, it makes clear how one event is linked to another, and it makes for easier reading. In fact, I will invite damnation by saying that chronology is almost the ONLY way to tell a story of any length.
>
> Generally, I'll try to write a top to summarize the story and the main points. I usually try to find a quick, compelling scene to which I will return later, or do a simple hard news lead. Then I'll write the nut graphs, some key findings, and a quick set of responses to the findings.
>
> That all should take up about 10 to 15 paragraphs. By then, the reader will have figured out whether it's worth spending his time to dive in. After the top, I hit the brakes, and the story unwinds more or less along the timeline.

As Miller's example illustrates, having a timeline or chronology worked out before writing can be a saving grace when the time to write arrives. Once the story has been set up, the hard work of organization has already been done.



## Story Structures

While following chronology can be the right choice for writing a story, there are multiple ways to construct a story, even when telling the events chronologically. In her classic textbook "Writing and Reporting News," Carole Rich describes six different possibilities for story structures:

- The *inverted pyramid* organizes information from most important to least important. The advantage for audience members is that they receive the highlights quickly. The disadvantage is that audience members have little incentive to finish the story.
- The *high fives formula* is a variation on the inverted pyramid. Its five elements are the News (what happened or is happening), Context (the background of the event or trend), Scope (the local event as part of a national event or trend), Edge (where the news is leading, what happens next) and Impact (why anyone should care).
- What has traditionally been called *The Wall Street Journal formula* goes from the specific to the general. There is a soft lead, a summary paragraph, a backup for those two elements, supporting points, explanations and an ending that ties back to the lead.
- The *hourglass story* provides the most important news at the top, and then proceeds chronologically.
- The *pyramid structure* frequently is the most appropriate for an investigative piece, as it consists of a lead, foreshadowing, chronological storytelling and the climax.
- The *sections structure* is akin to a book with well-crafted chapters, each with a lead, body and kicker. At the end of each section, readers should be compelled to move to the next section. The beginning of each section uses a list technique to introduce and summarize key points.

Other structures, not specifically identified by Rich, are the functional and organic structures.

- *Functional structure.* An investigative reporter examining a massive institution like the U.S. Agriculture Department might dissect it function by function. For the Agriculture Department, that might mean Part 1 focuses on meat inspection, Part 2 on farm supports and Part 3 on rural housing assistance.
- *Organic structure.* Writing coach Jon Franklin says dramatic stories usually consist of three major parts. In an organic structure, the opening focus in Part 1 is the complication. The three focuses in the middle part describe developments of the main "character" as he or she tries to resolve the complication. And the final focus, in Part 3, is the resolution.

Whatever the structure of the in-depth piece, writers and editors must decide whether to present the material all at once or divide it into a series of articles appearing on separate days. As when deciding how to write a single story, content should determine whether a series is necessary. A series can be appropriate if the project involves separate ideas or components that make sense when packaged together. Each piece should be able to stand alone, unless it is a narrative. For example, for a project on redlining --- a lending process that can discriminate against people of color seeking home loans --- Reveal/The Center for Investigative Reporting produced a first-day project on the overall issue, and a second-day package looking at the unintended consequence of the lending process that led to gentrification, in which middle- and upper-class homeowners flood into historically low-income neighborhoods.

A series is not what a journalist should turn to just to fit in more stuff. When all of the material is tightly interconnected, it makes more sense to run a story with sidebars and to edit judiciously. Readers are discerning; if they start Part 2 and it seems like they're not learning anything they didn't already get in Part 1, they will check out.

### Focus and Resolution

Even the best outline cannot overcome fuzzy thinking. Journalists should hone their focus at the start of a project by writing and rewriting a one-sentence summary, or focus statement, of no more than 25 words. When Laura Sessions Stepp, at The Charlotte Observer, was trying to identify an appropriate lead for a series on occupational hazards in the textile industry, she found herself struggling. Her editor asked her to summarize the months-long project. "Cotton dust is killing people," Stepp said. She had just verbalized the focus, which eventually came out in print as "Cotton dust is a killer in Carolina's mills."

Inherent in any focus statement should be tension and resolution. The tension might be between individuals, between an individual and an institution (such as an employer), an individual and the larger society, an entrenched belief and a newly discovered fact, or what is being done versus what should be done.

Sometimes resolution is real, but other times it is an imposed dramatic device. Perhaps the best-known apostle of the complication-tension-resolution model is Jon Franklin, author of "Writing for Story: Craft Secrets of Dramatic Nonfiction by a Two-Time Pulitzer Prize Winner." According to Franklin, almost any compelling story can be described like this: "a sequence of actions that occur when a sympathetic character encounters a complicating situation that he confronts and solves."

Franklin suggested avoiding projects that lack a basic complication of significance to the audience. A stolen car is not much of a complication to a wealthy person who can pay cash the next day for a new vehicle. But it is a complication to a poverty-stricken, single mother who depends on the car to drop her children at day care before driving from one house-cleaning job to another.

Franklin also suggested that journalists avoid stories that have no resolution, but that advice is not realistic for many investigative journalists. After all, the discovery of safety violations in an apartment building may not have a resolution until several stories later. And then there may be resolutions to choose from: A corrupt city employee is removed from his position or a federal investigation is launched into the bankrupt property management business that left creditors impoverished.

### Leads: The Opening Sentences

When journalists are ready to craft their openings, they will find that sometimes hard-news leads are best on complicated projects. The veteran investigative journalist Paul Williams believed a hard-news summary lead worked on a complex project if an overwhelming revelation, supported by unequivocal evidence, stood out after the reporting had been completed.

Here's how the Tampa Bay Times opened its scathing investigation of one Florida county's public school system. It started by bluntly telling readers that in only eight years, the school board "turned five schools in the county's black neighborhoods into some of the worst in Florida." Then, it described how:

> First they abandoned integration, leaving the schools overwhelmingly poor and black.
>
> Then they broke promises of more money and resources.
>
> Then --- as black children started failing at outrageous rates, as overstressed teachers walked off the job, as middle class families fled en masse --- the board stood by and did nothing.

The story concluded by stressing the impact: Thousands of students are now paying the price.

But when that kind of straightforward lead is impractical, there are other possibilities that are generally viewed as softer leads: the descriptive, the narrative and the anecdotal.

- The *descriptive lead* paints a picture of what is going on. For example, it can detail the living conditions in a housing project by noticing the details such as peeling paint, vomit in the hallways and the occasional sound of gunfire.
- The *narrative lead* recounts the action of a particular person or an event, such as a terrorist trying to cross into the United States from Canada to carry out a bombing, and uses that narrative to launch the investigative story into a larger issue or subject, such as the terrorist group itself.
- The *anecdotal lead* might detail the story of an uninsured person who was denied treatment at a hospital to show in human terms the shortcomings of a health care system.

Leads that focus on a person can be anecdotal or narrative or both. The key element, however, is the use of one person as the connecting thread for the entire project. That is different from opening with one person, using her as a symbol for a few paragraphs, moving to the summary (sometimes called the "so what?" or the "nut") paragraph, and then dropping all mention of that person for good or until a tie-back ending.

Skillful project journalists can tell a sweeping story through a particular person by including context. As author Tracy Kidder says, "You pluck a guitar string and another one vibrates."

Whatever type of lead you choose, you can use Donald Murray's 30 questions (from his book "Writing to Deadline: The Journalist at Work") to gauge its effectiveness. Here are some of the most pertinent questions for investigative projects:

- What one thing does the reader need to know more than any other?
- What surprised me when I was reporting the story?
- Where is the conflict?
- What is the appropriate voice for this story?
- What point of view should the story be told from?
- Can the essence of the story be captured by an anecdote, image, metaphor, quotation?

For their Pulitzer Prize--winning investigation into toxic ingredients in medicine imported from China for The New York Times, reporters Walt Bogdanich and Jake Hooker combined several elements to draw the readers into the topic in a few short paragraphs:

> The kidneys fail first. Then the central nervous system begins to misfire. Paralysis spreads, making breathing difficult, then often impossible without assistance. In the end, most victims die.
>
> Many of them are children, poisoned at the hands of their unsuspecting parents.
>
> The syrupy poison, diethylene glycol, is an indispensable part of the modern world, an industrial solvent and prime ingredient in some antifreeze.
>
> It is also a killer. And the deaths, if not intentional, are often no accident.
>
> Over the years, the poison has been loaded into all varieties of medicine --- cough syrup, fever medication, injectable drugs --- a result of counterfeiters who profit by substituting the sweet-tasting solvent for a safe, more expensive syrup, usually glycerin, commonly used in drugs, food, toothpaste and other products.
>
> Toxic syrup has figured in at least eight mass poisonings around the world in the past two decades. Researchers estimate that thousands have died. In many cases, the precise origin of the poison has never been determined. But records and interviews show that in three of the last four cases it was made in China, a major source of counterfeit drugs.

### Middles: Flow and Momentum

Middles of stories often receive little attention, overshadowed by a focus on leads and endings. A punster might say that too many projects consist of a beginning, a muddle and an end. But it's essential that a writing project filled with dense information does not flag in the middle.

Part of the trick is grounded more in common sense than literary skill. For example, handling numbers with skill can keep a project from losing momentum. When discussing a federal agency boondoggle as part of an overall budget topping \$1 trillion, a writer should never assume that amount can be understood without help. Readers are much more likely to understand if the story explains that it would take 31,688 years to produce a trillion dollars at the rate of a dollar per second. If there is a 250-square-mile oil slick off the California coast, a writer can explain that the slick is about twice the size of San Francisco.

Using the Franklin formula, the middle of the story can be built around the actions that the main "character" (which might be an institution) takes on the way to resolving the complication, the changes in the character wrestling with the problem, the insight (perhaps a flash, perhaps a slow unfolding) that leads eventually to the resolution. Broadcast journalists, in particular, excel at building the story around a character.

One technique for maintaining interest during the middle portions is writing in scenes. Scenes that are done skillfully show rather than tell, entertain as well as explain, and involve readers or audience members as if they were there. A project about unsafe bridges could include a scene of a school bus filled to capacity crossing one of those unsafe bridges every day. To make readers or audience members feel as if they are on the bus, the reporter would make the trip multiple times before composing the scene.

Whatever techniques journalists use to craft effective middles, transitions are mandatory. Transitioning from sentence to sentence and from paragraph to paragraph is taught in almost every university journalism course and is illustrated in almost every book on effective nonfiction writing. Writers implicitly know why they place sentence B after sentence A, paragraph D after paragraph C, but the logic might be less obvious to the audience. Transitions make the logic clear. A transition can be accomplished by repeating a word or phrase in paragraph D that appeared in paragraph C. A transition can be as simple as one word (a connector such as "however" or "then") or as complex as several sentences. Each transition is a conscious act by the writer but should appear seamless and effortless to the audience.

Pacing is another key element in writing effective middles. Varying the lengths of sentences improves readability generally and can help set the appropriate tone for the events described. The lead-in to paragraphs describing an act of murder might consist of mostly long sentences (35 to 50 words) explaining the backgrounds of the killer and the victim as well as how they came together. But the paragraphs depicting the violent act will almost surely be more effective if the sentences are short (10 to 15 words). Many journalists read their work aloud because doing so allows a writer to hear what works and what doesn't. Reading out loud can expose run-on sentences, awkward wording, a reliance on bureaucratic mumbo-jumbo and more. Several editors have said they can go into a newsroom and pick out the best writers by watching whose lips move as they type.

Skillfully rendered dialogue is another tool for making an engaging middle. Using dialogue in a story can help set a scene, build a sense of storytelling and capture a person's natural speaking voice in a way that quotes given in answer to a reporter's questions often cannot.

When using dialogue, handling attribution gracefully is one of those "little things" that matters. When one source dominates an extended portion, "he said" and "she said" should be used as little as possible. If your source says during an interview that she hitchhiked from Connecticut to California at age 16 and that fact can be verified, there is no reason to write, "She says she hitchhiked." Instead write, "She hitchhiked." The same is true of factual material; there is no reason to write, "He says the condemned building has broken windows," unless the investigator is unable to verify that the building is condemned or cannot see that the windows are broken.

Foreshadowing and flashbacks are helpful but must be used carefully. Foreshadowing is a literary device that gives the audience a taste of what is to come, while simultaneously promising more than a taste later. Broadcast investigative journalists frequently use foreshadowing.

For example, in a story about a crime, a writer might show how Jill talked regularly about committing suicide; her cousin Betty preached against it. Angry about the preaching, Jill threw a wine bottle at Betty, narrowly missing her; the full bottle shattered against the wall, and Jill said something menacing. The writer could end that section of the story like this: "That was when Betty began to be scared of her own cousin." The audience ought to be hooked at that point.

Flashbacks, unlike foreshadowing, are rarely imbued with such drama, but they can serve a purpose. A writer might tell of a black man who has lost his job because of workplace discrimination despite his unquestioned capability. Throughout the story, the writer flashes back to the man as a child, struggling in classrooms where teachers continually underestimated his abilities.

Occasionally, straight chronology --- eliminating literary devices like foreshadowing and flashbacks --- is the best way to handle an investigative project. Many readers naturally think chronologically. That approach also has the virtue of making cause and effect clear because the cause appears unambiguously before the effect. Beware, however, of falling into a post hoc fallacy, where you assume (or suggest) that X caused Y just because Y occurred directly after X. Cause and effect is a way of emphasizing the "why" in the how, what, when, where, who and why formula of journalistic writing. But chronology can be effective even when there is no cause and effect.

### Point of View and Tone

Point of view has a dual meaning. It can describe the conclusions reached by the reporter after gathering information, but it can also signify from whose vantage point the story will be narrated. Point of view can be chosen before the reporter decides on the story's tone. Possibilities for tone include formal, conversational, dramatic, skeptical and ironic. Don't underestimate the importance of tone. Choosing the right tone means the difference between a reader feeling manipulated by an emotional rendering of a single mother's plight with cancer or empathizing through a more objective but honest portrayal of her.

### Endings

If possible, an ending should leave deep thoughts and emotions in the minds of readers, without editorializing or preaching. Some investigative journalists end with a punchy quotation from their notes, letting somebody else's words do all the work. Others work hard to close a circle: They begin the story with an anecdote involving one person. That person then drops from sight until the final paragraph when the character is resurrected as a tie-back to the lead. But this approach can be a crutch; it's a very simple way to construct a story and can feel like the device that it is if it's not done well. It's better to keep returning to the subject of the lead throughout the story; that way, it doesn't feel like a gimmick.

Some reporters work hard to find clever endings, reaching into their files for a moment of surprise. But too often they overreach or make mistakes in judgment. For example, in a project about the unjustly convicted that focused on a woman who advocates for their release, the writer saved a surprise for the end --- the woman had turned in her own son for selling drugs. He served eight years alongside some of the inmates his mother was trying to assist. Wow! But why did the writer fail to develop that point? Should it have been the lead rather than the ending? Probably.

The bottom line is that the ending of a story should not feel forced or phony. So don't try too hard. There's nothing wrong with a strong fact, or a good summarizing quote, or a forward-looking fact (a new program meant to address the problem the story focuses on). Save the "big wow" ending for instances in which you've got the facts to really make it work.

# Chapter 7 Ethics of Investigative Reporting


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_r7f3vDllEu" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Maintain accuracy and credibility while doing investigations.
- Apply the concept of line-by-line fact-checking.
- Deal with anonymous sources.
- Approach sensitive stories.
- Deal with some of the common ethical dilemmas in investigative reporting.

</aside>

Investigative reporting not only demands the highest standards of accuracy but also delivers more ethical dilemmas on a daily basis than almost any other form of journalism. This means that as investigative journalists gather information, they need to ensure they are investigating fairly, with factual accuracy, with contextual accuracy and without any ethical breaches. They must be sure that the story that eventually appears is fair and not libelous. But they also must be aware of how they report and how they conduct themselves during the reporting. As trust in the media has dropped and the public has grown more skeptical, the need for bulletproof reporting and strong ethical guidelines has only increased.

In journalism, solutions to many ethical lapses are covered by a consensus. For example, it is generally agreed that journalists should never accept tangible gifts, travel or free meals from sources of any significant value. It also goes without saying that journalists should never invent facts, quotations or entire stories. Journalists should not plagiarize or make promises to sources that cannot be kept. But many of the ethical challenges journalists face are more subtle.

Journalists working in newsrooms with ethics codes should review those codes, and if they have questions about, or disagreements with, newsroom-specific codes, they should bring them up with their managers. Journalists working in newsrooms without codes can read the model codes from the Society of Professional Journalists, the Associated Press Managing Editors or National Public Radio.
