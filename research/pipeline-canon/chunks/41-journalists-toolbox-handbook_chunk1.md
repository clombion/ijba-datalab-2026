## The Journalist's Toolbox and the Dawn of the Internet Era

There were no start-up incubators or "a-ha" moments when I founded Journalist's Toolbox more than a quarter of a century ago.

It just sort of ... happened. And over time, it evolved into the AI tools and training hub it is today.

It started in 1996, when Professor Neil Chase invited me to teach some "new media" labs at Northwestern University's Medill School of Journalism. I was only a year removed from graduate school at Medill and needed the money, so I took him up on the offer.

I didn't have much time on my hands. I was one of the founding editors at [ChicagoTribune.com](https://www.ChicagoTribune.com) back then, and we hard-coded web pages as content management systems were only a twinkle in some developer's eye. So, on Tuesday evenings, I would trek to Evanston and teach print and broadcast students how to build web pages, code and edit photos in Photoshop.

Later that year, I started teaching newswriting and reporting courses part-time at Medill. Classroom management tools such as Blackboard and Desire2Learn didn't exist in the 1990s, so I hard-coded a website that hosted my course syllabi and other class materials. I posted it on the open web so anyone could learn from them, not just my students.

One page on that site was named "Toolbox," a list of 10 links to crime databases, [OpenSecrets.org](https://www.OpenSecrets.org), Congressional Quarterly and a few other sites and databases that students could use for reporting news stories. I taught the intrepid sophomores how to analyze crime data in spreadsheets to write stories, track how their home-state senators were voting in Washington and, more importantly, how to use OpenSecrets to find what special interest groups were making campaign contributions to the senators. It combined public records and database reporting with the newest technology available at the time.

Over the next four years, that page of links grew to several hundred tools as students returned from internships and shared resources they picked up in newsrooms. I started searching for more resources in Yahoo and an upstart search engine at the time called Google.


<em>Figure</em> <em>0.1</em> Intro <em>Source:</em> Illustration by Billy O’Keefe


By the time I left Medill for a [WashingtonPost.com](https://www.WashingtonPost.com) fellowship in 2000, the single "Toolbox" page had grown to several pages with a few thousand links. I moved the pages over to a free Yahoo GeoCities server -- a place where many people launched their first websites -- and continued to update it. I added a header graphic and called it "The Journalist's Toolbox."

At the Post, I noticed the site would appear on reporters' screens from time to time. Search engines were indexing it, and reporters, editors and news librarians were bookmarking it after stumbling across the site in broad web searches.

"Do more," the reporters told me. "This saves us time." That's because the site was doing something an algorithm could not: It was built by someone who thought like a journalist. I had spent nearly a decade as a reporter, copy editor and web producer at the Los Angeles Times, the Chicago Tribune and the Post. I knew how to research stories. I knew how to find things online. The site started to come together.

By 2002, the Toolbox was attracting several thousand visitors per month. I bought the [JournalistsToolbox.org](https://www.JournalistsToolbox.org) and. com web addresses. I emailed the links to hundreds of journalists around the world and asked them to share it with their staff. By the end of the year, I contacted several journalism organizations to see if they wanted to buy the site. Back then, there were no start-up incubators or funding, and it was difficult to charge people to use the site because secure credit card software was in its infancy, making it hard to set up a paywall.

The American Press Institute (API) bought the site from me and agreed to pay me monthly to update the site. After five years, API sold the site -- and my services as editor -- to the Society of Professional Journalists (SPJ).

So what started as an experiment for a rookie college journalism professor has blossomed over the next two decades into an international brand. Today, the Toolbox features links to thousands of resources.

SPJ defunded my [JournalistsToolbox.org](https://www.JournalistsToolbox.org) work in May 2023, ending my 27 years as that site's editor. Since I own the Toolbox trademark, I launched the new site, [JournalistsToolbox.ai](http://JournalistsToolbox.ai), with a focus on how journalists can use artificial intelligence to improve workflow, debunk fake news and more. Launched in June 2023, its sole purpose is to help journalists ethically and effectively navigate the murky waters of AI. It offers more than 90 training videos on how to use digital tools. A twice-monthly newsletter shares resources tips and tricks with more than 7,000 subscribers. My \@itsmikereilley Twitter account surpassed 50,000 followers by 2023.

\* \* \*

So it only makes sense that *The Journalist's Toolbox* would become a book. Not just any book, but a handbook that journalism professors could use to teach students starving for digital skills. And a handbook that provides professional journalists guidance and examples of how to expand their digital storytelling.

## A Cautionary Tale: It's About the Journalism, Not Just the Tools

Mackenzie Warren wore a variety of hats in 23 years working at Gannett newspapers. Shortly before leaving for Northwestern's Medill School to run its local news accelerator program in early 2023, he oversaw digital training for Gannett's USA Today Network as a senior director.

At Gannett, Warren encouraged newsroom staffers to advance their storytelling through interactive charts and maps, social media graphics, data analysis, video and audio, etc. Not only did this improve the reader's online experience, it also increased the amount of time readers spent on the websites.

It's easy for journalists to learn a new tool or technique, then rush to use it in a way that doesn't advance the story. What good is an interactive map with one or two pinpoints in it? Or a bar chart with no disparity in the data? Poor-quality video and audio will turn a reader off quickly. None of it belongs in the story just for the sake of it.

Warren warns to avoid "tool soup, which is trying to put a pinch of everything into a story, for the sake of seeming thorough or well-rounded. Often one single tool alone is enough to tell a story at a higher plane. It's easy to go overboard and overwhelm the reader."

In other words, put the story first and foremost, then try to find the tools to tell it.

## Organizing Your Digital Life

For several years, I worked with Victor Hernandez, chief content officer at WBUR in Boston, to train journalists at national conferences around the country. We'd speak at NICAR, SPJ and other conventions about how to use basic tools without breaking your budget or consuming too much time.

We heard common questions from our attendees: There are so many tools on the market; which ones are the best for me? How can I find the time to learn so many different tools? What tools can make me more efficient?

So during our talks, Hernandez always suggested journalists use this simple approach when deciding what tools or mobile apps to use: (1) Pick, (2) Stick, (3) Dig and (4) Dump.


<em>Figure</em> <em>0.2</em> Intro <em>Source:</em> Illustration by Billy O’Keefe


::: section
### Pick

Choose an app that you think would work well for you. Before downloading, check the app's reviews, privacy settings and what data it can gather and share about you (more on that later in the book). If you think the app could be useful and fill a specific need, download it. If not, move on.

::: section
### Stick

Use the app once you download it. Get a feel for it. If you can use it in your daily news coverage, go for it. If it's more challenging to use, practice with it in your free time or try it for personal use. In other words, "stick" with it for a while.

::: section
### Dig

Really dig into what the tool can do. Give it at least two or three months. Think: Is this tool or app saving me time? How is it enhancing my work, if at all? If you don't have a good answer for either question, it might be time to move on to the next step.

::: section
### Dump

After three months, it might be time to part with the tool or app. They take up space on your phone or computer, and if you're not using it, why bother to keep it on there? So delete it and make space for other apps and tools. You can always download the app again later. Keep your logins handy, and if it's a paid account, make sure to end the subscription before you delete the app.

Hernandez said he tries to "keep an open mind" when trying new tech in his job at WBUR or just for himself.

"I am generally willing to test out something that comes highly recommended and may offer value to my personal or professional life," he said, "especially if it's a free app. At most, it'll cost me 10 minutes.

"I'll try just about anything once. But I regularly go through my toolkit to clean out tech that has gone a while without any activity or perceived value. Digital clutter is still clutter and I'd rather take back valuable storage that could be applied to future downloads."

He said it is sometimes helpful to look at the last used data for apps on your smartphone.

"You might be surprised that you actually haven't engaged with services that are occupying valuable space on your device," he said. "My personal expiration date for lack of activity is three months. But your threshold may be different."

Jeremy Caplan teaches at the City University of New York's (CUNY) Newmark Graduate School of Journalism and writes a weekly newsletter called "Wonder Tools." When Caplan tinkers with the hundreds of productivity and reporting tools he reviews each year, he examines four core attributes:

- **Quality.** "Will this tool help me do better quality work than I could otherwise, or will it will help others I serve -- students, colleagues and readers?" he said. "I also assess whether it will help me do different work -- like creating a different kind of writing, multimedia or data analysis than I might be able to easily do without it? For example, Descript is a valuable tool for me because it allows me to do audio and video publishing and multimedia editing I might not otherwise do."
- **Time.** "I ask myself: does this save me time? Does it solve a problem I have or reduce friction in my workflow? Time is precious, so I prize tools that help me work more efficiently. Examples include Raycast and Alfred, application launchers that reduce the amount of time it takes to do common things on your computer."
- **Cost.** "Subscription costs can quickly accumulate, given that I use dozens of tools. And given that my students and readers may not be able to afford many costly services, I keep an eye on costs."
- **Reliability.** "Services rise and fall online. Remember Peach? Plurk? Jaiku? Fridge? So I try to get a feel for how likely I think the tool is to stick around, how committed its team is, and how strong its foundation."

Caplan said he tries to use a tool a few times in multiple contexts to see how useful it is in the long run, usually over a period of at least a few weeks. Unlike some, he rarely deletes tools, because he envisions giving them another look down the road.

"That's how I end up with hundreds of apps on my phone and laptop, even though I use only 10% of them frequently," he said.

## The Digital Graveyard

If you haven't been there before, visit the website [StartupGraveyard.io](http://StartupGraveyard.io). The site tracks failed startups and tech products and provides so-called "autopsy reports" to help entrepreneurs avoid making the same mistakes.

"Thousands of technologies go belly up every year," Hernandez said. "Some of the once-bright technology stars that occupied valuable presence in my toolkit over the years but have since faded include Google+, Periscope, Videolicious, Mailbox, Vine, Path, Wildcard, Sunrise, Meerkat, Rdio, Sonar, Tweetbot, Hyperlapse and Foursquare."

Warren recalls the fanfare over MySpace, which News Corp. spent \$580 million to purchase nearly two decades ago.

"In 2006, it was one of the high-water marks in the world's enthusiasm for breakout stars among digital platforms," he said. "MySpace had a bigger audience than Google or Yahoo! Then came Facebook, which smashed it to pieces."


<em>Figure</em> <em>0.3</em> The Startup Graveyard


Fast-forward to 2022, when the audio streaming chat app Clubhouse came onto the scene and attracted large audiences ... at first.

"At a micro level, when I think of all the alleged next-big-things, Clubhouse symbolizes a lot of how hard it is to break the lock the big dogs like Meta, Alphabet and Amazon have," Warren said. "A lot of initial hype, some manufactured scarcity and then ... kind of crickets."

[Storify.com](https://www.Storify.com) was another tool that headed to the tech graveyard after enjoying great success -- *for a decade*. Toasted by journalists, Storify was developed by former Associated Press journalist Burt Herman when he was a Knight Journalism Fellow at Stanford in 2009. His concept was simple: Create an embeddable interface where journalists could pull from Twitter, Facebook, Instagram and other social channels and add text and headlines to create curated social media collections.

Storify allowed journalists to arrange social media posts chronologically or as a narrative. So instead of doom scrolling or searching hashtags, the audience could read social media for the first time as a story with context. It got to the heart of what journalism does: select only the best information from a flood of facts, and give the reader a well-rounded story.

My students and DePaul University and I were beta-testers for the tool in 2009--2010 and continued using it after it launched on the full market. We used it to cover breaking stories from the 2012 presidential election, the NATO Summit in Chicago, local elections, feature stories and many more.


<em>Figure</em> <em>0.4</em> Storify social media curation tool


Herman's Storify was lauded by news outlets all over the world for its ability to package social media posts in a more contextual way. Newsrooms used it to cover violence and protests, crime and other breaking news stories. The tool was purchased by LiveFyre in 2013, where it continued to thrive for several more years until it was shuttered in May 2018. In its wake, a tool named Wakelet has continued to fill the void for social media content curation.

Hernandez said "can't-miss tech" comes and goes for journalists, and they have to adapt as the industry changes. Many good apps and websites have failed, not because they weren't useful, but that they were mismanaged, struggled to find a market foothold or had other issues.

"It's been that way forever," Hernandez said, "and there \[is\] no worse feeling than when you fall in love with a technology solution and incorporate parts of your life around it because it proves to be such a vital lynchpin for adding utility to your life -- only to see it acquired and shut down. Or it evolves into something much different than you knew it to be, or the people behind it stop maintaining it and it slowly dies.

"We wish we could get back all of the hours and perhaps dollars we'd invested into making it work so well for us, and we can't help but feel burned."

In his newsroom, Hernandez cautions his staff not to get too fixated on specific tools because of their proclivity to become obsolete. Tools come and go, and technology only moves forward, not backward.

"The apps on our phone, the bookmarks on our browser, the peripheral add-on equipment that adorn our gear -- they all get replaced or simply moved to the trash bin eventually," he said.

::: section

## Tools and Reporting

Digital tools and smartphone apps are never intended to replace boots-on-the-ground field reporting. If anything, they enhance the reporting and editing process and make journalists more productive. For example, a tool like Otter.ai can transcribe a long interview in a matter of seconds, something that could take hours back in the days of microcassette recorders. The service, which offers up to 600 minutes of free transcription a month, is also cost-effective for small newsrooms that can't afford expensive manual transcription services or that make the reporters do it themselves.

Search tools alone save reporters thousands of hours in research time. What's taken for granted today would have been a luxury half a century ago. For example, one of the famous scenes in the movie "All the President's Men" shows Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein searching through thousands of public records in the Library of Congress in the early 1970s as the camera slowly pans out, telling us the reporters were working well into the night. Today, that same records search could be done on the Library of Congress website ([loc.gov](https://www.loc.gov)) in a matter of seconds.

The key is to make the tools work for you, not against you. Doom-scrolling social media and noodling around on apps can be fun on your own time, but are incredible time-wasters for journalists on the clock.

## AI Tools Are Game-Changers

Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools have existed for many years, but a flood of new AI tools -- ChatGPT-4, DALL-E, MidJourney, Google Bard and thousands of others began to flood the market in late 2022 and early 2023.

This book will explore how AI tools are evolving, as well as legal and ethical issues that arise from them. They're changing the workflow landscape for journalists, particularly in smaller newsrooms that need automation to put human resources elsewhere.

Caplan said journalists will capitalize on AI in five ways in the next few years, building on prior experiments as the tech advances:

- **Organizing.** Reporters gather huge amounts of information, and AI will increasingly help them find what they need using natural language queries. Without having to manually file or tag notes, it'll be easy to ask the system to pull up all relevant quotes, facts and data related to a particular person, topic or place without complex queries.
- **Converting.** AI can already help translate research and documents across languages. It will go further in translating complexity by summarizing and synthesizing the essential information. It will also help convert data into preferred formats and convert images and video into text and audio for assistive technology.
- **Monitoring.** Keeping tabs on government and corporate sites for changes is already possible, but the capabilities in the realm of monitoring will grow more robust, allowing reporters to monitor more subtle changes in environmental and economic data over time. The monitoring may expand to include noting changes in sound, light and water pollution, or even noting changes in popular sentiment as measured by facial expressions shared on social platforms.
- **Analyzing.** Reporters will use AI to take complex datasets and identify outliers, and detect subtle patterns in data that might not have been otherwise evident. AI may also be useful in the analysis of reader data. It may be noted, for example, that readers of a particular newsletter have a much greater interest in topic A than in topic B based on their open and click-through patterns. This kind of analysis may be useful for the marketing and monetization arms of news organizations.
- **Presenting.** AI will be helpful in generating charts, images, infographics and even audio and video versions of stories to expand the reach and visual impact of news -- which you will learn to do in this book. AI may also aid in the personalization of news, so that a reader can elect to receive story summaries when busy or an audio version when commuting.

::: section

## Teaching Digital Concepts, Not Just Tools

Digital trends are much more compelling for journalists than stand-alone tools. They have real staying power that can last years and decades. As we learned earlier in this Introduction, tools, especially apps, can be red-hot one week and a deserted wasteland the next.

This textbook is targeted to college undergraduate and graduate students and their instructors, as well as early to mid-career professionals seeking to learn digital journalism skills. I've taught digital journalism skills to three generations of college students, and I use a tried-and-true approach: I teach journalism concepts, not tools.

For me, tools are a vehicle for journalists in their reporting, not a means to an end. So when I teach, I use apps and other tools to expose students not just to technology, but to great journalism lessons.

My students learn by doing. They publish on the website, The Red Line Project (redlineproject.news), which I founded in 2012 while teaching at DePaul.

I combine old-school "shoe-leather reporting" -- where students observe, document, conduct interviews, etc., with "new school," cutting-edge mobile and digital storytelling techniques to produce data-driven and multimedia stories. Their stories are work samples that help them get internships and jobs.

I stress law and ethics in my tech-driven journalism classes. Students learning Photoshop must first study the National Press Photographers Association Code of Ethics. They apply those ethics to photo editing (are we manipulating the photo, or improving truth-telling of the image?) They also learn how to fact-check images and deep-fake videos that may have been manipulated.


<em>Figure</em> <em>0.5</em> Intro RedLineProject.news home page during the 2023 Chicago mayoral election


Any student who has taken a class with me has a strong understanding of copyright law, fair use and other legal issues that arise in journalism (libel, slander, defenses, recording interviews, etc.) They also are taught how to protect themselves if they are harassed online by trolls on social media or through other methods.

When teaching, sprinkle the ethics, editing and journalism into the software lessons, and you have a captivated audience of students. Play a long slideshow preaching ethics, and you lose your audience. My aforementioned approach with Photoshop and ethics is a prime example of my approach: Teaching strong journalism fundamentals in a modern way.

This approach proved beneficial at the start of the pandemic. After the outbreak, the University of Illinois Chicago moved all classes online through the end of the 2020--2021 academic year. This left students to conduct interviews over Zoom or Google Meet as much of the city was shut down. So rather than publish Zoom video interviews -- yawn! -- I switched the students to more audio-driven stories and taught them more audio editing, SoundCloud, Headliner.app and other tools.

We also used Videoscribe to create whiteboard videos as we had little, if any, B-roll available. We used those whiteboard videos to explain to readers how mail-in voting worked for the 2020 election.

So whether you are teaching digital journalism, studying it or expanding your tech skills as a professional journalist, I think you'll find the lessons and exercises in this book useful. Take advantage of the training videos, and you'll be building cutting-edge stories in no time.

I hope you enjoy reading this book as much as I enjoyed writing it.

In the 1990s, Minneapolis Star-Tribune reporter Chris Graves spent a lot of time with Minneapolis gang members and their families to get perspective on the high number of murders in the city. People on the streets started calling her "murder girl" and "Ms. Chris." How they lived and what Graves saw informed her reporting in countless ways.

"It provided a very different, rich and stark view of the violence on the streets but also how often people were just trying to survive and get out of the life," she said. "I am more of a street reporter than anything, and so I spent about 80--90 percent of my time out talking to people and knocking on doors."

Graves has sworn by a simple acronym during nearly three decades of covering the criminal justice beat for the Star-Tribune, the Cincinnati Enquirer and the Lansing (Michigan) State Journal:

GOYAAKOD -- Get Off Your Ass and Knock on Doors.

"You talk to witnesses, suspects and crime victim survivors," said Graves, a Pulitzer Prize winner for her work on the Enquirer's "Seven Days of Heroin" project in 2017. "Not only does it make for richer, more meaningful and more complete stories, it will also demonstrate hard work, which is valued by every cop or investigator I have known."

To be a good journalist, you have to get out in the field and report. Interview people. Go to the scene of the crime, the public meeting or the event and document what you see and hear. Your personal observation makes for better descriptive writing and context in your stories. It helps you take the reader to the forefront of breaking news. It takes them *there*, and builds trust with readers and sources.

But field reporting comes with a technological twist, particularly with mobile reporting tools. Reporters use technology to pull public records, analyze data, crowdsource on social media, shoot photos and video and post stories remotely from their phones.

An editor once told me the best tools a reporter has are the "two things attached to your head -- your ears." Combine that with a pen, notebook, smartphone and a few other apps and gadgets, and you can generate news in ways we only dreamed of less than two decades ago. The tools we explore in this book will complement the reporting, writing, editing and production processes.

## Working With Free Digital Tools 

Mackenzie Warren, former director of digital strategy at Gannett, offers young journalists four tips for working with free digital tools:

- **Be a reporter first.** This is the skill from which all else flows. Don't get caught up in the tools.
- **Be a reader second.** You know you spend 30 seconds or less on anything you encounter online. Channel your own reading behaviors to inform what you produce.
- **Tell one story at a time, or make one comparison (X vs. Y axis) at a time.** If you have to explain what your tool is telling the reader, you've made it more complicated than you need. Tools are there to simplify, both for you and the reader.
- **Quality over quantity.** Just as you don't need 50 photos to tell a story when five will do, you don't need endless maps, charts and graphs on a single story. Be selective.

"At Gannett, we focused on tools that helped improve the reader experience for people at different places in the subscriber funnel," Warren said. "For example, search and social were high-priority tools for reaching seldom- or first-time readers and welcoming them into the top of the funnel. At the bottom, our most loyal, longtime, paying subscribers demand depth and sophistication for the price of their subscription.

"Advanced data analysis tools that help make sense of complex, subscriber-only stories enhance the value of a subscription and improve our retention at the bottom of the funnel."

## Getting Organized: Establish a Digital Workflow

Graves, now a professor of practice at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln College of Journalism and Mass Communications, said there's no magic to setting up a good reporting workflow. The process is tried and true: Research before you go, and then research some more.

"I ask myself some basic questions before I go: What am I writing and why?" she said. "What do I want or need to get from this source and when and how are the best ways to do that?"

For longer stories, such as a murder case and trial that covered several years, Graves keeps handy a high-level timeline and short bios of the key players she's writing about. Both can make for good sidebars to the main stories, but it also keeps her focused and provides quick reference when writing updated stories over a period of months or even years.

Graves keeps a couple files for magazine pieces -- usually a spreadsheet of each of her sources, their title, phone number and email, when she contacts them and each time she follows-up. She also does this with longer daily pieces, but keeps the information in a Google Doc.

To stay organized, she builds a separate folder for images, maps, data, records, etc., so she can access them quickly either to embed in stories and as a reporting resource. She uses her photos as reference when she's writing -- to take her back to an area, to describe a scene or look closely at what she observed.

"I also almost always ask subjects to share their own photos so I can enhance or enrich my stories," she said. "I take photos or their photos and use them."

Graves said she's the first to admit she goes overboard with preparation and planning for field reporting. She packs the trunk of her car with supplies that can help her handle almost any situation while reporting in the field. She often travels with her University of Nebraska-Lincoln students when reporting domestically and abroad.

::: section

## What to Pack

First and foremost, Graves brings an iPhone equipped with a recording device such as [Otter.ai](http://Otter.ai), either on the phone or laptop. For extremely important interviews, she brings a secondary recording device as backup for her iPhone in case there's a glitch or the battery runs out.

She also packs two Jackery external batteries to keep her devices fully charged. At the very least, she says journalists should have a car phone charger for both the laptop and phone. When reporting in more remote areas, she keeps a small solar-panel generator in her car that can charge everything.

"This is overkill for some, but I have been saved more times than not while on the road reporting," she said. "I also carry an electrical strip that includes USB ports in my car that can plug into the Jackery and my car's battery if I need more outlets."

If recording for radio or a podcast, Graves suggests bringing a podcast recording tool, such as a Marantz recorder. This is incredibly important if you need clean sound for radio or podcasts, which you can almost never get with Otter or an iPhone. She also packs SD cards or an external hard drive to back up files; especially photo/video.

Graves also has the online AP Stylebook open on a laptop browser tab next to the file where she's writing, making it easy to look things up. She also suggests carrying several different versions of notebooks: a small one that fits in your back pocket (out of sight), a reporter's notebook and a Steno notebook. Make sure to bring several pencils and pens; pencils are incredibly important in climates with a lot of rain and/or cold. Ink freezes and smudges.

She recommends carrying a printed atlas or at least a map in case you're in a remote location where Google Maps doesn't work.

To stay organized, Graves suggests using a Google Drive file-naming system that makes sense for stories, photos and video. Be sure to check with editors for a specific naming system, but this is an example of a good one:

- Assignment\_(Name)\_Date_StorySlugNOTES
- Assignment_Names\_(Dates)\_Story slug

Another good field-reporting tip: Reporters covering disasters such as wildfires often struggle to find Wi-Fi access and can find a good signal at a Starbucks, even in the middle of the night when they're closed. Starbucks leaves its Wi-Fi on after hours and it's accessible if you pull up in front or work outdoors at a table outside the coffeehouse. It's been tested many times over the years by reporters who cannot boost a cell signal or get a hotspot to work.

## Get the News in the Lead

As students at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, Graves and I took an Advanced Reporting class in the fall of 1987 with two professors: Al Pagel and Dick Streckfuss. Their course was the make-or-break bootcamp class for college juniors in the 1980s. If you survived, you moved on to internships and glory. If you didn't, it might be time to change your major.

Pagel was a short, fiery guy who had a striking resemblance and appreciation for Ernest Hemingway. Streckfuss, Pagel's sidekick in the dual-lecturer course, was tall, lean and could fill a room with his voice. Pagel was a writing savant, a storyteller and former Miami Herald medical reporter. Streckfuss had a firm grip on usage, style and the mechanics of writing a good news story.

They were also pretty damn funny.

I sat across from Graves in the course, and marveled at how the two professors inspired us to "write drunk and edit sober" -- meaning we should take twice the time to edit and rewrite our work than it took to write it in the first place. It's a process Graves and I follow more than three decades later.

One day, Pagel was making a passionate point about "getting the damn news in the lead" of a story. To drive home this point, he asked Streckfuss to stand on a table and hold his hands together over his head.

"He's the bell tower," Pagel said. "One if by land, two if by sea."

Pagel went on to tell the story of Paul Revere, and how he broadcast the news that the "redcoats are coming" from town to town during the Revolutionary War. Revere was a good reporter. He got the news in the lead. Three words. Concise, and to the point. He dropped the news and rode on to the next town.

"Now imagine where this country would be," Pagel said. "If Paul Revere wrote news leads like some of you."

Pagel began to gallop around the room like he was riding a horse. He rode past Streckfuss, who was still on the table, and yelled, "I have some news" and galloped out of the classroom.

Our class was rolling in laughter but the professors, in their own comical way, had made their point: Get the damn news in the lead.

This is particularly true today, when writing for online, and especially mobile, audiences that have precious seconds to skim an article to decide if they want to read it.

That rule is one of nine that journalist and author Paul Bradshaw outlines so eloquently in his blog post, "Nine Common Mistakes When Writing for The Web and What to Do About Them."

1.  **Getting straight to the most newsworthy, interesting piece of information in your first paragraph.** This was Streckfuss and Pagel's point: Get to the point. Anecdotal leads work well for long-form stories or news features, but not for a breaking news piece. Focus your lead in 25--30 words. KISS: Keep It Simple Sweetheart.
2.  **Linking to your source whenever you refer to a piece of information/fact.** You've done all that research, now work it into the story by hotlinking to it. Write what you know (reporting/interviews) and link to the rest (background research).
3.  **Linking phrases, ***NOT*** putting in full URLs (e.g. "[http://university.ac.uk/report](http://university.ac.uk)" instead of "a report."** Hotlinking is a challenge for some journalists. Look for proper nouns, short phrases, descriptions and attribution as targets for hotlinks. Try to avoid linking an entire sentence; just four to five words, maximum.
4.  **Indenting quotes by using the blockquote option.** Have a great quote from an interview? Highlight it in your story using the blockquote option in your content management system. This is similar to a pullout quote in newspapers and magazines.
5.  **Using brief paragraphs -- starting a new one for each new point.** I employ the "1-Through-Five Rule" of paragraph writing: 1 idea, 2--3 sentences, 4--5 typed lines per paragraph. It reminds me to keep my paragraphs concise. This is important for readers who are looking at your story on a mobile device. Large blocks of text reduce the readers' ability to retain the information.
6.  **Use a literal headline that makes sense in search results and includes keywords that people might be looking for, NOT general or punny headlines.** Writing search engine-optimized (SEO) headlines can be a bit boring. But writing a short one (75 characters or less) with keywords will help drive traffic to your story. Structure them in a subject-verb-object (who did what) and not a label or title.
7.  **Split up your article with subheadings**. For longer stories, the subheads help the reader identify key parts of the article and break up the blocks of text. Listicle articles also do this well.
8.  **End your post with a call to action and/or indication of what information is missing or what will happen next.** This is key for audience engagement. Ask them a question at the end of the article and show them how to respond. Give them resources to find more information. Embed an interactive chart or map. Give the reader *something to do*.
9.  **Embedding linked media such as tweets, Facebook updates, YouTube videos, audio or images**. Leverage your digital platform by doing what a print publication cannot: be interactive. We'll explore tools to do this in several chapters of this book.

::: section
### Great Reporting = Great Writing

Another lesson Graves and I learned from Pagel and Streckfuss: Great reporting drives great writing. If you cannot research, pull public records, interview sources and gather information, then your story won't work. It doesn't matter how talented of a writer you are. If you cannot report, you won't have a story.

"This simply can not be overstated," said Graves, reflecting on the class so many years later. "It is as simple as it is complex: You can not write what you do not know, what you have not heard, what you have not seen.


<em>Figure</em> <em>1.1</em> Chris Graves (right) reporting in the field <em>Source:</em> Photo courtesy Meg Vogel


"Again, I could write chapters and chapters on this. If there is a secret sauce to this work it is: Deep, rich, emphatic reporting that is so hard it sometimes hurts. You can never know too much, you can never report too much."

That's why Graves lives by the "Get Off Your Ass and Knock on Doors" approach. Sit with people and listen, see where and how they live. Have them show you pictures and tell you their stories from their life.

"This is true of sources, too," she said. "Go to the morgue, ask to watch an autopsy. Spend a day in court or with probation officers on his rounds.

"I remember hanging out for a week or so with a child protection officer \[social worker\] on her daily rounds just a few years ago. I had *no idea* the amount of work and difficulties and situations they are in. I went with homeless advocates when they were doing the homeless counts and then went back again to talk to the homeless people to ask them their input on being 'counted.'"

## Public Records Reporting

Graves also has to research and request public records as a reporter. Court documents are public records, as are crime logs, zoning permits, nonprofit Form 990 tax documents and millions of municipal, state and federal documents that reporters can use to strengthen their stories.

Government agencies, such as your state's attorney general, EPA, secretary of state and local municipalities, are in charge of caretaking these public documents. The agencies have public information officers who are tasked with helping anyone -- reporters, attorneys, businesses, the general public -- acquire the public documents.

Typically, reporters will visit the agency's website to see if the records have been posted online. Some records are maintained in data portals, such as the City of Chicago Data Portal. These portals are rich in data and stories. They include shape files of bus and train routes, city council districts, police precincts, and other data that can be mapped. They house crime data dating back decades. They also include more routine information that can help with reporting: pothole repairs, restaurant inspections, towed vehicles, red-light and speed camera locations, public health data and much more.

Most sites make it easy to download the files you need. Others put them in HTML "tables" that require you to scrape them (see the training videos in [Chapter 5](#ch05.xhtml__15031-5909_ch005) to learn how to do that.)

But many government agencies aren't cooperative, and won't hand over the records through a portal or through an email or call to the public information officer. In that case, you'll need to file a Freedom of Information Request (FOI) with the agency. The process varies by country, even state, but most U.S. agencies have 30 days to respond to the request. If denied, the journalist can file an appeal or tweak the original request and refile it.

According to the U.S. National Archives, FOI (5 U.S.C. 552, as amended), provides any person with the statutory right to request information from executive branch agencies of the U.S. government. This right of access is subject to nine statutory FOI exemptions, which provide agencies the authority to withhold records in whole or in part. FOI requesters may appeal any such withholding, or other adverse decision, back to the agency, and may also file a lawsuit to seek redress in federal court. Before going to court, requesters are encouraged to contact the agency's FOI Public Liaison at any time for assistance, and to utilize mediation services offered by the Office of Government Information Services (OGIS).

There are many FOI form letters available with a basic Google search. You can download them as Word or Google documents and simply fill out the form and send to the agency by either email or registered U.S. mail. But there are some free websites that help reporters not only write the letters but track them; [iFOIA.org](https://www.iFOIA.org) from the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press is one of the best, as well as the Student Press Law Center Public Records Letter Generator.

Once you set up a free account on [iFOIA.org](https://www.iFOIA.org), you can use several pulldown menus to select the agency and letter you want to use. When filling out the letter, be sure to be very specific about the records you want and in what format you want them sent (Excel, Word, shapefile, etc.)


<em>Figure</em> <em>1.2</em> Triangulating journalistic sources: data, documents and human sources work in concert with one another when reporting a story <em>Source:</em> Ilustration by Billy O’Keefe


Be specific about the type of record you want, what dates the records cover and what the topic is. Simply asking for "all of the mayor's email correspondence" is too broad. However, requesting the mayor's emails over the past three years discussing the public funding of a new bridge with the city's chief financial officer is more specific and gives the agency a better roadmap to find the records. This typically cuts down on denials.

## Finding Expert Sources

One of the biggest challenges for young reporters is finding expert sources to quote in stories. Over time, you'll build out detailed lists of expert sources as you do more stories and interviews. Some reporters turn to colleagues for recommendations, or pull experts used in previous stories on the topic. But quoting the same people over and over doesn't serve your audience.

It's particularly important to use a diverse group of experts in your stories regardless of topic. Are you quoting mostly men? Women? What experiences or ethnicities have you talked to for a story? Getting a good cross-section of the community -- not just with people you're covering, but also the experts -- is the best way to reach a broader audience.

Many newsrooms now track diversity in their coverage. They look at cross-sections of the community, how they're quoted, where they appear (stories/photos) and that includes expert sources.

## Using Transcription Tools to Speed Reporting

As any reporter who used a tape recorder prior to 2007 will attest, one of the best digital tools ever created has been the transcription apps and sites. Tools such as [Otter.ai](http://Otter.ai), Descript and many others have saved reporters thousands of hours transcribing interviews.

Graves finds Otter particularly helpful when transcribing interviews for longer, in-depth stories where the interview could have been weeks earlier. Her interviews typically last a long time, sometimes hours, and she approaches interviews in a conversational way.

"I love to audio record my subjects to hear their voice again, the tenor and timing, as well as to get their cadence down when I am quoting them," Graves said. "Hearing my interviews again is a way to 'go back' to the time and place of an interview."

Adam Rittenberg, an award-winning national college football writer for [ESPN.com](https://www.ESPN.com) and ESPN+ for more than 15 years, said Otter's mobile app "has been a game-changer for me."

"I can record interviews and see a live transcription, and then refer back quickly for quotes," said Rittenberg, who works both on quick-turn daily stories and longer, in-depth pieces. "Although the transcriptions aren't always accurate, they provide a baseline and significantly reduce my time to identify and use quotes for stories, especially on deadline."

Rittenberg touched on one of the big drawbacks for using transcription tools -- accuracy. Reporters still need to take good notes and listen closely to interviews as the transcription software sometimes misspells words, skips words, etc.

Another drawback is security. Many of them offer free versions in exchange for sharing your data with third-party sources. This is particularly troublesome for investigative reporters who interview on-background sources or victims of crimes. The potential of the person's name or parts of the interview being leaked are realities.

This issue was chronicled in a Feb. 16, 2022, Politico post titled "My journey down the rabbit hole of every journalist's favorite app." Reporter Phelim Kine wrote, "We make privacy versus utility tradeoffs all the time with our tech. We know Facebook sells our data, but we still post baby pictures. We allow Google maps access to our location, even though we know it leaves an indelible digital trail. And even savvy, skeptical journalists who take robust efforts to protect sources have found themselves in the thrall of Otter, a transcription app powered by artificial intelligence, and which has virtually eliminated the once-painstaking task of writing up interview notes. That's an overlooked vulnerability that puts data and sources at risk, say experts."

Kine continued: "Otter and its competitors, which include Descript, Rev, Temi and the U.K.-based Trint, are digital warehouses whose advantages of speed and convenience are bracketed by what experts say can be lax privacy and security protections that may endanger sensitive text and audio data, the identities of reporters and the potentially vulnerable sources they contact."

Trint, Otter, Temi and Rev all claim compliance with all or part of the user data protection and storage standards of the European Union's flagship data privacy law, the General Data Protection Regulation. But cybersecurity experts say that the sharing of user data with third parties creates privacy and security vulnerabilities.

Otter "shares your personal data with a whole host of people, including mobile advertising tracking providers, so it strikes me that there's an awful lot of personal data and the potential for leakage of sources for journalists," Paul Rosenzweig, former deputy assistant secretary for policy in the Department of Homeland Security, and founder of Red Branch Consulting, told Kine. "They also quite clearly say that they respond to legal obligation \[law enforcement data requests\], so any journalist who transcribes an interview with a confidential source and puts it up on Otter has got to live with the possibility that Otter will wind up giving that transcript to the FBI."

So the best advice for reporters using transcription tools: Proceed at your own risk. The tools are fine for routine interviews, covering public meetings, etc. But think twice before using them with sensitive stories and interview subjects. In those cases, transcribe it the old-fashioned way -- by playing the audio back and retyping what is said.

"If I need to record sensitive information, I don't use a cloud-based system," Graves said. "I use a recorder and then transcribe the parts that I need later). Or I just use handwritten notes."

Graves said transcription tools are helpful but can waste a ton of time. She finds that taking physical notes still is a tried-and-true way to interview subjects -- especially in person.

"It is my first best editing on-the-fly approach," she said. "It also signals to my sources that I am working and we are not just talking. When they see me writing, they know I am engaging, listening and taking notes. I often only use my transcripts to confirm context and check for accurate quotes -- which is what I find recording the best for these days."

Graves requires her University of Nebraska-Lincoln journalism students to use [Otter.ai](http://Otter.ai) or another transcription when they are conducting all their interviews. She wants them to use it *only* to check quotes and for context when they are writing. She also requires them to take handwritten notes. In her more advanced reporting and writing courses, they are required to upload the audio file, the transcript and their handwritten notes with full source name and contact information.

"I spot check these files and use these files to fact-check their work when I am grading," she said. "I want to begin using the audio in my reporting classes as a tool on contextualizing quotes and best practices or using examples of when interrupting a source can change the direction of an interview."

## Transcription Without Tech 

An old reporter's trick for transcribing interviews: Unless you absolutely have to, don't transcribe the entire interview. Just transcribe some of the key quotes you'll need for the story. This is extremely important on deadline stories such as sports, late meetings or election night.

But how do you do it? As you're recording the interview, keep track of the time code on the recorder. In your notebook, list the times when an interesting quote starts or ends, and work forward or backward from there.

This will save many hours of time with transcriptions. With experience, you'll get better at flagging the strong quotes in your notes and become even more efficient.

## Using Otter.ai

Otter is considered the Ferrari of transcription tools for its accuracy, speed and ease of use. It's available as both a desktop tool and phone app, the latter is popular with reporters working in the field.

The tool has a variety of pricing plans based on the number of minutes of audio transcribed. At the time this book was published, Otter offered 600 free minutes of transcription a month, more than enough for most reporters.

## Editing Your Work and Writing Headlines

Copy editors and web producers are typically tasked with editing and writing headlines for stories once the reporter files it. They also add keywords, metadata and other behind-the-scenes coding to make stories searchable. But with shrinking newsroom budgets, many reporters must write their own headlines and self-edit much of their work.

If you are editing your own story, it's best to step away from it for a minute, clear your head and then start the editing process. Begin by just reading the story start to finish, without editing anything. Read it as the reader would see it for the first time. Then make your editing pass, fixing grammar, AP Style, punctuation, usage, spelling, etc. Make a second pass through for fact-checking names, data and hotlinks. Run grammar and spellcheck. Write the headline last, though some editors sometimes start headlines after their first reading pass. It's up to you.

For longer, non-deadline stories, some reporters and editors will print out a story and edit it by hand. This obviously isn't a good process for every story, but it can be an effective approach for in-depth stories that need extra care. Backlit screens often make eyes lazy, and editors can catch mistakes on paper that they would mess up when editing on a screen.

## Headline Writing

People tend to read several headlines, but they read only stories that capture their interest. While everyone is interested in different things, a good headline will pull a reader into a story, or will summarize a story to the extent that the reader is satisfied and moves along.

A headline writer's job is to describe in a few choice words what the story is all about. The most important requirement of headlines is that they be accurate.

This is particularly important with search engine-optimized (SEO) headlines for digital news stories. Readers give precious little time to online reading, so an accurate headline that summarizes the story can mean the difference between the reader moving on or clicking on the story and reading it. SEO-driven headlines also appeal to search engines and help their ranking, and they make it easier for readers to capture the gist of a story when scrolling through social media feeds.

Reporters and editors should re-read the article, identify the underlying theme and focus on the most recent development -- get the news in the headline and give the story a sense of immediacy. Make sure to give the casual reader an impression of the entire story, and that the headline can stand on its own.

Here are some do's and don'ts of writing good SEO headlines:

::: section
### Do

- Summarize the story accurately
- Write in present tense and subject-verb-object structure (who-did-what). Exception: Past tense on arrest stories: Chicago Man Arrested in Ponzi Scheme.
- Keep headlines at 8--10 words (around 75 characters) maximum and contain keywords (proper nouns) from the story. Think of what the reader might search for when looking for the story. Those keywords also can be used in the metadata
- Proofread the headline before publishing. Many typos are found in rushed headlines
- Use shorter words: tie, link, end, etc. Use [Thsrs.com](https://www.Thsrs.com) for this
- Use numerals; do not spell out numbers. *Examples:* 3 Gunmen Rob Local Bank \| City Faces 4% Budget Decrease.
- Use single quote marks around titles and quotes in headlines. *Example:* 'An Act of War'

::: section
### Don't

- Use puns and metaphors. Search engines are literal, so a snappy print headline with a pun won't translate to digital. Search engines need specifics: A search for Bears can produce stories about polar bears, grizzly bears or the Chicago Bears.
- Write the headline as a title or label. Better: who did what?
- Be clever or witty with a sensitive story.
- Editorialize or misrepresent the story through a "click-bait" headline

::: section

## Upstyle and Downstyle Headlines

Downstyle headlines capitalize only the first word and proper nouns in a headline:

- Food banks can't meet demand
- Fraud scandal shakes real estate industry
- Layoffs not part of Microsoft's plan

Upstyle headlines capitalize the first letter in each word except for prepositions, articles, conjunctions or words like iPhone:

- Battered Stores Pare Forecasts
- Plan to Cut Foreclosure Rate Clears
- A Man's Home Is His Versailles
- Apple Announces New iPod Launch

Google Trends ([trends.google.com](https://www.trends.google.com)) also is a useful tool for generating SEO-friendly headlines. You'll learn how to use that tool in [Chapter 2](#ch02.xhtml__15031-5909_ch002).

## Google Search Operators

Even with generative AI, journalists still use search operators to expedite their search queries.

Samantha Sunne, a freelance investigative data journalist and co-author of the textbook "Data + Journalism," uses Google search operators to search more efficiently. She often uses filters such as "site:" to better perform searches for specific phrases or specific files buried deep within a specific site.

"The general rule, when searching for a person, a story, or just about anything, is to start specific and work your way out from there," Sunne said. "There are many times when I think, 'There is no way this website is going to have exactly what I'm looking for.' But I type in the exact term, usually using quotes to denote an exact phrase and see what comes up.

"Every once in a while, it will work. More often, I will need to cut out a word or search for a more umbrella term, and then skim or read whatever comes up."

Search operators help expedite that process. Dan Russell, formerly a senior research scientist at Google, has compiled a list of dozens of search operators to help you find the information you want.

**Filetype:** This operator helps you search the web for a specific type of file:. doc,. xlsx,. ppt,. pdf. Type this into a Google search field and see the results:

Filetype:xlsx Sydney Australia positive COVID-19 cases

The Sydney and New South Wales government health data pages typically show up on the first page of search results, along with a page of Sydney COVID-19 data from Our World in Data.

**Site:** Search with a specific website for information on sublevel pages. This is particularly helpful with government websites -- and some media websites -- that have poor search engines built in. Use Google to work around it by typing this into the Google search field:

Site:[cdc.gov](https://cdc.gov) SARS

The result is a deep list of SARS-related pages useful to journalists: About page, fact sheet, FAQ, historical timeline, etc. ([Figure 2.3](#ch02.xhtml__15031-5909_ch002_Figure_003)).

There are many other useful search operators that Russell and others recommend:


<a href="#ch02.xhtml_R_15031-5909_ch002_Figure_003"><em>Figure</em> <em>2.3</em></a> Site operator search for SARS on <a href="https://www.CDC.gov">CDC.gov</a>


- **Site:[youtube.com](https://youtube.com).** YouTube has a good search engine, of course, because Google owns it. But if you're not getting the results you want, try Google.com. Type in Site:youtube and whatever topic you're searching. Try [video.google.com](https://www.video.google.com) to search several platforms for video, including many news sites.
- **Site minus site.** A search like: \[site:[nyc.gov](https://nyc.gov) -site:[www.nyc.gov](http://www.nyc.gov)\] will give you sites in [NYC.gov](https://www.NYC.gov) that do NOT begin with WWW. That's handy for finding subdomains within a particular site, which you can then use "site:" to search.
- **Stars in site search.** A search like \[site:\*.law.\*.edu\] will find all of the. EDU sites with ".law" in the domain name. Also try: \[site:\*.[nyc.gov](http://nyc.gov)\] will match all of the [NYC.gov](https://www.NYC.gov) sites with a subdomain. Also, \[site:\*.nasa.\* inurl:education\] gives lots of good clues about education sites at NASA.
- **Quotation marks.** Using quotation marks in your search terms lets you search specifically for those words. It means all of your results will have your search terms in them. Example: "BLS unemployment data New York."
- **Hyphens.** If you want to exclude a term from your search, include a hyphen before the word. Example: If you're looking for information on dolphins and don't want to include the Miami Dolphins, try dolphins -football or dolphins -miami
- **Tilde (\~).** Use the tilde when you also want its synonyms to appear in the result. Example: Music \~classes will search for music classes, lessons, coaching, etc.
- **Vertical bar (\|).** This query will search websites that have any one/two/all of the search terms. Example: Biden \| economy \| stock market
- **Two periods (..).** Use this when you want to search within a range of two numbers. Example: DSLR camera prices \$500.. \$900
- **AROUND.** Search expert Henk van Ess teaches this operator in his Google sessions. AROUND (be sure to use all caps so Google doesn't include it in the actual search) lets you search for two terms within a range of words from each other. For example, "gdp" AROUND(20) "Ireland" gives you all results about Ireland's GDP when the two words are within 20 words of one another. With changes to Google's search algorithm in the last few years, this operator has become much more popular.
- **\@twitter keywords.** Just type \@twitter and a few keywords after it to search tweets for a specific topic. You can include locations, too, to help with coverage of local breaking news.
  - *Example:* Search \@twitter F1 race Monte Carlo in Google and it gives you accounts and tweets from the famous auto race. In my results, it listed the official race and F1 twitter accounts first, followed by links to various tweets. I've had mixed results using this search operator, but it has produced some worthwhile searches. It's worth a try on a breaking story.

Sunne often uses search operators when setting up news alerts. She sets up new Google searches via Google Alerts, or other more specific tools like Google Scholar or web-update monitors like Visualping or Distill.io.

A Google Alerts search would be something like: site:[lla.la.gov](https://lla.la.gov) investigative ("new orleans" OR "orleans parish")

The search pings Sunne when the Louisiana Legislative Auditor (with the website [lla.louisiana.gov](https://www.lla.louisiana.gov)) posts a new investigative audit that mentioned either the city of New Orleans or its surrounding county. Google Alerts aren't instantaneous -- they arrive only within a day or so of a new Google result for that search. But it is still a good way to use filters to stay on top of specific files, agencies or news topics.

Sunne also uses some paid "people searcher" sites that are powerful, but can also be costly. LexisNexis is one example. In recent years she has been using a combination of free sites, which you can find on Google, and TLO, a paid "people searcher."

## Google's Dataset Search Engine

In 2018, Google launched Dataset Search, which allows you to search millions of datasets posted by academic researchers, nonprofit organizations, companies and government agencies. Google's reasoning: Datasets are easier to find when they include clear and consistent supporting information, such as the data's description, source and file format. Those details can help journalists determine whether a dataset is worth downloading, thus saving hours of research time.

As you type keywords into the Dataset Search box, Google suggests possible datasets. Moreover, you can filter the research results by when the data was posted, its format and whether you must pay to download the data. Just use the filters tab at the top of the interface.

The tabs down the left side of the interface allow you to move to various datasets. As you click each tab, the description of the dataset -- provided by the data's producers -- appears on the right side of the interface. This includes the format, any special permissions (most are public record or available under Creative Commons licenses) and detail the datasets methodology.

It's important to note that Google doesn't fact-check the datasets. It merely guides you to the datasets and provides information about them so you can vet the data yourself. It's an incredibly efficient tool for finding clean datasets on deadline.

Journalists also can have their datasets listed in the search engine by completing the steps outlined on the tool's developer page.

## Tracking Search in Google Trends

Journalists use Google Trends ([trends.google.com](https://www.trends.google.com)) to track what people are searching for on Google and to test SEO keywords to see how they're performing. As of June 2023, there are 8.5 billion searches per day on Google, and this database tracks them back to 2004.

The simple interface lets you compare multiple search topics at once and see how they perform in a basic line chart, and, in some cases, maps. You can look at search on a minute-by-minute basis, hourly, weekly, monthly, annually or select a period of time you want to focus on (e.g. election year).

You can do worldwide search, by country, state/province and even larger cities. This helps you drill down and see how a global search term is performing in your area, or vice versa. Let's say you are covering a story that has gone viral. You can load the search term in to see how it's performing globally, or in Pakistan, Mexico or Russia.

The pulldown menu to the right narrows your search. You can track web searches, YouTube searches, Google Shopping, Google News and more.

Trends was particularly helpful at the start of the pandemic, when you could track what people were searching for: Clorox wipes, toilet paper, surgical masks and other PPE. People also searched for symptoms.

Trends also is helpful for local and national elections. For example, let's answer the question: Who was searched more during the 2016 U.S. presidential election: Donald Trump or Hillary Clinton? Follow these steps:

1.  Open [trends.google.com](https://www.trends.google.com) and type "Hillary Clinton" in the field at the top. Make sure you spell it right; Trends doesn't account for misspellings the way Google search does.
2.   A line chart will show on the screen. Type "Donald Trump" into the "Compare" field next to Clinton's name. Make sure on both names to select "Search Term" from the pulldown menu. You also can select subtopics for context (Clinton as secretary of state, for example).
3.  In the pulldown underneath Clinton's name, you'll see it defaults to "United States." Click in the field and you can type a name or select a country, state, province or city from the pulldown menus. Type in "Chicago."
4.  In the next pulldown menu, labeled "Past Day," hit the menu arrow and select "Custom Time Range" at the bottom. Hit the radio button that says "Full year" and from the pulldown menu select 2016, the campaign year. Your graphic should appear ([Figure 2.5](#ch02.xhtml__15031-5909_ch002_Figure_005)).
5.  You can link to the page by copying the link in your browser field at the top. If you want to embed it, click on the button labeled "\< \>" in the upper right corner of the graphic to get JavaScript embed code. The page also will give you other embeddable charts, maps and lists of search terms from that topic and area.

## Analyzing the Chart and Data

It's easy to see in the chart that Trump dominated search during most of the campaign. He said controversial things and stole headlines. But note that Clinton surged ahead on a few dates:


<a href="#ch02.xhtml_R_15031-5909_ch002_Figure_005"><em>Figure</em> <em>2.5</em></a> Google Trends 2016 search results (<em>Interactive chart</em>)


- **Week of June 5:** FBI email investigation heated up, according to a search of her name in Google News.
- **Week of July 24:** The Democratic National Convention.
- **Week of Sept. 11:** Clinton passed out at the 9/11 memorial and it made international headlines.

Be careful not to draw assumptions about those surges in traffic. I searched in Google News thoroughly for those headlines and dates before using them in a story or presentation.

You also may need to explain the Trends chart's y-axis to readers in the story, so they understand that the 0 to 100 represents a normalization, not the actual traffic numbers. This explanation can be accessed on the "?" button in the top left of the graphic. It reads:

> Numbers represent search interest relative to the highest point on the chart for the given region and time. A value of 100 is the peak popularity for the term. A value of 50 means that the term is half as popular. A score of 0 means there was not enough data for this term.

**A word of caution:** Don't draw gross generalizations from Google Trends. Just because candidates are being searched more than others doesn't mean they're more popular or are going to win the election. They might be getting searched for all the wrong reasons.

When attributing Google Trends results in a story, it's best to use: "According to an analysis of Google search data." It's clear to the reader what you're doing with the data, as many readers don't know what Trends is or does.

## Using The Wayback Machine

For more than a quarter of a century, journalists have used The Internet Archive Wayback Machine ([https://archive.org/](https://archive.org)) to visit archived versions of websites, including those that have been taken down. The site creates an Internet library for researchers, historians, scholars and others, and it collaborates with many institutions, including the Library of Congress and the Smithsonian. (Fun fact: it's named after Mr. Peabody's WABAC -- pronounced "way-back" -- machine from the old "Rocky and Bullwinkle" cartoon show.)

ProPublica fact-checking guru Craig Silverman loves the "Changes" tool on the Wayback Machine. The button appears in the top toolbar. Put it to the test: Paste the link of the National Rifle Association's About page into the search field at the top of the page: [https://home.nra.org/about-the-nra](https://home.nra.org).

Then hit the "search archived websites" radio button, then the "Go" button. The "Changes" button at the top of the interface will give you calendars going back several years, including a few dates with blue marks on them. Those were dates that the page was updated. As you can see, the NRA about page was updated twice in April 2023 and twice in February 2023. This is helpful for seeing if an organization is changing or hiding something. Select two captures and then click the "Compare" button at the top of the page to see a side-by-side view of the captures.

## Hacking a Web Address to Find Archived Data

Many times, government agencies don't maintain an easy-to-navigate archive of their records on the web, providing only the most current dataset. To find older datasets, you can hack the web address of the most-recent dataset as agencies have consistent URLs for naming files.

Sunne experiments with URLs to see what is buried deep on a website. She figured if Google can search within a domain like *site:[fbi.gov/news](https://fbi.gov)/*, meaning the News section of the FBI's website, she could move around within those URLs as well.

She experimented with the U.S. Department of Defense for a story she was working on for Reuters. To her surprise, she was able to manually change URLs and find new information.

How she did it: Simply change the ID or the section in a URL to pull up a document she was looking for. For instance, the DOD uploaded reports on its website with a format like, "[www.defense.gov/reports/loc_531/20190430/](http://www.defense.gov)." This meant the report was filed on April 30, 2019, for a location with the ID "loc_531." If she changed the URL to read "20180430," it would show her a report from that same location for the same day a year earlier. She could also change locations if she knew the ID number, which she easily found online.

"This method worked better than searching the DOD's website," Sunne said. "It was an interesting exercise in how much you can circumnavigate a website's structure, and also how much you can stumble upon online."

## Glossary 

It's important when fact-checking to know the difference between misinformation and disinformation. The fundamental difference is intent.

- **Misinformation:** The person shares the information without knowing that it is wrong. The person believes it. Example: Your Uncle Bob shares a magic cure for COVID-19 on his Facebook page: Gargle with salt water. "We're all saved. Pandemic over!" he writes. Until he learns that's not the case.
- **Disinformation:** The person shares the bad information knowing that it is false. It's a malicious act to mislead others.

For Adhikari, fact-checking comes down to time and resources. Before launching NepalCheck, he spent 2 1/2 years as editor of South Asia Check, where he was frustrated by the fact that he didn't have the tools and staff to do real-time fact-checking.

"I wanted to fact-check a prominent politician's speech in real-time," he said. "But I soon realized that given our small team of four people and lack of databases and credible facts online, it was impossible to do it. I had read about real-time fact-checking, though I hadn't watched it live.

He had come close in February 2021, when Prime Minister KP Sharma Oli delivered a speech in Kathmandu. He reviewed the fact-checks of Oli's interviews and speeches on South Asia Check's website. Politicians often repeat the same false claims several times, so Adhikari was expecting Oli to repeat already-debunked false claims.

"I watched his speech and quickly identified several claims for a fact-check report," he said. "Oli concluded his speech around 4 p.m. I researched, reported, and published the fact-check at 6 p.m, two hours after his speech.

"It was published in English two days later \[they first publish fact-checks in Nepali\]. I fact-checked three claims Oli made in his speech that day and added one more from his earlier speech. EKantipur, Nepal's largest digital outlet, published the fact-check."

He also debunked conspiracy theories circulating among Nepali Facebook users that were picked up by several Nepal digital outlets. They were also complex issues to tackle, but after talking to experts and conducting a digital investigation, he debunked them.

"I still think we as fact-checkers need to be clear about what we know and what we don't," he said. "At the time, the science around the COVID-19 pandemic was still evolving. Confronting such a huge crisis and lack of knowledge about it, the best possible way was to acknowledge the challenges."

Unlike NepalCheck, Nicholson's team of more than 140 digital investigation experts produces, on average, more than 150 fact-checks per week. In 2022 alone, AFP did 8,000 fact-checks.

"The claims we check are more often about images than just text but they can also originate from a political speech, for example," said Nicholson, who previously worked as a foreign correspondent in Mexico City and has reported from more than 15 countries.

## Fact-Checking and the Rise of Fake News

As Adhikari noted earlier, the 2016 U.S. presidential election was a landmark moment in fact-checking. Trump galvanized fact-checkers looking for misinformation and, in many cases, disinformation. The election also marked a surge in fake news sites that spread misinformation and disinformation over social media channels. Some were simply propaganda to generate influence over voters and election results. Others wanted commercial gain through advertising due to the large traffic numbers that came to the site.

In his book, "The Online Journalism Handbook" (second edition), Paul Bradshaw wrote about how Facebook took most of the criticism over fake news, though Google, Twitter and other platforms also shouldered blame.

Early in 2016, Facebook had fired the human editors overseeing its "Trending Topics" section that had led to fake stories appearing in that section within days, Bradshaw wrote. Its feed algorithms gave less weight to established publishers and brands. Bradshaw noted that one Buzzfeed investigation into more than 1,000 posts on hyperpartisan Facebook pages found that "the least accurate pages generated some of the highest numbers of shares, reactions and comments on Facebook." They had more engagement than mainstream news pages.

By November, Zuckerberg had announced plans to tackle fake news through better reporting and detection systems.

Google followed suit by attaching fact-checks to stories in its Google New search, which eventually led to the Google Fact Check Explorer tool that allows users to search fact-checks by person or topic.

## Debunking Fake News Stories

You can save yourself a lot of grief and embarrassment by following a few simple steps before sharing a story or quoting from it.

- **Think before sharing and read beyond the story's headline.** Never retweet, like or share anything until you've clicked the link and read the story, even if it comes from a familiar, reliable source. Apply critical thinking skills: Does anything not seem right with the piece? Then like or share it. Most social media tools now stop you and ask if you want to click on the link before sharing.
- **Search if other news outlets are reporting it.** This is one of the first things I do after reading the story, especially if it involves a death or someone is accused of something. Can I confirm the story through another news outlet?
- **Check for fact-checks.** There are hundreds of fact-checking organizations around the world. Google the topic or a person from the story. Look at [Snopes.com](https://www.Snopes.com), PolitiFact or another fact-checking site to see if it's part of a prank or disinformation campaign. Search the topic or person in the Google Fact Check Explorer. Both are quick ways to avoid the pain of getting something wrong.
- **Check the news outlet that published it and who the author is.** Are you familiar with the outlet and the author? Check their social media channels. What are they sharing? How are they engaging readers? Many times fact-checkers will debunk a fake news story in Twitter replies, Facebook or story comments. Take the time to look for them.
- **Look at the publish date and time.** How old is the story? From today, yesterday, a week or years ago? Sometimes people will share stories on social media in the context of breaking news, when the story occurred days, weeks or even years ago. Check the date; when a story happened matters.
- **Look at what links and sources are used in the article.** Is the information attributed to an on-the-record source? To a study? How current is the data? Is the story linking to credible examples, reports, public records, etc.? If the links don't work or you don't see any attribution, chances are you may be looking at a fake news piece.
- **Look for questionable quotes and photos.** Remember, photos and words together don't equal facts. Pursue the truth. Look closely at the photos: Are the person's eyes, ears and hands disproportionate (a key sign for AI-generated images). Do searches on the images (which you'll learn how to do later in this chapter). Copy the quote and Google it: Where has it been previously published? Maybe the author stole it from another story or made it up.
- **Beware of confirmation bias.** This is more for political or ideological stories, but make sure your own beliefs aren't interfering with your ability to seek the truth in the story. Even if you disagree with what some sources are saying in a story about abortion rights, it doesn't mean what they're saying is inaccurate. Check yourself and make sure to approach stories on controversial issues with an open mind.

## Fact-Checking Images

When fact-checking images, First Draft New, the former fact-checking organization at Harvard, recommends following these tips:

- **Provenance:** Are you looking at the original piece of content? Give the image the "eye test" -- does it look altered in any way?
- **Source:** Who captured the original piece of content? You can find this through a reverse image search.
- **Date:** When was the piece of content captured? You also can verify this through an image search.
- **Location:** Where was the piece of content captured? In [Chapter 8](#ch08.xhtml__15031-5909_ch008), we explore how to use the Google Earth suite and other tools to confirm a photo's location.
- **Motivation:** Why was the piece of content captured? Look at the context in which it's presented, the reputation of the organization or person sharing it. Is the photo from a journalist documenting a protest, or from a protester's perspective? Maybe it's a photo from an eyewitness, or a police officer documenting what's happening for legal reasons. Who is shooting it and why is important for providing the reader with context, or determining if it's valid.

I always encourage students to give the photo an "eye test." For example, if someone you don't know tweeted a photo to you of several cars submerged or nearly submerged in a Missouri flood from today, you'd look over the photo closely. What if the area looked more urban, like New York City? What if the license plates on the cars all said New York? It's highly improbable that the photo was taken in Missouri. You could check weather reports and see if there was even flooding in Missouri. Look for news stories. Check [Snopes.com](https://www.Snopes.com) and a couple of fact-checking sites.

Then, to be sure, you could move the image into Google Image Search, TinEye, Bing or another tool to see where and when the photo was first published. If it shows up today in Missouri, you're good. If it shows up as far back as a year ago in New York state, you're being duped by someone.

## Ethics and Building Trust With Readers

Lauren Camplin took five journalism courses with me when she was a student at DePaul University. Ethics were part of every class she took with me, whether it was newswriting, online journalism, sports reporting, news editing, etc. I baked the SPJ and NPPA Codes of Ethics into Photoshop training. I examined case studies on airing suicide details or footage of a stunt performer falling to his death in a public square in Seattle. I talked about real-life coverage of local news stories.

Many students in those classes groused and rolled their eyes when handed an ethics code, but Camplin never did. She was always thoughtful and showed good judgment while standing her ground at sometimes unpopular decisions in class discussions.

Upon graduation, she scored a job as an overnight producer at ESPN. She was promoted to the day shift in 2013 and her first day on the job was April 15, Patriot's Day. The day of the Boston Marathon.

A day later, I received this email:

> *From: Lauren Camplin*
>
> *Sent: Tuesday, April 16, 2013 10:40 AM*
>
> *To: Reilley, Michael*
>
> *Subject: Boston*
>
> *Hey*,
>
> *Hope you're doing well. Just wanted to shoot you a note saying thanks for all the ethical teaching you did in classes because it definitely helped me yesterday*.
>
> *I was doing media for the 3 p.m. SportsCenter when the bombs went off and was solely responsible for all the video, sound, etc. we ran for about four straight hours since we dropped commercial breaks*.
>
> *You sit in a classroom thinking you won't have to deal with those kinds of situations and make those calls but it really comes in handy. So, thanks! Hope DePaul is treating you well -- and your students too!*
>
> *Lauren Camplin*

Ethics training might not be the sexiest skill you hone during college. But, as Camplin well knows, it's pretty handy when you're on the hotseat overseeing live coverage of a terrorist bombing.

Like many journalists, Camplin faced some tough decisions that day. Does she allow the live feed of the bombing victims on air before the families had been notified? The Society of Professional Journalists' Code of Ethics tells us to minimize harm. Should she airtight shots camera operators took of the devastation? Ethic codes tell us not to pander to lurid curiosity and to consider the harm it may cause victims and their families.

Ethics decisions come in many shades of gray. There are absolutes -- don't accept money and gifts from sources, don't alter news photos, etc. But many times ethics boil down to not just what we have the right to do legally as a journalist, but what's the *right thing* to do.

## Trusting News

Fact-checking efforts have accelerated at a time when trust in news by audiences have reached historic lows. According to a 2022 Gallup survey, only one in three Americans trust mass media to report the news "fully, accurately and fairly." Only 7% of Americans have "a great deal" of trust and confidence in the media, and 27% have "a fair amount." Meanwhile, 28% of U.S. adults say they do not have very much confidence and 38% have none at all in newspapers, TV and radio.

In that same study, Americans were 17 percentage points more likely to say they trust reporting by local news organizations "a great deal" or "quite a lot" than to trust reporting by national news organizations.

Trust numbers in the United States have been on a steady decline since the mid-1970s, when trust hovered at 75%. The current U.S. numbers are significantly lower than in other countries, such as China, where trust levels hover near 80%.

While trust may be higher in local news, a 2022 research project the Radio Television Digital News Association (RTDNA) conducted with Magid shows local news is not exempt from the factors that lead to distrust. According to the research, "if just one journalist from a local news source is not trusted, audiences are overwhelmingly less likely to use the source overall."

When RTDNA and Magid leaders released the study, they led a discussion at the RTDNA national conference about solutions for building trust:

- **Be Right:** News consumers trust journalists when they explain how stories developed and changed over time.
- **Be Your Community:** News consumers trust journalists who are knowledgeable about the local community and what issues are important to them.
- **Be Real:** News consumers trust journalists when they are authentic, genuine and own up to a mistake.
- **Be Neutral:** News consumers trust journalists who leave personal opinions out of news coverage.

In 2016, USC associate professor Amara Aguilar and eight Annenberg School for Communication and Journalism students founded Dímelo, a storytelling platform where Latinx students could see themselves and their communities better represented in news coverage from other student media outlets at the school.

Dímelo, which means "tell me" in Spanish, started as a Facebook page but quickly expanded to other social media platforms -- Instagram, YouTube, Spotify -- as well as websites on Medium and Annenberg Media. Their most popular platform is Snapchat, where they have about 185,000 subscribers on Discover.

Aguilar said Dímelo's goal is to reach young Latinx and Hispanic adults with stories and content experiences that matter to them.

"Students originally wanted to start a Spanish language broadcast, but as we talked through the audience they wanted to reach, it became apparent the goals needed to shift to meet the target audience of young Latinx adults where they were consuming news," Aguilar said.

Prior to the emergence of social media, news traveled pretty much on a one-way street. It came from one entity, person or organization to a larger group, which tended to be a passive audience. Even with reader comments and newsgroups emerging in the mid-1990s on news sites, traditional news media still preached "content is king" with an "if you build it, they will come" approach to audiences.

But the audience responded by seeking *community* online. Places like Reddit, various social sharing apps, hashtags, Facebook group pages and other platforms gave people a sense of belonging. Suddenly, news became a discussion beyond a letter to the editor.

In this chapter, you'll explore how to build content for social channels, reach an audience, cover breaking news over social media, crowdsource user-generated content to enhance reporting and deal with online harassment. You'll also learn about third-party tools and apps that enhance social media reporting, and how to study social media analytics.

## Building an Audience

Developing an audience over social channels isn't done overnight. It takes time and study. Dímelo covers community stories, including those in Los Angeles, but also focuses on content that goes beyond boundaries -- beyond geographic borders, cultural borders, stereotypes and beyond the surface of important issues that affect our world.

"We had the students do extensive research on the audience they wanted to reach, and also examine their own habits and community," Aguilar said. "At the time, media organizations like mitú were also popular and the students were inspired.

Dímelo publishes in English and Spanish and aims to amplify unheard voices, engage audiences, create community and focus on issues that diverse audiences care about. ## Developing Social Audiences at Large Newsrooms

Aguilar and her students quickly found a niche audience with young Latinos through social media. It can be even more challenging with large legacy media outlets that have embraced one or two approaches to audiences over decades. In the last several years, roles such as "audience engagement editor" have emerged in newsrooms with the sole purpose of finding and nurturing new digital audiences.


<em>Figure</em> <em>4.1</em> Dímelo Instagram story on artisans


Tom Johnson, the head of audience development for the Guardian US, oversees the audience and social storytelling teams. He has spent the last decade working in a variety of audience strategy roles with an emphasis on data-driven experimentation, subscriber growth and creative visual storytelling.

"For publications of any size, it's important to think about what the primary audience objective is with any platform and then making sure that your work there ultimately supports that goal," said Johnson, who also has done audience engagement work with The Washington Post. "Social platforms can be useful in making strong first impressions with new readers, but it's important to consider the longer-term objective. If it's building an engaged community around a specific topic or coverage area, what are you doing to foster and encourage thoughtful discussion?"

The same is true for how to approach existing online communities as a publication. When Johnson was at the Post, he worked with the social media team to refine the Post's Reddit strategy and the Post's presence there across different subreddits.

"It's a strong example of how you can engage and grow an off-site audience over time by bringing added value to a discussion that falls within your coverage areas," he said. "This can take many forms -- hosting an AMA on an important news topic with your reporters who can provide expertise or simply finding a trending Reddit thread discussing one of your stories and commenting with additional context from your previous reporting."

Encouraging reader participation on a website is also an effective way to build an engaged audience over time. This can be done through recurring, consistent live chats or even incorporating reader input into live coverage, which is something that the Guardian's soccer live blog often does effectively.

"Creating regular programming with distinct voices, which the Guardian's live blogs are known for, can help build a connection with readers over time and hopefully turn some of those people who found the page via search into regular home page visitors," Johnson said.

## How Big Is the Social Media Audience?

In its spring 2023 global social media research summary, Smart Insights reported that 60% of the world's population -- about 4.8 billion people -- uses social media. From 2022 to 2023, more than 150 million new social media users have come online, and the average daily usage is 2 hours and 24 minutes for all who are using the platforms.

The study also found that the rapid growth from TikTok showed no sign of slowing down: TikTok had 1.4 billion monthly active users in 2022 and was expected to reach 1.8 billion by the end of 2023.

Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter has led to many unpopular changes to the platform, including paid verification checkmarks, metrics on viewership, an algorithmically curated "For You" feed, which mimics a popular feature on TikTok, and a "Following" tab. But for the first half of 2023, the platform has remained in principle a place to share news and interact with readers. Tools such as Post.news, Bluesky Social, Threads and Mastodon emerged as competitors, but many journalists continue posting to Twitter.

While some platforms continue to show growth for news consumption, social media news consumption overall has slowed worldwide, according to a 2023 Reuters Institute Digital News Report. The study said that news consumption on vertical video platforms like TikTok and Instagram has not grown fast enough to offset the reduction caused by Facebook's pullback of sharing news stories. A 2022 study by the Pew Research Center showed similar findings.

### ***Interactive Chart***

For years, news organizations have offered digital stories through e-newsletters, mobile apps, podcasts and other channels. All help direct more traffic to your website, but it's not enough.

That's where audience engagement and development come in, and social media plays a big role. Building social media campaigns around stories -- complete with teases, videos, graphics and other storytelling materials -- requires a strategic approach and requires people like Johnson to guide it.


<em>Figure</em> <em>4.2</em> Bar chart of active users on social platforms. Graphic/Mike Reilley.


At the Guardian US, Johnson finds that it helps to apply a scientific method to building audience campaigns -- start with a hypothesis, determine the best variables and data to track, run the experiment and then distill your findings into an analysis. His process is as follows:

1.  **Outline your audience goal at the beginning of the campaign -- and be specific.** The goals can be quantitative, qualitative or both, but they need to be established at the very beginning. For instance, let's say the goal is to grow the student media outlet's email newsletter list by 20% over the course of the semester. One of the first things they should do is conduct competitive research and see what other similarly sized student media outlets have tried in that space. What worked for them and what didn't work? Ideally, do some reporting and reach out to them.
2.  **Formulate a few hypotheses for what you will test.** Maybe it's a specific part of *how* you will accomplish this goal, or simply testing an assumption -- e.g., if we offer an inbox exclusive guest op-ed from a notable alumnus each week, this will drive newsletter signs up. Again, documentation is key. Make sure this lives in a document somewhere that you can revisit at the end of the campaign.
3.  **Track as much as data you can.** If you were to test the impact of visual Twitter threads on newsletter sign-ups, for instance, use a distinct [bit.ly](http://bit.ly) URL for those sign-up links so you can track exactly how many people are clicking on the link and then calculate a click-through rate based on the total number of tweet impressions. (More on analytics later in this chapter.)
4.  **At the end of the campaign, pull out your original doc and revisit those hypotheses.** What surprised you? Even if your hypotheses turn out to be wrong, there's still so much you can learn from the data. In fact, those moments when our initial hypotheses are wrong are often more useful in the long run and can inform your next experiment.
5.  **Document, document, document.** Don't wait until too long after the campaign to analyze the campaign and document your findings. What would you do differently next time? Which additional hypotheses do you wish you had included if you could do it over again? Chances are you'll have found the idea for your next campaign over the course of the experiment. If so, repeat the process. Over time, this will make future audience campaigns that much more effective, and you'll start to build out a library of useful insights.

In his book "JournalismNext," Mark Briggs writes that the challenge with social media campaigns is that not everyone is on all of the platforms and the audience keeps shifting. Pinterest and Tumblr were once all the rage, but not so much today. While TikTok has become a global phenomenon and produces results for some, others have left over privacy and security concerns. The same with Twitter. It's always a moving target.

Briggs offered some broad guidelines for targeting audiences on each platform:

- **Twitter:** Breaking news, sports, politics, business news, raw, real-time reactions to live news events and TV. It's also a great place to reach journalists, who hang out on Twitter a lot. I've used my \@journtoolbox Twitter account to target journalists with Journalist's Toolbox site updates.
- **Facebook:** The best referral source for website traffic, Briggs says. It continues to drive more clicks to news sites than Google, even after it shifted its algorithm in 2018.
- **Instagram:** This tends to attract lighter features, trend and pop culture stories from news outlets (see Dímelo's use of Instagram in the next section).
- **Pinterest:** Food, fashion, fitness and trends on this lifestyle network.
- **YouTube:** Beyond posting your content, this can be a reporting tool. Follow channels of the people and organizations that you cover. Create playlists of ongoing topics and issues in the community so people can follow them.
- **Reddit:** The community is very skeptical of news users, and can often reject posts that are relevant to various communities. Johnson said the Post built a presence on Reddit in part by engaging in subreddits where its journalism was already being shared and discussed. Sometimes this requires reaching out to the moderators, as each subreddit can have its own community rules.

## Social Media: Past, Present and Future

Aguilar's approach to using targeted social media coverage has been honed over 18 years of working on social media. Since the emergence of Facebook (2004), Twitter (2006) and Instagram (2010), media outlets have scrambled to find ways to tell stories in a way that reached the Millennial and Gen Z audiences that flock to them for everything -- news, sports, entertainment and more.

The struggle early on was real. Beyond posting stories there, what else could journalists do with it?

Two early case studies were the "Miracle on the Hudson" in January 2009 and the summer 2009 Iran election protests and violence. When Sully Sullenberger famously landed US Airways Flight 1549 on the Hudson River, most of the early photos and video didn't come from news outlets. They came from eyewitnesses who were on ferry boats crossing the Hudson. Those images and videos were quickly shared over social media, and news outlets used them in their breaking coverage. Among the very first reports was a Twitter post by Janis Krums from Sarasota, Florida, who was on a ferry that arrived on the scene just a few minutes after the plane had crashed into the freezing water.

This is a technique called crowdsourcing -- where mainstream media ask for or search for eyewitness accounts from breaking stories they're not staffing or haven't arrived to yet. It's a best practice to ask the person posting who shot the photo and get that person's permission to use it, though you can embed tweets rights-free.

Several months after Sullenberger's water landing, violence broke out on the streets of Tehran, Iran, due to public unrest over the election results. State-sponsored media spread propaganda and any independent media were quickly shut down. But with social media, journalists, protesters, eyewitnesses -- everyone could post. There were no printing presses or TV satellites to shut down.

On June 15, The New York Times reported: "Iranians are blogging, posting to Facebook and, most visibly, coordinating their protests on Twitter. Their activity has increased, not decreased, since the presidential election on Friday and ensuing attempts by the government to restrict or censor their online communications."

While some of this was grounded in activism and rebellion by the protesters, personal accounts and reporting also became part of the discussion. Hashtags were shared. Video and photos of protesters being attacked emerged. Without social media channels, much of that would not have been seen by the millions who did access it.

Both of these landmark news events became case studies for how crowdsourcing and breaking news coverage would be altered for years to come.

## Crowdsourcing and Live-Tweeting a Murder Trial

Social media can be helpful for covering all kinds of breaking news events, including trials. Reporters can use Twitter, Facebook and other social platforms to background witnesses, find expert sources and the accused, provide updates during the trial and more.

In [Chapter 1](#ch01.xhtml__15031-5909_ch001), we learned how Pulitzer Prize winner Chris Graves reported on the high-profile Pike County murder trials. She used crowdsourcing and live-tweeting while covering the case as a Washington Post freelancer while also teaching a remote college course on the case for the University of Nebraska-Lincoln.

Sitting a few feet away from her in the courtroom was her longtime friend and former colleague Patti Gallagher Newberry, who was covering the case for the Cincinnati Enquirer and the USA Today Network.

Both reporters covered the trial using social media, but had different assignments.

Graves was writing more "big-picture" stories and backgrounding for a book she's writing on the murder trial of George Wagner IV, which began Aug. 29, 2022, and concluded Dec. 19 that year, when Wagner was sentenced to eight consecutive life sentences and 121 additional years for his role in the shooting deaths of seven members of southern Ohio's Rhoden family and one future member.

Newberry was writing wrap-up or look-ahead news features that focused on any legal moves or major developments that week.

Both reporters faced one major challenge: a ban on phones in the courtroom. This made it difficult for Newberry in particular, as she couldn't provide on-the-fly updates over Twitter without leaving the courtroom and potentially missing something important. So she and her editor agreed that she would tweet during breaks in the proceedings and post Twitter threads -- longer narratives where subtweets support a lead tweet -- at mid-morning, lunchtime, mid-afternoon and the end of the day.

"I would aim for a thread of five to 10 tweets from witnesses and other court action in the preceding hour or two," said Newberry, who added that she has a "general distaste for live-tweeting."

"I typically restricted threads to one topic, with a quote or two in each post. When possible, I grabbed photos from our photographer, who filed on a shared Google Drive for all participating media."

The approach worked. Newberry provided some masterful courtroom reporting over those updates and threads ([Figure 4.4](#ch04.xhtml__15031-5909_ch004_Figure_004)):

"I essentially made up this 'strategy' on my own, with limited prior Twitter use, as the best approach to our coverage. Graves took a different approach. She often uses it to find expert sources and asks her followers what they're curious about and what questions they would ask. She DMs people to ask them questions and get in contact with them for potential interviews. She also sets up Twitter lists, a collection of specific accounts around a certain topic, which she did for the trial to follow key reporters who were covering the case. She also used the list to triple-check her story quotes and could see what other reporters were posting.

"The Civil-War era courtroom made it *very* difficult to hear during testimony at times," she said.

Other tools she used were:

- **Facebook:** She uses Facebook to follow specific audiences and to stay in contact with many of her sources. Sometimes, it is the best place to engage. In the Rhoden family case, she found many local sources this way who were afraid to meet her in person or discuss information. It is important, however, to set up your rules for engaging on these platforms: Are they on or off the record? Can you use information that people give you here? Can you use comments they make? Get the OK first.\
   Several Facebook groups were dedicated to the Rhoden case. Many of the people following it had contacts and insights into the case before the arrest, during the pending trials and even after the trials. Most of these groups are private, and Graves either asked or was asked to join.\
   She always made it clear that she would not offer her opinion about what she thought about the case, etc. But she would engage or ask questions if members had them. She found this to be beneficial during the reporting on this case because it has gone on for so long. Several members of these groups are very knowledgeable, and she would tap into that knowledge or query them on what they were most interested in and why.
- **Reddit:** In the early days of the investigation, she spent a lot of time on Reddit threads and local message boards scanning for information. The majority of the information was garbage, but it helped inform some of her reporting, such as discounting rumors, etc.

## More Social Media Tips from Chris Graves for Covering Trials and Breaking News 

- Set up Twitter lists to cancel out all the Twitter noise.
- Research handles and hashtags so your content is distributed correctly.
- Do not speculate. Write only what you observe or know.
- Write conversationally. Strive to follow AP style, grammar and punctuation rules. Twitter is not kind to those who don't. But also know that you can use numerals and understood abbreviations to save room.
- Never delete tweets. But, do correct if you make an error. Most people forgive reporters who are live-tweeting. It is ruthless and hard -- especially if you are outside and it's cold, rainy or hot.
- Alert your audience when you will be live-tweeting. I try to include a photo of myself as I am beginning.
- Be serious when you need to, but also include your observations. LIke all good reporting, Twitter is a great place to try to put your reader into a scene. People want you to take them there as much as possible.
- If you are in court, follow the rules of the court. In the Rhoden trial and in federal court: No laptops or phones were allowed during 99% of the trial, which meant live-tweeting happened between breaks as a summary of what had just occurred. Other days, if testimony was being streamed, I watched in my car or the media room and tweeted as I watched. I did not do this very often, however, as part of my work needed to happen in the courtroom.
  
  
  <em>Figure</em> <em>4.6</em> Photos Graves shot from protests when she was working for Minnesota Public Radio.
  
- I am not great at video, so I don't often record myself on Twitter. But I enjoy when other reporters do either before or after an event. I do use video, however, to share scenes (i.e.: Trump rally/protests).
- Live-tweeting is the best on-deadline writing there is: There is often little time to overwrite or overthink. We write the newest news/the most interesting/most compelling -- which is *exactly* what makes for the most powerful inverted pyramid/summary leads and stories.
- It's time-consuming, but when I write Twitter threads. I write live on Twitter and thread as I go, but I do think there is something to be said for writing in a file and then moving that over to Twitter in storyform. Number your threaded tweets to make it easier for your reader. Threaded tweets can be unfurled as well and often are an excellent way to create a running notebook of your coverage.

## Social Media Safety: Dealing with Trolls and More

Gamergate and the misogynist online attacks against women have been commonplace since social media first emerged. Female sports journalists, in particular, are targets of aggressive attacks and even threats of physical harm.

In 2014, Michelle Ferrier founded Troll-Busters ([www.troll-busters.com](http://www.troll-busters.com)), a resource for journalists and others dealing with online harassment to seek help. A former journalist and columnist, Ferrier received hate mail and death threats for writing a lifestyle column in her local paper. Troll-Busters was her method of saying, "Enough!"

The site offers training, research, tips and contacts for getting help when harassment occurs. The Committee to Protect Journalists and the Student Press Law Center also offer resources.

There are many types of harassment ([Figure 4.8](#ch04.xhtml__15031-5909_ch004_Figure_008)): implied and explicit threats, doxing, libel, impersonating your account and posting sexually explicit material, among them. The response depends on the severity and how often the threats occur, but there are some basic steps you can take to help deal with the threats:

- **You are not alone:** Seek help from your editors and colleagues. Make sure you report it to your employer right away and ask for help. Ask a colleague to monitor your social media accounts and take a break from it. Talk to colleagues who have been through this; they're always eager to offer good advice in these situations. Also ask for help from organizations like Troll-Busters and the Student Press Law Center.
- **Block and report:** Block the account harassing you and report them to the app or platform. Ask colleagues to do the same and get the account removed. Muting the offensive account temporarily is an option, but not if the person is making threats.
- **Take care of yourself:** Dealing with trolls and online harassment can take a toll on your mental health.

Some other resources that can help:

- TRFilter
- [www.trfilter.org/](http://www.trfilter.org)
- This tool from Thomson Reuters allows users to document and manage online harassment and abuse, so they can take control of their social media feeds and focus on their professional activities.
- Dart Center: Self-Care Tips for Journalists
- Troll-Busters also encourages taking pre-emptive measures to ward off harassment before it occurs. In [Figure 4.9](#ch04.xhtml__15031-5909_ch004_Figure_009), Troll-Busters outlines some basic and even extreme measures some journalists need to take.

## Fact-Checking on Social Media

If you are passing through the American History Museum at the Smithsonian in Washington, D.C., you may see an early version of an iPhone on display. The phone once belonged to former NPR journalist Andy Carvin, who used his Twitter app on that phone to cover the Arab Spring in 2011.

Carwin communicated with protesters in the Middle East over Twitter to verify eyewitness accounts from the front lines. He debunked rumors, crowdsourced, fact-checked photos and built an elaborate network of sources and fellow fact-checkers. Carvin's widely considered the first journalist to use Twitter in such a way.

In a world where everything is true and nothing is true on the Internet, fact-checking over social channels can be challenging. Misinformation and disinformation flowed freely during the early years of the COVID-19 outbreak. Former President Donald Trump's Twitter account was a conga line of disinformation and attacks on the press.

There are some basic steps we can take to verify what we find online. Here's a list of steps to follow, taken from some of my own lectures as well as the book "Social and Mobile Media Journalism" (Routledge, Adornato):

1.  **Be skeptical.** If it's too good to be true, it probably is. If there's a link to a story or a graphic, click on it. Always read past the headline and check sourcing and attribution in the story.
2.  **Review the original source's social media accounts.** What else is the person or organization posting? Is it reliable? Who are they retweeting or sharing content from? How long has the account been around?
3.  **Cross-check the information with other sources.** Contact the source directly. Ask for a phone number. Vet them. It's worth the extra few minutes to get it right.
4.  **Check the geo-location of the post.** Where is the post coming from?
5.  **Seek corroboration on social media.** If someone sends you a photo of a fire in a downtown Chicago high-rise building, do an image search on it to see if it appears elsewhere online. Search for other photos and eyewitness accounts on social channels.
6.  **Seek official corroboration.** Call the fire department to confirm the fire. Look it up online.
7.  **If an image seems suspicious**, **reverse image search it in Google ([images.google.com](https://www.images.google.com)).** Check images' metadata if you can. Many social channels remove metadata and Exif data from the image when you post. But Twitter and some other tools now give users the chance to include Alt information by tapping on the photo. Any image you see with "Alt" in the lower left corner includes information about the photo. Tap on it to see.
8.  **Use Google Earth to verify photo locations.** More on this in [Chapter 8](#ch08.xhtml__15031-5909_ch008).

Elliott Ramos, an investigative data journalist for CBS Chicago, noticed in late 2021 that the station's tip line was inundated with calls of Kia and Hyundai owners' cars getting stolen.

Several Chicago reporters covered the incidents as they happened each day, but then Chicago Police began to issue more alerts about the car thefts happening dozens of times in a night, affecting multiple neighborhoods.

Ramos is responsible for the station's data-driven investigations, specializing in data visualizations and obtaining and analyzing large government databases via FOIA requests. He thought it was time to take a big-picture look at the theft trends through data storytelling. Maps could show where the thefts were happening. Charts and graphics could explain increases over time and, with some extra legwork, focus on the most popular makes and models of the stolen cars.

"When the initial reports came in during the summer, I would check the city's open data portal to see how many thefts were occurring," said Ramos, who also has taught data journalism part-time at Chicago universities. "At the end of 2021, Chicago was averaging 1,000 motor vehicle thefts a month. By summer that jumped to 2,000, and by October it reached a 22-year record for most in a month with more than 3,100 reported vehicle thefts.

"But we were only able to pin some of the car thefts on Kias and Hyundais. The manufacturers weren't in the open data reports."

This led to a series of stories on car thefts that aired on the station along with stories and visualizations on its website. For the first story, they mapped the worst blocks in the city, and anchor/reporter Brad Edwards hit the streets to find affected owners. Residents already knew why he was there when he walked up with a camera crew.

Meanwhile, Ramos was tasked with getting the makes and models of the cars to add to the dataset. He knew vehicle makes and models were recorded on incident reports when they're stolen, so he filed a FOIA request for 12 years' worth of data so he would have a basis of comparison. He found that Kias and Hyundais accounted for about 38% of motor vehicle thefts.

"I created some area charts at first so people would have an idea of how much those cars were accounting for the spike in \[2021 and\] 2022," Ramos said. "I wanted to convey the rate at which they were getting stolen, which by October had reached 65 a day."

Ramos loaded the data into Flourish's animated bar chart template to illustrate the change. He worked with the broadcast editors and produced static maps that work well on TV, and used the same file to produce an interactive one on Flourish.

Ramos' process is one many data journalists follow when reporting and visualizing investigative and even day-turn stories.

In this chapter, we'll explore how to think like a data journalist, find data and visualize it. You'll also learn how to focus your data stories with a human-centered narrative approach, how to simplify your writing with just a few numbers per paragraph, and how to scale the data to make sense to the reader. Then you'll learn how to visualize data using several free, intuitive tools.

## Making an Impact: The Need for Data Journalism

For many years, data journalists -- or computer-assisted reporters, as they were once called -- worked in far corners of the newsroom to analyze large datasets for investigative stories. But as digital tools began to become more mainstream and visualizing datasets became easier, data skills spread throughout the newsroom to other departments, said Andy Boyle, former data editor with the Chicago Sun-Times.

"Sports reporters already know this: Data is used constantly," he said. "A quarterback's passing yards. The average spin on a pitcher's fastball. The average free-throw percentage in basketball. They use data constantly in their storytelling, as an often objective measure to show how players and teams are doing in comparison to others."

This approach applies to other beats. Doing culture writing? How often do the local concert venues book artists of one race versus another? How about men versus women? Covering education? You've got enrollment data, you've got attendance data, you've got student testing data. You've also got disciplinary data (are certain groups being disciplined more than others?), teacher retirement data (are teachers being forced to retire more?), and school budget data (what are they spending money on?).

"Everything you cover in journalism can have a data component," Boyle said. "And being able to understand the basic concepts of how to use data to empower your storytelling not only makes you a better journalist, but also a more versatile one."

Once that process is complete, journalists must translate those findings into language free of jargon that makes it clear what is at stake -- and who is impacted by the facts underlying the data. In addition, articles that rely on data should show their work and allow others to replicate -- and expand on -- their findings.

"It's one thing to have a handful of anecdotes saying something is bad," Boyle said. "It's another to have data that shows that thing is bad. If you combine them, you've got the most powerful version of a story possible."

Data reporting makes a difference, as Boyle's and Ramos' work shows. Prior to joining CBS Chicago, Ramos worked as a data journalist for NBC News, covering a range of topics including energy, public transit, policing and the pandemic -- analyzing CDC data and reporting on vaccinations, hospitalizations and virus hotspots.

He was previously the data editor for WBEZ, Chicago's NPR station, where he examined the disparate impact of Chicago's ticketing and debt-collection practices as part of a collaboration with ProPublica Illinois. The work spurred numerous legislative reforms and triggered several class-action lawsuits. He has also done investigations into the city's towing practices.

## Data Visualization on Deadline

Data editor John Walton and his team at the BBC produce data visualizations both on deadline and for big investigative projects. As the data journalism editor for BBC News, he oversees journalists and designers who build primarily for digital graphics but they also work with BBC's broadcast team with converting some graphics for television.

One of the biggest projects the BBC undertook was building a COVID-19 infographic titled "UK: How Many Coronavirus Cases Are There in My Area?" that's been updated regularly since the start of the pandemic. It was produced as a collaboration between the BBC's Visual and Data Journalism teams.


<em>Figure</em> <em>5.1</em> BBC Coronavirus map


"The challenges here were large, although perhaps no different to the issues that face most data projects," Walton said. "Mainly, how to make a complex story understandable, and how to enable the audience to find their place in that story."

The BBC broke the story down into a series of key elements to allow the reader to keep up to date with the figures on the most important aspects of the pandemic. For example, they showed how many cases, deaths and hospitalizations were happening, and also whether the number for the pandemic was rising or falling.

They produced a daily overview graphic, for the top of the page, to give people a quick but comprehensive grasp of the story. They also had a local view of the cases happening in each area of the UK, which people could search through using their postal code, as well as a map where they could look up cases per 100,000 people.

Data journalism sharts with counting. How many homicides on your site this year? Last year? Two years ago? Then think through how you can represent that change over time, which we see in the next graphic.

By grouping information by dates, whether they be days, weeks, months, years, etc., you'll see patterns form. In Ramos' example, he appears to have been hungrier on Tuesday than he was on Friday, unless the tacos were different sizes. You need to take many factors into account when evaluating data.

Not only can you group data over time but also by what type. For example, what makes and models of cars are stolen most often in your city? What color are they? Model year? When are they stolen? Which month has the most or least? What day of the week is most common for having a car stolen? These questions can be answered through a simple sorting and filtering of crime data in a spreadsheet. Crime data is public record, and you can access it through law enforcement portals or by contacting the FOIA officer in your city or county police or sheriff's department.

## Organizing with Spreadsheets

Spreadsheets are handy for more than just data analysis. Freelance reporters use them to organize assignments and track budgets. Teams of investigative reporters use them to manage workflow and share updates and information.

ESPN national college football writer Adam Rittenberg uses Excel for larger projects in which he needs to share the information with colleagues. ## Elliott Ramos: Five Tips Every Young Data Journalist Should Know: 

1.  Familiarize yourself with whatever open data is available in your city or state. It can be a lifeblood of information that you can keep tabs on while others aren't looking.
2.  Document your methods and back up your data. When you get in the weeds, it'll help navigate you back to where you want your story to be.
3.  Put in FOIAs early and often. When stuff starts to happen, it's likely been happening for a while. Getting ahold of data ahead of everyone else is a surefire way to break the story.
4.  Interactive graphics are great, but there is an earnestness and wider understanding of bar and line charts. They can do a lot of heavy lifting for your story.
5.  Learning code is important, but Excel basics can carry you far in your career while you pick up the more complicated stuff. Great feats of journalism have been accomplished with pivot tables.

## Building Charts: Types of Charts

A good chart can make readers smarter. They show trends and patterns that might not be obvious when looking at numbers in raw data. But, as Cairo has pointed out, charts can lie. And charts can be confusing or misleading, especially if the journalist chooses the wrong chart to present the data. Dozens of types of charts are available, so to get started, let's focus on some of the basic charts and what data they can visualize.


::: section
### ***Bar/Column Chart***

Horizontal bar charts -- or vertical column charts -- feature rectangular bars with heights or lengths proportional to the values that they represent.

They're often used to compare year-to-year figures, percentages, etc., usually over time or between entities. For instance, it can show an increase in campaign contributions to a candidate over a 12-month period, or an increase in COVID-19 positive tests over a few weeks.


::: section
### ***Line Charts***

A line chart displays information as a series of data points called markers (data points) that are connected by straight or curved lines. Line charts are popular for sports data and stock market/finance charts, as it shows an asset's historical price action that connects a series of data points with a continuous line. The charts show continuous growth or decrease over a scale (typically time).

Line charts and bar charts can be interchangeable with some datasets, but bar charts break out individual points (years, etc.) whereas line charts represent continuous growth and change.


::: section
### ***Pie Charts and Treemaps***

Pie charts and treemaps are typically used for budgets or breaking down parts of a whole. The "pieces of the pie" must be proportional to the data corresponding with it. So if 10% of a city budget is going to the police department, the proportion in the graphic should amass 10% of the overall chart (or pie).

Treemaps are a popular and effective way to break down a budget and other data. They are ideal for displaying large amounts of hierarchically structured (tree-structured) data. The space in the visualization is split up into nested figures, usually rectangles that are sized and ordered by a quantitative variable. In this example: <https://bit.ly/treemapexample>, Chicago's 2022 city budget by department has been organized into a treemap. The hierarchy shows which departments are getting the most funds. For the smaller areas, you can hover over the interactive chart to see the data pop up for each department.


::: section
### ***Interactive and Animated Charts***

Interactive charts allow the user to control what they see: zooming, hovering over a marker, using a search bar or a pulldown menu, for instance. It also can enable the exploration of data via the manipulation of chart images, with the color, brightness, size, etc. This gets the reader engaged and *involved* in the data.

Animated charts illustrate data by creating changes and movements in the chart. Changes over time are a great way to use animated graphics, for example:

- 2020 COVID-19 Cases by State and Country (<https://bit.ly/covidcaseschart>)
- Baseball home runs in the steroid era ([https://public.flourish.studio/visualisation/8443792](https://public.flourish.studio))


::: section
### ***Venn Diagrams***

Lucidcharts defines Venn diagrams as "overlapping circles or other shapes to illustrate the logical relationships between two or more sets of items. Often, they serve to graphically organize things, highlighting how the items are similar and different." Common elements of the sets are represented by the areas of overlap among the circles, which shows relationships or common areas of interest.


::::: section
### ***Hierarchy and Organizational Charts***

According to [OrgCharting.com](https://www.OrgCharting.com), hierarchical charts are typically used to show roles, ranks, levels or positions of people or things. They are designed in a format that shows the relationships between the entities, with the top of the chart typically kept for the most important or significant part of the system. These diagrams look like organizational charts in some ways, and you can use org chart makers to draw hierarchy charts as well.

There are many other types of charts -- timelines, scatterplot, etc. Be sure to look through chart libraries in tools like Flourish, Datawrapper and other tools we explore later in this chapter to find more templates and designs. Many of the templates have "dummy data" in them so you can see how to format the spreadsheet to make the chart work seamlessly.

## ***Learn to Build Graphics by Playing with Legos*** 

Before opening any design software, here's a creative way for you to learn how to build a graphic.

Matt Waite, a professor of practice at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, was searching for a way in 2015 to get his students to understand form, function and scaling a graphic. He reached back to his childhood for the answer -- Legos.

Waite had his students create bar charts using a set of Lego blocks to illustrate Super Bowl statistics. Students had to grapple with scale: what did each block size represent: 10 yards? 20? 100?

Students worked in teams to scale the bar charts. Sticky notes can serve as labels and other "scaffolding" that Cairo refers to. The exercise is an effective way to help students understand design concepts before ever touching software.


<em>Figure</em> <em>5.9</em> University of Illinois at Chicago students use Legos to build bar charts during the second week of class. (Photo/Mike Reilley)


"The Legos let them focus on representing the data with shape," Waite told The American Journalism Review. "There wasn't any, 'Did I miss a semicolon? Did I use the wrong function? Did I press the wrong button?'"

In the past several years, professors from many other universities have experimented with using Legos. It works with many datasets -- homicides, COVID-19, sports, etc.
