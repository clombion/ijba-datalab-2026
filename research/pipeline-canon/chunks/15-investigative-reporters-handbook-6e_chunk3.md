## Delving Into Different Crimes

Each type of crime has different roots, different types of perpetrators and victims and different investigation techniques. Federal laws alone identify about 3,000 crimes. All that makes the challenge of covering law enforcement even greater for journalists.

### Murder

The Washington Post conducted an investigation of more than 50,000 homicides in major American cities, identifying neighborhoods where the arrest rate for killings was significantly lower than elsewhere, and cities where overall arrests are low. The Post reported that "some cities, such as Baltimore and Chicago, solve so few homicides that vast areas stretching for miles experience hundreds of homicides with virtually no arrests." In contract, the reporting found that some cities, including Atlanta, have high arrest rates, even in the most violent neighborhoods. The project analyzed why such disparities exist and focused to a great extent on the importance of police agencies having connections and lines of communication within the communities they protect.

This underscores that when reporters chronicle murder investigations, they need to explain the "why" behind the progress, or lack thereof. Journalists can get a better understanding of police investigative procedure by enrolling in police administration courses (offered by colleges with criminal justice degree programs); studying police manuals; and reading books like "Homicide: A Year on the Killing Streets," by former Baltimore Sun police reporter David Simon, or "Criminal Investigation," by James W. Osterburg and Richard H. Ward, former New York City policemen-turned-academics.

Reporters investigating murder cases should ask:

- How well did police record the crime scene through photographs, sketches and notes?
- How well did they collect and preserve physical evidence, allowing them to reconstruct the crime, identify the substance or object used to kill and link a suspect to the victim or crime scene?
- Have police considered all possible motives, including financial gain, sex and self-protection (during an interrupted burglary, for instance)?
- With evidence in hand and motive in mind, how thoroughly have detectives followed people trails and paper trails to gather further information?
- Have police made serious efforts to reach out to the community in which the crime occurred?
- Do homicide detectives refuse to ask for help, even when they need it? Detectives are considered the elite within the department, and often work long hours and earn overtime pay.
- Do the detectives use profilers? Profilers can be wrong, of course, but at times their educated guesses are uncannily accurate.

Additionally, the substance of homicide reports can make a difference on public perceptions. For instance, are police reports written in a way that shows how many murders are committed by strangers to the victim? This could affect levels of fear within the community.

There are several additional factors to consider:

- :::
  *Weapons.* It's important to take guns into consideration, whether that be understanding how they are acquired, or researching where subjects may be able to purchase a gun to get around local laws. Some law enforcement departments contribute to the gun problem by selling or trading older weapons, supposedly to avoid high disposal costs. The licensed dealers who end up with the old weapons then sell them to the public. Some of those weapons later become involved in murders.
  :::

- :::
  *People involved.* Every murder is major to the victim's relatives, friends, neighbors, schoolmates and coworkers. It's important to spend time gathering information on the deceased, talking to neighbors, coworkers and friends. Did the victim have a criminal record? Had he or she been in any recent trouble or had any tense relationships?

  It's also important to examine the background of the accused, not only looking for problems but for evidence of redeeming qualities. Remember that many innocent people are accused of crimes. This may seem like an obvious point but in many cases journalists are quick to accept the initial narrative provided by authorities.
  :::

- :::
  *Additional documents.* Autopsy reports and death certificates can provide detail for stories. The local coroner or medical examiner therefore should be cultivated as an adjunct to police. KHOU-TV in Houston used autopsy reports to uncover a pattern of cases in which men who were clearly the victims of violent crimes were determined to have committed suicide, even when the method of death (such as multiple gunshot wounds) would have been impossible to have been self-inflicted. This allowed officers to avoid spending time investigating these cases and for the department to report a dropping homicide rate.
  :::

When murders remain unsolved, it is legitimate to ask whether a specific law enforcement agency, perhaps working with medical examiners and other experts, can send those murders to a cold-case squad. There are examples throughout the nation of cold-case detectives solving long-ago crimes by looking at evidence with a fresh perspective.

### Rape

Rape is a devastating crime for the victim and can frequently end in homicide. But for many reasons, rape cases do not always seem to be a priority, and some police departments intentionally underreport them to keep crime statistics lower.

Jeremy Kohler, a reporter with the St. Louis Post-Dispatch, noticed St. Louis' low number of reported rapes compared with other cities. After a lengthy public records battle, Kohler reported in 2005 that St. Louis police had failed to file official reports on many sex crimes over the past 20 years. Instead, police wrote informal memos on cases so the rapes would not be counted in the city's crime statistics. In analyzing many of the records of cases, the Post-Dispatch discovered that police "often discounted claims by women who were reluctant to testify, easy to discredit or difficult to locate."

Marisa Kwiatkowski, whose investigative work at The Indianapolis Star exposed years of sexual abuse with USA Gymnastics, discussed in an IRE tip sheet ways reporters can look into allegations of sexual assault. Among her recommendations:

- Helpful public records include police reports; local, state and federal criminal court records; lawsuits; coroner records; disciplinary action records; complaints.
- When reaching out to a sensitive source, try to find an intermediary who knows about the allegations and might help connect you with the survivor. This could be an attorney, advocate or relative.
- When interviewing an assault survivor, overcommunicate. Be very clear about what you are doing, explain why you'll need to ask detailed questions, discuss the individual's willingness to be named and let him or her ask you questions before the interview begins.
- Ask a survivor to sign a waiver of confidentiality that allows you to access key records. It is also important to explain that you will need to investigative his or her story before you can report it, and explain why.

Journalists have investigated how many rape kits available to law enforcement investigators and DNA samples are actually analyzed. Investigations have been conducted throughout the U.S. showing that, time and again, law enforcement agencies are not processing these evidence kits. In addition to lack of action by police, it is also worth examining whether hospitals are equipped to take the evidence in a way that would hold up in court and to store it securely so that a defense attorney cannot question whether it could have been tampered with.

Those convicted of sexual assault are generally released into the community, where they are supposed to register as sex offenders. Most jurisdictions now aggressively publicize the names and addresses of such offenders, and journalists have done extensive investigations mapping where offenders are said to live to see how close to schools, school bus stops and day care centers the offenders are living. Reporters have also found, however, that in many states, the sex offender lists are out-of-date, with missing names and old addresses.

Parents and other members of the community understandably want to know if a convicted sex offender is living among them. Yet violence against convicted offenders and the stigmatization associated with the conviction could be considered a form of double jeopardy. Some convictions are the result of consensual sex between young teenagers for which the older teen was prosecuted. It's important to consider as many details involved in the case as possible to be sure its investigation is handled with the utmost care and concern.

### Domestic Violence

Domestic violence is one of the toughest crimes police and the criminal justice system must deal with. Journalists can delve into the training police officers receive and the procedures they follow in domestic violence cases.

Police usually fear domestic violence calls because of their volatility. Hostage situations are sometimes connected to domestic violence, as the perpetrator holds a weapon on the spouse, a significant other or a child. Even when no hostage is involved, a common occurrence is for a family member to call the police, who expose themselves to danger by responding to the incident, and then refuse to press charges.

Journalists in South Carolina documented the high number of domestic violence cases in their community and the subsequent lack of prosecution of the accused in an investigation described in [Chapter 2](#hou_9781319102845_ch02_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_m4qJC2nJr9). The journalists also took the time to write profiles of the victims and compiled a gallery of photos, creating a powerful way to personalize a crime that can become so common that communities stop paying attention.

### Narcotics

The war on drugs has been fought for decades without any clear resolution. Huge amounts of money and effort have been poured into the drug war, particularly on the southern border of the U.S.

In documenting how domestic law enforcement agencies are dealing with drug-related crime, journalists should look at their tactics, which tend to fall within these 10 categories:

- Observation arrests.
- Undercover operations.
- Examination of physical evidence.
- Electronic surveillance.
- Community-based intelligence gathering.
- User-control programs.
- Drug education by police officers.
- Asset forfeiture.
- Historical conspiracy investigations.
- Ancillary approaches, such as traffic enforcement or housing code violations.

Because so many local drug crimes involve interstate movement, journalists should develop contacts at the Drug Enforcement Administration, which is part of the U.S. Department of Justice. Drugs are mainly a cash business. Anyone trying to figure out how the cash reaches its ultimate destination has to understand money laundering through banks, otherwise legitimate retail businesses (which falsify receipts to account for the cash flow) and offshore entities. One job of law enforcement, with support from the DOJ's Criminal Division specialist prosecutors, is to detect money-laundering techniques.

Furthermore, journalists can explore how law enforcement handles the emergence of new narcotics. During the height of the methamphetamine crisis, The (Portland) Oregonian showed that Congress and the DEA could have stopped methamphetamine growth across the West during the 1990s, and that it still could. The newspaper explained how the drug can be controlled because it relies on chemical ingredients produced by only a handful of factories worldwide. The newspaper disclosed that two clampdowns on the legal trade of the chemicals caused meth shortages, prompting users to quit and meth-related property crime to fall. But, the newspaper said, the drug trade survived because of loopholes and lax enforcement.

As part of the investigation, The Oregonian examined DEA drug seizures, DEA-registered sellers of the drug, ephedrine drug shipments, ephedrine seizures, congressional records, the federal budget, federal audits, property tax records, patents, academic studies and public policy.

As opioids became the predominant drug problem for much of the U.S. a decade later, journalists explored many facets of the issue, from the political power of drug companies to illegal "pill mills" run by doctors to the federal DEA's efforts to control the epidemic's spread.

Unfortunately, illegal drug trafficking continues to flourish despite huge numbers of imprisoned dealers and users. Law enforcement officials, frustrated by the lack of progress in the drug war, will sometimes share information that allows journalists to more clearly see the network of drug trafficking. Some journalists also have successfully mapped drug raids in a particular jurisdiction, looking especially for repeat visits by law enforcement officers to the same site.

On the other hand, police have been found to make unfounded drug accusations or overly aggressive arrests, enabling them to seize property in a proceeding known as civil asset forfeiture. Forfeiture laws can allow an entire farm to be seized because marijuana plants are growing there, and it can be done before the grower is convicted or acquitted. A forfeiture law might allow seizure of an automobile after a routine traffic stop simply because the driver is carrying lots of cash and the police assume, however arbitrarily, that the cash is the fruit of a drug sale.

Most forfeiture laws and practices, despite efforts at reform, operate on the assumption of guilty until proven innocent. Reliable federal statistics on forfeiture can be found, but getting state and local statistics require many Freedom of Information requests.

The U.S. Marshals Service also maintains an online list of private companies contracted to sell seized assets, and even a current list of what's for sale. The incentive for an overzealous seizure policy is clear: Law enforcement agencies use the assets thus acquired to fatten their budgets and spend with little oversight.

Furthermore, drug cases are worthy of scrutiny for yet another reason: They rely heavily on the police use of informants. That is always risky; informants tend to be unreliable. Many have criminal records and receive a reduced prison sentence in exchange for their information. The Columbus Dispatch found that informants who faced drug trafficking and related murder charges fabricated evidence in exchange for favorable judicial dispensation, cash payments or both. "Professional" informants, especially those who are incarcerated and thus in a position to report jailhouse talk, incriminate cellmates. Some accusations, made for mercenary reasons, lead to wrongful convictions. (The phenomenon of wrongful convictions, and what journalists can do about them, is covered in [Chapter 11](#hou_9781319102845_ch11_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_X7iMQhEVT5).)

### Vice

Because drug transactions involve willing parties, legalization advocates sometimes refer to them as victimless crimes. Prostitution is also frequently referred to as a victimless crime. But society has gotten a better understanding of the issues surrounding human trafficking, and the problems created by prostitution have gained a higher profile. Young women and men can be forced into prostitution, sometimes having been brought to the U.S. on false pretenses, sometimes being hooked on drugs that are then used as a way to force the addict to work in exchange for a fix, sometimes having been abducted. Often, the prostitution trade has moved on to the internet, where journalists can easily see where businesses are flourishing.

Vice officers, who focus on issues such as prostitution, pornography and gambling, see other problems. They see those infected with HIV engaging in sex for money without warning their paying customers. They see prostitutes beaten and robbed by customers. They see the prostitutes' customers (already lawbreakers by definition) beaten and robbed by prostitutes and pimps. Some of the altercations end in murder.

Prostitutes, pimps and johns are also vulnerable to corrupt or brutal police officers. Some officers pressure prostitutes on the beat, taking a percentage of their earnings in exchange for freedom from arrest. Others demand free sex. By working human sources and looking for complaints filed with a police internal affairs unit, a citizens' review board, a licensing or disciplinary agency or in court, journalists might uncover officers taking advantage of their vice assignments. To supplement people and document trails, journalists can observe the life of the streets firsthand or carefully navigate websites. They will see how prostitutes sometimes organize their soliciting around the schedules of vice officers.

One of the most controversial duties of vice officers is policing pornography. When does a book, website, video or live show cross the line of constitutionally protected free expression to become illegal? Moreover, combating pornography that features children has become a significant issue. At the federal level, the DOJ's Criminal Division helps investigate and prosecute sexual exploitation of minors, including the possession, manufacture and distribution of child pornography, plus the often related crimes of selling, buying and transporting children to engage in sexually explicit conduct.

### Missing Persons

The topic of missing persons is fraught with statistical peril. In a groundbreaking story in the 1980s, The Denver Post did an award-winning investigation that showed that the statistics put out by missing children's groups were greatly exaggerated and in many cases involved custody disputes.

But there are numerous cases of missing persons that deserve attention. Families of missing persons are almost always upset at police. In the early stages of the investigation, family members perceive police as overly skeptical that foul play is involved. During later stages, if police believe the missing person might indeed have been met with violence, family members become upset that the case remains unsolved.

Journalists can explore police policy on missing persons cases by asking these questions:

- Do police always wait at least 24 hours before initiating a missing persons case, based on the unvarying assumption that the person might have disappeared voluntarily?
- If that is the policy, is the assumption behind it sometimes counterproductive?
- At what age does the policy kick in: 16, 18 or 21?
- When police receive a missing person report about a child, they usually operate differently. Should that mode of operation be the norm for missing adults, too?
- When the missing person turns out to be a runaway under legal age, to whom do police refer the juvenile?

Journalists should be professional in these cases more than in any others.

### Stolen Property

Crimes against persons are a higher priority for police than crimes against property. But property crimes might outnumber violent crimes by 6 to 1 in a jurisdiction. Property crimes are seldom solved, which can leave negative impressions about police among the citizenry.

Stolen property crimes leave police in a dilemma and lead to these questions:

- If the department is overworked, does it make sense to respond promptly to every call, especially when the amount of stolen property is small and nobody has been injured?
- Do police feel they must respond immediately to every burglar alarm at a private home or business, when studies indicate that as many as 97% are false, triggered by bad weather, faulty equipment, forgetful homeowners or roaming pets?
- Has the locality instituted fines or jail time for the property owner when a false alarm sounds?
- Do the police refuse to respond unless the alarm company verifies the trouble first? It often makes sense for law enforcement agencies to focus their resources on stolen property cases in which the criminal has threatened the owner of the property with violence or has acted violently.

One type of property crime of special interest to journalists working in large cities and resort areas involves thefts from hotels and motels. Are the buildings staffed by private security guards as well as covered by city police? If so, how effectively do they share their jurisdiction? Do they undercount or completely suppress crime reports to avoid damaging tourism?

Property crimes are also sometimes inside jobs. If a business or home is protected by a private security firm, that firm might use its knowledge of the premises and its ability to disconnect the alarm system temporarily from the main office to perpetrate the crime. Some property crimes are staged so the purported victims can file false insurance claims.

Another angle on such crimes is the destination of the stolen property. Journalists should examine pawnshops, flea markets and street-corner vendors. At the Sun-Sentinel, reporters Scott Glover and Evelyn Larrubia established a link between Fort Lauderdale's high burglary rate and the high concentration of pawnshops. The reporters obtained about 70,000 pawn slips collected by police from the shops over a year's span. Those slips showed several individuals visiting pawnshops more than 100 times during a year. Almost all the frequent pawnshop customers had criminal records. In some states, pawnshop owners must report every transaction to a statewide police database. If police are looking for an item stolen from a crime scene, the linked database should make discovery of the item possible within minutes, instead of the days, weeks or months it used to take police to search the hundreds of pawnshops in the state.

Automobile theft is one category of burglary with a high recovery rate when the motive is only a joy ride. An aggressive approach to seeking and checking abandoned vehicles can improve arrest rates because the thieves will have less time to cover their tracks.

A persistent reporter can uncover the locations of the chop shops, which are often in league with salvage yards, auto salesrooms, auto auctioneers, body repair garages and insurance companies.

### Arson

As journalists examine suspicious fires, they should ask the following:

- Has the owner incurred unexpected financial obligations: back taxes, alimony or child support payments, for example?
- Has the owner lost a business license, zoning case, property tax appeal or major investor?
- Is the building in violation of code? Have city building inspectors or fire department inspectors ordered expensive renovations?
- Were the owners planning to go out of business anyway?
- Is there reason to believe a disgruntled current or former employee set the fire?
- If the fire occurred at a personal residence, is it possible the owner planned to apply insurance proceeds to business debts?

Witnesses --- neighbors, tenants, competitors, business partners --- who saw the owner remove items from the building shortly before the fire could provide valuable information.

Not all cases of arson are connected to business difficulties. Other motives include pyromania, vandalism, revenge against an unfaithful spouse or concealment of another crime, such as incineration of a body after a murder. In a classic story, "the Star Tribune, Minneapolis, Minn." documented an arson ring led by a fire chief, his brother, plus 15 associates (including an insurance adjuster and construction company executive), who had reported 51 fires during a 25-year span.

When talking with officials, police academy trainers may assume the fire department will take the lead in such cases. But because police are often the first on the scene of a suspicious fire, they should know how to handle evidence of arson. Arson is usually a property crime foremost, but it always harbors the potential of maiming or killing human beings.

### Bombings

Journalists worldwide must be more prepared than ever to cover bombings. They should know police procedures for handling bomb threats and the protocol of investigations into bombings and bomb threats by local police and federal authorities in the U.S., such as the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives.

Bombs are generally the weapons of choice by the perpetrators of terrorism and hate crimes. Such crimes are not only violent in nature but also clearly violate civil rights laws, adding another dimension to the investigation. These are a few of the questions journalists can ask to familiarize themselves with the way the data is collected:

- Who keeps records on bomb threats locally?
- Who are the best contacts in the police and fire departments on bomb threats and bombings?
- Who is licensed locally to sell explosives?
- What are the ATF's investigative procedures for tracking explosives and investigating bombings?

### Traffic Violations

Traffic violations should be investigated both on how these incidents affect the public and how they are handled by police officers. Journalists should scrutinize police on traffic patrol. Journalists should also examine how police recruits are taught in the academy to handle traffic stops, and then determine whether officers are following the procedures to minimize dangers.

Catching drunken drivers is also part of the traffic patrol's work. Questions surrounding enforcement of driving-under-the-influence laws include whether police should set up sobriety checkpoints at which every passing driver is pulled over and tested and whether the blood-alcohol test being used by the police is the most accurate available.

The officers themselves can also be subject to scrutiny. Police scandals sometimes have their roots in traffic patrol. Reporters for KHOU-TV in Houston obtained data listing tickets given to drivers for running red lights, which in the city required a second officer as a witness. When they analyzed the data, they found that a group of officers were signing on as witnesses for tickets their colleagues issued when they could not have been at the scene because they were elsewhere in the city, writing other tickets at the same time.

Additionally, officers might work out a sweetheart deal with a towing company. As a result, when the traffic patrol sees an accident requiring a tow truck, the same company gets the call every time.

Speed traps or red light surveillance cameras set to ensnare motorists going too fast sometimes lead to ticketing of motorists who were obeying the law, as police realize they can abuse their authority to raise revenue, not to mention the potential for possible harassment that could occur to the drivers. Journalists can examine the number of tickets written per year by each officer to determine if there is a quota system.

Another potential story revolves around the targeting of minority motorists for traffic violations, even when they are not breaking any law. Racial profiling is the term used to describe such activity. Newsrooms throughout the U.S. have done numerous stories on racial profiling, and some states have mandated the creation of databases on traffic stops as a way of combating racial profiling.

A TV station in Austin used ticket data and dashcam video to build the case that Texas Department of Public Safety officers were pulling over Hispanic people and listing them as white, to skew the data. Their report included footage --- recorded by the officer's own dashboard camera --- in which a Hispanic driver is pulled over, and when he cannot reply to the officer's question about race (asked in English) because he doesn't speak English, the officer lists him as white.

If a traffic stop leads to an arrest for a nontraffic offense, journalists can ask whether police had legitimate reasons for their search. What is police policy? Journalists can also check to see whether judges regularly set defendants free as a result of traffic stops that result in nontraffic charges.

### Organized Crime and White-Collar Crime

Organized-crime syndicates are often involved in drugs and vice. Seemingly legitimate businesses controlled by organized crime families and other syndicates should be the focus of coverage, in accord with the maxim "follow the dollar."

Some organized-crime enterprises know no ethnic or national boundaries, making it especially challenging for journalists to identify them with precision. Much of the effort to combat organized crime is federalized, which means journalists should develop sources within the U.S. Department of Justice's Criminal Division, as well as the Federal Bureau of Investigation.

Meanwhile, white-collar criminals practice organized crime without physical violence in the financial markets, stealing millions of dollars while misleading investors and the public. White-collar crime involves a network of individuals linked together in an illegal relationship. With white-collar crime frequently emanating from corporate suites, the crime can consist of manipulating energy prices, avoiding taxes, selling sham products or violating environmental laws. The policing of white-collar crimes usually comes from a government agency not normally associated with law enforcement, such as the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission or the National Association of Security Dealers, or from divisions of other departments. Some policing is regulatory. For example, the SEC enforcement files sometimes lead to criminal investigations and can tip off a reporter to businesspeople or businesses that should be scrutinized.

The criminal investigations are led by state attorneys general or U.S. Attorney offices, and the filings by those offices often outline and make clear the alleged criminal wrongdoing. Tracking organized crime can cross international boundaries. The Organized Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP) was created in Eastern Europe to cover the intersection of crime and government officials. It has reporters in multiple countries and excellent online resources for tracking connections between businesses and officials.

Journalists should ask law enforcement agencies these questions:

- Does the agency have a detection policy, or does it simply wait for disgruntled insiders to blow the whistle?
- Are any officers trained in uncovering enterprise crime?
- Is surveillance --- through undercover infiltration, wiretapping, neighborhood stakeouts and the like --- part of the crime-fighting strategy?
- Are investigators using laws aimed at money laundering to full advantage?

### Juvenile Crime

About one-third of arrests for violent crimes nationwide have historically involved young people under age 18. Unless police, prosecutors and judges agree that a youthful perpetrator should be tried as an adult, the name of the accused is normally kept confidential. The idea is to give juveniles a chance to turn their lives around before being publicly labeled as criminals.

But journalists need to determine if the juvenile is a habitual offender and find out how the law enforcement system deals with that kind of offender and juvenile crime. ([Chapter 11](#hou_9781319102845_ch11_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_X7iMQhEVT5) explains the juvenile justice system in more depth.)

Sometimes juvenile criminals are part of gangs. Some law enforcement agencies train officers as gang specialists and place them in an anti-gang unit. That can be a useful tactic, but a journalist needs to look at the entire approach to juvenile crime and how law enforcement is working with parents, schools, social workers and other agencies.


## Investigating Other Aspects of Law Enforcement

There are numerous other areas of law enforcement to investigate, ranging from administration and budget, medical examiners and evidence rooms to civil rights violations. Good journalists can find investigative stories even in the areas that seem the most rudimentary and routine.

### Patrol Officers, Dispatchers and 911 Operators

The Fort Worth Star-Telegram once reported that the Fort Worth Police Department received a call every 39 seconds --- what some law enforcement officials term "the tyranny of 911" because so many of the calls are not emergencies or are phony. With 942 officers, the department had a ratio of 2.1 officers to every 1,000 citizens, which was then below average for large cities. As a result, some calls labeled "emergency" failed to receive the desired emergency response. The story tracked one emergency call, from a woman with a 2-year-old daughter who rang 911 to report four men trying to break into her home. When the call arrived, the 911 operator (one of seven then on duty) sent the information to one of four police dispatchers working that shift, but no on-duty patrol officer was available.

The Star-Telegram's investigation found that one-third of all calls received did not meet the police department's own response-time goals. Response times are often excellent in some parts of the city (usually the most affluent) and substandard in other parts (usually the poorest and most crime-ridden). Overall response time is determined by a variety of factors, and thus consists of multiple data sets. Many news organizations document response time disparities by analyzing computerized records of 911 calls, and this can be a useful resource for journalists putting together an investigation. It's important to keep in mind, however, that law enforcement agencies may also erase 911 recordings after a set period of time, so journalists must move quickly if they want data from the recordings.

Journalists can examine many topics within these calls, including the quality of the local 911 system, asking whether it is enhanced so it does not have to rely on callers to provide their location, an especially important matter with the proliferation of cellphones, whose calls can be easily tracked now. They will also discover how patrolling means hours of routine activity interspersed by minutes of action. Furthermore, they can examine whether patrol officers are using ineffective techniques, such as arriving at the scene with sirens and lights on.

### Evidence Rooms

If evidence is misplaced, destroyed or stolen, a guilty person might go free. A Kansas City Star story told of 13,000 guns jutting from shelves and dangling from hangers in a police subbasement. The story also noted "evidence bags stuffed with pills, pot, speed and LSD spilling from shelves in a long, store-sized room." Kansas City police were trying to control internal theft from the storage areas, but without total success.

Journalists can ask these questions to understand procedures:

- How often do officers take evidence for personal use?
- Whose responsibility is it to track down mishandled evidence?
- What happens to officers who are caught stealing evidence?

### Coroners and Medical Examiners

In some counties, coroners are elected; they may be funeral home directors or other people without medical training. In urban areas, there may be an appointed medical examiner, often a physician trained in forensic pathology, rather than an elected coroner. Journalists should inquire whether a medical examiner has other specialized training that might help increase the certainty of the autopsy results.

Reporters can check whether the findings of the coroner or medical examiner at the early stage of an investigation disagree with the preliminary findings police have announced. Medical examiners and coroners are in a peculiar position, balancing on the tightrope between law and medicine. They are responsible for determining the deceased's identity, plus the time and cause of death, which are medical questions; they also determine the legal issue of the manner of death. The cause of death refers to the medical reason for death, such as heart failure or a knife wound. The manner of death refers to the circumstances --- natural, accidental, homicidal or suicidal. Despite many successes by coroners and medical examiners, investigations in the IRE Resource Center show incompetence and corruption, too. As a result, killers literally got away with murder, or innocent people were convicted.

### Civil Rights and Community Relations

Many police departments have negative images in communities of color. Journalists can look into what law enforcement agencies are doing to alleviate those negative images and whether the image is justified. Some questions to ask are these:

- Is the department hiring officers who reflect the diversity of the jurisdiction where they work?
- Are officers from all ethnic, racial and religious backgrounds trained in dealing with sensitive, sensitized portions of the population?
- How do police deal with panhandlers, transients, squatters in vacant buildings and other down-and-out citizens who are not breaking any laws?
- If community relations positions exist, are the officers assigned to them full time? Or is the community relations work an additional, occasional responsibility?

When trouble refuses to die down, do local police ever consult the Community Relations Service of the U.S. Department of Justice? Its mission is to support communities in preventing racial and ethnic tensions, as well as hate crimes, a category added to the Service's mission in 2009. The U.S. DOJ's Civil Rights Division often enters locales far from Washington, D.C., to investigate and prosecute discrimination in any program receiving federal financial assistance. The division's investigators and prosecutors can extend their reach even when no federal money is involved, as in cases when intimidation is used to deprive citizens of specific rights, such as voting, housing, employment, education, health care, use of public facilities or exercising religious preference.



## Resources for Investigating Law Enforcement

There are numerous resources for every kind of investigation into law enforcement. The suggestions below touch on just a few of them.

### Crime Statistics

Journalists often rely on the FBI's Uniform Crime Reports (UCR) for statistics that can be compared from state to state, or that track change in a given location over time. The UCR data is valuable but has limitations. The data covers only eight categories: murder and non-negligent manslaughter, rape, robbery, aggravated assault, burglary, larceny/theft, motor vehicle theft and arson. Reporting is voluntary, and some definitions have changed over time (including in the rape category), so making comparisons and drawing nationwide conclusions can be tricky.

Another problem with the data is that law enforcement agencies that funnel information to the FBI have varying systems of crime statistics management. In some states, certain police departments refuse to provide thorough data to the state police or the FBI. The lack of cooperation can render law enforcement ineffective, as when police fail to catch a serial rapist because they are unaware of an unreported sexual assault with the same modus operandi in a nearby community. Inadequate statistics also mean that police within the jurisdiction may be unable to deploy their patrol officers and specialized units efficiently.

Numbers can lie locally as well as nationally. Many journalists have examined local crime statistics and found patterns in which certain crimes were being mischaracterized by police. This is especially common in cases of sexual assault, which can be categorized as a different type of assault or even a completely unrelated category. The result is that the number of crimes in a certain category appear lower than they actually are, making a geographic location look safer than it actually is.

The U.S. Bureau of Justice Statistics is an excellent companion to UCR data because it conducts surveys that collect crimes that some citizens don't think are worth reporting because of lack of police response. Journalists can also inquire into police practices that discourage citizen reporting, such as refusing to accept complaints by telephone.

A reported crime is considered cleared by police if it results in an arrest or in the gathering of enough evidence that an arrest could have been made if not for external factors. When the emphasis is on clearing crimes for statistical reasons, quite a few arrested suspects turn out to be innocent. If they are eventually freed due to lack of evidence, the line on the statistical chart might still read "crime solved." As reporter Ryan Gabrielson points out in an IRE tip sheet, it's important to understand what clearance figures really mean. Was an arrest made, but then the case wasn't pursued by prosecutors? Was an investigation suspended or shut down because of lack of results?

Gabrielson also recommends looking into conviction rates:

> When dealing with conviction rates, it's best to start with what's missing. Request logs of all cases police send to prosecutors and look for types of crimes that don't result in charges. If police reported 50 sexual assaults last year, but prosecutors only brought charges in five of them, that's a red flag. Prosecutors might be avoiding difficult cases that can deflate their conviction rates. Alternatively, they might be avoiding cases in which police investigations didn't gather sufficient evidence.

### Budgets and Contracts

As discussed in other chapters, journalists can uncover stories by scrutinizing law enforcement budgets. In the case of police, overtime payments might indicate the shifts on which additional officers are needed to combat crime increases or on which specialists are spending extra hours combating a particular type of crime that has spiraled out of control. Journalists also might be able to determine from the budget how much money is used to pay informers and see, when informers are used, whether they are already in prison or under threat of imprisonment.

In a number of cities, journalists have also used budget records to show that officers nearing retirement are being given large amounts of overtime. The reason? If a city has a pension system that bases retirement payments on the last few years of employment, accumulating overtime can result in a much larger payout. This can have fiscally damaging consequences to a pension fund because individual officers are getting more money than expected, leaving less for future generations.

Contracts should be scrutinized, too. The Grapevine Sun in Texas received a tip from a police officer resulting in a story describing questionable spending for repair and maintenance of city police vehicles. The stories told how the chief funneled business to a local garage where his son-in-law worked, paying excessive prices for parts and labor. The reporter relied on repair shop invoices, vehicle maintenance logs, request-for-repair forms filed by police and monthly department expenditure reports.

Some agencies operate outside their budgets because of circumstances they could not have predicted, such as the sudden increased availability of a particular street drug like methamphetamine or heroin. A concerted effort to fight meth or heroin sales can bust a budget quickly, but police administrators might believe they have no choice but to accumulate a deficit.

### Records Divisions and Public Information Officers

Journalists are always interested in the accessibility and completeness of police records. Complaints initiated by citizens and those initiated by police themselves ought to be organized logically. Too many departments organize them only by date and time of day. That is of little help to journalists who have only the name of the complainant or the alleged offender.

Journalists should scrutinize the reporting forms. Do they capture the relationship between the victim and the offender? If not, how can police know in a systematic way whether they are more often fighting crimes involving people who know each other or crimes involving strangers? Are crime statistics charted by neighborhood rather than citywide only? Relying on citywide statistics does not allow police to plan their crime fighting according to each precinct where officers are stationed. For example, a citywide drop in aggravated assaults might mask an increase in knife fights between rival gangs within a particular precinct.

Public information officers can help journalists figure out how reports are organized and can assist with accessibility to the documents or databases. Crimes on campuses of colleges that accept federal money are covered by a specific federal law that requires annual reports in a specific format. Many campuses post and update the data on a website. (The higher education section in [Chapter 13](#hou_9781319102845_ch13_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_pKN1iBrXBE) covers this topic more fully.)

### People Trails

As with many other investigations of a business or bureaucracy, current and former employees should be cultivated as much as possible. Sources in other law enforcement agencies can provide perspective, especially if the agencies have worked together on solving crimes that cross jurisdictional lines. Journalists can meet connected sources simply by hanging out in police station waiting areas. Private detectives, also known as private investigators, can be sources about what is going on inside police departments. Many are former police officers. They do favors for police and sometimes receive favors in return. Private detectives sometimes help journalists by obtaining unlisted telephone numbers, running credit checks and finding missing persons. Journalists should expect to pay expenses for such assistance some of the time.

If the prosecutor's office or the police department is in need of reform, there is always the chance one will blow the whistle on the other. Police often dislike prosecutors because they will not file charges without question. Prosecutors often become angry at police for bringing poorly prepared cases or for violating suspects' rights. Each side possesses files on specific cases that journalists would not normally see. The same is true for public defenders and defense attorneys in private practice. (Prosecutors and defense attorneys as sources are treated more fully in [Chapter 11](#hou_9781319102845_ch11_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_X7iMQhEVT5).)

Victims' compensation programs mandated by federal or state law can lead to cultivation of citizen sources if a victim's family members are reluctant to talk about the crime itself. The National Organization for Victim Assistance and the National Association of Crime Victim Compensation Boards can provide specifics about local programs (benefits vary widely), as well as techniques for gaining access to reluctant potential sources. The DOJ's Office for Victims of Crime tries to ensure respectful treatment of victims throughout the nation.

Other types of sources who are aware of police conduct include coroners, medical examiners, funeral home directors, emergency room doctors, nurses, ambulance personnel, citizens crime commissions, scholars in university criminal justice departments, psychologists and psychiatrists.

Journalists can connect with sources by covering crime as a public health issue. Among the questions to ask:

- How typical is this type of violent crime in this community?
- What is the relationship of the people involved?
- Have those people been in trouble with the law before?
- Do they have jobs? Do they earn enough to support a family above the poverty level?
- If a gun is involved, how did it come into the possession of the accused? Is it legally registered?
- Is alcohol involved? What about narcotics? If they are involved, how did they come into the possession of the accused? Is it easy to obtain alcohol nearby? Narcotics?
- After a crime, who pays medical expenses, law enforcement investigative expenses and salaries of courthouse personnel? Put another way, what is the tangible cost to the larger society?

### Document and Data Trails

Journalists will find that police department manuals describe established document trails. After studying such a manual, a journalist might know precisely what form to request and thus might get it by dint of that knowledge.

The National Crime Information Center, coordinated by the FBI, contains a wealth of data that could play a role in a document or data trail. Records are entered by local law enforcement agencies. Each state maintains a control terminal, which is supposed to help with data input and retrieval. The NCIC files include missing persons; wanted fugitives; stolen and retrieved but unidentified guns; and missing stocks, bonds, cash, license plates and boats. The computer system is off limits to journalists, but sometimes an insider leaks information from it. Such data could be invaluable to putting the pieces together in an investigation.

Search warrants filed in local courthouses can help journalists break open a case. The San Francisco Bay Guardian used search warrants while investigating the apparently unnecessary fatal shooting by police of an Oakland, Calif., resident in his home. "According to the seven-page warrant," the story said, "police were looking for equipment used to alter the encoded magnetic strip on credit cards and any goods purchased with the stolen or altered cards." The warrant named the man's wife, who was not home at the time of the fatal raid. The raid turned up no such evidence as listed on the search warrant, and the dead man's wife never was arrested.

If police conduct a warrantless search, that is a story in itself. The Las Vegas Review-Journal investigated incidents in which the police failed to obtain search warrants. Three officers eventually faced criminal charges in connection with the death of a man during a warrantless search. Warrantless searches are often conducted on a hunch that could reveal bias or a lack of respect for the law, but they are allowed under certain categories such a felony arrest in public, traffic stops for reasonable suspicion, or suspicion of an ongoing criminal activity.

Additionally, local jails maintain databases listing people who have been or are incarcerated there. These databases contain such information as the person's name, date of birth, address, gender, race and physical description; the name of the arresting agency; the date of commitment to the county jail; who made bond and how; the discharge date; and the suspected offense.

# Chapter 13 Investigating Education


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_q2TO7OVG5Y" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Dig into issues including testing and quality in K--12 education.
- Investigate school shootings and other acts of violence on campus.
- Examine equity issues, from race and gender to services for students with special needs.
- Conduct a variety of investigations in higher education and at public and private schools.
- Analyze the performance and funding of sports departments.

</aside>

No beat seems to touch more lives than education. Reporters who cover elementary and secondary education, or colleges and universities, have the opportunity to dig into every facet of a society. Do schools provide equal opportunities for all? Are tax dollars being spent equitably? Are all children, regardless of race, gender or special needs, getting what they need? The education beat has evolved into an area of great focus for enterprise and investigation. The topics can include teacher certification, deteriorating facilities, faulty test scores, school discipline, campus crime, academic fraud, tuition loans, questionable contracts, fraternity misdeeds and the influence of corporations on education.

While there are numerous intersections between secondary education and higher education --- such as SAT scores --- this chapter will look first at K--12 education and then move to higher education, since most resources and publications are split along those lines.



## Issues in K--12 Education

Examining quality and equity in public schools is essential, and increasingly there are more resources available to journalists who want to dig into the issues. Everything from test scores to disciplinary records to student--teacher ratios are readily available in most states because schools must report this information to the federal government, and that data is public information.

### Test Scores

Many newsrooms focus almost exclusively on standardized test scores to measure the effectiveness of schools and districts. There is an overreliance on this data because many factors can influence a school's performance on statewide testing, from the economic demographics of a school to the way resources are distributed by districts. That said, there are good stories to be done by examining school testing data.

Holly Hacker and Joshua Benton of The Dallas Morning News expanded a story about one school's alleged cheating on standardized tests into a piece about cheating across the state. Measuring performance over time, Hacker used regression analysis to show suspiciously remarkable improvements among historically low-performing schools. She and Benton pinpointed one impoverished school where the fourth-graders had trouble adding and subtracting yet nearly all the fifth-graders got perfect scores on the math portion of the Texas Assessment of Knowledge and Skills.

In another story, Hacker and Benton used Texas reading test scores to show that the top school in terms of third-grade test scores was an unlikely champion. They wrote, "Wilmer Elementary --- a perennial underachiever in a district many consider the state's worst --- beat out the scores of 3,212 other elementary schools. But substantial evidence, including a Dallas Morning News data analysis, indicates that cheating may be behind that success."

Hacker and Benton noted that the school's high scores for third-graders were not repeated in higher grade levels: "The amazing scores came only in the one grade where poor test scores have severe consequences --- and, according to cheating experts, educators have a greater incentive to fudge."

Some investigations by newsrooms have found that the low income of a family can sometimes predict low test scores, but others have found that other factors can come into play that instead shift responsibility to teachers and district administrators.

While it is important to report on the test results, keep in mind that the placement of children in classes on the basis of standardized tests has itself been questioned. Professor Stanley Greenspan and Washington Post reporter Jacqueline L. Salmon, co-authors of the book "Playground Politics," wrote that standardized testing is unfair and ineffective: "Most standardized tests don't pick up on many of the skills that really count in the 'real world' --- creativity, persistence, dedication, problem-solving abilities, the ability to think on your feet even when tense and anxious, a gift for seeing the 'big picture' and a gut-level instinct for making the right move."

### School Violence

In city after city, schools have been putting new security plans and measures into effect because of concerns over school shootings. Yet journalists have found many flaws in those strategies. The South Florida Sun-Sentinel won a 2019 Pulitzer Prize in Public Service for its examination of the flaws in safety planning, and the execution of those plans, when a gunman opened fire at a high school in Parkland, Fla. Seventeen people were killed and more were injured. Reporters at the Sun-Sentinel immediately went to work to not only paint a complete picture of what happened, but to look at the measures in place that failed to protect students. Here's how they described their work in their submission for an IRE Award:

> In the days and weeks immediately after the shooting, our reporters quickly broke a series of stories that indicated deeper, systemic problems: delayed security cameras that duped rescuers; multiple deputies who hid instead of rushing in to rescue children; police radios that failed; insufficient active-shooter training of school children and rescuers; an ever-growing number of red flags that went unheeded along the killer's path to Marjory Stoneman Douglas High.

Looking at the documents that the paper collected provides a road map for reporters covering similar tragedies in the future:

> We filed hundreds of records requests with the school district, the sheriff's office, local police departments, the state law enforcement agency, state prosecutors, the FBI. We sought police and fire rescue reports, radio transmissions, 911 calls, surveillance video, body camera footage, personnel files, emails, school safety reports, discipline statistics, and draft versions of reports.

Reporters also used data from a state safety reporting system, where schools were supposed to report a wide range of incidents. They highlighted schools that reported few incidents, then checked news reports and discovered that assaults, rapes and murders had occurred at those schools but had never been reported. This lack of reporting has been documented in districts around the country. In five Tacoma, Washington, high schools that KIRO-TV in Seattle looked at, "there were 468 students disciplined for fights, 180 assaults and 123 violent threats," yet police were called just 13 times for fights, 95 times for assaults and 29 times for threatening behavior. "District policy mandates police get called whenever a student gets assaulted or hurt in a fight, but that doesn't usually happen," the station reported. Digging into whether districts follow their own policies when dealing with violence, and a host of other issues, is fertile ground for journalists.

### School Discipline

One sensitive question to explore is whether some teachers and administrators abuse authority by singling out students of particular racial or ethnic groups for punishment. Using data collected by the U.S. Department of Education, ProPublica built a national database to examine racial disparities in school discipline, among other things. The material they used --- Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC), administered by the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights --- has also been used by many local news organizations. When paired with population data, journalists can determine whether students of color are being disproportionately disciplined. ProPublica also used the data to examine whether white students were more likely to be placed in gifted and talented programs.

Chalkbeat, in an investigation using the data to examine schools in Indiana, found that "white students are three times as likely as black students to be enrolled in gifted programs, state data ... show. In high school, white students are twice as likely as black students to be enrolled in Advanced Placement classes, according to federal education data."

Misbehavior by students in school often is related to trouble at home. When teachers and administrators hear about sexual abuse, domestic violence, divorce and extreme poverty, what steps are they supposed to take? How can they support the student during the school day? Some misbehavior is tied to alcohol and other drug abuse. Police officers in the narcotics division might discuss their perceptions of the school drug problem. Journalists must verify the alleged problem with arrest reports, records of overdoses, results of locker searches and interviews with students in treatment.

### Student Retention

In some schools, students automatically progress to the next grade no matter how poor their academic performance or how disruptive their behavior. In other schools, such students must repeat grades. A journalist can compare a high school's pass-on, repeat and dropout rates, daily attendance and graduation percentages with national and state averages.

One compelling topic to look at is whether poor-performing students are being promoted to the next grade to make the school look good, as reporters Hacker and Benton in Dallas found. Another topic to investigate is dropout rates. Some school districts ask students why they drop out in order to provide statistics about the reasons. But investigative journalists have found dropout statistics to be confusing, unreliable or outright false. Education Week reported that states, school districts and federal researchers may all use different methods and definitions to tally up how many students have dropped out of high school. Thus the results can vary enormously --- which is why high school dropout numbers can be notoriously unreliable.

The LA Weekly reported on how an inner-city Los Angeles high school masked its dropout rate by keeping "ghost" students on its enrollment list. The fraud gave the school "a breather from chronic overcrowding" and allowed it to keep its funding. It pointed to the larger problem that the state of California did not conform to widely accepted standards for counting dropouts honestly and accurately. In fact, class overcrowding is a common problem. Journalists should visit schools in a district and look for tangible evidence of overcrowding. One measure is the student-teacher ratio; another rough but objective measure is square feet per student, which can help determine whether a facility is sufficient for the number of students in attendance.

### Examining Resources

There are a number of ways to examine the quality of education in individual schools and districts.

#### Textbooks

Journalists should investigate textbooks when considering a school's choice of curriculum. In many districts, teachers, administrators, parents or school board members may sit behind closed doors to choose textbooks. Despite their deliberation, they may choose books that are factually inaccurate, poorly written or biased. In some school districts, religious and ideological groups pressure school boards and publishers to delete or change the text.

By understanding the selection process and seeking documents related to it, a journalist can ferret out which books were chosen and which rejected, and why.

Additionally, in many communities, schools or teachers may not be able to afford books or may be provided extremely out-of-date materials due to lack of funding.

#### Class Size

Investigating class size is not complicated because it is a quantitative matter. Journalists writing about class size should know about state laws, district policies and national guidelines for class size. Journalists should also investigate issues that can lead to larger class sizes. Is there a shortage of teachers? Does each school receive equitable funding and supplies? It's also important to look into whether class sizes differ in different demographic areas within a school district. Do schools that serve wealthier neighborhoods have smaller classes?

#### Technology and Corporate Sponsorship

As schools become increasingly dependent on computers, committees created to choose hardware and software will hold the same type of power as those choosing textbooks. Schools attended by wealthy families and with parent-teacher associations that are good at raising money tend to have more computers than schools in low-income areas. If a computer manufacturer offers machines at little or no cost, should the school board accept, thereby helping the company build its customer base to the exclusion of competitors? Corporations are influencing the availability and content of instructional materials in classrooms by supplying them for free in times of tight budgets. Local businesses get their messages into schools through the noblest sounding of programs, such as "Partners in Education." A business "adopts" a school. Journalists should find out what the school's policies are on accepting corporate sponsorship.

An education reporter at the Columbia Daily Tribune in Missouri examined money and resources provided by PTA funding and corporate partners in elementary schools. She found that some schools in wealthier areas had extra money for safer playgrounds and better lab equipment, or business partners that provided new computers, while schools in low-income areas often had no PTA at all, and in one example a fast-food chain served as a corporate partner that provided coupons for free burgers.

The urban-rural divide can also create great inequities in schools. The lack of high-speed broadband access in many rural areas can create huge limitations on what schools can provide to their students, as well as students' ability to do research and use online teaching tools. Because much broadband funding comes from federal and state money, examining how those funds are being distributed can lead to important stories.

### Special-Needs Students

In most schools, the term "special needs" is used to refer to children who have mental or physical disabilities. Federal regulations identify more than a dozen disabilities, and these can present themselves in a number of ways, both visible and invisible.

An investigative journalist should inquire whether the school district neglects special-needs children, forcing them into distant state schools, private schools or home schooling. If the local school district does have special-education programs, a journalist can find out whether the special-needs students are segregated or mainstreamed. Mainstreaming, sometimes called inclusion, means that the special-needs children take classes with nondisabled students of the same age at the neighborhood school. Both approaches --- segregation and inclusion --- raise difficult issues such as extra costs and how best to balance serving both special-needs students and the rest of the student population.

In one notable example, the Houston Chronicle was a finalist for the Pulitzer Prize for its investigation of how disabled children were being denied specialized care in Texas schools. Reporter Brian Rosenthal documented the way tens of thousands of children were removed from such programs because of a wording change made in state law that had gone unnoticed when it was passed. Rosenthal learned of the story while sitting in on a public hearing and found many of the subjects for his story by reaching out through social media to parents whose children had been kicked out of their classes. Rosenthal's work illustrates the importance of attending routine meetings and hearings and of using all the tools at a reporter's disposal to humanize their work.

In another story, U.S. News and World Report found too many students being placed in special education because of financial incentives from the federal government. The results: students with relatively minor learning disabilities being unfairly labeled as impaired learners, a bloated bureaucracy with a separate transportation system and angry parents and teachers.

Because the federal government requires extensive documentation for every special-needs student, schools should have plenty of material for journalists to examine. Upset parents may be willing to share their children's records, as well.

### Racial, Class and Gender Equity

Although many school districts are considered officially desegregated, minority students sometimes end up clustered in certain schools. A journalist should look closely at the student numbers by school to see whether the school is truly desegregated. Where the public schools are open to all, white children may attend all-white private schools, creating de facto segregation. The white children remaining in the public schools may come from low-income families, creating segregation by economic class. Journalists should also study whether busing students outside their neighborhoods, often over long distances, has a negative impact on their learning.

A team of reporters in Tampa, Florida, took a comprehensive look at the quality differences in schools in an ambitious Failure Factories project. The Pulitzer Prize--winning project examined how decisions made by school leaders led to a drop in quality in several schools that served a largely low-income, black population. The team in Tampa made dozens of record requests and examined a host of documents and data to prove that the district had made a series of decisions that made failure at the schools inevitable. Among the data they used were 10 years of standardized testing data, school-level financial reports, teacher personnel records and complaints about teachers (along with the subsequent investigations).

When investigating gender equity, journalists should explore these issues:

- In physical education classes and after-school athletics, are female students given the same options as male students?
- Do interscholastic sports for females receive as much funding as sports for males?
- Are female students encouraged to take advanced science and math classes and are they included in extracurricular activities and clubs in those areas?

### School Choice and Charter Schools

Sometimes students attend schools outside their neighborhoods because of mandatory busing to achieve desegregation or because of centralized special-needs classes. Other times, the reasons are questionable or skirt the rules, as when parents use permits to get their children into schools outside their neighborhoods because of alleged child care arrangements or by claiming medical problems. Beyond such individual situations, school choice has become a sociopolitical movement.

The rise of charter schools also provides story ideas for journalists. The Education Writers Association, a great resource for story ideas and explanation of key issues, describes charters as schools that are publicly funded but run by independent boards, though they can also be operated by school districts. Teachers often aren't unionized (and often are paid less or have worse benefits than teachers in other schools) and do not have to adhere to the same regulations as traditional schools. Critics worry that charters erode funding and support for the public education system and can pull students away. Supporters, EWA notes, say charters can be more innovative and can provide families, especially low-income ones, with more options.

As the number of charter schools has risen, so have press inquiries into them. A 2017 investigation by ProPublica found that some schools in Orlando, Florida, send their struggling students to charter schools, thereby boosting their graduation numbers and standardized test scores. The results for those sent away, however, were alarming. Once enrolled at the charter school, "hundreds of \[students\] exit quickly with no degree and limited prospects," a ProPublica investigation found. "The departures expose a practice in which officials ... have for years quietly funneled thousands of disadvantaged students ... into alternative charter schools that allow them to disappear without counting as dropouts."

Quality can vary widely at charter schools, as it can at public schools. But charters often do not get the scrutiny that school districts receive because they don't have public board meetings and many news organizations' beat structures focus on traditional school district models. There is great opportunity for a beat reporter to uncover important stories by expanding that scope.


## Investigating School Personnel and Facilities

In many cases, investigating education --- as with many other beats --- can begin with investigating the people and places involved: teachers, administrators, school staff and the school buildings themselves.

### Teachers

When Chicago Tribune team members examined the city's schools, they found a fourth-grade classroom in which all 22 students had to attend summer school. The reason? Their teacher was so incompetent that the children failed to learn enough to move to fifth grade. Four previous schools had tried unsuccessfully to remove the teacher from classroom work.

Obviously, it pays for journalists to examine the quality of teaching. States require teachers to pass an examination; many states use the Praxis exam administered by the Educational Testing Service. A journalist can check a state's certification standards, including how they compare with other states. In Mississippi, reporters found that the required score was so low that only 3% of those taking the test nationally would have failed.

In Florida, a series of articles in the Sarasota Herald-Tribune examined equality among schools by looking specifically at teacher qualifications. The reporters found that thousands of teachers struggled to pass basic skills tests and that one in three teachers failed an exam at least one time. Furthermore, the reporters found that the teachers who struggled the most on the tests were funneled into poor and minority schools, while those who did well went to wealthier white schools.

Reporters should look at the larger picture of teachers' responsibilities and workloads to contextualize their situation. Are their class sizes larger than normal? Do they take on other activities within the school outside of their teaching? Teacher burnout is an underreported problem. Some advocates add that low teacher salaries are a factor causing burnout and affecting the overall quality of instruction.

In addition, journalists have found working teachers who are convicted felons or of questionable character by comparing a database of teachers with prison records. School districts also often fail to discipline teachers and coaches, instead allowing them to resign, which can create future problems in other districts. The Boston Globe found more than 110 private schools in New England that had faced allegations that educators sexually abused or harassed students over a 25-year period, including many cases where schools covered up the abuse or quietly helped teachers land jobs at other schools. A journalist should delve into a district's procedures for checking potential teacher applicants to see how the school board and administration handled past cases of misconduct.

### Administrators

Many principals and districtwide superintendents previously served as teachers (and often coaches) before becoming administrators. A journalist should check whether they received any training in administration before or after they were promoted. Furthermore, a journalist should check to see whether a local or national search was conducted and whether an excellent local candidate was ignored during the excitement of conducting a high-profile, high-cost national search. In addition, a journalist must always check on the accuracy of an administrator's résumé. There is much pressure in education to exaggerate credentials.

The ultimate supervisors of principals and superintendents are usually elected, unsalaried school board members. They are likely to be dependent on the administrators for information, a system that often reduces board members to nothing more than rubber stamps for everything from curriculum changes to salary increases. On the other hand, individual board members may be extremely ideological or disruptive.

One study of school boards found that although 30% of public school students are nonwhite, only a small percentage of local board members are nonwhite. In many school board races, candidates run on single-issue platforms, with low voter turnouts making it more likely they will be elected. Journalists should research whether school board members are steering school district contracts, administrative jobs and teaching positions to business partners, friends and relatives. These are not theoretical situations; the IRE Resource Center contains examples of each of these abuses.

### School Staff and Services

It takes more than teachers and administrators to make a school work. But because support personnel tend to be poorly paid, schools can have difficulty attracting good candidates. Journalists have obtained lists of bus drivers from school districts or transportation companies, matched names with criminal databases and found speeding tickets, alcohol-related offenses and other violations, including child molestation.

Even if bus drivers are safe, the buses may not be. A Hartford Courant investigation found that the safety inspections of 30,000 Connecticut school buses showed that one in four buses was unfit to carry children. Such situations raise questions about why the bus company received a transportation contract in the first place and whether the district administration was monitoring the contract. Similar stories by newspapers and television stations have found safety problems ranging from bad brakes and tires to poor maintenance records.

Additionally, school lunch programs sometimes present an opportunity for patronage and favoritism. Journalists can examine contracts and ask about inspection records for school cafeterias. It can also be wise to look at what food is being served to the students. Is it an adequate amount? Is it relatively nutritious? Is the food being sourced from quality businesses? There are many different avenues that can be explored in relation to the resources schools, and thereby its students, are receiving access to.

### School Buildings

In a project that spanned more than two years, the Philadelphia Inquirer's Toxic City exposed the environmental hazards that children faced. The 2018 installment of that project focused on schools. In its IRE Awards contest entry, editors described their findings: "Children in Philadelphia public schools endure environmental hazards --- deteriorated asbestos, damaged lead paint, festering mold and rodent droppings --- that deprive them of a healthy place to learn and thrive." The paper conducted scores of tests in elementary schools, often recruiting teachers to gather samples in their classrooms. The testing "revealed that classrooms, hallways, auditoriums and gymnasiums were contaminated with millions of cancer-causing asbestos fibers found in surface dust; and that hazardous levels of lead dust coated classroom window sills, floors, and shelves in classrooms."

An investigator who looks at buildings from the inside will often notice significant problems and find the reasons for such deterioration, including such factors like lack of funds for repairs.

School districts often sell bonds to generate money for repairs to schools or for constructing new schools. The most complete view of income and expenditures of a school district often can be obtained from information about these transactions. Journalists should investigate any drop in a district's bond rating and investigate its cause. The principal independent bond-rating agencies are Standard & Poor's Corp. and Moody's Investors Services, both in New York City.

In one investigative story, the Fort Worth Star-Telegram exposed massive fraud and abuse in a bond program intended to lead to needed improvements at schools throughout the district. Two employees siphoned off millions of dollars and proposed work that was never completed, while district officials completely missed or ignored the issues. The reporter requested all the details on the expenditures of bond funds and did her own analysis to uncover the missing money.

Another building-related issue that journalists should consider investigating is fire safety. Nate Carlisle and Jessica Ravitz of The Salt Lake Tribune reported on the state of fire inspections in public schools, following a fire that destroyed a junior high school that was old and did not have modern fire safety features. They wrote that state records show that some Utah schools had no record of fire inspections for decades. Journalists can work to determine if the buildings are up to fire code and if said codes are being followed appropriately, both in how the buildings are maintained and how the staff follow the guidelines.



## Approaches to Investigating the Schools

Covering a school district is, in many ways, like covering any other beat. It's essential for journalists to build sources by visiting classrooms and getting to know teachers, support staff and parents. Reporters should make frequent open-records requests for key documents and become familiar with all the school data available in every state thanks to federal reporting requirements. Attending meetings, open houses, and sporting events, and becoming as much a part of the school community as possible will allow journalists much more access to records and interviews.

In a 2017 IRE Conference presentation, education reporters Tawnell Hobbs, Eva Parks and Francisco Vara Orta offered tips on how to investigate schools and districts. Among their recommendations:

- During times of state budget battles, look into crowding in classrooms, student load on school counselors and student-teacher ratios.
- Conduct background checks on school board candidates and new hires such as principals and coaches.
- Check statewide teacher discipline records, but know that they can be incomplete, so do your own backgrounding as well.
- Examine the haves and have-nots by looking at private donations, PTA funding and other revenue sources that are school-specific.
- When you find a problem at one school, look for it elsewhere. Parks learned that one Texas school district was using isolation rooms, or "recovery rooms," to punish special-needs students, and using the same terminology, she did open-records requests to search for it elsewhere.

And of course, it makes sense to follow the money.

### The Money Trail

Schools are big-money operations, which means that they can be scrutinized like other government programs: by following the money. A journalist can begin with the budget, comparing line items and totals with previous years to see what shifts in funding have occurred. All educational institutions purchase goods and services, from test tubes for chemistry class to drug abuse workshops for high school teachers. A journalist should look at what goods or services require bids (see [Chapter 9](#hou_9781319102845_ch09_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_VMsQoqljd9)) and then monitor the process. Most of all, money in education is supposed to be a means to an end: learning.

Keeping buildings safe, reducing class sizes and supplying up-to-date textbooks all require money. A district's revenue stream affects what occurs in the classroom, even if there is no direct correlation between dollars spent and quality of education. While wrestling with the relationships among revenue, expenditures and quality of education, an investigative journalist must first determine how much money schools have available to educate students.

One approach is to break out expenditures school by school. Many journalists do their calculating at the district level rather than the individual school level. The trouble with that approach is that many districts allocate expenditures unequally from school to school. Whether this is done intentionally or not, schools in well-to-do neighborhoods can fare better than schools in lower-income neighborhoods, an equation that favors political clout over real need.

In some locales, the largest percentage of funding comes from a central appropriation (usually tax revenue allocated by the legislature to state education agencies), supplemented by the federal government (funds usually earmarked by Congress to be channeled through executive branch agencies to local districts). The nature of funding sources can point to many possible stories. For example, a school district can be overly dependent on federal funds, making it especially susceptible to unforeseen budget cuts. Another school district may fail to take advantage of federal money, depriving its students of opportunities.

### Private Schools

Private schools are tougher to investigate than public schools. Most states do provide information about accredited private schools, allowing an investigator to compare class sizes, test scores, teacher salaries and elective offerings. But finding out about unaccredited private schools can be more difficult. A local private school association might supply some information. County health and fire departments should share information about the school buildings. Many private schools are nonprofit and tax exempt. That means they might file a Form 990 informational tax return with the IRS, a document that must be checked. A journalist should check to see whether there are any lawsuits involving the school. It can also be beneficial to speak with any contacts or parents associated with the school.

### Homeschooling

Reporters can check whether the state education department issues guidelines for homeschooling. Some states treat home schools similarly to private schools, with no certification required for teachers. The states' lack of common guidelines for homeschooling raises questions:

- If a homeschooler wants to enroll in public school or plans to enter college, what documentation must parents show about the coverage of certain subjects?
- What if a homeschooled student appears to be way ahead of his or her public school peer group but does poorly on standardized tests because of inexperience with such tests?
- If parents are teaching racial discrimination, condemnation of the LGBTQ community or other doctrines that would be unacceptable in public school classrooms, should authorities be able to intervene?

### Vocational and Trade Schools

An investigative journalist should research how well public vocational schools and private trade schools provide vocational training. Here are some questions to ask:

- Are the programs set up so that participants avoid the label of "low achiever"?
- Do the programs offer employers the quality and quantity of workers needed?
- Are students prepared for high-technology industries as well as for traditional manual-labor jobs?

State employment agency officials can be interviewed, as can employers themselves.

Overall, journalists tend to overlook public vocational high schools, even though they deserve scrutiny. Many of the students who attend these schools come from lower- and middle-income families and neighborhoods that get little attention from news organizations, so providing coverage can help reach communities that may be otherwise overlooked.

Reporters who have looked into these schools sometimes have discovered school officials having free work done on their cars and houses by vocational students and funds being diverted for unnecessary travel. Trade schools for high school dropouts and graduates should also be investigated.


## Issues in Higher Education

Billions of tax dollars and other public support go to higher education because the public depends on its colleges and universities to prepare students for jobs and vocations that keep the country healthy and moving forward.

But colleges and universities are often resistant to scrutiny, reluctant to release information and have open discussions, and more tempted to create educational policies and practices based on politics or government and corporate funding than on students and educational philosophy.

Because of these shortcomings --- real or potential --- journalists and student journalists need to routinely perform watchdog journalism on campuses. Such investigations touch on numerous areas: campus crime, research grants, annual spending, the influence of donors and corporations, misconduct in sports programs, alcoholism, courses and nonprofit institutes associated with the university.

### Document Trails

No matter how a school tries to keep its activities private, an abundance of information is available, including a long trail of documents revealing how the school functions.

Whether it is a public university, a private college or a community college, each school has its own associations, accrediting bodies, publications and books written about it. Its website will be full of information about the school. Its revenues and expenditures will be public in one format or another. Its property will be listed in local county offices. Many of its contracts will go out to bid. The lawsuits in which it is involved will be at the local courthouse. For private universities, an IRS Form 990 can provide salaries, names of directors, major companies an institution does business with, and much more.

### Public University Revenues and Expenditures

As mentioned throughout this book, a useful way to start an investigation is by following the money. Universities are big-budget enterprises. Income arrives from state legislative appropriations, congressional funding funneled through the U.S. Department of Education and other federal agencies, tuition and fees from students, gifts from institutional and individual donors, athletic revenues and sales by university-owned businesses. Much of this income can be tracked, even when the university is uncooperative. For example, gifts from overseas must be reported to the Department of Education as part of legislation intended to track foreign influence.

A large part of funding involves athletics programs, which may or may not be the big moneymakers a university says they are. Reporters often have to file freedom-of-information requests to obtain athletic department budgets, and sometimes separate independent funds are not included in response to those requests.

Furthermore, local universities sometimes take money that legislators have earmarked for a specific project that escapes competitive merit reviews. It is common for public universities to employ lobbyists at the state level or even the federal level or to help fund lobbyists for a group of universities. Just following the activities of the university lobbyists can lead to fascinating stories.

At the same time, universities seek to establish or increase large endowments from which they use investment income to fund programs. Endowments give universities independence from political meddling by legislators and also sometimes independence from public scrutiny of how they invest or use the money.

Tracking how the endowment money is invested and spent can be another productive route for journalists. Activists have criticized universities for what they considered morally incorrect investments, and investigative journalists have looked at the cost of managing the money.

Journalists also can examine the compensation of high-level administrators and some professors. Universities will say they are simply being competitive with private industry, but a reporter should check that assertion. Some university officials fail to understand how perceived lavishness will upset students, faculty, staff and taxpayers. They may ignore the anger that their high salaries and subsidized housing will produce when contrasted with financially strapped students who are taking full course loads, simultaneously working one or two jobs and accumulating significant debt from student loans.

### Accreditation Documents

A university's accreditation process can provide a journalist with vast resources on the quality of its education. When dealing with public institutions, obtaining the accrediting body's full report, sometimes running to hundreds of pages, should pose few problems because of access laws. If a private university refuses to provide a copy, the state agency for higher education may have a copy. If the university operates a teaching program on a military base, the base commander or educational liaison officer may also have a copy. The following documents often emerge during the accreditation process.

#### The Self-Study

Compiled by the university's faculty-administration committee, the self-study provides an internal look at strengths and weaknesses in curricula, faculty credentials, administrative operations, financial stability and the gamut of other issues that the university faces. Self-studies usually must be obtained from the university.

#### The Visiting Team Report

This report is likely to be more objective and hard-hitting than the self-study and should include the following:

- A recommendation on accreditation status.
- A statement on the university's mission and whether it has been achieved.
- An evaluation of the short- and long-range planning mechanisms at the school.
- An assessment of administrative performance, particularly if the school faces morale or financial problems.
- An assessment of general education patterns.
- A judgment on financial stability.
- An analysis of development activity.
- An analysis of faculty credentials overall.
- A comparison of faculty salary levels with those at competing institutions.
- The role of faculty in governing.
- An analysis of students' academic credentials, geographic spread, educational interests and retention rates.
- Evaluation of the physical plant.

#### The Final Action

The final action of the accrediting association's governing body may be obtained from the accrediting association or the individual school. The final action accredits the school while suggesting improvements or may withhold full accreditation until changes are made.

#### Follow-Up Reports

Accrediting associations might require universities to submit follow-up reports on trouble spots, such as a weak core curriculum or financial instability, within a year to three years after accreditation is extended. A university may receive full accreditation for 10 years, but a follow-up report may prompt downgrading to provisional accreditation or withholding of further accreditation.

### Enrollment and Retention

With the budget information and accreditation report, a reporter can start to focus on different areas of the school, particularly the students and how they are selected and encouraged to finish their degrees. A good place to begin is to look at admission policies. Here are some questions:

- What goals in student test scores and scholarship does the university have?
- What is the disparity in tuition for in-state and out-of-state students?
- What are the policies on encouraging diversity and affirmative action?
- What is the acceptance rate of students by the university and the acceptance rate by the students of the university?

States gather information on universities, and in many cases the data is available online and searchable. Agencies to check, depending on the state, include higher education coordinating boards or commissions, departments of higher education and boards of regents.

### Diversity, Gender and Equity

Some of the data collected by colleges and universities involves campus diversity. That information is required by the federal government, which tracks graduation rates by total student population, gender and race. That data is collected by the federal National Center for Education Statistics and is made available online through the Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System, or IPEDS. The IPEDS data also tracks enrollment, tuition and fees, retention and accreditation, among other things.

Issues of gender equity on campus are handled by Title IX offices. Title IX prohibits gender discrimination in higher education. It governs everything from sports to educational opportunities. Campus Title IX offices also handle cases of sexual assault, and while the details of those cases are typically private, schools report overall statistics in annual reports.

A crucial area for diversity on campus is financial aid. Once accepted to a college or university, students from disadvantaged backgrounds are less likely to enroll in the school if financial aid is unavailable. Financial aid falls into three general categories: grants (including scholarships and tuition waivers), part-time employment and low-interest loans. An investigative journalist can ask these questions:

- Is financial assistance available to all students in need?
- If so, how is assistance allocated?
- If not, how is the scarce money allocated?
- How many students have loans tied to the Student Loan Marketing Association (Sallie Mae), a private company chartered by the U.S. Congress as a government-sponsored enterprise?
- What is the default rate on student loans?
- Does the school effectively collect delinquent loans?
- Does the school get federal money by misrepresenting the qualifications of students?

Each type of financial aid has its procedures, which a journalist can learn by reading the legislation that created the aid and regulations implementing it. Then the journalist has the guidelines and laws against which to measure the school's performance.

### In the Classroom

Most class syllabi are published on the web but are seldom scrutinized by journalists, even though syllabi present a clear outline of what's being taught on campuses. Among the questions journalists should be asking are these:

- Is the reading list current?
- Who decides what values are taught?
- Do the course materials reflect multiculturalism or some kind of bias?
- Do the course materials avoid certain facts because of fear of controversy?

Furthermore, journalists can look at how student or faculty conduct affects education and also how a school deals with fraud and plagiarism. When questions about student conduct come up, universities sometimes convene disciplinary boards, consisting of faculty, students and administrators. These groups deal with questions of cheating and are often confidential. Nonetheless, they should keep aggregate numbers each year on the cases they deal with.

Grading policies are also worthy of inquiry. Journalists can look at the number of A's and B's given and consider whether the school is devaluing grades or just ensuring that students have a chance at graduate schools that ignore students who receive C's. Journalists can ask whether, in a time of overall grade inflation, educators are considering whether it is better to just have high pass/fail standards. In addition to the policies themselves, grades of student athletes often come under scrutiny, especially given scandals in higher education where administrators have fraudulently allowed athletes to pass classes so they would be eligible to play sports.

### Outside the Classroom

Many universities are major property owners, not just on the campus, but throughout town. Newsrooms at colleges have often reported on fire code violations at dorms and off-campus housing, especially after fires have killed one or more students. For example, a year after three students were killed in a Seton Hall dorm fire in New Jersey, journalists found that most dorms at the university lacked sprinklers. But journalists can perform an important public service by methodically examining building and fire inspections before a tragedy occurs.

Journalists should examine the condition of private rental properties for students and determine whether landlords are following the laws regarding evictions and security deposits. In addition, journalists should scrutinize not only the living conditions and code violations at fraternities and sororities but also the issues of hazing, underage drinking and violent activities at some houses.

Another area to scrutinize is the health services provided for students. Questions can be asked and documents checked about accessibility, services offered, the cost and the professional qualifications of the staff at student health clinics.

At many universities, waiting time for student health clinics --- particularly for mental health appointments --- can be quite lengthy, lasting weeks or even months. In most cases, students are underwriting this service through mandatory fees, meaning they're not getting the care they're paying for, and the results can be serious, leading to outbreaks of illnesses and serious mental health issues.

### Athletics

Improprieties in sports programs have become a staple of investigative reporters who now look at the money required for these programs, the influence of boosters, the prevalence of special tutors for the athletes and the program's general management.

Since the athletes are also supposed to be students, a journalist can explore these issues:

- Do athletes graduate at much lower rates than other students, and what are the graduation rates for student athletes in each sport?
- Do the athletes receive special treatment to keep them eligible for competition?
- Did the athletes attend a community college before going to the university, and what were their academic records at those colleges?
- Are professors and teaching assistants pressured to pass star athletes?
- Do athletes receive special privileges or access? (An investigation in California revealed that football players were using disability tags to get prime parking spots.)

The range of potential wrongdoing can be breathtaking. The Raleigh News and Observer has conducted multiple investigations of the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Among the newspaper's exposés was a series of stories on academic fraud documenting "no show" classes that many student athletes took, meaning that they got course credit without doing the work. At the University of Missouri, a tutor announced that she had been taking tests for athletes. A University of Arizona basketball coach was reportedly paying a player thousands of dollars a month.

Sometimes the scandals reach horrific levels. At Penn State University, an assistant football coach was using university facilities to lure and molest children, and Penn State officials lost their jobs over questions of what they knew, and when.

An IRE tip sheet that pulls advice from more than half-a-dozen great sports investigations suggests looking into a host of issues and digging into a number of documents:

- Summer jobs. Athletes at many schools must fill out forms listing their summer jobs. What did they do, how much were they paid and who did they work for?
- Corporate sponsorship contracts for athletic departments and head coaches.
- Coaches' contracts. The salary they receive is often only a small amount of what they're actually paid.
- Sports program budget data. (See Knight Commission report below.)
- Team and coaches' travel and expense records, including flight manifests. (Were boosters or politicians given free rides?)
- Student housing subsidies.
- Graduation data for athletes.
- Nonprofit institutional data from the IRS on "exempt organizations."
- Foundations that support athletic departments. Their IRS 990 forms can be illuminating.

The annual reports that university athletic programs submit to the NCAA can provide a wealth of information. While the NCAA typically will not provide the reports, public universities must give them to anyone who asks. These documents include everything from ticket sales to expenditures per student athlete, and provide the clearest available picture of the financial health of a college sports program. In 2019 the Knight Commission on Intercollegiate Athletics created a searchable database for Division 1 programs (the largest) using some of this information.

### Campus Safety

The lack of investigation of sexual assaults on university campuses became a huge public issue in the past several years. Journalists have discovered that schools discourage reporting of sexual assaults, create complicated systems for victims who want to pursue cases, and often try to keep incidents of rape and assault under wraps. ESPN investigative reporter Paula Lavigne uncovered instances where athletes assaulted students, including other athletes, at Baylor University and other campuses. ESPN's reporting exposed the lack of action by school leadership and local law enforcement, leading to the ouster of the head football coach.

Campus crime reports are gathered by the federal government and made available online, through the Jeanne Clery Disclosure of Campus Security Policy and Campus Crime Statistics Act. The Department of Education has a website where the public can examine campus crime numbers for any school in the U.S. However, multiple reports have shown that many crimes, such as sexual assault, are greatly underreported on campuses. In fact, sometimes a campus that lists a larger number of sexual assaults might actually be safer because it is doing a better job of policing and publicizing such cases. Journalists can investigate the following safety issues on campuses:

- How easy is it to walk in and out of classrooms and dorms?
- When crime occurs, does jurisdiction belong to the university's security force? Is it made up of competent law enforcement officers? Do they carry guns? What training do they receive?
- How often are students the criminals instead of the victims?
- Does the university keep and release information about campus crime, as required by federal law?

### Faculty

Journalists should look at the quality of the personnel at universities and colleges, just as they would for any corporation or institution. It is important to realize that universities and colleges do have a different personnel culture that is full of tradition and hierarchy and the idea of permanent job security --- tenure --- for some. Starting with faculty, here are some questions to explore:

- How are faculty recruited and hired?
- How are faculty evaluated by staff, deans and administrators?
- Do faculty show up for their classes? How often do assistants teach their classes?
- How do salaries and benefits compare with those of other schools and the private sector?
- How are faculty disciplined, and is discipline overly influenced by politics or ideology?
- How much outside work and consulting are faculty permitted to do?
- What are the demographics of the faculty? Does the racial and gender makeup mirror that of the student body? If not, what efforts are being made to recruit and retain faculty of color and women?

Journalists should also look at adjunct faculty members who are hired to supplement or substitute for regular faculty members. Good adjunct faculty members who are working in the field can be a great boon to a school because they have up-to-date knowledge. But they often are paid very little and sometimes have little extra time for students. Larger universities may rely heavily on teaching assistants. These questions should be asked if teaching assistants are used:

- How qualified are they?
- Are they screened and trained before teaching?

Journalists should be alert for professors taking advantage of students psychologically, sexually or professionally. Journalists should see if the university keeps records on cases and should look at the outcome of those investigations and whether they were fairly conducted.

### Campus Leadership

There is a long history of journalism investigations of university and college administrators that have turned up questionable travel, expenditures and cronyism. Among the documents that journalists can search are account ledgers, invoices, audits, minutes of regents' meetings and college credit card reports. More broadly, journalists should look at whether a university is top-heavy with academicians-turned-administrators who never enter classrooms or research laboratories. One way to do this is by comparing administrator-faculty ratios with other similar universities.

Like examinations of faculty, journalists should look at the hiring practices of administrators and what extra benefits they receive, such as housing and cars. The Chronicle of Higher Education polls universities to document administrative salaries. Universities and colleges with nonprofit status might report their highest salaries to the IRS on Form 990.

Another key question to ask is how many women and people of color hold administrative positions. If the number is low, what programs are in place to address the shortcoming? Do top officials act decisively when lower-ranking administrators or faculty prove to be bad actors? An investigation by the Los Angeles Times found that a University of Southern California medical school dean had been using drugs with known addicts and criminals within hours of seeing patients. The dean was fired following the newspaper's work.

In naming the story a finalist for an IRE Award, the judges noted that reporters started with only a tip and the first name of a woman who overdosed in a hotel room with the dean. The newspaper's persistence when met with a refusal to hand over a basic document is instructive of the ways in which not taking "no" for an answer can pay off. "The police report about the overdose was important corroborating information, and it was obtained only after a months-long battle with the Pasadena police," the Times wrote in its IRE Awards entry. "The department initially refused to provide a report, saying responding officers had not prepared one. The Times pointed out that failing to do so violated departmental policy and pressured officials, including the mayor, to comply with the policy and the California Public Records Act. The department eventually produced a retroactive report and turned over a heavily redacted version to the newspaper."

### Governing Boards

The governing boards of regents, curators or trustees oversee universities and colleges. These boards meet only periodically and can easily be buffeted by politics or overly influenced by administrators.

Some governing boards are rubber stamps for the campus president or chancellor. As rubber stamps, they grant huge compensation packages and golden severance parachutes. Sometimes board members are disengaged from the school and only hold their seats because they have made large donations. Among the issues to explore are these:

- How and why are the members of an institution's board chosen? In many states, the governor appoints curators of public universities, placing them in the political realm.
- Why do they serve? Are they directing university business to friends, or are they promoting their own careers?
- Do they ever mingle with students, faculty, administrators and staff?
- Do they have private business dealings with the school?

### FERPA

One great stumbling block that education reporters encounter is officials who hide behind the federal Family Education Rights and Privacy Act, or FERPA. As attorney Frank LoMonte wrote when he was running the Student Press Law Center, FERPA "requires schools to enact and enforce policies to safeguard the confidentiality of students' education records." Virtually every court that has been asked to define "education records" has applied a limited and common-sense understanding of the term.

To be a confidential FERPA record, LoMonte wrote, "a document must not merely mention a student; it must actually be about that student." For example, in a January 2011 ruling, a Florida state court ordered the University of Florida to release tapes and transcripts of Student Senate meetings that the college was withholding under FERPA. The judge ruled that, although the documents contained the names and voices of students, they were not the records "of any particular student for FERPA purposes."

Generally, FERPA applies specifically to student records. Among the things that LoMonte points out it does not apply to are parking tickets, complaints against employees that mention a student, crime reports from campus police or "safety" offices. As with other open-records issues, the existence of some legitimately FERPA-protected information does not mean an entire document is protected because it can be redacted.

# Chapter 14 Investigating the Environment


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_WugjbJHPYx" title="Chapter Objectives">


## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Investigate key environmental issues, including air, water and soil pollution.
- Determine which government agencies have documents and data that can help you examine everything from polluted sites to drinking water quality.
- Look into the impact of climate change and other key environmental issues.

</aside>

Flint, Michigan, was at the center of an environmental storm. Journalists from all over the world had descended on the city to report on the dangerous levels of lead that had been found in the water and the city's struggles to deal with the crisis. To a national audience, the story seemed to have blown up overnight. But readers of the Flint Journal had been following the growing horror for months. Local journalist Ron Fonger had been on the story almost since it began, right after the city switched from getting water from Detroit to pulling it from the Flint River in an effort to save money. The new water was corrosive and started leaching lead from the city's aging pipe system.

Fonger covered complaints about the water that arose at city council meetings almost immediately, tracked down reports of illnesses related to the water, filed open-records requests for documents that would illuminate just how bad the situation was --- and how the city had tried to cover it up --- and broke news about a scientific study that directly contradicted the official line that the water was safe.

National investigative journalists were late to the story, but Fonger's work shows the impact that can be made by a beat reporter who isn't afraid to dig and who knows where to look.

Environmental investigative reporting has grown even more in importance as some of the key issues of the 21st century have revolved around matters including climate change, the impact of long-term exposure to chemicals dumped into water and belched into the atmosphere, and industry efforts to roll back regulations to prevent that pollution. Although covering the environment may require delving into sometimes complex science, there is an abundance of resources and guides to help investigative journalists.

Reporters can also probe how politics, lobbying and funding affect environmental legislation and scientific studies, and they can investigate the conduct of both industry and advocacy groups.


## Investigating Climate Change

While scientists have produced multiple studies examining the reasons for climate change, journalists have played a key role in investigating its impact. Many of the agencies and resources described in this chapter are essential to providing thoughtful coverage of the issue.

The nonprofit investigative newsrooms such as ProPublica, the Texas Tribune and Reveal partnered to look at the increased risks posed to the city of Houston by climate change's contribution to hurricanes and flooding. Their project, "Hell and High Water," focused a large part of its work on looking at the risks posed to the region's huge petrochemical and oil/gas refining industries and the industrial plants located near the Gulf of Mexico. The project also examined the failure of local governments to address the increasing threat of flooding that put millions of residents at risk. Reporters used geographic databases obtained from researchers and universities to project the potential impact of future storms.

In another example of examining climate change's impact, Reuters embarked on an ambitious yearlong project to look at the effect under the sea. Warming water temperatures have had a fundamental impact on fish, causing them to migrate both deeper and toward the Earth's poles. Reuters examined the human impact on the food supply vital to many nations, the fishing industry and more. Data used for the project included temperature readings from the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, federal trawl survey data, U.S. Coast Guard reports on the location of fishing vessels, as well as data on fisheries collected by the United Nations and multiple countries.

The Society of Environmental Journalists maintains a wealth of information on its website, including links to dozens of scientific and environmental organizations that track climate change, explanations of the scientific concepts surrounding it and much more.



## Federal Regulation: The Environmental Protection Agency

Potential and actual polluters come under scrutiny from city, county, state and federal government agencies. A good place to start any investigation is at the government regulatory offices.

At the federal level, the first agency to go for documents, databases and human sources is the Environmental Protection Agency. To find documentation for a story or to get story ideas, investigative journalists can browse the EPA website and read the agency's annual and periodic reports. Journalists can research all forms of pollution in their communities, see what the latest regulatory issues are and download databases.

In one example, journalists with the North Jersey Record revealed that the chemical company DuPont knew that toxic material dumped into groundwater could vaporize and rise into homes. The Record examined hundreds of pages of emails and stacks of state and federal documents from regulatory agencies obtained through open-records requests. The paper was then able to report that the company worked to keep the existence of the pollution secret for decades, and then downplayed the extent of the problem. The resulting stories talked about volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and parts-per-billion of various chemicals. But the lead of the story is anything but technical:

> For decades, an underground plume of toxic chemicals --- about eight football fields wide and covering 140 acres --- has lurked beneath 400 homes in the shadow of a now-shuttered munitions plant in suburban North Jersey. The groundwater contaminated with cancer-causing solvents migrated from a century-old DuPont facility ... which produced ammunition that helped America win two world wars.

### Other Federal Agencies

Besides the EPA, there are many other executive-branch agencies with some jurisdiction over environmental enforcement, including the Army Corps of Engineers, the Council on Environmental Quality and the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, as well as various bureaus and offices within the Agriculture, Energy and Interior departments. Also, the Securities and Exchange Commission requires publicly held companies under its jurisdiction to disclose environmental information.

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service can also be a good source of information. The Times-Picayune, in New Orleans, did an award-winning story on the conditions that threaten the world's fish supply, using documents from the Fish and Wildlife Service, among other agencies.

Within the legislative branch, numerous House and Senate committees have jurisdiction over environmental matters. The Government Accountability Office performs numerous studies in a year on environmental issues and makes them available for download on its website. In the judicial branch, courts regularly decide environmental lawsuits, some of them argued by the Justice Department and some by the solicitor general.

At the state level, the lead executive-branch agency is usually called something like the Department of Natural Resources or the Department of Environmental Protection. It can include divisions overseeing environmental quality and state parks. In Missouri, the DNR generates reports like a registry of "Confirmed, Abandoned or Uncontrolled Hazardous Waste Disposal Sites in Missouri," which has a detailed narrative about each location. Many employees in these departments care deeply about their work and make excellent sources for stories.

State legislatures also have multiple committees with environmental jurisdiction; the state courts, like their federal counterparts, hear environmental cases, some of them argued by the state attorney general.

It can also be worth investigating that sometimes legislators, regulators and judges are part of the problem rather than part of the solution. The revolving door between public and private sectors can result in poor regulation.


## Using the Toxics Release Inventory

Journalists can begin checking on their own communities by examining the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) kept by the EPA, as mandated by the Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act. Although it is a federal database, its usefulness for local stories cannot be exaggerated.

The annual TRI reports are used for daily reporting on the environmental beat and for quick enterprise stories, such as looking for the top polluters in a news organization's coverage area. They also can provide the framework for many long-term investigative projects. USA Today did a story investigating pollution from mercury in cities and towns across the United States that used the TRI as a starting point. The newspaper documented how mercury, a poisonous metal, continued to come from the nearly 500 coal-burning power plants that at the time supplied half the nation's electricity.

The TRI data includes names and addresses of factories that manufacture, process or otherwise use any of hundreds of toxic chemicals and then discharge those chemicals into water, air and soil. A journalist can search for his or her area by using the TRI Explorer on the EPA's website. It is possible to search by report types (onsite or offsite "releases," for example) or by different categories, such as chemicals, industries and facilities.

While the data is generally a year or two old and has other limitations, it can lead to many public-service stories. It can also be used in conjunction with information obtained through the Toxicology Data Network (TOXNET), a superb site maintained by the National Library of Medicine. TOXNET has a cluster of databases on toxicology, hazardous chemicals and related areas.

With the TRI, journalists can answer such questions as how much benzene industrial plants in their county released into a particular river, the names and addresses of steel plants importing lead and the amount of chlorine gas released by a company into the air.

In one investigation, The Hartford Courant used the TRI to uncover the following facts:

- Connecticut manufacturers had failed to report millions of pounds of emissions that the law required them to disclose.
- Of the emissions reported, ozone-eating Freon was on the increase, despite pledges by industries to cut back.
- Some factories were dumping toxic chemicals into city sewage-treatment plants.
- The state environmental agency had inaccurately reported emissions to downplay the magnitude of the problem.

The Courant matched the TRI database with a state database on pollution from sewage-treatment plants, used the TRI for daily stories and for a longer story on ozone-eating chemicals, gave information from it to many bureau reporters, and analyzed transfers of chemicals to landfills and private disposal treatment companies.



## State and Local Agencies

While the EPA plays a huge role in regulating the environment, states also have environmental regulation agencies that have responsibility for monitoring a wide range of activities. Some of these agencies are strong watchdogs, others aren't, but all deserve scrutiny.

A joint investigation by the Center for Public Integrity, InsideClimate News and The Weather Channel focused on regulation of fracking in Texas. Fracking is a process in which liquid is injected at high pressure into rocky ground to extract oil or gas; its use began spreading widely in the 2000s as other, easier-to-reach sources were drying up. The three news organizations found major flaws in the way Texas was monitoring one of the largest fracking regions in the country. Among the findings of an investigation called "Big Oil, Bad Air" were:

- The state did little monitoring of emissions. Only five permanent air monitors had been installed in the 20,000-square-mile region, and they were located far from the heavy drilling areas where emissions were highest.
- Thousands of fracking operations were allowed to self-audit their emissions without reporting the results to the state.
- There was little enforcement of regulations. Of more than 280 complaints, only two resulted in fines.

That last point is key: It's important to look at more than just the regulations on the books, but to investigate whether, and how, they are being enforced. At both the federal and state level, it is not uncommon to find few fines being imposed, and even fewer being collected, as lawyers for companies and agencies often negotiate them down to little or nothing.

There are interesting stories in local government as well. For example, during a period of extreme drought, Reveal/The Center for Investigative Reporting examined records kept by the city of Los Angeles to determine who was using the most water. Journalists found one residential home that had used a stunning 11.8 million gallons in one year, which Reveal reported was enough for 90 typical households. It also reported that the city had done nothing to stop the usage, nor had several other California cities with huge individual water users.

The bottom line is that every environmental reporter, and any beat reporter whose city or county includes industrial plants, mining or drilling operations, landfills or a host of other potential hazards, must be familiar with the state and local agencies that enforce environmental regulations.


## Investigating Air Pollution

Under the federal Clean Air Act, EPA headquarters, its regional offices and state pollution agencies compile information about the locations of hazardous substances as well as emissions from specific work sites. But state and local agencies also have great authority to enforce the Clean Air Act.

In addition to the TRI, a good place to start an investigation is with the EPA's Aerometric Information Retrieval System and AIRS Facility Subsystem, where it's possible to search by several categories, including facility, geography and pollutant.

Journalists can also use the AirData search tool to gather information from the Air Quality System and the National Emission Inventory. Both data sets allow a journalist to see if communities are meeting the standards to lower air pollution. Many of these EPA data tools are part of its Envirofacts online portal.

Indoor air pollution can be a problem in homes, offices and public buildings. Regulations from the Occupational Safety and Health Administration about indoor air pollution have spawned legal battles about whether building owners or tenants have responsibility for compliance.



## Investigating Water Pollution

Throughout the United States, journalists have found that they don't have to venture far from home to find unsafe water, as local reporters did in Flint, Michigan. Following that story, journalists around the country examined the lead levels in their own community's drinking water.

In another startling investigation, The News & Observer in Raleigh, N.C., found that the state's efforts to ensure safe drinking water fell far short of protecting the public. The newspaper said its investigation revealed "a disregard for safety of private wells, weak regulation of public water systems and widespread problems with lead testing." Among the newspaper's findings were:

- State law doesn't require that private wells be tested. More than 2 million North Carolinians get their water from private wells, many located in areas where arsenic and radiological contaminants might be present.
- When contamination is found in a private well, state officials aren't required to notify nearby well owners, whose water could also be contaminated.
- Only 14 counties have requirements for well testing.
- The state has standards for well construction but only eight employees to enforce them.

As with every other type of pollution, water coverage revolves to a large extent around sweeping federal laws, in this instance, the Clean Water Act and Safe Drinking Water Act. Factories, sewage-treatment plants and other facilities are supposed to report discharges into waterways. Documents generated by the process include national pollution discharge system permits, discharge monitor reports, quarterly noncompliance reports, violation warnings, violation notices and orders to cease.

When The (Fort Wayne) Journal Gazette received a tip about local companies discharging chemicals into the municipal sewer system without adequate government monitoring, a reporter found a list of companies tied into the system, their discharge permits, inspection reports by the city, court records of companies fined and the sewer department's annual reports. With that information in hand, he had his story.

The EPA's Office of Ground Water and Drinking Water is a good starting point, providing reports of the quality of local drinking water systems and links to state programs.

Among water-quality reports, the 305(b) report, a federally mandated inventory of water quality produced every two years, characterizes water quality, identifies widespread water-quality problems of national significance and describes various programs implemented to restore and protect water. As part of the 305(b) reporting process, states must compile a list of "impaired" streams and rivers. This is generally referred to as the 303(d) list and is available from state regulatory agencies.

Another database to pursue is the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System database, which allows journalists to look at water-discharge permits and violations. Like other EPA search tools, different categories can be selected, including geography and chemicals.

One key issue to examine is the total maximum daily load of contaminants. States are required, through the Clean Water Act, to establish TMDL levels for their water bodies. A TMDL is a cleanup plan for a particular body of water. It specifies the maximum amount of a pollutant that a body of water can receive from all sources and still meet water-quality standards. TMDL reports and lists can be accessed on the EPA website by searching for the Assessment and Total Maximum Daily Load Tracking and Implementation System (ATTAINS) site.

Another resource is the Safe Drinking Water Information System, which is a database of public water systems and their violations. USA Today discovered flaws in the database but still was able to use it for a major project on violations by thousands of water systems. A query form for this database allows a journalist to localize the data.

The EPA has tackled the problem of point-source pollution and has turned its attention to nonpoint-source pollution, a nebulous area of environmental protection, in which it is difficult to detect the origin of the pollution. It's also a big concern because of urban sprawl. Storm water is a big topic in the area of nonpoint-source pollution because regulations that took effect in 2002 require local ordinances to protect against storm water pollution and erosion. Many news organizations have uncovered trouble in their towns with sewer systems after heavy rains. Rainwater runoff overflows into streams and rivers without being treated first.

As water shortages reach many locales, some treat wastewater heavily and then use it as drinking water. If so, is the treatment process considered safe by relatively unbiased experts outside the locale? Has the citizenry been informed? Details and the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System program are available on the EPA website.

Agricultural land is sometimes the source of water pollution, as pesticides, fertilizers and other chemicals applied to the soil run off into the water. Wetland areas, meant to filter water naturally, sometimes become the center of controversy because farmers want to use the land to plant, and developers want to drain the land for apartments, offices or residential subdivisions. The National Conference of State Legislatures provides perspective in the "Wetlands Overview" section of its website. Pollution from corporate hog farms has also been well documented for decades, beginning with a series in the 1990s by The News & Observer in Raleigh, N.C.

The operation of dams by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers or other government entities is yet another possible cause of environmental degradation. Sometimes dams lead to new recreation areas. Journalists near water recreation areas can examine how much pollution comes from pleasure boaters, many of whom never think of themselves as polluters. The EPA has recognized pleasure boats as a source of water and air pollution.


## Investigating Soil Pollution and Waste

The term Superfund refers to a program created by Congress and implemented by the EPA as part of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation and Liability Act of 1980. The purpose was to identify, seal off and clean up sites laced with toxic wastes. The identification process seems to work well, giving journalists the opportunity to find out about sites in their locales. But the sealing and cleaning tasks have been less successful.

The worst hazardous waste sites are placed on the National Priorities List, and there were more than 1,300 sites on the list as of 2019. The EPA devotes a whole section of its website to Superfund information.

Journalists can go beyond federal TRI data to study state hazardous waste manifests meant to track toxic material shipped from an organization's premises. The Resource Conservation and Recovery Act requires shippers to complete a uniform hazardous waste manifest.

Many states have agencies that also track hazardous waste, and those agencies and their documents and databases should be checked. Among the EPA databases journalists can use when examining hazardous waste in their community are:

- The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Information System, widely known as CERCLIS, has information on all possible hazardous waste sites.
- The Record of Decision System, known as RODS, shows the justification for actions taken at Superfund sites.

A reporter at The Burlington Free Press in Vermont knew a barge canal site had been on the Superfund list for years and decided to check on the progress of the cleanup. She read documents at the EPA regional office and the state natural resources agency and conducted interviews. She found bungled scientific studies, inexplicable delays and cost estimates that had jumped from \$3 million to \$50 million.

Journalists should find out whether the EPA has asked site owners and managers to complete "information requests" to identify "potentially responsible parties" for cleanup costs. If a cleanup has begun, who won the contract, and how? Who is auditing that company's performance, and what auditing reports are available?

Beyond the Superfund sites, there are other dangers to the soil. Pesticides, insecticides and fertilizers used in agriculture often increase yields per acre, but the downside can be serious for the environment. Rachel Carson's 1962 book, "Silent Spring," still reverberates, as the problems Carson discussed persist today. Understanding the leading federal law, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, can help journalists sort through the issues, and the EPA has a lengthy explanation of the requirements on its website. Knowing state and local law is also important.

Soil might undergo long-term contamination, with consumers who eat the produce grown in that soil ingesting harmful substances that can cause or hasten a variety of ailments, including cancer. The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide and Rodenticide Act requires producers of the substances to file reports with the EPA. Another federal law, the Toxic Substances Control Act, triggers reporting requirements, too. The cumulative effect is shown by no-till farming, which has cut back soil erosion by leaving the previous season's fields undisturbed and in turn has reduced pesticide runoff into nearby streams. But to increase crop yields in an untilled operation, farmers have started using more herbicides.

There is more than toxic material to monitor. Journalists can look at the solid waste disposal of trash. Controversy exists about the appropriate location of landfills, whether incineration is effective, how much landfills pollute subsurface water and whether closed landfills have been properly sealed. Among the questions to pursue are:

- Is the waste handled by government employees or private companies?
- Do the workers have the training to handle the waste in accordance with the law?
- Who monitors the transportation of the waste?

An investigation into waste disposal should involve spending time at the dump to see what really is discarded, how it is handled by the employees and how little space is left. Another technique is to follow waste from its source.

Military bases are exempt from some laws and regulations; even when they are subject to the rules, they often act as if they are not. Journalists near military bases should inquire about who collects the toxic and nontoxic wastes generated there and where the wastes go. They should then independently verify that what they've been told is true. The IRE Resource Center contains an entire section on military mishandling of wastes. Other federal lands are also polluted heavily, including mines, oil and gas wells, irrigation drainage canals and factories manufacturing radioactive materials.

Extracting natural resources such as coal, copper and gold from the ground causes its own brand of environmental degradation. Numerous mining laws and regulations are supposed to minimize long-term damage but sometimes are ineffective.



## EPA Summary

This chapter has detailed a number of key resources offered by the EPA. Here is a quick summary of many of them, plus some we didn't have time to delve into:

- AirData: Access to Air Pollution Data.
- Local Drinking Water Information, for investigating quality.
- Emissions Monitoring, for data on possible air pollution.
- Envirofacts Data Warehouse, a great resource for a wide variety of issues and stories.
- Mapping tools for monitoring and Assessing Water Quality.
- Nonpoint Source Pollution, for investigating polluted runoff from sources, such as runoff from fertilized fields.
- National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System, for data on water pollution.
- Office of Ground Water and Drinking Water, for a variety of information on water quality.
- Superfund, for data and reports on some of the most polluted sites in America.
- Total Maximum Daily Loads, which is the amount of pollution allowed in water (more is a violation).
- Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) database, a great resource for toxic chemical release data.


## Other Federal Regulatory Agencies

These other federal regulatory agencies or federal databases might prove useful:

- Army Corp of Engineers, which oversees a wide variety of areas, including flood control and levies.
- Council on Environmental Quality, which is involved in environmental and energy policy.
- Nuclear Regulatory Commission and its Electronic Reading Room, for information on nuclear energy, plants and other uses of nuclear material.
- U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, which oversees areas including endangered species.
- Toxicology Data Network (TOXNET), which has databases on toxicology, hazardous chemicals, environmental health and more.

# Chapter 15 Investigating Transportation


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_gRghHAh9Fr" title="Chapter Objectives">


## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Find documents and sources to investigate transportation accidents.
- Find documents and sources on deteriorating infrastructure.
- Find documents and sources on transportation related to stories on crime and environment.

</aside>

In 2018, a footbridge over a busy street at Florida International University collapsed and killed six people. Investigators found the collapse was caused by faulty design and poor oversight.

In 2013, a bridge over the Skagit River in Washington state collapsed when a trailer truck hit a crossbeam. No one was killed but a new peril was uncovered. The bridge was deemed "functionally obsolete" because it did not meet current design standards, and the design was "fracture-critical," meaning that it had no redundancy if one of the supporting beams failed. A Seattle Times investigation pursued the simple question of how many of these kinds of bridges were in the U.S. The result was a stunning finding by the newspaper, which stated: "Thousands of bridges around the U.S. may be one freak accident or mistake away from collapse, even if the spans are deemed structurally sound."

In 2019, according to a report from the American Road and Transportation Builders Association, more than 47,000 bridges in the U.S. are in poor condition and in need of urgent repairs. Investigative reporters can wait for bridge disasters and then report diligently on government reports, or they can provide a public service by doing their work, starting with an analysis of a federal database known as the National Bridge Inventory, an annually updated collection of data on nearly every bridge in every state. Reporters can interview transportation officials and engineering experts, examine state databases, and go out into the field to photograph and observe local bridges firsthand.

Over the years, reporters have produced stories identifying a pattern of underfunding bridge maintenance in every region of the nation that resulted in structurally deficient and sometimes hazardous bridges going unrepaired. Reporters have found that of the nearly 600,000 bridges in the national database, at least one-quarter were identified as structurally deficient or functionally obsolete. Because of the spotlight that the reporting placed on the deteriorating bridges, repairs are being done on some of the worst bridges in many states.

As these examples show, some of the most compelling transportation investigations are about safety and often involve matters of life and death. In general, there are two types of opportunities for conducting transportation safety investigations: After an accident in which injury or death occurred and before the tragedy strikes. The latter --- the preventive investigation --- is without question preferable.

Safety investigations can target any form of transportation --- motor vehicles, trains, planes or boats --- and there is a wealth of documents and data available for enterprising journalists.

In safety investigations, government reports and databases are invaluable, but simple day-to-day observations can also lead to questions like:

- Which streets or highways have the highest accident rates?
- Which local railroad crossing is the most dangerous?
- Which airport has the highest rate of near misses between planes?
- On which lake do most boating accidents occur?

Compiling a list of the top ten locations where the highest accident rates are would itself be a public service, but an investigation into why they occur would be even more valuable.


## Investigating Land Transportation

There are many investigations involving land transportation that go beyond crashes and collisions. Some transportation investigations probe fraud and other abuses, such as construction delays and cost overruns, unfair seizure of citizens' property for rights of way, and waste and abuse in highway projects. Newsrooms in Boston kept the pressure on highway contractors and state officials with their investigations into "the Big Dig," a problem-ridden project created to put extensive new highways and traffic tunnels in central Boston.

Some transportation investigations focus on design flaws not of highway infrastructure, but of vehicles or on the shortcomings of drivers. Others look at the dangers of what is being transported by truck or train and whether the material is hazardous or flammable.

### Cars

Many transportation investigations focus on cars. After all, tens of thousands of people die every year in auto accidents in the U.S. and throughout the world.

Many news organizations make use of the Fatal Accident Reporting System (FARS) in the U.S. FARS data details fatal crashes on roads across the nation. In addition to traffic data and accidents by state, journalists can find the most dangerous intersections and roadways, measured in terms of both fatalities and accidents. Internationally, the World Health Organization has embarked on a campaign to lower accident deaths and is not only collecting data country by country but also issuing studies and reports.

The safety of the cars being driven, including the tires, is monitored by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration. The agency issues general standards, rates specific models for crashworthiness and fuel economy and tests tires for tread wear, temperature resistance and traction. If enough problems appear, the agency can order the manufacturer to take corrective action, often through a nationwide recall.

The Houston TV station KHOU-TV was the first news organization to call attention to the seriousness of Firestone tire defects at the beginning of the 21st century. It began investigating car tires that lost their tread "after receiving viewer complaints and a tip from a local Houston lawyer." Through interviews with victims and lawyers, KHOU found 30 deaths that were connected to tread separation on ATX tires. It then expanded its investigation to other states and countries. As a result of the investigation, Firestone recalled 14.4 million tires.

More recently, numerous investigations found that Volkswagen conspired to elude emissions standards in the U.S. by selling vehicles with illegal software and continuing to do so even after the scheme had started becoming public. Many more investigations can be generated by examining the National Highway Transportation Safety Administration database of vehicle complaints, recalls, service bulletins and inspections.

#### Car Inspections and Driving Records

The (Raleigh) News & Observer compared local car inspection stations licensed by the state. At one, 90% of inspections led to approval. At another, two miles away, only 20% of inspections led to approval. Tire stores with inspection authority found tire problems more often than other inspection sites did. Muffler shops found muffler problems more frequently than other sites. Maybe the specialists spotted problems more often because of their expertise, or maybe they were hoping to increase sales.

By going to the state motor vehicle departments and by studying related laws and reports, journalists can come up with numerous topics to investigate. They can examine how easy it is to obtain a driver's license in their state and how stringently the requirements for obtaining a driver's license are enforced. Journalists can look at driving tests and see if the failure rate is low. They can look at accident reports from police to see how frequently the drivers involved are uninsured, and then return to the motor vehicle department with probing questions.

Some investigations have revealed the weak enforcement of license revocations. The Virginian-Pilot did an exposé in which they found that "thousands of drivers banned from the road chronically flout the law" and that "a recent study says they are four times more likely to kill than properly licensed drivers." Other investigations have found drivers still behind the wheel after multiple drunk driving convictions. The Dayton Daily News found a man convicted of drunken driving 19 times who was still driving. The Atlanta Journal Constitution identified more than 83,000 offenders with at least three charges against them and 43 with at least 15 convictions.

Journalists can also use state databases to examine driving records. At the Portland (Maine) Press Herald, reporters used a database they obtained through Maine's Freedom of Access Act to analyze about 160,000 motor vehicle crashes. They found that accidents involving suspended drivers "are six times more likely to be fatal, four times more likely to lead to an 'incapacitating injury' and 10 times more likely to involve alcohol or drugs."

### Motorcycles and All-Terrain Vehicles

The risks of driving motorcycles either with or without helmets is another area that deserves investigation. In a classic story, Thomas Hargrove of the Scripps Howard News Service used federal highway data for an investigation called "A Fatal Freedom: Deaths in Motorcycle Crashes on Rise." Hargrove reported, "Deaths in U.S. motorcycle crashes have nearly doubled in a decade, mounting to 4,000 annually, as more states have repealed mandatory helmet safety laws, according to a Scripps Howard News Service study." He further noted that reporters can look at the effect of anti-helmet laws by reviewing the highway accident data.

Other journalists have looked at all-terrain vehicles and found similar disturbing numbers. The (Portland) Oregonian reported that "the machines have soared in popularity, with 7.6 million in use. The result: Record numbers of riders end up in emergency rooms and morgues as accidents kill about 800 people a year and injure an estimated 136,700."

### Trucks

Numerous investigations can be conducted on the trucking industry. Here are possible topics:

- Overworked, exhausted drivers.
- Safety issues surrounding the transportation of hazardous materials.
- Violations of weight standards and the resulting damage to roads and bridges.

A journalist can investigate any local trucking company by using the Surface Transportation Board, a federal agency created by Congress to succeed the Interstate Commerce Commission, and state regulatory records, locating lawsuits and developing human sources within the companies themselves.

The Milwaukee Journal Sentinel found widespread falsification of drivers' logs at one local company, JDC. The Sentinel reported:

> Logbook falsification by truckers at the 589-driver firm was so widespread, federal auditors discovered last August, that the company was slapped with a \$92,000 fine.
>
> That ranks among the largest fines against a trucking outfit in recent years, a Journal Sentinel analysis of federal records shows.
>
> Coming on top of \$63,000 the firm was assessed for similar rule-breaking in 2004, the latest penalty put JDC in rare territory: Out of more than 50,000 truck and bus companies audited by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration in the last six years, only six have been fined more. Trucking industry spokespeople said the violations were unusual, but other news organizations have found similar violations.

The Los Angeles Times reported on truckers who avoided scrutiny of their equipment after a reporter rode with an independent trucker. The reporter observed the trucker swerve onto a gravel back road to avoid a weigh station. The trucker knew that if he had stopped at the weigh station, as required, he would have been fined for a missing mud flap, a blown tire, a log book that was many days behind and possession of an illegal radar detector.

Further contributing to the problems is the Federal Highway Administration. The regulatory agency allows the biggest vehicles with the heaviest loads to travel without regard to state size and weight restrictions. Journalists can measure the effects of this by looking at safety issues or infrastructure issues like pavement damage.

In addition to the FARS data, reporters investigating trucks can use such databases as the Department of Transportation's Truck Accidents, which contains information about accidents on U.S. roads involving a commercial vehicle weighing more than 10,000 pounds, including semi-tractor trailers, buses and hazardous material carriers. The DOT also has a truck census database that contains records on each company with commercial interstate vehicles weighing more than 10,000 pounds.

There are plenty of trucking incidents to investigate. WCCO-TV in Minneapolis received a tip from a truck driver who claimed that his employer made him operate unsafe vehicles. He reported the company to state regulators, but nothing happened. WCCO found that the problem was not isolated. As explained in an IRE awards entry, the series "showed that one out of three trucks inspected on Minnesota highways is so unsafe it should be pulled off the road; some truck companies tolerate shoddy maintenance, routinely allowing their trucks to operate with defective brakes, no lights and missing safety gear; trucks routinely dodge state inspections; judges and prosecutors have allowed companies and drivers that cause serious accidents to go virtually unpunished."

In a comprehensive 18-month investigation, The Dallas Morning News revealed many more safety problems in the industry. It used federal, state and local accident and inspection reports and databases, court records, criminal public records databases and interviews with truckers, company owners, law enforcement officers, lawyers, academics and safety experts. One of the findings it reported was that truckers have one of the highest on-the-job death rates among U.S. workers.

### Buses

Bus investigations include school buses, charter buses and commercial buses on public routes. Stories about school bus safety and drivers' records are explored in [Chapter 13](#hou_9781319102845_ch13_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_pKN1iBrXBE).

The regulation of charter buses is often loose at best. After 21 elderly people died because their tour bus drove into a river, The Sacramento Bee found that lax state regulation combined with charter company greed led to unsafe vehicles operated by unfit drivers. The Bee used state and federal laws, driving records of individual operators obtained from the state motor vehicles department, inspection records from the state highway patrol, licensing records from the state public utilities commission and regulations from the Surface Transportation Board.

After an investigation into a Texas accident found similar problems, reporter Michael Grabell of The Dallas Morning News put together a tip sheet that listed the documents available to reporters. He wrote:

- Check the bus company's safety record on the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration's websites: SafeStat, SAFER and Passenger Carrier Safety.
- Don't forget to check with your state department of transportation. Companies are regulated by the feds for interstate travel and by the state for intrastate travel.
- Make a federal public records request for the company's most recent Compliance Review from the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration.
- SafeStat has summary information on Accident Reports, Vehicle Inspections, Driver Inspections and Citations. Request the full reports from the state or federal law enforcement agency that issued them.
- In addition to lawsuits, check for complaints with your state attorney general's office and the Better Business Bureau.
- Check for recalls with NHTSA's Office of Defects Investigation.
- For vehicles, SafeStat's accident and vehicle safety pages will give you VIN numbers and license plates. Track registration and ownership records with your state DOT. In our situation, we had a bus that was operating in Texas, leased in Maryland and registered in Oklahoma by its owner in British Columbia. The bus had also operated for many years in Georgia, Ontario and Quebec.

As for commercial buses, intrastate companies tend to be watched less carefully than the interstate carriers, which are dominated by Greyhound.

Among the areas to look at are:

- Who is inspecting buses, and how often?
- Who licenses the drivers, and how frequently are they retested?
- Are drivers checked for alcohol- or drug-related arrests before they are hired? Are they rechecked periodically for drug use?
- How often does equipment fail?
- Where do bus companies provide service, and are illegal van services filling the gap?

### Taxis and Limousine and Ridesharing Services

In an excellent investigation, U.S. News and World Report looked at cabbie scams in 10 cities. By taking lots of cab rides and by interviewing drivers, fleet owners, dispatchers, taxi commissioners, police officers, hotel doorkeepers and airport officials, the team documented a host of problems:

- Rigged meters that ran faster than allowed by law or that added charges illegally.
- Allegedly broken meters that were used as an excuse to charge flat fares far above the actual cost.
- Impermissible baggage fees.
- Drivers pretending to be lost while the meter ran.
- Low-pressure rear tires that added revolutions per mile, making the meter rise faster than it should.
- Drivers picking up passengers headed for different locations and then charging each the full fare, even though the second passenger to be dropped off should be charged the differential only.
- Racial, ethnic, physical and geographic discrimination as drivers illegally refused to pick up passengers based on appearance, confinement to a wheelchair or destination.

With the emergence of ridesharing services such as Lyft and Uber, new avenues of investigations have opened up. Uber, in particular, has been the subject of a series of investigations ranging from exploitation of its contract drivers to allegations of sexual harassment in its administrative offices. In an investigation done by The Guardian, investigators recounted the

> plight of its impoverished drivers, who are earning low and unstable wages in a job without security or benefits, or struggling to pay off loans for their Uber cars --- debts that some have equated to dodgy subprime mortgages. Classified as contractors, the drivers have little recourse to deal with a litany of workplace challenges and hazards, including wage cuts by the company, harassment and sexual assault by passengers and a rating system that some say is plagued by racial biases.

### Roads

Road building at all levels of government is often a story about contracts: Who decides where the pavement should go and its design; who is willing to bid; whether contractors collude to rig bids, thereby spreading out the business (with designated "losers" later receiving subcontracts from the winner) and driving up costs to taxpayers; whether public officials awarding the contracts accept payoffs or steer awards to relatives or business partners.

The Independent Weekly in Durham, N.C., showed how campaign contributors to legislators ended up with the roads they sought. For example, one road bypassed a country club so that its wealthy members could play golf without the distracting noise of traffic. The series also documented how the governor's campaign contributors bought their way onto the state transportation board, which allocated several billion dollars of road money annually. Most of the board members had occupations that benefited directly from the roads they voted for themselves: Land developers, contractors, bankers, lawyers, rock quarry owners, engineers, trucking company executives and automobile dealers.

Journalists can find stories by simply reading a state government manual, which explains the transportation agency's responsibilities on how multimillion-dollar contracts are awarded and how they are monitored. Good investigations can also be done by watching the activities of the division of right of way in state transportation departments, which acquires land for highway projects. By following what happens when the agency decides that residences, commercial buildings or productive farmland must be bought or destroyed to build roads, journalists can uncover waste and misdeeds.

In one example, The Orange County Register found that landowners were forced to sell their homes cheaply to the state and that the homes and land deteriorated while the projects were delayed or never went through. Federal and state governments have the right under eminent domain laws to buy land from private citizens for public projects like highways or housing, even if the owner doesn't want to sell.

In a story with the opposite finding, The Phoenix Gazette, which is now out of business, focused on the most expensive land purchases by the state transportation department to demonstrate how the agency was wasting taxpayers money by overpaying for property owned by speculators who had bought it to make a profit from road building. Abuses included the agency paying \$350,000 an acre for vacant land and more than \$1 million an acre for desert brush in freeway corridors. Appraisers and county assessors had judged the land to be worth much less.

The St. Louis Post-Dispatch determined that Illinois highway officials frequently awarded contracts without competitive bidding, costing taxpayers millions of dollars annually in unnecessary expenses. The reporters made the discovery by analyzing Federal Highway Administration data on every highway construction project involving U.S. government money for five years. The data showed the location, date of contract award, completion date, estimated cost, final cost, name of contractor and number of bidders.

Journalists should ask how federal highway trust fund money is allocated by U.S. government officials in a local area. They can also track how local members of Congress are able to dip into the trust fund for pork-barrel highway projects that fail to meet the criteria of the interstate highway program.

### Railroads

Journalists who work in locales that depend on railroads for freight hauling, employment and passenger service ought to keep up with Surface Transportation Board publications and develop human sources within the agency. The agency can provide information about the financial health of railroads, mergers, new construction and repair, abandonments that can leave a community without traditional shipping services, rates and attempts to begin or improve commuter and long-distance passenger service.

Sharing jurisdiction with the STB on some issues is the Federal Railroad Administration, which is supposed to concentrate on safety, including track maintenance, equipment standards and operating practices. Reporters should consult the information and numerous databases available at the Federal Railroad Administration.

By reviewing federal railroad data and conducting numerous interviews, The New York Times won a Pulitzer Prize when it reported that dangerous railroad crossings throughout America were not being repaired or redesigned, leading to unnecessary deaths. A similar story can be done anywhere that railroads run. Railroad crossings are a problem in many locales, as poor markings (no flashing lights or warning bells), visual obstructions (such as untrimmed trees), inadequate restraints (such as no gates) and risk-taking drivers, bicyclists and pedestrians lead to death. When the Lansing State Journal in Michigan started investigating the death of a teenager at a railroad crossing, the reporter learned that the state was supposed to oversee about 5,500 crossings. The newspaper showed that recommendations from rail inspectors received no attention or met with delays lasting years.

In another story, WJLA-TV in Washington, D.C., revealed alarming evidence that America's largest railroad companies were running unsafe trains. The investigation documented trains leaving rail yards with defective equipment, improper train inspections, inadequate record keeping and a repeated pattern of safety violations on every major rail line. The stories also showed that federal regulators were well aware of these problems, but the information obtained from their files had never been made public.

### Mass Transit

Alongside railroads, mass transit is another category worth investigating. In cities, suburbs and rural areas with subway or bus systems, the Federal Transit Administration pays a portion of construction and operating costs. Journalists should explore whether transit systems serve all neighborhoods equally or whether low-income or minority neighborhoods lack equal access and whether rider numbers are being inflated to continue to qualify for federal money.

### Hazardous Waste

One important railroad and trucking story involves the transportation of nuclear wastes and other toxic materials from power plants and ethanol producers. Residents frequently have no idea that such shipments are passing through their neighborhoods. Sometimes nuclear waste is shipped by truck, which means it might be passing through downtown and residential areas. Ethanol is shipped by trucks and railroads but the majority of spills are from trucks.

One investigation, done by Phil Pitchford, David Danelski, Ben Goad and Mark Kawar of The (San Bernardino) Press-Enterprise, begins like this:

> A Union Pacific train hauling 28 cars of hazardous materials was headed north from the Colton switching yard when the crew felt the lead engine jerk and shudder to a stop.
>
> Behind them, two tank cars of deadly pressurized liquid chlorine and 12 other cars had jumped the tracks and now lay twisted and dented in the evening darkness. Residents a few hundred feet away were barely aware of the sudden danger facing their west San Bernardino neighborhood.

The investigation recounted that more than 1.5 million Inland residents lived close enough to railroad tracks to be at risk from a serious spill and the risk was growing. U.S. Department of Transportation regulations require that heavily populated areas be bypassed whenever feasible. Journalists should check with local power plant operators and the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission about the timing and routing of shipments.

To do their investigations, The (Cleveland) Plain Dealer and other newspapers used the Transnet database, developed by Sandia National Laboratories, Albuquerque, under contract with the U.S. Department of Energy. Transnet shows shipments and accidents in the transportation of radioactive materials; it also offers routing models that allow users to plan the safest highway and rail lines for shipping "hot" material. The Los Angeles Times used a different database, the U.S. DOT's Hazardous Material Incident Reporting System, to cull compelling examples from the tens of thousands of shipments.



## Investigating Aviation

In a classic, award-winning series, Keith Epstein of The Plain Dealer used National Transportation Safety Board reports, court records, cockpit recordings and other sources to examine delays in implementing agency recommendations meant to improve airline safety. He showed that minutes before takeoff, USAir Flight 405 from New York City to Cleveland had ice on its wings. The pilots knew a last de-icing would be useful, but there was pressure to take off on time and no regulator absolutely required a final de-icing.

As Epstein reported, the pilots "did not realize just how precarious their situation was. The federal government knew for seven years. U.S. safety officials repeatedly had pinpointed the cause of nine crashes of planes similar to that of Flight 405 --- the wing's high vulnerability to trace amounts of ice." Yet, the investigation found, aviation regulators had done nothing to require simple solutions, such as checking the wings by hand or spraying with a stronger antifreeze just before takeoff. Twenty-seven people died when the plane crashed into Flushing Bay.

Journalists could serve the public interest simply by following up on recommendations from the NTSB, which investigates certain types of accidents, or on airworthiness reports issued by the Federal Aviation Administration. After a crash, journalists frequently find recommendations that might have helped prevent the accident, but manufacturers and regulators sometimes ignore or try to reverse NTSB or FAA recommendations.

Some airplanes have design or manufacturing flaws. Consequently, journalists investigating safety must develop sources within companies that design and manufacture airplanes, be on the alert for litigation and check airworthiness certifications granted or denied by federal inspectors. In one case, investigative reporters found that the Boeing company had ignored safety warnings and the FAA had given up a significant part of its regulatory authority during the construction of the 737 MAX airplane. Flaws in its software and design had resulted in two airline crashes that killed a total of 346 persons. The Wall Street Journal reported that "interviews with current and former Boeing employees, pilots, airline officials, federal regulators and documents reviewed by The Wall Street Journal show that Boeing repeatedly minimized the risks posed by MCAS \[a flight control system\], without detailed scrutiny or pushback from U.S. regulators."

### Pilots and Flight Crews

Journalists should ask questions about pilot training and experience. They should inquire at the FAA about alcohol and drug prevention and testing programs for each airline. If no pilots or other crew have been sanctioned, then the reporter should probe more deeply. In the past two decades, there have been well-publicized instances of pilots trying to board planes while drunk.

Journalists should also look for violations of federal rules concerning crew fatigue. Crews are supposed to be on the ground for a certain number of hours after flying for a certain number of hours. During that stretch on the ground, they are supposed to have the opportunity for adequate sleep.

### Air Controllers and Ground Crews

Many air controllers are expected to retire in the coming years, and there is concern about replacing them. Journalists can do a public service by reviewing air traffic controller staffing and experience. At the same time, there is deep concern over the age of the radar and computer equipment that the controllers must use.

The Plain Dealer found that a new radar system meant to help controllers worked poorly, despite its \$1 billion cost to taxpayers. The newspaper relied on complaints filed by controllers with their union and with the FAA; the stories provided figures for individual airports. Journalists can use this approach to monitor how well the air traffic control system is working.

### Security

Since the 2001 terrorist attacks using planes, airport security has been a high-profile issue. Before that, news organizations had been doing stories for years on poor airport security, but nothing had been done about it. In the late 1990s, Cincinnati's WCPO-TV "uncovered major holes in airport security, behind the scenes where the public can't see. \[It\] found \[that\] a Delta subcontractor called Intex Aviation, employing hundreds of people in high security jobs at the Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky International Airport, did not always do the required background checks ... \[The undercover\] investigation revealed that almost anyone could be hired, and once hired, it would be possible to sabotage a plane."

Before the devastating terrorist attacks, the FAA's inspector general had reported on problems in airport security, but, even more shocking, the inspector general, congressional investigators and journalists found major flaws even after the attacks, including the continued employment of convicted felons. After Sept. 11, journalists also discovered scathing General Accounting Office reports on airports' and airlines' consistent failure to keep passengers safe, an extensive database on FAA enforcement actions in response to security violations, a well-documented history of investigative stories and a history of problems going back more than 15 years.

Since then, investigative journalists have continued their vigilance in investigating airline security and have found further security failings in a system that the government had vowed to improve. The Transportation Security Agency has a database of confiscated weapons and dangerous weapons and publishes a blog on the confiscations, which can be an excellent starting point for an investigation.

### Commuter Planes, Cargo Planes and Government Flights

Private planes have some of the same safety problems as large commercial airliners, but sometimes journalists must raise different questions about manufacturing, maintenance, pilot competence, airport conditions and air traffic controller conduct than with large commercial airliners. Standards and training are more lax for aircraft that fly fewer than 30 passengers.

The pilots tend to be younger and less experienced and fly more hours per week (sometimes on schedules that allow for little sleep). They are not as well paid as pilots of large commercial carriers and may not receive extensive training.

After a commuter airplane crashed in Minnesota, the St. Paul Pioneer Press found out that the pilot, who died in the crash, had failed six flight checks in a 13-year stretch, punched his first officer while in the air, sought turbulent pockets so passengers would complain to the airline he hated so much and verbally abused passengers. If journalists had checked pilot records routinely, they could have warned potential passengers before it was too late.

The operations of cargo planes should also be examined. In a lengthy investigation involving the analysis of government documents, The Miami Herald discovered that 69 cargo planes had crashed during a five-year period, claiming the lives of 85 people, thus "making air cargo the nation's deadliest form of commercial aviation." The newspaper said that "pleas to apply more stringent safety regulations on cargo flights have been ignored. Worse yet, when these lax safety standards result in fatal crashes, the pilots are often saddled with the blame."

Helicopters also deserve examination, especially when they are operated commercially as part of commuter and tourist services. Investigations after helicopter crashes have found shortcomings in rescue equipment and emergency procedures.

Journalists should routinely examine the safety records of local helicopter companies. They may discover unreported crashes and safety violations. In particular, scrutiny should be given to emergency medical helicopters that fly seriously injured patients to hospitals. Information about accidents involving medical helicopters is included in the NTSB's databases.

### Airports

In addition to safety and security questions, investigative journalists can look at airport costs to travelers and to taxpayers. In many cities, airports are subsidized heavily by governments. Those airports might serve a relatively small number of travelers, but the federal and local governments sometimes operate an airport no matter how busy it is.

Journalists should check an airport's costs and benefits by studying budgets, calculating subsidies, conducting interviews with experts and seeing if it is part of the federal "essential air service" (EAS) program. The program was created by Congress to ensure that in the wake of airline deregulation, smaller communities in the U.S. would still receive access to commercial airline service.

Because deregulation meant that market forces would eliminate air service to some small cities, Congress passed laws to keep local airports open.

Another area to investigate is on-time service; many newsrooms have been using federal data to monitor increasing delays. In recent years, there has been a barrage of stories on the problems with delayed flights because reporters have been analyzing DOT databases on arrivals and departures.

### Using Air Databases

To see how serious the problems might be at a particular airport, journalists should check the FAA database on near misses and another database known as the Aviation Safety Reporting System (ASRS) that contains anonymous complaints about airport safety from pilots, ground crews and other employees. A group of nonprofit newsrooms collaborated on a series of stories about safety issues at regional airports, using ASRS, and revealed near misses, plane equipment failures, pilot inattention fatigue and perils from wildlife on runways.

In addition to ASRS, the "service difficulty reports" database is useful because it contains information about serious in-flight incidents --- such as doors coming open --- or flawed equipment.

Other routine data sets a journalist should check and analyze in any investigation are:

- *FAA Accidents and Incidents.* A database of mainly U.S. flights where there was an accident or an incident, including crashes, collisions, deaths, injuries, major mechanical problems or costly damages.
- *FAA Aircraft Registry.* A listing of all aircraft and aircraft owners registered in the U.S. This data set also includes tables on registered aircraft dealers and individuals/companies that reserved the N-number for their planes, an alphanumeric string similar to a car license plate.


## Investigating Water Transportation

Investigations into water transportation face many of the same challenges and questions as ground and air transport. There are questions of regulation and agencies, as well as issues of security and safety.

### Commercial Shipping

Commercial shipping receives little attention in daily reporting, so it is a fertile area to investigate. The U.S. Maritime Administration helps commercial shipping by subsidizing private companies, collectively called the U.S. Merchant Marine. The Federal Maritime Commission, an independent agency, shares jurisdiction, with an emphasis on setting rates and licensing ocean freight businesses. Journalists in locales with large Merchant Marine employers can inquire about subsidies for construction and repair of private vessels, as well as the building and maintenance of port facilities.

Journalists in port cities can also monitor the ships that are coming and going. Many are staffed by international crews who know little about their rights, are cheated out of their wages, receive substandard food and may be badly treated by tyrannical captains. These ships may be owned by U.S. corporations, which means that they may have filed information with the Securities and Exchange Commission or with other financial regulatory agencies.

### The U.S. Coast Guard

Investigative journalists would do well to give more attention to the Coast Guard. Besides serving as a law enforcement agency, the Coast Guard is charged with formulating, administering and enforcing safety standards for the design, construction, equipment and maintenance of U.S. commercial vessels and with enforcing safety standards on foreign vessels subject to U.S. jurisdiction. Investigations are conducted of reported marine accidents, casualties, violations of law and regulations, misconduct, negligence and incompetence occurring on commercial vessels subject to U.S. jurisdiction. The Coast Guard conducts surveillance operations and boardings to detect violations, and it administers a system for evaluating and licensing U.S. Merchant Marine personnel. This program develops safe staffing standards for commercial vessels.

The Coast Guard also maintains a valuable database on boating accidents, which many journalists have made effective use of. The boating accident database contains information on recreational boating accidents in the U.S., and the boat registration database contains information on registered recreational and commercial boats.

The Kansas City Star reported that as boat traffic increased in Missouri, so have accidents, arrests, injuries and deaths. The Star said Missouri is one of the most dangerous states for boaters, according to its analysis of Coast Guard data. More recently, state databases on boating accidents have become more useful as fewer states send data to the Coast Guard.

Journalists should also investigate the environmental damage resulting from boating accidents. The Coast Guard is responsible for enforcing the Federal Water Pollution Control Act, now known as the Clean Water Act, and various other laws relating to the protection of the marine environment. U.S. and foreign vessels are prohibited from using U.S. waters unless they have insurance or other guarantees that potential pollution liability for cleanup and damages will be met.

The Coast Guard's other functions include providing a National Response Center to receive reports of oil and hazardous substance spills, investigating spills, initiating civil penalty actions when warranted, encouraging and monitoring responsible-party cleanups and, when necessary, coordinating federally funded spill-response operations. The program also provides a National Strike Force to assist federal on-scene coordinators in responding to pollution incidents.

The Coast Guard can become involved in accidents that appear to concern other agencies. For example, 47 people died as a National Railroad Passenger Corp. (Amtrak) train derailed on tracks that were out of alignment. The misalignment happened after a towboat accidentally rammed a railroad bridge while trying to pass under it. The NTSB investigated.



## Regulatory Agencies

One good way to start an investigation is to look at the reports, audits and investigations done by the federal transportation agencies themselves. Numerous agencies are responsible for the oversight of transportation issues. Under the U.S. Department of Transportation, which establishes overall policy, are --- among many others --- the National Transportation Safety Board, Coast Guard, Federal Aviation Administration, Federal Highway Administration, Federal Railroad Administration, Federal Transit Administration, Maritime Administration, Office of Hazardous Materials Safety and Office of Pipeline Safety.

The Surface Transportation Board oversees surface transportation that crosses state lines, with an emphasis on trucks, railroads, buses, barges and some coal and chemical pipelines. NTSB programs and DOT units overlap, so journalists frequently get different answers and findings from the various agencies.

In addition, state governments oversee transportation and have their own laws to enforce, and cities and towns have their own budgets and programs. Going to several agencies can be advantageous to journalists because they can get documents and information from one agency when another denies access.

# Chapter 16 Investigating Social Justice Issues


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_dw3NAxidas" title="Chapter Objectives">

## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Investigate issues of racial inequality.
- Investigate issues of LGBTQ discrimination.
- Investigate questions of gender inequality.
- Reframe investigations away from determining intent and toward examining results.

</aside>

For decades, women have been subjected to sexual harassment in the workplace. Offenses have ranged from demeaning jokes and gender bias to inappropriate comments, to sexual assault. But over the past few years, a growing number of women became willing to come forward and tell their stories --- and news organizations were ready to listen, verify and share those stories with the world --- so that the abusers could be held accountable.

In these and other stories examining social injustices, including racial and gender inequality, investigative journalism plays a vital role in spurring societal change. From the civil rights movement to the fight for equal protection under the law for the LGBTQ community, reporters have exposed discrimination and flaws in government regulation and enforcement. Investigative journalism often deals with unfairness or exploitation in a system, but these kinds of investigations require extraordinary sensitivity and well-researched inquiries to expose injustice and inequity.



## Racial Equality

The Tampa Bay police were issuing numerous tickets to bicyclists. So many, in fact, that the number outstripped the total in four other Florida cities combined, including Miami. And eight out of 10 of those tickets were being issued to black residents, the Tampa Bay Times discovered: "There was the 56-year-old man who rode his bike through a stop sign while pulling a lawnmower. Police handcuffed him while verifying he had, indeed, borrowed the mower from a friend," the Times reported. "One woman was walking her bike home after cooking for an elderly neighbor. She said she was balancing a plate of fish and grits in one hand when an officer flagged her down and issued her a \$51 ticket for not having a light." The Times reported that some riders were stopped more than a dozen times and issued as many as 17 tickets; some people were ticketed multiple times in a single day. What the Times discovered was that the police department had a policy of pulling over mostly black residents in high-crime areas for small bicycling violations, as a precursor to searching them for more serious crimes. However, the Times found that in most cases the police weren't finding any. The paper also went to a more upscale neighborhood where the mayor lived and observed multiple bicycling violations. But only one ticket had been issued in that area all year --- to a black man.

The Times article not only highlighted an unfair policy, but also illustrated a larger fact of life in the city: That law enforcement wasn't being carried out equitably. What is important to note about this case is that the biased behavior being highlighted by the paper wasn't illegal and was, in fact, being done openly.

In another example, journalists in the small urban cities of Champaign and Urbana, in Illinois, found police issuing large numbers of jaywalking tickets to black members of the community. In the award-winning story, Hoy Chicago and the nonprofit newsroom CU-CitizenAccess.org found that in a five-year period 658, or 88%, of the 744 jaywalking arrests were of black people. In Urbana, the percentage of black arrestees was even higher --- 91% --- during those same years. One reason cited was a lack of sidewalks in those neighborhoods, but the arrest rates plummeted after the ticketing inequity was revealed.

Journalists often focus their attention on illegal activity, or violations of written policies or guidelines. But that approach fails when the policies, or the laws themselves, are part of the problem. Or, when policies and laws that could address an inequity exist, but no one is enforcing them. Nikole Hannah-Jones, an investigative reporter who has focused much of her work on issues of equity and discrimination, says that journalists err when they focus on trying to prove intent. In an IRE presentation, she highlighted a quote from Washington State University professor David Leonard: "Racism gets reduced to intent, as if intent is all that matters." Hannah-Jones stresses that while it can be difficult to prove intent in these cases --- what someone was thinking, or what motivated their actions --- doing so is not necessary if the result of that action is a policy, procedure, program or regulation that results in injustice.

In one example of such an investigation, the Tampa Bay Times detailed how a local school district had become increasingly racially segregated. By adopting a series of policies that led to the segregation and resulted in major quality differences in majority black and white schools, did the school board intend to create such a system? Proving that could have been difficult or impossible, but it didn't matter. The end result was schools that were separate and unequal, and children were suffering. (For more on this project, see [Chapter 13](#hou_9781319102845_ch13_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_pKN1iBrXBE).)

### Laws, Policies and Procedures

It is important for journalists to understand the laws that govern many of the issues involving racial equity. Hannah-Jones' work has documented many cases in which laws exist that could address problems, but the government has failed to enforce them.

Civil rights doctrine states that practices may be illegally discriminatory if they have an adverse impact on racial or ethnic minorities. This is a way to examine practices that appear race-neutral but harm members of federally protected classes, when the organization or individual enacting the practice cannot justify it or show there were no other, less discriminatory actions or policies they could have enacted instead. Among the federal laws Hannah-Jones cites as vital to holding officials accountable are the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Fair Housing Act, the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act, the Housing and Community Development Act, and the Equal Employment Opportunity Act.

It's crucial to remember that in investigative reporting, the existence of a law, regulation or policy often isn't key to the story. The enforcement, or lack of enforcement, of that law or regulation can have huge impact on the lives of individuals. Examining how policies and regulations are being followed is, in fact, a key way to determine why a system might be racially unequal. Such inequities can and should be explored on many beats. In education, for example, unequal schools impact not only student education but also other issues that affect neighborhoods, as discussed in the next section of this chapter and in [Chapter 13](#hou_9781319102845_ch13_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_pKN1iBrXBE). Hannah-Jones and other journalists recommend looking at the following areas:

- Teacher quality (years of experience, level of college degree, whether they are teaching in the area of their expertise).
- Funding disparities among schools.
- Are extracurricular activities (sports, clubs, newspaper, etc.) comparable?
- Is teacher turnover higher at some campuses than at others?
- Are cultural offerings (art, music, dance, etc.) equitable?
- Are supplies (library books, lab equipment, etc.) equitably distributed?

### The Wealth Gap

Susan Smith Richardson, as editor of the Chicago Reporter, oversaw a great deal of investigative work looking into issues of the "racial wealth gap" and its impact. In an IRE presentation, Richardson included data showing that blacks in America saw their median income drop in the early 2000s, while whites, Hispanics and Asian Americans all had increases. Issues that contribute to that kind of inequity include years of home ownership, education level, unemployment and family support (inheritance, financial support from relatives, preexisting family wealth) --- these are all possible topics for investigation.

A Chicago Reporter investigation into the Chicago public school system's closure of 50 campuses, mostly in black neighborhoods, touched on the link between education and the wealth gap. While the most important factor is the impact on students and their future, as Richardson notes, the existence of quality neighborhood schools affects many factors beyond education, including home values. The Reporter created a searchable online database where residents could track what happened to their schools. While those school buildings were supposed to be repurposed, many in low-income neighborhoods remained vacant. And the Reporter debunked the idea that the students displaced by the closures were going to better situations. In many cases, the students moved to new school districts, only to end up in segregated, low-income suburban schools.

As Hannah-Jones notes earlier, documenting such results doesn't require that a reporter prove that school officials or county officials intended such results, only that the consequences of their decisions didn't result in students or neighborhoods being better off.

There are, in fact, many facets of government that contribute to wealth inequities. The Chicago Tribune analyzed property valuations and determined that the county's system of determining the value of a piece of property tended to increase values for low-income people more than for wealthier residents. While this might at first sound like a positive outcome, the result was that low-income residents saw their property taxes increase at a higher rate because taxes are based on valuations. Newsday in New York City did an analysis of property values showing that a newly enacted system for appealing higher assessments resulted in wealthier landowners lowering their valuations while many low-income residents did not see the same benefits.

Ultimately, journalists should keep in mind that reporting the kind of results found by the Chicago Reporter, Chicago Tribune and Newsday doesn't require that a reporter prove intent; as Hannah-Jones notes, documenting the impact of those decisions on the communities affected is the key.


## LGBTQ Issues

The odds of getting murdered for the entire U.S. population were one in 19,000 in 2016. For young, black transgender women, the odds were about eight times higher.

These were the findings of a data analysis conducted by the online news site Mic. But the reporters working on the story discovered that the reality is worse because the actual number of transgender people murdered in the U.S. each year is greatly undercounted due to the way gender is recorded (victims are often listed as male or female, with no other distinction). The reporters also found that those who murder transgender people --- especially black transgender women --- tended to get shorter prison sentences. The Mic project's findings were a stark example of the inequities facing many members of the LGBTQ community.

In the U.S. and many other countries, there is no federal law that prohibits discrimination based on sexual orientation in areas such as employment and housing, although some states and cities have enacted such laws. As a result, the National Lesbian & Gay Journalists Association (NLGJA) states that reporters cannot rely on federal employment data to determine sexual orientation and gender identity. Journalists must largely rely on surveys and studies, the results of which can vary widely.

LGBTQ issues arise in all areas of journalism, from business and education to politics and much more. On the business side, reporters have compared corporations' well-publicized advertising and marketing efforts for events such as Pride Week (a celebration of LGBTQ rights), while those same companies pour hundreds of thousands of dollars in campaign donations into the coffers of candidates with strong records of opposing LGBTQ issues.

On the crime beat, there are even more angles to explore. An Australian news organization reviewed unsolved murder cases suspected of having been hate crimes to show that police were still not seriously investigating the cases. Journalists found that as many as 30 hate crimes were likely committed against men because of their sexual preference. In one case, their review, which relied in part on court records and interviews, showed that police had reported that a murder didn't occur because officers were spelling the victim's name incorrectly and failed to locate the records, indicating a lack of effort in solving the crimes.

Moreover, journalists from Fusion found that transgender people seeking asylum in the U.S. were housed with the wrong gender population in immigration detention centers. One result: Though transgender immigrants were estimated to represent only one of every 500 detainees, they accounted for one in five confirmed sexual assaults. Reporters told IRE in a contest entry form that they analyzed federal court documents, agency medical records and internal investigative documents, supplied in part by attorneys and advocates.

The Religion News Foundation and other journalists who work on LGBTQ issues stress several key points when advising journalists who want to dig into these issues:

- Consider the impact of the work on those you are writing about when making decisions such as whether to identify victims.
- Be careful, when interviewing those who oppose LGBTQ issues or politicians who pursue an anti-LGBTQ agenda, that your work does not give credibility to dangerous viewpoints. The trick is to explain an issue without providing false balance that appears to give equal weight to views that are demonstrably inaccurate.
- Along those same lines, work to include moderate voices, not only those on the extremes.
- Understand that there is a lack of solid data on LGBTQ issues, and that void can be even greater in countries where discrimination is more commonplace.
- When trying to document someone's allegations of mistreatment, look for emails, text messages, printed policies and court documents. Potential sources can include activists, former company employees, community leaders, human rights organizations, health professionals and legal experts.
- Make sure to use the right terminology when discussing members of the community. There are a number of resources that can help. These include the GLAAD Media Reference Guide, the National Lesbian & Gay Journalists Association's Stylebook Supplement on Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, and Transgender Terminology, and Taboom Media, which has published reporting guides for journalists covering these issues globally.



## Gender Equity

Many issues fall under the umbrella of gender equity, but some of the most prevalent deal with sexual harassment and assault, access to similar resources and equal pay.

### Sexual Harassment and Assault

In 2019, The New York Times and New Yorker magazine shared a Pulitzer Prize for reporting on sexual abuse of women in the workplace, helping to launch what became known as the #MeToo movement. The work done by these journalists focused on an area that had long been a known secret in Hollywood and many other professions: That women were expected to submit to sexual harassment, or worse, to get ahead in the workplace or even to keep their jobs.

What the reporting did was get the details and evidence necessary to name names --- in many cases, high-profile figures that gained instant public attention. Powerful men in the media and entertainment industry, finance, academia and other fields were exposed, and many lost their jobs over behavior that, in many cases, had been brought to the attention of their superiors in the past. Often, nothing happened until journalists told the survivors' stories, added context and built a case using documents such as emails, texts and internal memos.

The #MeToo reporting illustrates a key role that investigative reporting can play in spotlighting and documenting issues that may already be on the public's radar to varying degrees. Reporting that exposed sexual abuses by the Catholic Church by the Boston Globe and many other media organizations fell into a similar category. The fact that priests abused children was known; the scope of the abuse, the documented details and the evidence that leadership ignored or enabled the abuse made the difference.

Working with survivors who have been subjected to sexual harassment or assault takes special care, a topic discussed in [Chapter 12](#hou_9781319102845_ch12_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_uEfDFmhpJK) on law enforcement. Reporting on the cases that launched the #MeToo movement involved such relationship building, but the scope of the reporting also included examinations of gender inequity in various professions, issues that go beyond harassment and abuse.

### Unequal Wages and Resources

The pay gap between men and women in many fields and the glass ceiling that limits promotion for women have been issues explored by journalists for many years. In 2016 --- two years before the #MeToo movement reporting --- The Wall Street Journal found a way to quantify the pay gap in a multitude of professions. The Journal journalists told IRE in a contest entry form that they started out reading academic research and interviewing experts on the topic. They then mined two key data sets, one from the U.S. Census Bureau and the other from the Federal Bureau of Labor Statistics. The Census material included American Community Survey data that listed median pay, as well as the number of workers, by occupation and gender. The Bureau of Labor Statistics data included earnings by gender and education level. A key goal was to examine the pay gap across occupations, while also including factors such as education level, which can affect pay.

The Journal found that men earned more than women in virtually every job category, with greater differences in those requiring the most education, such as law, medicine and finance. The journalists created a searchable interactive database that readers could explore to examine different professions. Among other findings: "The Journal's analysis of Census Bureau data for the five years through 2014 found male doctors working full time earned about \$210,000 annually on average. Female physicians made 64 percent of that, about \$135,000 a year. Among personal financial advisers, men took in about \$100,000, while women made about \$62,000."

Another area in which stark differences in equal treatment can be found is college athletics. The federal Title IX regulation calls for equity in sports offerings, but data from sources including the NCAA illustrate that such equity is lacking at many universities. The pay gap for those coaching men's and women's sports is wide, and the resources made available to men's and women's teams can be similarly uneven.

The Knight Commission on Intercollegiate Athletics in 2019 created an online searchable database that allows journalists to examine university athletic departments and to compare them, on a wide range of issues, including gender equity and other key areas.


## Investigating Hate

While some issues are specific to certain groups, there are overarching themes that are true when investigating hate crimes based on race, gender or sexual preference. An incident becomes a hate crime when the offense was motivated in whole or in part by bias based on race, religion, disability, sexual orientation, ethnicity, gender or gender identity. Hate crimes can be difficult to prosecute because doing so can involve proving state of mind: Showing why someone assaulted someone, not just proving that they committed the act. In the U.S., most states have hate crime legislation, though the specifics can vary and some state laws are stronger than others.

In an effort to get a handle on the extent of hate crimes in the U.S., ProPublica created its Documenting Hate project, a crowdsourcing effort that involves journalists at multiple news organizations collecting and verifying examples of hate crimes, which often go underreported. In the analysis ProPublica conducted of data on officially reported hate crimes, massive discrepancies were found from state to state and city to city. For example, Florida --- the third most populous state in the U.S. --- reported far fewer hate crimes than much smaller states including North Carolina, Oregon and Minnesota. Therefore, it's vital that journalists step in and document what officials often won't. Rachel Glickhouse of ProPublica put together a list of tips for IRE on investigating hate crimes. Among the recommendations:

- Read police incident reports carefully and keep a lookout for slurs and other signs of bias, whether in their language or otherwise. Just because police didn't identify something as a hate crime doesn't mean it isn't one.
- Find out how police track hate crimes internally. If officers don't know how to correctly code an incident, dozens or even hundreds of crimes can be misclassified.
- Compile a list of hate crime incidents in your area using news clips and cases tracked by nonprofit groups. You can then compare your list to the official data and look for discrepancies.
- Look at what people are saying on social media about an incident. This could provide new leads.
- Establish relationships with diverse communities. Many victims don't report to police, in part because of a lack of trust.



## Immigration and Refugees

Immigrant communities face discrimination and challenges that are often exacerbated by government rules and regulations. Many of these issues explode into public view when a crisis in one country sends its most vulnerable citizens in search of safe haven elsewhere. Immigration issues are a flashpoint in many countries. Politicians play off fears that immigrants pose a threat to jobs and safety, while government agencies and social service organizations struggle to meet the needs of new residents who can face a host of challenges created by the language barrier and cultural gulfs. War and violence against vulnerable communities can drive waves of refugees to nearby countries, leading to strains on government resources and backlash from residents.

Journalists face challenges in reporting and writing about immigration issues. Solid statistics can be hard to come by, especially when trying to get a handle on the number of undocumented residents or refugees. A great deal of documentation and data is available related to immigration cases, including court and police records, and that can be rich material for journalists. For example:

- The Guardian analyzed federal court settlement data to show that the U.S. government paid out more than \$60 million in legal settlements where border agents were involved in deaths, assaults and wrongful detention.
- ProPublica examined police reports and call logs from shelters where the children of immigrants captured by U.S. officials were being kept, separated from their parents. Journalists found more than 125 calls reporting sexual offenses at the shelters.

Further questions to ask when looking into issues surrounding migrant children, according to an IRE tip sheet, include:

- Who keeps national data about migrant children?
- Who has access to that data?
- What level of access do they have to the data?
- Which legal advocates, contractors, state agencies, social workers, medical teams, etc., work with them?
- If you get data, who can help you independently corroborate and cross-check it?

There are many resources for investigating immigration issues. In the U.S., one great source of data on immigration is the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse, or TRAC, based at Syracuse University. TRAC monitors government enforcement and spending, and one of its key focus areas is immigration. TRAC offers data on everything from immigration courts to the activities of the federal Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agency. For investigating immigration globally, reports and data are available from the International Organization for Migration, the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development, the United Nations and the World Bank, among others. More resources are also available from the Global Investigative Journalism Network.

There are immigration stories in every community, not just those near borders. Reporter Maria Perez of the Milwaukee Journal Sentinel lists topics including education, housing and labor conditions as centers for immigration stories in an IRE tip sheet. Among the sources she recommends are local attorneys who focus on immigration and labor issues, churches, advocacy organizations, bodega owners and volunteers who donate their time to organizations that help immigrant communities.


## Experts and Advocates

In addition to the journalism-related organizations listed earlier in this chapter, a great deal of research is being conducted into a range of social justice issues, and journalists can benefit from both reading the work and talking with those doing the research.

The International Network for Hate Studies provides a forum for academics and advocates dedicated to the issue. The network's website includes a lengthy list of both experts and research that can be invaluable for journalists. Additionally, the Southern Poverty Law Center tracks and maps hate crimes in the U.S. The nonprofit organization describes its mission as "fighting hate and bigotry" and "seeking justice for the most vulnerable members of our society."

Human Rights Watch is an advocacy organization that tracks abuse on a range of issues, including women's rights and LGBTQ issues. Their reports highlight key issues in a host of countries, and its staff can provide expertise.

The International Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Trans and Intersex Association, which has been around for decades, funds research into key issues. Among the work it provides that can be of benefit to journalists is a ranking of how European countries handle issues including discrimination and human rights.

The American Civil Liberties Union is involved in many issues addressed in this chapter, including the rights of immigrants, women and members of the LGBTQ community.

# Chapter 17 Investigating Poverty Issues


<aside class="learning-objectives c3 main-flow v1" epub:type="learning-objectives" id="bra_9781319362065_cT9VcP5Lln" title="Chapter Objectives">


## Chapter Objectives

*In this chapter you will learn how to:*

- Examine government programs for the poor that fail to fulfill their goals.
- Find documents and sources to investigate deficient housing.
- Find documents and sources on children in poverty.
- Find documents and sources on nutrition and food issues.

</aside>

In 2016, author and sociologist Matt Desmond described the plight of poor families in Milwaukee who had lost their homes through the inability to pay their rent and how, without their homes, they sunk further into poverty. While Desmond's award-winning book was impressive in its research and its writing, Desmond decided to probe further to determine how widespread evictions were.

Desmond created a team at Princeton, where he taught, to collect court records of evictions across the U.S. and compiled a database of 83 million records from 2000 to 2016. From that database --- stored at the website Eviction Lab --- his team found that landlords in 2016 were evicting at least one in 50 renters nationwide, with the highest rate at one in six in North Charleston, South Carolina. As described in his book, many of those evicted were already impoverished and had no resources to fight the eviction in court.

From that data, reporters told the stories of people losing their homes just as they did after the recession of 2008 when people were unable to pay their mortgages because they had been lured into loans with low interest rates that suddenly soared when the economy went bad. The stories of eviction also show how the loss of a home begins a cascade of other troubles that can lead to a life of extreme poverty and sometimes drug addiction.


## A Wide Array of Approaches

Housing and homelessness is just one area in which investigative reporters can reveal the impact of poverty on people and communities. Others include poor nutrition, low wages, and sparse health care or the lack of it.

In 2017, a U.S. family of four was considered poor if their pretax income fell below about \$26,000. About 37 million people were in poverty in that year-nearly 14% of the total U.S. population. But a large percentage of the poor is called the working poor --- estimated at 9 million people in 2019 or roughly one-fourth of those in poverty. The "working poor" are those who are trying not to live on government subsidies, but who spend 27 weeks or more in a year in the labor force either working or looking for work and whose incomes still fall below the poverty line.

These figures give a broad and abstract view of poverty in one of the world's wealthiest nations, but investigative journalists can focus and localize the impact of poverty by identifying the impoverished areas and groups in their communities through a review of documents and databases and through aggressive fieldwork. By doing so, reporters will find numerous investigative stories. These investigations generally fall into three areas: Exposing appalling living conditions and societal injustices, showing how businesses or con artists may exploit the poor, and uncovering fraud and waste in government programs intended to help impoverished families and children. Some investigations may include elements of all three.



## Investigating the Issues

Having low or no income affects every part of a person's life --- from housing to health care to environmental hazards to mistreatment within the court system. (Many of these areas are covered more in depth in other chapters, particularly in those chapters on law enforcement, the courts and health care.) Among the many issues an investigative journalist may look at are lack of access to health care and health care insurance, residential proximity to pollution and hazardous waste, lack of city or county services like police or street maintenance or public transportation, lack of sufficient food and nutrition for children, high frequency of crime and shortage of adequately paying jobs.

In a thorough series of classic investigative stories entitled "The Black Belt," The Birmingham (Alabama) News showed just how widespread problems created by poverty were in 12 counties in Alabama: "Pick a statistic: Poverty. Infant deaths. Poor education. Births to single mothers. Unemployment. Gaps in health care. As Alabama and Mississippi lag the nation in most of those categories, this swath of counties trails the rest of the state." For the story, the reporters gathered statistics from numerous agencies and institutions, including the U.S. Census Bureau; the Alabama departments of Public Health and Education; the U.S. Department of Labor; the Center for Demographic Research at Auburn University, Montgomery; and the Center for Business and Economic Research, University of Alabama. But they also talked to officials and social scientists and traveled through the 12-county region, extensively interviewing those living in poverty and demonstrating the deep effect of poverty on individuals and families.

At The Columbus Dispatch, journalists did a series on poverty in the Appalachia region of Ohio and used similar reports and studies and traveled the region. But The Dispatch reporters focused on the failure of federal programs, noting that their investigation revealed that "billions of federal tax dollars dedicated to helping struggling residents of Appalachia have missed their mark in the core of the impoverished region."

### Special Challenges for Reporters

In the past, most U.S. newsrooms were staffed with white, college-educated reporters and editors who often had little street knowledge about impoverished communities. While there has been a substantial rise in the number of ethnic media newsrooms (newsrooms that cover a specific ethnicity in a non-English language) and a rise in different ethnicities in newsrooms, mainstream newsrooms are still largely white --- about 77% in 2018 --- journalists covering poverty still face the challenge of going into unfamiliar areas where they are regarded with suspicion.

But like the editors and reporters at The Birmingham News and The Columbus Dispatch, journalists can prepare themselves for investigations by researching the issues of poverty and reading government and nonprofit institute reports. Census Bureau and Government Accountability Office reports can provide not only numbers but also context and story ideas. State census data centers can also be helpful for getting a better idea of the local issues. Among the many other valuable organizations to check are these:

- The Urban Institute, which has lots of research and discussion on its website.
- The National Poverty Center at the University of Michigan.
- The Institute for Research on Poverty at the University of Wisconsin.
- The Southern Poverty Law Center in Montgomery, Alabama.
- The National Center for Children in Poverty at Columbia University in New York City.

From there, an investigative journalist can start to reach out to human sources, whether it's an urban or rural story. Longtime investigative reporter Tom Brune shared his ideas for finding and contacting those sources in a tip sheet in the IRE Resource Center. He suggested going to the following:

- Community organizations. These groups include the most active residents of an area. The activists in turn know people whose stories can illustrate your report.
- Legal aid groups, such as legal assistance foundations, public defenders, public guardians and public interest law firms. These groups have clients who often want to tell their stories.
- Local and federal court officials and the files they keep.
- Churches and church-based agencies like Catholic Charities, Lutheran Social Services and the Jewish Federation. They deal directly with people you might want to interview.
- Other charities, like the United Way, that provide direct services or are active in the issues of race and poverty.
- Social workers at welfare agencies. They have many cases to choose from if they want to provide information.
- Interest groups, such as organizations created to push for better housing.
- Human relations commissions, part of a local or state government structure.
- People who write letters to the editor of the local newspaper on poverty issues.
- Cold calls into a neighborhood based on names from the city directory or some other source.

The above combination of documents and sources --- along with monitoring social media of impoverished neighborhoods and anti-poverty groups --- can provide investigative reporters with a direction for their investigation, but the reporting will be detached until journalists get out into the community and begin seeing poverty through the perspective of those who live it. Reporters who have not grown up in impoverished circumstances will need to think about how to present themselves and about what guidelines they may want to follow.

Among the consistent tips given by experienced reporters over the years are:

- Think about how you dress and the car you drive. You don't want to stand out or look like a city official, bill collector or police officer.
- Know where you are going, be aware of your surroundings and take sensible precautions. It's often a good idea to go with someone else, perhaps a photographer or another reporter. If possible, make connections in the area ahead of time so you will know some residents and they can put the word out that you are a reporter and can be trusted. Know when to end the interview if those you are interviewing are getting upset.
- Be trustworthy. If someone asks for confidentiality and you grant it, keep that confidentiality. Both you and the interviewee should be very clear on what is for attribution and what is not.
- Be empathetic, but do not make promises on what you will write or try to act as a social worker for those you are interviewing.
- Leave information on how to get in touch with you by phone, text or email. The first interview may be short and without much information given. But the interviewee may decide to reach out to you later.

### Financial Exploitation

Although the poor by definition have less money than most, con artists and businesses prey on them financially. Con artists lure the poor into purchases they can't afford or small investments they can't recoup. Businesses charge them high prices or loan money at exorbitant rates. Even state governments get into the game through their lottery systems, which some critics call another "tax" on the poor because it lures the poor into buying lottery tickets although they have a small chance of winning. (To see if this is happening in a particular state, journalists often compare the database of location of lottery ticket sales with census data on income levels in those areas.)

Previous chapters have covered subprime lending for housing and insurance ploys in which salespeople get the poor or elderly to buy insurance policies for more than they will ever pay off. But there are numerous other financial areas to investigate.

Investigative journalists Ron Nixon and Holly Hacker put together a stellar tip sheet on the topic for IRE following a project they did for the Star Tribune in Minneapolis. They write:

> Credit is the lifeblood of a community. It enables people to invest in their future by buying homes, send their children to college with the promise of a better life and it allows them, among other things, to purchase things such as furniture or a car.
>
> But for many moderate and low-income Americans, credit can come at a steep price. For these residents, there is a two-tier economy, one made up of traditional banks and financial institutions and a second made up of what is known as the "fringe financial industry." This industry posts revenues in the billions of dollars each year mainly from customers who lack access to banks or other mainstream financial institutions.

Nixon and Hacker further break down the industry into sections that explore possible routes of financial exploitation of low-income communities. These include:

- *Payday lenders.* These lenders offer small loans backed by a postdated check. The consumer borrows a certain amount --- typically between \$150 and \$500 --- until his or her next payday. In some states the amounts may be larger.
- *Car title lenders*. These lenders offer small loans where a vehicle is used as collateral against the loan. These loans are typically for a period of 30 days. The issuer of the loan will take possession of the car if the loan is not repaid in the time allotted.
- *Check-cashing outlets.* Check-cashing outlets perform a variety of services, from cashing payroll and government checks to selling money orders or transferring funds. These stores usually charge about 3% to 10% of the face value of a check to cash it. Some of these stores also offer payday loans.
- *Pawnshops*. Pawnshops provide a small loan to customers in exchange for merchandise held for a period of time by the shop with the agreement that the customer will reclaim the property in the future. The customer must also pay interest on the loan and a storage fee.
- *Rapid refund loans.* Although most people think of rapid refunds as tax refunds, they are actually loans made by national banks through companies like H&R Block for a fee in anticipation of a refund from the IRS.
- *Rent-to-own stores.* In these stores, customers rent furniture on a weekly basis. The stores cater mostly to low-income communities, where residents pay \$25 or more a week for items like televisions.
- *Subprime loans.* These high-cost loans are usually intended for borrowers with poor credit, heavy debt or both. Subprime loans often carry interest rates of 8% to 20% and fees of 4% to 10% of the total loan. The high rates and fees are designed to protect the lender in the event that a borrower defaults on the loan.

Nixon and Hacker then suggest these steps for getting the story and exposing examples of financial exploitation:

1.  Know the law. Check the statutes that govern payday lenders, check cashers and others. In most states this is a largely unregulated industry. In states where the industry is regulated, the statutes will give you some idea of what information or data these industries have to provide for reporting purposes.
2.  Check court filings. Court files are a rich source of information about the practices of the industry and can also point you to people who feel they have been wronged.
3.  Get a firm understanding of how the fringe financial sector works. Several books can be helpful in understanding the business: John Caskey's "Fringe Banking," Robert Manning's "Credit Card Nation," Mike Hudson's "Merchants of Misery" and David Caplovitz's "The Poor Pay More." The Consumer Federation of America and an industry trade group, the Financial Service Centers of America, can also provide information.
4.  Get census data to study the demographics of the areas surrounding payday lenders, check-cashing stores and so on. These data are especially important for mapping.
5.  Get payday loan financial information. Some states like Minnesota require lenders to provide information on the number and amounts of loans, defaults and carry-over loans. Check with state regulators to see what is required.

Since the Star Tribune stories, many others have been done in which reporters in such states as New Hampshire, Missouri, Illinois and Washington have written about the high interest rates --- often higher than 500% annually --- being charged to low- and moderate-income people, including military personnel who could lose their security clearances because of large, high-interest debts.

### Employment

Another area to investigate is how the poor are taken advantage of by employers in both urban and rural areas who pay low wages to the employees, whether they are immigrants, elderly or disabled.

Reporters from both The Palm Beach Post and the Midwest Center for Investigative Reporting found migrant workers in Florida and the Midwest were earning extraordinarily low pay while living in squalor. The Midwest Center also tracked how job recruiters under contract with big agribusiness companies lured the workers from Texas with false promises of higher wages to toil in cornfields in the Midwest. In other states, particularly in the Midwest and South, journalists have also exposed low pay and dangerous working conditions for illegal immigrants who work at food-processing plants in rural areas. The Plain Dealer in Cleveland reported on companies employing inner-city people, mostly elderly, disabled or welfare recipients, to work in their own homes putting together screws and washers for automobile manufacturers. The workers earned \$1.50 for every 1,000 screws --- less than \$1 per hour. The workers kept their work hidden for fear of losing their welfare, and so the underground laborers were isolated from the IRS and the protections of federal law. Other newsrooms have found workers, often illegal immigrants, toiling in factories, restaurants and motels for inexplicably low wages.

In a more general investigation, The Wall Street Journal explored the hardships of the working poor and found that those earning minimum wage often are worse off than those who receive welfare because they can't qualify for as many government benefits. In the classic book "Nickel and Dimed," author Barbara Ehrenreich personalized the investigation into the working poor by posing as an unskilled worker earning low wages. Through that approach she detailed the plight of America's working poor by taking low-skilled jobs for a month at a time. She worked as a waitress, a cleaning woman, an aide in a nursing home and an employee of Walmart. She found that her income did not cover her expenses unless she worked two jobs seven days a week. Even then, paying for adequate housing was nearly impossible.

Exploitative employment practices ensure that the poor are financially locked into poverty with little hope of getting out despite government programs created to alleviate or eliminate poverty. Indeed, these practices often increase the demand on government programs and undercut their potential successes.

### Children and Families

Because children cannot provide for themselves, they sometimes suffer abuse and neglect at all income levels. Writing in the 1990s Nieman Reports magazine, Carol Kreck of The Denver Post said that when she was handed the children's beat, she realized she had a blank check: "Played the right way, all the beats were mine --- health care, courts, cops, the legislature, Congress, education, urban affairs." That observation is still relevant and a reminder of the importance of focusing on children's issues and the role they play in every realm of poverty and inequity. Investigations surrounding children can be related to topics like child abuse, juvenile justice, foster care and day care.

The Annie E. Casey Foundation ran a program for journalists covering children's issues until 2014. The program gave out annual awards for excellent work on that topic and those awards left a legacy of stories that showed the range of investigations that could be done. Here are summaries of just three of the winning entries:

- In "Bury Your Mistakes," The Philadelphia Inquirer documented the Philadelphia Department of Human Service's callous neglect of abused children it was charged with protecting. When officials refused to provide information on many cases, the reporters combed neighborhoods to give the stories scope and depth. The response was significant: Moved by the children's stories and nailed by the hard-nosed reporting, state legislators toughened laws and city officials had no choice but to clean house at the agency.
- In its story "Lethal Lapses," The Belleville (Illinois) News-Democrat documented how Illinois' Department of Children and Family Services mishandled cases involving 53 children who died in its care between 1998 and 2005. Reporters demonstrated great enterprise --- scrutinizing Social Security Administration death records, coroner and police reports and countless other materials --- to connect the dots and identify the child victims. They got beyond confidentiality laws that too often leave the state's most vulnerable wards insufficiently protected and unknown even in death.
- In "Failures by State, Caregiver Kept Secret in Child-Rape Case," The Seattle Times combined exhaustive investigation and skilled storytelling in a devastating account of systemic problems within Washington's child welfare system. The story went beyond one terrible anecdote, giving it breadth and lasting impact. It was an increasingly rare example of a newspaper investing brawn and real resources in its watchdog role.

The IRE Resource Center also has many investigations on issues relating to families and children, offering numerous tip sheets on how to conduct these kinds of investigations.

### Homelessness

No matter how much low-cost housing is available, most communities still have to deal with the problem of homelessness. Some homeless people are mentally ill, some are mentally sound but impoverished and others don't want to find shelter on their own.

Journalists can investigate how police and social welfare agencies deal with the homeless. They can check on what happens when homeless persons are arrested and where they are taken. They can also check on the living conditions and financing of shelters and the ability of the shelters to meet the demand for emergency housing and to deal with the kind of clients they get. With the rise in home foreclosures and housing costs in the U.S., the potential need for shelters and other temporary housing will also rise.

### Health Care

Another major issue facing the poor is health care. Despite the Affordable Care Act, passed in 2010, tens of millions of Americans have no health care insurance and seek help at emergency rooms and local clinics when they become ill. Some hospitals will refuse care (which has led to successful class-action lawsuits). Some private hospitals will "dump" an uninsured patient on a publicly funded hospital by transporting the patient from their emergency room to the emergency room of the other hospital. This means the private hospital does not have to provide services that it thinks it will never be paid for.

Another burgeoning crisis is dental care. Many rural communities have no free or low-cost dental care, and poor people have no way to get to a larger community for help until their dental problems become critical. (For more information on investigating health and dental care, refer to [Chapter 20](#hou_9781319102845_ch20_01.xhtml_bra_9781319362065_HRsQOgSB4a).)


## Investigating the Programs

In 2017, the Government Accountability Office issued a report describing the various laws governing programs for low-income people. It also described six major programs totaling \$540 billion, most of which have experienced withering audits by the GAO or by other government investigative agencies. The list provides a quick guide for journalists on which agencies to investigate:

- *Temporary Assistance for Needy Families.* TANF permits a state to give ongoing basic cash aid to families that include a minor or a pregnant woman. Work and other requirements must be met.
- *Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP).* The program provides certain allotments --- food stamps --- to individuals to purchase food, based on the individual's level of eligibility and need.
- *Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants and Children.* WIC provides benefits for low-income mothers, infants and children who are considered to be at "nutritional risk."
- *Medicaid.* Medicaid provides payments to health care providers in full or via copay for eligible low-income families and individuals and for long-term care to eligible individuals who are aged or disabled.
- *Housing Choice Vouchers.* The program provides rental assistance through vouchers or rental subsidies to eligible low-income families or single people.
- *Earned Income Credit*. This program provides tax credits ranging from hundreds to thousands of dollars for low-income families.

Some programs are funded entirely by the federal government, and some have been turned over to the states or are run by the states under contract to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. Other programs are largely paid for by the states without federal involvement.

Many programs are overseen by the Administration for Children and Families, under HHS, and information for each program can be found at its website.

One of the most effective and traditional ways to investigate the failings of a system is to combine documentation and interviews with officials and experts with the detailed story of a person trying to get help from the system.

### Temporary Assistance for Needy Families

A major program, TANF replaced the Aid to Families with Dependent Children and the JOBS programs in 1997. States and territories now run their own TANF programs; Native American tribes can also choose to run their own.

With TANF, states, territories and tribes each receive a block grant allocation. The block grant covers benefits, administrative expenses and services. States, territories and tribes determine eligibility, benefit levels and services provided to needy families, and there is no longer a federal entitlement. Journalists who investigate local TANF programs often find that unqualified individuals are getting assistance or that the program is so confusing that qualified individuals do not get the help they need. In the pursuit of any stories of fraud or mismanagement, journalists should routinely check inspector generals' websites and reports of the relevant agencies.

### Child-Support Enforcement

Contributing to the problems of the impoverished is the lack of support by parents who abandon their families and children. Federal, state and local governments try to collect delinquent child-support payments on behalf of custodial parents, and laws have been strengthened to the point where absent parents can be imprisoned. Some states contract with county governments to handle enforcement and to locate the parent, establish paternity when necessary, set a payment level, monitor compliance and disburse any money collected. To force payment, the government agency can garnish wages, intercept tax refunds, file tax liens on properties and refer the case to the local prosecutor for court action. Many custodial parents are TANF recipients.

Examining child-support enforcement procedures and the enforcers themselves can be worthwhile. When the St. Louis Post-Dispatch matched a database of state employees against a database of absent parents, they identified some Illinois state employees who owed child support, including some whose job was to enforce child-support laws.

### Food Stamps

The Department of Agriculture's food stamp program, now known as SNAP, uses state welfare agencies to distribute stamps to needy families and individuals. Recipients can redeem their food stamps at authorized stores. However, the program has been accused of enabling widespread fraud. Food stamp fraud encompasses ineligible recipients, authorized retailers selling unauthorized nonfood items (from toilet paper to cocaine) for the stamps, and retailers exchanging stamps for cash. Also, individuals have been found working from a private residence, setting themselves up as "stores" to redeem stamps. Journalists can spend time at food stamp pickup points if they are interested in documenting fraud.

A San Francisco Bay Guardian reporter accompanied a recipient to a check-cashing store to pick up the monthly food stamp allotment. That same outlet was a regular site of food stamp--related criminal activity: Recipients were robbed of stamps as they left the store, they were offered cash for stamps at 50 cents on the dollar and stamps were traded for drugs.

After visiting distribution sites, journalists can walk down the street to retailers authorized to redeem stamps. The journalist can watch to see if food stamp recipients are offered cash, drugs or other commodities for stamps. Some states are trying to combat fraud by instituting an electronic benefits transfer program. Having recipients use a computerized card at the cash register leaves a paper trail and reduces street trafficking.

The GAO has noted "the Food Stamp Program remains vulnerable because retailers can enter the program intending to traffic and do so, often without fear of severe criminal penalties, as the declining number of investigations referred for prosecution suggests." Journalists can find out what anti-fraud techniques are being tried in their jurisdiction and what prosecutions are being carried out. At the same time, political attacks on the program have led to severe cuts in the program or changes in eligibility that lead to poor families losing access to the program.

### Other Child and Family Nutrition Programs

While probing the SNAP program, journalists can ask recipients whether the poor are able to use other assistance to feed their families nutritious meals every day. Often, the programs may not be coordinated, leading to confusion and ineffectiveness. All programs, many of which are run by the USDA, are worth investigating individually or collectively.

Another program to examine is the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants and Children (WIC). WIC is supposed to provide adequate nourishment for pregnant women and mothers and their young children. Recipients receive vouchers for use on specific items at grocery stores and farmers' markets. Coverage extends beyond pregnancy for women and for children to age 5. The program contains an educational component about nutrition and limited health care.

Journalists can check to see how many needy women are being denied benefits because of funding limitations. Unlike many other hunger and poverty programs, WIC is dependent on annual appropriations, rather than serving everybody who meets objective requirements.

### Other Assistance Programs

When journalists do find trouble or fraud in welfare programs, they often find abuse of a related program, Supplemental Security Income. SSI was originally established to help elderly people who were ineligible for Social Security, but government bureaucracies have extended SSI eligibility to middle-aged and young people who claim mental disorders, including hard-to-prove (or disprove) anxiety attacks and depression. Using appeals filed at the Social Security Administration and word-of-mouth in neighborhoods, The Baltimore Sun produced a series following case histories of SSI recipients, complete with real names and specific income figures.

Other journalists have looked into whether adults have become foster parents in order to collect benefits rather than provide food and shelter for needy children. Many reporters have made it routine to run databases of foster care parents against databases of felons when the data are available.

### Welfare Investigations

On any welfare investigation, journalists can contact recipients through social workers or employees at other charitable agencies or by simply approaching them outside public agency offices. After making contact and winning the trust of the recipients, journalists can accompany them through their day or week or month and record their frustrations.

Or, like Barbara Ehrenreich, the journalist can go underground as one reporter did while on assignment for the Los Angeles Times Magazine. Posing as an applicant for assistance, the reporter experienced firsthand the difficulties and hostilities that many welfare recipients experienced.

In addition to investigating the flaws of the U.S. welfare system and fraud in federal and state programs, journalists can follow the history of welfare reform and the government's objective of moving the impoverished from welfare to jobs. Shortcomings of the new system as it develops have been looked at in several stories.

Investigating the issues of the impoverished is discouraging and sometimes dangerous. But it can also lead to some of the most important and rewarding work a journalist can do --- the kind of work that can make an immediate difference in people's lives.
