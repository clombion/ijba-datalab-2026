**Abstract**
Using data collected in the assessment survey of a series of data journalism
(DJ) training activities in Hong Kong, this study compares participants’ learning
activities. These participants consist of students, scholars and professionals from
diverse academic backgrounds. The findings show that significant differences exist
between learners with journalism-related backgrounds and those with science
backgrounds. Compared with journalism majors, during the activities, science
majors interact with others more and get more practice; they also gain more
theoretical and practical outcomes alongside higher levels of learning enjoyment.
Interviews with journalism majors indicate that lack of fundamental knowledge,
time and support in practice brings difficulties to their DJ learning. Suggestions
for an interdisciplinary approach to journalism education were also collected
through interviews, which include more information on DJ, a deeper level of
interdisciplinary interaction and more interactions with the industry. Survey and
in-depth interviews are both used in this research to provide references for DJ
students and instructors.

**Introduction**


The ‘age of data’ has seen a proliferation of large digitized data sets alongside an
increase in data-driven journalism or news cultivated and visualized using such
data. As more and more data are being recorded by a variety of institutions in a
nearly ubiquitous way, today’s environment of complex data sets and convergent
media has made it imperative for both working and future journalists to be skilled
and routinized in reporting data-driven stories. Technology, along with the
abundance of data, has profoundly changed the landscape of news media. Many
novel devices and concepts, as well as hybrids of journalism and technology, have
emerged, making the practice of journalism vastly different from what it was a
decade ago. As Doherty (2012) said: ‘Journalism has pushed itself in new directions before, incorporating skills and techniques from other disciplines to evolve
the practice’ (p. 118).
Data journalism (DJ) originates in the integration of journalism and technology.
It involves various intellectual areas including journalism, computer science,
statistics and visual and graphic design. The interdisciplinary nature of DJ requires
media practitioners to have skills in other fields. Many news organizations are
desperately looking for data journalists who possess a certain level of technical
skills but, as of now, very few journalism graduates are readily equipped with that.
This calls for an interdisciplinary approach in journalism education.
In recent years, some scholars have discussed the education of DJ from different
perspectives, such as the type of DJ education provider, form of DJ education and
attitude of students towards it (Hewett, 2016; Nguyen & Lugo-Ocando, 2016;
Splendore et al., 2016; Yang & Du, 2016). However, what is missing is the
consideration of learners’ various academic backgrounds. Some researchers limit
their definition of DJ learners to journalism majors, while others give no special
emphasis to that. Although journalism students account for a large proportion of
DJ learners, many science majors (students from scientific and technological
majors, such as engineering, information science and mathematics) are interested
in DJ, where they can apply their technological skills in practice. Actually, the
group of data journalists and DJ learners shows a high level of diversity in their
backgrounds. In terms of their primary majors, learners can be divided into
different groups. Analysing and comparing each group’s learning activities can
give a picture of their respective advantages and disadvantages in learning DJ.
Based on a specific understanding of the learners, educators can modify their
teaching to suit the special conditions of each group.
This research was conducted to explore the effect a learner’s major has on
DJ education. To achieve this purpose, a sample of individuals from diverse
backgrounds is first needed. Samples of this study are participants in a series of
DJ training activities in Hong Kong, who are students, scholars and professionals
from different academic backgrounds. Thus, the diversity requirement was met.
Through quantitatively analysing participants’ feedback on the activities, this
study compares journalism majors [1] with science majors [2] . The findings showed

that science majors were more proactive in interacting with others and participating
in practice; they also gained more theoretical and practical results with higher
levels of learning enjoyment. Because of the disadvantages journalism majors
have, in-depth interviews were conducted to summarize the difficulties journalism
students encounter in their learning, and suggestions were collected to develop
training for future data journalists.


**Literature Review and Research Background**


_Data Journalism and Its Education_


Initially DJ was primarily perceived as the use of numerical data to report a story
(Berret & Phillips, 2016). Some regard DJ as a variation of computer-assistedreporting, or CAR (Coddington, 2015), which was mostly driven by precision
journalism (Meyer, 2002). Yang and Du (2016) claimed that DJ builds on the
older practices of CAR and takes them further by engaging with data analysts,
infographic designers and visualizers in producing and communicating stories via
multimedia platforms. Other scholars see DJ as an evolving form of investigative
journalism (Appelgren & Nygren, 2014). DJ has also been considered as a more
advanced combination of mathematics, statistical computation (Nguyen, 2010),
application of basic statistical analysis (Flew, Spurgeon, Daniel & Swift, 2012)
and an array of techniques to find data. From an operational perspective, Howard
(2014) defined DJ as ‘gathering, cleaning, organizing, analysing, visualizing,
and publishing data to support the creation of acts of journalism’ (p. 4). Parasie
and Dagiral (2013) pointed out that DJ uniquely focuses more on data visualization,
large data size, together with response to the unbound information scale.
Although the definitions of DJ are diverse, the technical factors embedded in
this concept are emphasized frequently. The incorporation of computer science
and statistics in journalism leads to new requirements for journalists to meet.
Most theorists agree data skill sets include accessing data, analysing data and
developing stories on the basis of data analysis (Treadwell, Ross, Lee &
Lowenstein, 2016), which are beyond traditional journalistic skills like conducting
interviews and writing reports. As such, the curricula of journalism education
should respond to changes in practice.
DJ has been included in the curricula of many journalism schools, but it has not
matured enough to be an individual sub-major. Universities in Hong Kong have
programmes showing the integration between journalism and technology, but
none of them focus specifically on DJ. This is a similar situation to journalism
schools in the USA and UK. The US journalism education is not changing far or
fast enough in the DJ direction (Hewett, 2016). One journalism educator in the
UK stated that a period of studying journalism does not seem to be the route into
DJ (Hannaford, 2014).
In reality, there is no readily generalizable career path for data journalists.
Some data journalists began their career as graphic designers or computer scientists

rather than reporters (Fink & Anderson, 2015). Based on their educational
background, data journalists’ development paths can be summarized into two
categories: the journalism path, which is based on a journalistic background
complemented by technological skills, and the technology path, which contributes
to news production from the starting point of offering technological support. This
means that there are DJ learners from majors outside of journalism; however, they
are neglected in the research on DJ education. Existing research generally focuses
on journalism students’ views (e.g., Davies & Cullen, 2016; Hewett, 2016;
Treadwell et al., 2016; Yang & Du, 2016). This neglect calls for research that gets
people from diverse backgrounds involved.
Scholars have found that the prior knowledge background of journalism
students inhibits their learning of DJ. Students who apply to programmes in
journalism schools are typically those who have a passion for humanities generally
and lack the skills in data analysis (Treadwell et al., 2016). In Davies and Cullen’s
research on DJ education in Australian universities (2016), academic staff from 11
of the 25 universities offering DJ education stated that ‘students’ low levels of
mathematical and quantitative literacy skills made teaching DJ difficult’ (p. 142).
Many journalism majors want to be journalists because they like to write, and
therefore they are likely to resist the learning of computer science skills
(Doherty, 2012). Moreover, data work might be off-putting for students and cause
tension with their satisfaction (Hewett, 2016). Journalism students do not seem to
be fit for DJ learning in terms of their prior knowledge, learning willingness and
satisfaction. More research should be done with the aim to offer effective DJ
education to journalism majors.


_Interdisciplinarity_


Multidisciplinarity, cross-disciplinarity and interdisciplinarity are all variations of
disciplinarity, indicating the relationship between different disciplines. According
to Davies and Devlin (2010), multidisciplinarity refers to the coexistence of
several disciplines which includes neither practical nor intellectual exchange,
while cross-disciplinary work generally involves the use of techniques and tools
from foreign fields but does not include any methodological transfer. As for
interdisciplinarity, Rogers, Scaife and Rizzo (2005) defined it as ‘the emergence
of insight and understanding of a problem domain through the integration or
derivation of different concepts, methods, and epistemologies from different
disciplines in a novel way’ (p. 265). Angus and Doherty (2015) summarized that
interdisciplinarity involves both collaboration and a transfer of concepts between
disciplines. As the integration of journalism and technology in DJ is deep and
novel, ‘interdisciplinarity’ is considered to be most appropriate to describe DJ in
this research.

_Interdisciplinary curriculum design:_ Interdisciplinary curriculum design is regarded
by scholars as being synonymous with curriculum integration (Burton, 2001).

According to Beane (1995), in curriculum integration, ‘knowledge from the
disciplines is repositioned into the context of the theme, questions, and activities
at hand’ (p. 620). Beane also proposed that in the process of repositioning
knowledge, within a separate discipline area, sequences of the flow of knowledge
tend to be rearranged and some information and skills may be excluded in the
context of curriculum integration.
Jacobs (1989) pointed out that there are two problems when planning interdisciplinary courses: (a) the potpourri problem, which is caused by random samplings
of knowledge, lack of focus and the absence of inherent scope and sequence,
and (b) the polarity problem, which results from putting too much emphasis on
either interdisciplinary or discipline-field perspectives while ignoring the other.
To avoid these two problems, effective interdisciplinary programmes must have
carefully conceived design features (a scope and sequence, a cognitive taxonomy
to encourage thinking skills, behavioural indicators of attitudinal change, etc.) and
use both interdisciplinary and discipline -field-based experiences for students.


**The Initiatives for DJ Education**


This research involves two DJ educational initiatives in Hong Kong. One is
the Data & News Society (D&N) and the other is Data Visualization for News.
In Asia, Hong Kong has a strong media industry because of its open society and
press freedom. Although now under the rule of Communist China, Hong Kong is
still a major centre for broadcasting and publishing and home to global media
players in Asia such as the Asian Wall Street Journal and Bloomberg. Journalism
education is popular in Hong Kong, and half of its 10 public-funded institutions,
which are its leading universities, provide either a bachelors or higher degrees in
journalism or related programmes. As in other advanced parts of the world, Hong
Kong’s journalism or media programmes in higher education have begun to offer
courses and training in DJ.


_The data and news society:_ D&N, established in 2015, is an interdisciplinary
Community of Practice based in Hong Kong with initial funding from Hong Kong
Baptist University. The Society involves students, scholars and professionals
from diverse academic backgrounds, including journalism, computer science,
statistics, visual communication and more. It serves as a colloquium for media
professionals, instructors and students who are interested in the collaboration of
data and news. The Society holds various activities such as monthly colloquiums
and open lectures to train future journalists in data skills, which are open to all
faculty members and students university wide, in order to promote a campus
culture of interdisciplinary exchanging and sharing. Working professionals from
the local and global media industry are also invited to share their experience in
data news production. All participants take part in the activities held by the D&N
voluntarily.

The goals and missions of the D&N are (a) Networking: to connect ‘news
reporting’ people with ‘technology’ people, to broaden the horizon of journalism
majors to data and programming and to broaden the horizon of science majors to
news making; (b) Training: to organize and host intensive workshops on DJ;
(c) Promoting: to showcase high-quality student works of DJ via exhibitions and
publications; and (d) Resourcing: to facilitate knowledge sharing via online
discussions/references and offline small gatherings with presentations.
During the 2016–2017 academic year, the D&N held four monthly colloquiums
and one open lecture, organized one field trip to the data desk of a Hong Kong
news organization and undertook one student exchange visit to Taiwan, thus
connecting a total of over 200 students, scholars and professionals from diverse
academic backgrounds. These include journalism, computer science, statistics
and visual communication. These participants are regarded as a mixed-background
sample and they were invited to participate in an assessment survey. The results
were used to explore the following questions:


RQ1: Is motivation for interdisciplinary learning influenced by a learner’s
major?
RQ2: Is participation in interdisciplinary learning influenced by a learner’s
major?
RQ3: Is the outcome gained from interdisciplinary learning influenced by a
learner’s major?


_Data visualization for news:_ It is an elective DJ course offered by the Hong Kong
Baptist University Department of Journalism to undergraduate students in Spring
2017. This course has an emphasis on visualization technologies and tools for
news production in which HTML/CSS, JavaScript101, Tableau Pro and CARTO
are included. The aim of the course is to examine DJ at its core through
case studies and hands-on practice of data visualization skills. Throughout the
course, students learn how to conceptualize, design, visualize and produce more
sophisticated data-based news stories.
The class was taught by an instructor with academic training in information
technology (IT) and work experience in DJ. The instructor and students from this
course as well as all D&N participants were invited to participate in this research.
Through the in-depth interviews with them, the following questions were
explored:


RQ4: What difficulties do students have in interdisciplinary learning?
RQ5: What suggestions do survey respondents have for schools offering DJ
education?

**Methods**


_Quantitative Research_


_Sample:_ Participants for our survey were defined as those who provided their
email addresses when registering for D&N activities. As a result, a total of 127
potential participants were identified. The survey was conducted between
September 2016 and May 2017. An invitation e-mail with a link to the questionnaire was sent to each of them, 49 of which responded to our survey after the
initial invitation and 2 rounds of follow-ups. These 49 respondents formed the
actual sample for quantitative analysis, resulting in a response rate of 39 per cent.
Respondents from two kinds of majors were selected as the sample: (a) majors
related to journalism and communication (J&C majors), including financial
journalism, international journalism and media studies, and (b) science majors.
Participants from other arts or humanities majors like English and literature were
excluded to make this study focus more on students in the journalism school.
Furthermore, including learners from other soft disciplines may cause confusion
in measurement and discussion. Data journalists need to be knowledgeable in
both technology and journalism. Journalism students and science students have
already be equipped with the knowledge in their own fields, but it is hard to say
whether arts or humanities majors have the same level of journalism training
as their journalism counterparts. Meanwhile, in this research, the number of
participants from other arts or humanities majors turned out to be only five, which
is adequate for a meaningful statistical analysis. Therefore, to avoid inequalities
in comparison and ensure the validity of this research, learners from other soft
disciplines were excluded.
Mathematics, biology and computer science are typical science disciplines.
Learners from this background have pre-existing skills in data processing that can
match the technology version of DJ. Finance and business management actually
are social science disciplines and in this research, they were categorized as science
majors because mathematical and statistical analysis is also emphasized in these
fields.

_Design of the questionnaire:_ Based on a situated learning perspective (Young,
1993), a commitment of D&N is to engage students in situated practice in various
tasks of using data in news storytelling. The D&N Activity Assessment Survey
questionnaire was designed in light of the models of assessing situated learning
activities (Young, 1995). It consists of four parts. The first three parts measure
learning activities in terms of motivation, participation and outcome, and the
last part includes demographic variables covering undergraduate major, gender
and age.
Guided by principles of designing questionnaires (Dillman, Smyth & Christian,
2009), all questions in the first 3 parts are in the form of narratives measured on a
5-point scale from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree).


_Zhu and Du_ 23


Many variables were broken down into lower order components and measured
by different items. Scores of items testing the same variable were added and then
averaged to generate an index to represent the variable.

_Motivations:_ This variable was measured by four components: networking purposes
(Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.85), training purposes (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.84), promoting
purposes (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.93) and resourcing purposes (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.95).

_Participation:_ The level of participation, which judges how proactive the
participants were in the activities, was measured by interaction with others and
involvement in practice.
Interaction with others was gauged by the statements: ‘When I participated in
activities, I asked others who were involved in the activities for their thoughts or
opinions’; ‘I had more interaction and communication with classmates/other
audience members’; ‘I had more interaction and communication with the
instructor/speaker’; ‘When I participated in activities, I was attentive to the
activities as I worked with others on it’; and ‘When I participated in activities,
I showed interest in the activities as I worked with others on it’ (Cronbach’s
_α_ = 0.83).

Involvement in practice was measured by: ‘When I participated in activities,
through my effort, I participated in getting certain tasks done’ and ‘activities have
given me the chance to practice what I learn’ (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.76).

_Outcomes:_ This variable was divided into two aspects _:_ theoretical outcomes and
practical outcomes.
Theoretical outcomes refer to gains in understanding the concept of DJ and
were gauged by three statements: ‘activities have allowed me to improve my
understanding of the basic elements of data journalism’; ‘activities have allowed
me to appreciate the important issues in data journalism’; and ‘I feel more
scientifically-minded after participating in the activities’ (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.76).

Practical outcomes are closely related to DJ practice and were shown by the
answers to: ‘I’ve learned to identify data sources for news stories’; ‘I’ve learned
how to analyse data for (news) story-telling’; and ‘I’ve learned how to visualize
data for (news) story-telling’ (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.87). Data acquisition, analysis
and visualization are frequently mentioned typical skills that data journalists
should master (Gray, Bounegru & Chambers, 2012; Karlsen & Stavelin, 2014;
Treadwell et al., 2016; Yang & Du, 2016); thus, practical outcomes are broken
down into these three items to measure.

_Learning enjoyment:_ Four items were rated: ‘When I participated in activities,
I felt a sense of enjoyment from doing the activities’; ‘When I participated in
activities, I had fun interacting with my group members’; ‘I think the learning
environment was interesting’; and ‘When I participated in activities, my interest
in data journalism grew’ (Cronbach’s _α_ = 0.76).

_Data analysis:_ From RQ1 to RQ3, several rounds of independent-sample _t_ -tests
were conducted by SPSS 23.0 to see whether there were statistical differences.

_Qualitative Research_


_Sample:_ The sample for the qualitative study comprises students and alumni
involved in our DJ education initiatives. Snowball sampling was used to form a
sample of students. Alumni research subjects were identified as those who met the
following requirements: (a) joined the activities held by D&N when they were
students and (b) had a job related to DJ after graduation. The instructor of the
course Data Visualization for News communicated with the students directly,
knew students’ feedback and had a clear understanding of students’ learning
performance; thus, he was also invited to offer his views on R4 and R5.

_In-depth interviews:_ A total of 10 semi-structured interviews were conducted in
May 2017. The interviews asked the following key questions: (a) Do you think
learning things in the scientific field is difficult? (b) What kind of difficulties do
you have? (c) What suggestions do you have for the school to offer DJ education,
from an interdisciplinary point of view?
All the interviews were recorded and transcribed. For the three interviewees who
were not available in Hong Kong, interviews were conducted online. All other
interviews were conducted face to face, each lasting an average of 30 minutes.

_Data analysis:_ To preserve anonymity, each interviewee received an alphanumeric
code. The first digit refers to the interviewee’s occupation (‘S’ for student, ‘A’ for
alumni, and ‘I’ for instructor). The second digit is the number to differentiate them
from others with the same occupation.
The transcribed interviews were coded and analysed by NVivo 11. By reading
and re-reading the text, coherent information was organized into categories.
A descriptive label was assigned to represent each category, and in this way major
themes appeared. Major themes were then broken down into lower-level
subthemes to sort the responses more specifically. Supporting quotes were then
collected and placed next to the themes.

_Qualitative Results_


The sample was made up of 10 interviewees. Six of them were students
(coded from S1 to S6), three of them were alumni (coded from A1 to A3) and
another one was a DJ instructor (I). The transcripts of the interviews were imported
into NVivo 11. After collecting and sorting them, themes related to RQ4 and RQ5

were generated. The number of times each theme was mentioned by interviewees
was counted to show how popular it was. Representative quotes of each theme
were selected.

_Difficulties:_ Of the three alumni and six students only one of them thought, as a
journalism major, that it was not difficult to learn DJ. The others all stated that
learning computer science and statistics was difficult, although the specific
obstacles they faced and the extent to which they struggled varied. Two of them
said it was not easy but after learning they found scientific subjects not as tough
as they had thought. The major themes, frequency, subthemes and supporting
quotes are presented in Table A1.

_A lack of prior knowledge:_ A lack of prior knowledge emerged as a major theme
related to difficulties in learning DJ. According to the instructor, ‘The biggest
difficulty is that the students have no foundation in this field and without prior
knowledge, they had nothing to connect to and understand the new subject’.
The only students who did not think computational skills were difficult were
those who had accumulated basic knowledge in this field before. The knowledge
here includes two parts: computer science knowledge and DJ knowledge. This
reflects the need for a better-designed curriculum, which should involve a series
of courses that are organized systematically, helping students to learn step by step.

_A lack of time:_ According to the J&C majors, their DJ learning is constrained by
a lack of time. They have a heavy workload of news seeking, interviews and story
writing, which are all time-consuming. Moreover, interviews must be conducted
at an interviewee’s convenience, making for a tight schedule full of uncertain
factors.
Additionally, a lot of time is needed to master the requisite technological skills.
According to the instructor:


The efforts the students made after class, the practice they had, led to differences among
students. The excellent students in my class all spent a lot of time exploring more after
class—they even gave assignments to themselves—while there were also students who
just reached the minimum requirements of the homework.


Both the primary major and new major demand a lot of time, making it hard for
students to keep up with interdisciplinary learning.

_A lack of support in practice:_ Support from both the teachers and peers is lacking
here. First, the difficulties in learning DJ make students need greater individual
attention; thus, more teachers and teaching assistants are needed. The reality is
that the number of teaching personnel is not adequate. Second, students are not
active in discussions, which inhibits them learning from each other.

collected. Interviewees offered opinions from different perspectives and the

_More hands-on practice:_ The results showed that what the learners needed most
was more hands-on practice, although the specific forms of the practice vary a lot,
which could be included in workshops, internship programmes or monthly talks.
The frequent mention of hands-on practice indicates that DJ learners are willing
to receive training on practical skills.

_More information of DJ:_ According to the learners, journalism educators should
inform them of changes in the media industry. At the same time, elaboration on
the basic concepts and issues of DJ is necessary to give them a good understanding
of the theoretical side of the subject.

_A deeper level of interdisciplinary interaction:_ In DJ practice, journalism and
technology are deeply intertwined. Correspondingly, the sense of interdisciplinarity
cannot be ignored in DJ education. Three aspects appear under this theme: more
interdisciplinary courses; more courses open to students from different majors;
and setting a new major.
The key idea here is to enhance the level of interaction between journalism and
other fields in DJ. This interaction is expected to take place not only by coordinating
knowledge but also by communication and cooperation between learners with
different majors.
The instructor suggested: ‘The ultimate solution is to set a new track, just as
Chinese journalism and financial journalism, DJ could be an independent area in
the future’. By doing so, the requirements for other aspects of journalism that are
relatively less related to DJ can be reduced; thus, students can focus more on DJ.

_More interactions with the industry:_ Interviewees hoped to get connected with the
real world to see what is going on there. As one alumna mentioned: ‘It is not easy
for students to get a clear study goal. When you become a worker, with a clear
goal to solve the problems you meet in your daily work, your study can be more
efficient’. More interactions with the industry, such as inviting practitioners to
deliver lectures or visiting newsrooms, can help learners have a concrete
understanding of DJ and make their learning more goal oriented.


**Discussion**


_Conclusions_


The findings show that science majors may have an advantage over J&C majors
when stepping into the interdisciplinary field integrating journalism and technology. They are more proactive in participating in learning activities and gain a
better outcome. The instructor being interviewed in this research, who had both
practical and teaching experience in DJ, observed that in the short term it is easier
to enter DJ via the technology path.
Difficulties for journalism majors in DJ learning may have origins in the broad
interdisciplinarity in DJ. The scope of interdisciplinarity refers to conceptual and


30 _Asia Pacific Media Educator 28(1)_


cultural distance between the participating fields (Huutoniemi, Klein, Bruun &
Hukkinen, 2010). Computer science belongs to hard science while journalism is
social science; they are different in terms of both epistemology and methodology.
Thus, obstacles do exist when one transfers from one field to another. However,
when compared to computer science, aspects of journalism, such as news coverage,
reporting and public discussions on social issues, are much closer to daily life.
Although science majors have no prior professional training in journalism, they
have already gained some concrete experience and understanding of this field,
which could be referred to and thus support their journalism learning. On the other
hand, because the application of science and technology is the result of the
execution of complex systems behind visible scenes, people may not be easily
exposed to theoretical scientific concepts and principles in daily life. Therefore,
the difficulties science majors have in picking up journalism knowledge are less
onerous than those faced by journalism majors in gaining new technical skills.
At the same time, DJ gives science majors a chance to apply technology in practice
and generate some real results. This means they can easily gain new knowledge
by making use of their existing skills. That may be why they have more sense of
enjoyment in DJ learning activities.
The findings also indicate that science majors enjoy advantages in DJ learning,
but this does not mean that journalism majors should give way to science majors.
The wide scope of interdisciplinarity in DJ also indicates that it is hard for one to
master all the skills; there are but a few people who can really be called ‘a data
journalist’—a person with a complete mastery of journalistic and technical skills
necessary for producing DJ news stories. Using data to tell news stories is a team
effort in which reporting meets programming and involves reporters, editors,
programmers, graphic artists, web designers and app developers. One opinion
(Doherty, 2012) is that for journalists, the aim of mastering programming languages
is to empower them to create stories for digital platforms with the same precision
as traditional media, so they do not need to become an expert in computer coding.


_Implications for DJ Education_


After using NVivo to conduct qualitative analysis, this research found that a lack
of prior knowledge, time and support in practice constrained interdisciplinary
learning activities. As to suggestions for DJ education, four major themes emerged,
including more hands-on practice, more information of DJ, a deeper level of
interdisciplinary interaction and more interactions with the industry. Ways to
stimulate interdisciplinary interaction include more interdisciplinary courses,
more courses open to students from different majors and the setting of an entirely
new major. These can be referred to when a DJ training programme is designed in
the future.
The quantitative part of this research showed that some science majors are
interested in journalism and have good performance in interdisciplinary learning


_Zhu and Du_ 31


activities. Taking this into account, it would be good if more science majors
enrolled in journalism courses. Science majors and journalism majors have
complementary skills in DJ production and can help each other solve problems.
Cooperation between them can enhance learning efficiency.
Students also should cooperate with each other as it is necessary to cultivate
the learning community. If several students could form a learning group to
encourage each other, they could do some projects based on what they have learnt
in class. In this way, they can practise more.
Another way to help students deal with the difficulties is to engage them in
realistic, problem-centred activities. The visible outcomes, such as building a
website, could bring students confidence and a sense of achievement. These
instant and apparent rewards can motivate learners to achieve more.
As aforementioned, the wide scope of interdisciplinarity in DJ implies that it is
hard for one to master the entire skill set required to accomplish such a job. This
calls for more efforts in setting the priorities for each separate discipline in the
integrated curriculum design. It is unnecessary to bring all the knowledge and
skills involved in the training of computer science and journalism into the training
of DJ. In fact, using data for news storytelling requires only a very basic set of
coding and programming skills. Moreover, for the interdisciplinary educational
materials to be organically absorbed by the learners, they should be organized into
a logical and systematic structure, in order to effectively guide the students
towards the integration of multiple subject areas step by step.
To avoid the polarity problem, both discipline-based and interdisciplinary
experiences should be taken as essential in the curriculum design. In terms of the
discipline-based approach, students should be equipped with the necessary
knowledge and skills of their own major, be it journalism, computer science,
visual design or others. As for interdisciplinary experiences, it can be achieved
through in-depth interdisciplinary interactions, such as practical case studies,
team projects, field trips to the industry as well as exchange visits with other
schools and departments.


_Limitations_


As DJ itself is such a new field, research on DJ education is still rare and limited.
Combining quantitative and qualitative methods, this study took an early step to
examine the state of interdisciplinary education in journalism and explore the
challenges and implications.
Two limitations in this research should be noted. First, the sample size was
rather small. As mentioned earlier, we found it very difficult to get a large number
of individuals from diversified backgrounds who at the same time have some
learning experience in common so that specific comparisons can be made among
them. Second, this research is based on two DJ initiatives and focused on the early
stage of DJ learning. The influence of the primary major in terms of the long run
was not assessed here. As the core values of journalism remain largely the same


32 _Asia Pacific Media Educator 28(1)_


even though the forms of journalism are constantly evolving, it is well possible
that journalistic training, which emphasizes skills in interviews, storytelling,
ethics, accountability and responsiveness, could be of advantage to journalism
majors in DJ works in the long run. This aspect of DJ education can be further
explored in future studies.
