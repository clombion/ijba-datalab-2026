## **BEST PRACTICES GUIDE** FOR DATA JOURNALISTS

###### AUTHOR: EDITOR:


SUMMARY


In the age of datafication, journalists are more necessary than ever to find the truth within the enormous amount of digital content available. Their task consists of filtering, verifying, analyzing and synthesizing data and presenting them with an attractive narrative that encourages audiences to engage with data and appreciate its social potential.


This Best Practices Guide offers basic recommendations for journalists who wish to use data in their stories, introducing concepts of statistical analysis, _storytelling_ and visualization.


The challenge lies in converting data into a comprehensible narrative and preventing incorrect use of data from contributing to misinforming the public.

### **INTRODUCTION**
# 01

Data journalism has become a trend in the newsrooms
of major [national](https://datos.gob.es/es/blog/la-reutilizacion-de-datos-abiertos-en-el-periodismo-una-ventana-abierta-nuevos-modelos-de) and international media outlets.
The existence of supranational entities such as the [European Data](https://www.europeandatajournalism.eu/)
[Journalism Network](https://www.europeandatajournalism.eu/) and the successive editions of [The Data Journalism](https://s3.eu-central-1.amazonaws.com/datajournalismcom/handbooks/The-Data-Journalism-Handbook-1.pdf)
[Handbook](https://s3.eu-central-1.amazonaws.com/datajournalismcom/handbooks/The-Data-Journalism-Handbook-1.pdf) corroborate the consolidation of this journalistic modality
in the current landscape.


With a growing amount of data, now more than ever it is important that the journalist understands how to adapt to the information environment generated by Big Data. In this context, the data journalist is responsible for finding the truth within the enormous amount of digital content available. In this way, in the age of datafication, journalists are more necessary than ever to filter, verify, analyze and synthesize data with a multidimensional narrative, helping readers to explore the data underlying the news and encouraging them to participate in the process of creating and evaluating their own stories.

In short, it is urgent to raise awareness of how, thanks to data, we can have a more exhaustive vision of what is happening around us. For this reason, data journalism becomes a fundamental tool for society.


_Source:_ _[The Curious Journalist's Guide to Data - Columbia Journalism Review (cjr.org)](https://www.cjr.org/tow_center_reports/the_curious_journalists_guide_to_data.php#communication)_

Information professionals are the depositaries of an enormous responsibility: thanks to their work they make data accessible to civil society, without the need for society to have specialized knowledge. They are natural users who bring data to end users and contribute to strengthening essential principles such as participation and open government.

Mirko Lorenz, Co-CEO and founder of [Datawrapper](https://www.datawrapper.de/), one of the most used online visualization tools in the information sector, defends why journalists should view data as an opportunity, not a threat.


_The use of data transforms something abstract into something that everyone can understand and_
_relate to. [The use of data] can help people make decisions, whether_
_buying a car, a house, deciding which school to choose or which job_
_to accept. [The use of data] can show fallacies and help everyone see possible_
_solutions to complex problems._ **(Mirko Lorenz)**


There is no need to fear data. This journalistic modality will not make the values of traditional journalism disappear, but it does allow us to orient the profession toward new goals in a hyper-(mis)informed society where it is necessary to offer a quality product. It is simply a willingness to accept that possibilities multiply when the traditional "nose for news" and the ability to tell a compelling story are combined with data.

This is the idea underlying the present Best Practices Guide, where some basic recommendations are offered for those journalists who wish to take the leap from reporting on the use of data to using data in their stories. Only in this way can we avoid the danger of becoming, in the words of Leila Haddou, former investigative journalist at The Financial Times, a 'statistics zombie.'


_Data journalism is different from pure statistical analysis, (...). After_
_analysis comes communication. This makes journalism different from_
_scholarship or science, or any field that produces knowledge but does not_
_feel the compulsion to tell the public about it in a comprehensible way._
_Journalism is for the audience, which is often a very broad audience,_
_potentially millions of people._ **(Jonathan Stray, professor of data journalism**
**at Columbia University)**


The fundamental premise when using this guide is to help you identify and tell stories that have not been discovered by the methods of conventional reporting. Data helps us find relevant information, follow complex stories with global implications (for example, connections between power and the business world), endow them with greater reliability and find new narratives to communicate them quickly and effectively. In short, to differentiate ourselves from other media outlets and involve the audience through innovative formulas and personalized narrative approaches.

Jack Dougherty and Ilya Ilyankou, authors of the volume _[Hands on Data Visualization](https://handsondataviz.org/)_, offer a simple protocol for successfully discovering the stories that data conceal:

Identify the problem that motivates your journalistic work and define what purpose guides you to do it: why it is of interest, how it affects citizens and how it does so in different geographic contexts over different chronological stages. Doing so helps you to focus on a specific dataset, discarding numerical noise. Journalists Raúl Sánchez and Analía Plaza, from eldiario.es, set out to discover why Spain had opted for an urban model where apartment blocks are prioritized, concentrating the population in reduced spaces. [of News Media](https://wan-ifra.org/) (WAN-IFRA) as the best data visualization in digital media in 2021.

Once the useful databases for composing the information have been located, it is time to ask ourselves who, how and why the data was collected, what the reliability of the catalog is, what interests lie behind the publication, etc. As Hassel Fallas, founder of [La Data Cuenta](https://ladatacuenta.com/), states, datasets become 'subjects' that must be interrogated to discover their history, to understand their potentiality as sources.


.


.


Prioritize access to raw data rather than those that have already been aggregated, since this will introduce constraints and biases. Raw data allow broadening the perspectives from which we view the phenomena analyzed to discover trends that might have gone unnoticed.

Also ensure that the requested data are returned in a [reusable format](https://datosabiertos.jcyl.es/web/es/catalogo-datos/formatos-datos-abiertos.html) and clean them before setting to work with them. There are many tools available. The most common among data journalists are spreadsheet programs (Microsoft Excel, Google Sheets) and [OpenRefine](https://www.juntadeandalucia.es/datosabiertos/portal/tutoriales/usar-openrefine.html), an open-source tool designed to clean and transform messy data. Thanks to them you will be able to remove spaces and non-printable characters that can cause unexpected results, round decimals, reformat dates, clean misspelled words, remove duplicate rows or columns, convert non-tabular formats, etc.

At the following links you can find more information on the main problems that can be resolved by properly cleaning data: [Google Refine for Investigative Journalists](https://dannguyen.github.io/NICAR-Google-Refine/) and the [Spanish version of Christopher Groskopf's guide for Quartz](http://es.schoolofdata.org/guia-quartz/).

Imagine what type of narrative (plots, characters, settings) and visual resources will be the most attractive for telling an interesting story. We will look at this in a section of the guide, but as a first idea we can say that the key lies in combining data and emotion to achieve an attractive narrative.

Bear in mind what your potential audience may know about the topic and frame your work within this level of knowledge to facilitate their understanding. Audiences are never uniform: people have different expectations, concerns and worries. What someone understands when they look at the data depends on what they already believe. A citizen with a job has to 'imagine' what an 'unemployed person' is like in order to make sense of the idea of an unemployment rate. That image can be positive or negative, it may be justified or false, but it is the one generated in the citizen's mind.


Always be transparent in your methods and open your data, as organizations such as [CIVIO](https://civio.es/nosotros/impacto/#datos-ineditos) open the data they have used and indicate the methodology they followed to carry out their journalistic work.

Take advantage of the publication of catalogs by public bodies to report on how the data has changed since the last version, what new rankings have been generated, what trends have been maintained and which have changed, etc. The news value is motivated by the publication of the data itself but, as they are public catalogs, the novelty cycle is very short and journalists have only 24-48 hours to publish a story with impact.

A breaking news event can prompt us to search for data to generate context that allows interpreting what has happened in a broader and more complex way. We must ask ourselves: is it a common occurrence? Has it happened before? How many times? The goal is to make the event evolve from what happened to the broader phenomenon in which it is embedded, in order to explain a current topic in a more complex way. As in the previous case, the window of opportunity is brief, requiring the story to be published in less than 24-48 hours so that it does not lose its novelty and motivate audience reaction.


.


Updating and adapting are the key actions here. The main challenge is to make the story fit well with cultural differences. ### **FROM DATA TO NARRATIVE**
# 04

_Telling stories is not something we do, it is what we are._
**(Sejal Vora, data analyst and** _**storytelling**_ **expert)**


Telling stories is a constant in the evolution of humanity. Humans have been telling stories since the Cro-Magnon era to communicate with other individuals, to record the accounts of our daily life and to transcend through time.

Our brain functions by telling stories and there is a psychological explanation for this. We receive so much information every day that we need to determine what is important to process and remember and what can be discarded. Stories help us achieve these two goals, relieving the tension experienced by our brain in a hyperinformed society.

Through _storytelling_ techniques we help data to be understood by integrating it into a narrative, giving it context, a logical structure with easily recognizable patterns for our brain. Journalist and graphic novelist Joe Sacco, author of _Footnotes in Gaza: A Tribute to the Land_, calls it the 'big picture':


_For example, many events around Palestinians are usually_
_presented as terrorist acts. If we focus only on the cases of kidnappings and_
_hostages, we are talking about facts, yes, but if we do not consider the context, the_
_precedent history, we will never understand what is happening. Context is something that is beyond_
_the facts. The facts alone don't tell you the whole story._ **(Joe Sacco)**


When storytelling is compelling, the user understands the perspective of who is narrating. What is called neural coupling occurs and, thanks to this, coherence is generated between the subjects at both ends of the communication chain, increasing empathy. If data has more meaning for our audiences, we can achieve more effective response actions, since the public will have been able to identify their concerns and interests in the narrative.

The challenge lies in converting data into a comprehensible narrative for any human mind, as journalist specializing in economic information Beth Stackpole declares. To do this, we can follow these simple rules:

Always convey one key idea. Eliminate the noise generated by the data and focus the audience's attention on what is essential. [James G. Robinson](https://open.nytimes.com/the-3-questions-i-ask-when-interviewing-job-candidates-dc51e8eb51dd?gi=a1c8e34dc1a1), director of 'Data Products' at The New York Times, talks about the type of interview he conducts with candidates who aspire to a position in the data section of the 'Gray Lady.' The veteran journalist asks aspiring candidates to tell him a story, the most surprising one they have covered during their professional life, and to recount why they chose it and how they communicated it to the audience. As Robinson declares:


_Your story must have an element of discovery. [...] We don't simply exist_
_to confirm preconceived ideas, that would be a rather boring career,_
_we exist to challenge the status quo, to reveal new paths and to bring_
_clarity amid so much noise._ **(James G. Robinson)**

A story involves a sequence of events and related actors, with a beginning and an end. We have a context, characters who must face a decision, a situation that breaks the equilibrium, causing a narrative turn (what in dramatic narrative techniques is known as an 'aha moment') and a result.

When we tell stories with data, the [inverted pyramid](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2011/07/13/the-inverted-pyramid-of-data-journalism-part-2-6-ways-of-communicating-data-journalism/) works, although it is not the only structure we can use.


_Source:_ _[6 ways of communicating data journalism (The inverted pyramid of data journalism part 2) | Online Journalism Blog](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2011/07/13/the-inverted-pyramid-of-data-journalism-part-2-6-ways-of-communicating-data-journalism/)_


Brent Dykes, author of _[Effective Data Storytelling: How to Drive Change with Data, Narrative and Visuals](https://www.effectivedatastorytelling.com/)_, opens the door to other narrative formulas such as 'Freytag's pyramid,' with an introduction, the rising action, the climax and, finally, the denouement (resolution and conclusion). We can thus try replacing the linear structure with an 'arc' structure where we highlight how something has changed in the environment, introducing a new problem or opportunity.


_Source:_ _[Data Storytelling: Separating Fiction From Facts (forbes.com)](https://www.forbes.com/sites/brentdykes/2016/07/13/data-storytelling-separating-fiction-from-facts-2/#86389963a7cd)_


For those who wish to expand narrative options and experiment with new practices, podcasting is carving out a niche in data journalism. [Tim Harford](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2020/11/24/tim-harford-on-telling-data-stories-with-audio/), author of the book _How to make the world add up_, offers some keys for transferring data narratives to the audio environment. Three are the key ideas of this recognized podcaster: 1) simplify all the figures you use (it is better to use approximations), 2) maintain the suspense of the story and dose out what is going to happen; 3) make sure the audience shares the meaning of the concepts discussed in the podcast.

A step further would involve including techniques of ["sonification"](https://onlinejournalismblog.com/2015/05/11/leveraging-music-to-help-people-understand-data/), converting data into music. You can use [MIDI](https://www.twilio.com/blog/working-with-midi-data-in-python-using-mido), if you are familiar with the Python environment, to convert your datasets into music and increase the potential impact of your stories.

In 2010, The New York Times published ["Fractions of a Second"](https://archive.nytimes.com/www.nytimes.com/interactive/2010/02/26/sports/olympics/20100226-olysymphony.html?_r=0), a piece that allowed listeners to hear the difference between the sound of a gold medal and a bronze medal in different sporting events at the Winter Olympic Games. This other proposal from [The Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/80269930-40c3-11e9-b896-fe36ec32aece) puts music to the yield curve of the United States Government from 1979 to 2019.

Another example is the project ["Egypt Building Collapses"](https://egyptbuildingcollapses.org/). In this publication, sound becomes a complement to emphasize the impact of this problem on people's lives and to raise awareness about the tragedies behind the statistics on building collapses. The soundscape is composed by representing each accident of a building that collapses. The sound sample increases as the accident rate grows.


.


.


Select a compelling headline, appealing context and a narrative that makes the audience interested in a story. As Kiko Llaneras, data journalist at El País, states: "Write headlines for the attention market and design for information to flow."

Offer simple, clear but exhaustive explanations. You can start simply and gradually increase the complexity and quantity of data. The following chart on how electoral polls work shows how layers of difficulty can be added without the story losing its explanatory power: _[Rock 'n poll (rocknpoll.graphics)](http://rocknpoll.graphics/)_

Add credibility: you must be able to present information without adding biases, recognizing what is important and what is not with the aim of maintaining the coherence of the discourse. Sometimes, the only data you have comes from an unidentified source. Always verify with another expert and do not publish biased data unless you have substantial evidence to corroborate them.

Do not forget the ethical impact of the story. Researchers Mª Ángeles Chaparro Domínguez and Jesús Díaz-Campos remind us in their article _["Data Journalism and Ethical Best Practices in the Winning Projects (DJA, OJA and Sigma Awards)"](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17512786.2021.1981773?scroll=top&needAccess=true&role=tab&aria-labelledby=full-article)_ of the care that must be applied when we collect data, the necessary protection of matters such as privacy and the transparency of the methodological process we have followed to analyze the data. Only in this way can the dissemination of distorted information based on manipulated or poorly contextualized data be avoided.

But the essential key lies in humanizing the information, in making visible who the real people are behind the figures. Here lies a large part of the correct functioning of our narrative.


### **HUMANIZING THE STORY:** **BEHIND EVERY DATA POINT** **THERE IS ALWAYS A PERSON**
# [05]

_Journalism is about people. Do not prioritize data over people. Humanize_
_your data by telling the stories of the people who have been affected by them._
**[(Kuek Ser Kuan Keng, founder of Data-N)](https://www.data-n.com/)**


Data are linked to the lives of real people. When numbers become our journalistic raw material, we must consider the world from which they come. Data communicates better when connected to the stories of individual lives it represents. In this sense, we must ask ourselves: Who is hiding behind the data?

Ana Tudela, journalist at [Datadista](https://www.datadista.com), reveals the maxim of the work they carry out at this specialized data journalism outlet created in 2016:


_[...] after the data, shoe leather. You have to go to places and you have to see what_
_the data is saying, because if you don't, you are not telling anything. [...] finding_
_the people who identify and who help you understand what the large_
_chunk of data you are covering means._ **(Ana Tudela)**


The tendency to humanize stories in data journalism is an essential aspect of narrative. Professor of the University of Oregon [Paul Slovic](https://psychology.uoregon.edu/profile/pslovic/) explains that, if our brain does not connect numbers with human qualities, we are less capable of relating information and, therefore, our capacity to use it effectively decreases. As the expert notes, people care about individuals. For example, the photograph of [Alan Kurdi](https://www.lavanguardia.com/cultura/20150905/54436260490/foto-aylan-kurdi.html), who died on a beach in Bodrum (Turkey), generated more public debate than thousands of anonymous data on [immigrant deaths](http://www.themigrantsfiles.com/) in the Mediterranean.


As the number of victims grows, our willingness to help decreases. This is what the American Psychological Association defines as 'compassion fatigue': the fading of our capacity to experience the suffering of others. Our empathy does not get along very well with numbers and we can only combat indifference by remembering that behind each number there is a human being. This is what Italian designer Federica Fragapane understood in [The Stories Behind a Line](http://www.storiesbehindaline.com/), a project about the story of six refugees who arrived in Italy seeking asylum. Or the documentary _[Man on Wire](https://www.filmaffinity.com/es/film978531.html)_ (2006), by Barry Singer, which attempts to clarify the identity of one of the people who jumped from the North Tower of the World Trade Center after the September 11 attack. The story of one single life against the 2,996 who died.

To humanize our story and ensure audiences feel the data as their own, we can apply the following strategies in our story:

Establish a strong connection with the audience(s) in the story. The story belongs to the audience, not to the journalist. An interesting example is the project _["Your mayor, under scrutiny: how does your municipality arrive at the elections?"](https://www.elconfidencial.com/espana/2023-05-14/alcalde-a-examen-como-llega-tu-municipio-elecciones_3628265/)_, from El Confidencial.

Personalize events through the eyes of those who experience them, for example by including 'you' in the headline of the information. We bring the project from the digital outlet specializing in data journalism [The Pudding](https://pudding.cool/) _["How are you doing?"](https://pudding.cool/2022/12/emotion-wheel/)_, which proposes a therapeutic journey through the world of each user's emotions.


.


.


Find an anecdote, an event that hooks the reader. Data journalist Manyun Zou does this through an internationally successful series that has been subjected to censorship in China. _["The Big Censored Theory"](https://pudding.cool/2022/08/censorship/)_ offers an analysis of the silences and omissions (about sexual content, or references to the LGBTQ+ community, for example) resulting from the government's decision to review foreign television programs before broadcasting them to the public to accommodate them to the morality of the Asian country.

Invite the audience to discover what may interest them in the story through elements of [interaction](https://lab.rtve.es/elcano/es/), [gamification](https://ig.ft.com/climate-game/), [immersive realities](https://www.insider.com/refugees-journey-how-a-fraction-of-refugees-come-to-us-2022-6), etc. The following work from National Geographic uses various analogies to help readers understand what the figure of 500,000 deaths in the United States from coronavirus means. _[Visualizing 500,000 deaths from COVID-19 in the U.S. (nationalgeographic.com)](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/science/graphics/what-500000-united-states-covid-deaths-look-like)_


Let us remember that the most important thing when telling a story with data is to empathize with the reader. The story is not the data, but what these represent for readers. It is, in short, a matter of generating emotions to achieve the loyalty of our audience.


_One of the best ways to make data more meaningful is_
_to make them yours._ **(Usman Haque, journalist at The Guardian)**


### **CHOOSING THE BEST** **VISUALIZATION**
# [06]

In a media landscape like the current one, where we must compete for the limited attention of users, we have to resort to graphic and visual means to present data briefly and concisely. This is where visualizations come into play, which help us convey dense and complex information to the audience quickly.

Combined with the _storytelling_ techniques explained in the previous section, we can ensure that the visualization captures the reader's attention and involves them in the story.

The use of narrative and visual elements activates both sides of the brain, providing an analytical and emotional experience at the same time, ultimately helping our audience to make the information they consume their own. As Paul Bradshaw indicates, it is necessary to think strategically about the visualization to ensure there is more than data when the user accesses it.


To achieve this objective, the visualization must meet three basic principles, according to the specialized website [Material Design](https://m2.material.io/design/communication/data-visualization.html#principles):

**Accuracy:** clarity and integrity are key to not distorting information.
**Usefulness:** the design must facilitate the user's exploration of the data and interaction with it.
**Adaptable** to consumption environments and to users' needs in terms of depth, complexity and data modality.

Finding the appropriate visualization method for our story is an arduous task and, on many occasions, a failed one, if we let ourselves be seduced only by the spectacularity of the design and forget the narrative keys.

Knowing the structure of the dataset and using the classic journalistic 'wh questions' is a very useful resource. We start from the following key idea: the chart must always answer a series of questions: Who? / Where? / When? / What? / How? / Why? / How many?

Taking these questions into account, [Vizualism](http://www.vizualism.nl/infographic-taxonomy/) has developed a matrix to help us choose the chart that best fits our story.


_Source:_ _[¿Cómo contar una buena historia con datos? (ladatacuenta.com)](https://ladatacuenta.com/2018/01/27/como-contar-una-buena-historia-con-datos/)_


Once the chart type is chosen, always think about what you want to communicate and what reaction or consequence you seek to generate. [DataViz](https://datavizcatalogue.com/ES/) offers a broad catalog for selecting the most suitable chart, depending on the preferences of our story.


_Source:_ _[https://datavizcatalogue.com/ES/buscar.html](https://datavizcatalogue.com/ES/buscar.html)_

Once the chart is chosen, we must take into account other recommendations for designing an effective visualization, with quality, precise and meaningful information.

Resist the temptation to make spectacular and highly decorative visualizations because it may cause the story told by the data to be lost. It is not a time to astonish the audience but to think about them in order to generate relevant insights. If this idea has not become clear to you, you can visit the website [W(hat)T(he)F(uck) Visualizations](https://viz.wtf/) where you can see some of the most outlandish and hilarious proposals.


.


Think about simplifying and pruning your visualization so that what the data are telling you is immediately clear. To make the most of the power of visualization, suppress data that are not critical for telling the story. Less is more.


_Source:_ _[https://www.reuters.com/graphics/USA-ELECTION/ECONOMY-POLL/akvezdbzrpr/](https://www.reuters.com/graphics/USA-ELECTION/ECONOMY-POLL/akvezdbzrpr/)_

If there are several connections between the data and they are not well represented with one chart, it is more advisable to separate the data into several visualizations. Complex visualizations only cause distraction. The journalist may have been working with their data for a long time, but the audience is seeing it for the first time and may not be familiar with the way of reading and assimilating these values.


_Source:_ _[https://www.metroecuador.com.ec/ec/noticias/2020/03/26/1403-casos-de-coronavirus-en-el-ecuador-cifras-de-este-26-de-marzo-1700.html](https://www.metroecuador.com.ec/ec/noticias/2020/03/26/1403-casos-de-coronavirus-en-el-ecuador-cifras-de-este-26-de-marzo-1700.html)_


Don't let design complexity cloud understanding. The role of data journalists is especially relevant for bringing complex stories closer to audiences in a simple, easy and clear way. Prioritize the clarity of the data. Only then will the time come to make them more attractive. Not the other way around. A balance between beauty and the effectiveness of visualization must be achieved to aid in its dissemination.

#### How to use text in visualizations?


_Text is perhaps the most underestimated element in any visualization of_
_data. There is a lot of text in any chart or map: titles, descriptions, notes,_
_sources, bylines, logos, annotations, labels, color keys, information about_
_tools, axis labels, but often it is the last thing done in the_
_design process._ **(Lisa Charlotte Muth, from Datawrapper)**


Text is important because it helps us explain the data in the visualization, not just mention it. To do this, we can insert 'reading instructions' in visualizations through textual elements.

The first instruction can be given through a description of the visualization in the title. Make sure it is descriptive and focuses on underlining the trend of the data or highlighting the main story. It should contain no more than 10 words.

The idea is that the title should not be a summary of everything the chart says, but a tool to prioritize the main message. Two important ideas can help us: 1) focusing on the topics that interest users rather than the official names of the data or statistical designations from data sources; 2) using terms and phrases that reflect the uses of our readers. You can orient yourself by including the words users would use in search engines. For example, it is more likely that the word 'employment' is searched rather than 'labor market.'


Ask yourself, when you are going to include a text: 'What is the simplest way to say it?' We must dedicate time to writing, even when we are designing a visualization.


You can see the following example by designer Ann K. Emery, from [Depict Data Studio:](https://depictdatastudio.com/)


_Source:_ _[https://depictdatastudio.com/how-to-tell-a-story-with-data-titles-subtitles-annotations-dark-light-contrast-and-selective-labeling/](https://depictdatastudio.com/how-to-tell-a-story-with-data-titles-subtitles-annotations-dark-light-contrast-and-selective-labeling/)_


Label your data directly rather than including legends or keys, since this allows readers to see the data and the label at the same time, without having to look at the chart, then the legend and then back to the chart to decipher it. Access to the visualization data is thus speeded up. Labels should be as close as possible to the points of interest, to prevent readers from constantly moving their eyes around the visualization.


_Source:_ _[https://www.reuters.com/graphics/UGANDA-LGBT/movakykrjva/](https://www.reuters.com/graphics/UGANDA-LGBT/movakykrjva/)_


Labels not only help readers identify them but also help recall the narrative to readers. Labels should not only contain a number, but also the category to which the value makes reference. This way we can remind the reader of the main idea guiding our visualization. You can see this in the following example: _[LeBron James Scores 5,995th Playoff Point, Breaking the Record From Michael Jordan - The New York Times (nytimes.com)](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2017/05/25/sports/basketball/lebron-career-playoff-points-record.html)_

Along with labels, we can also use annotations to include tips and explanations, thanks to which readers get more out of the visualization. In these, outliers, historical milestones, etc. are highlighted.


_Source:_ _[https://www.reuters.com/graphics/PAKISTAN-WEATHER/FLOODS/zgvomodervd/](https://www.reuters.com/graphics/PAKISTAN-WEATHER/FLOODS/zgvomodervd/)_


To avoid information overload, it is convenient to prepare users for intensive visualizations by including one or two introductory paragraphs before launching into the visualization. As a summary, we can follow the following recommendations from the [UK Office for National Statistics](https://www.ons.gov.uk/) for writing it:

Use simple language with terms and phrases that users will probably use, opting for colloquial writing.

**-** Do not exceed 160 characters, including spaces.

**-** Put the most important information at the beginning. We first offer the main statement, then the precise explanation of the data.

**-** Include the space and time period covered by the data.

**-** Do not include abbreviations.


_Source:_ _[https://depictdatastudio.com/how-to-tell-a-story-with-data-titles-subtitles-annotations-dark-light-contrast-and-selective-labeling/](https://depictdatastudio.com/how-to-tell-a-story-with-data-titles-subtitles-annotations-dark-light-contrast-and-selective-labeling/)_


The last 'instruction' we must consider is the source of our visualizations. It should be included as follows:

**[PUBLICATION OR DATA SOURCE] from the [ORGANIZATION], [YEAR]**

We have indicated some keys on how to compose the text of our visualizations at the content level. It is now time to look at the formal aspect. Typography goes beyond choosing a pretty font. Fonts that have been designed to ensure easy reading should always be used. Highly readable fonts require less effort to interpret, which ensures more agile consumption for the reader. Guaranteeing readability is paramount; never sacrifice functionality for aesthetics. Therefore, avoid handwriting-style typography in a visualization.

Each typeface directly influences readers, through the associations it generates. A chart that uses 'Comic Sans' typography will probably be perceived as a playful design,


whereas one using 'Times New Roman' would be considered more serious and elegant.

Fonts in data visualizations must match the intended theme and tone of the image so that readers understand the information. The following image from the visualization _[Why do cats and dogs…?](https://whydocatsanddogs.com/)_ by Nadieh Bremer opts for informal typography, to address an everyday and intimate topic such as relationships between people and their pets.


_Source:_ _[https://whydocatsanddogs.com/dogs](https://whydocatsanddogs.com/dogs)_


To achieve visual coherence, we must always maintain the same typeface and font size in the chart and in the accompanying text. Including many font sizes can cause disorder.


If you are using abstract shapes and there is no room for text, it is most advisable not to use text and to activate labels when we hover the cursor over these shapes that have remained 'silent.' In the following work, Reuters' graphics section compiled all Eurovision songs available on Spotify to create an audio file for each country. These files were analyzed using the six metrics that Spotify assigns to each song (acoustics, valence, tempo, cadence, rhythm, volume), associating them with a petal of a specific color. When the cursor is moved over each flower, the artist's name, the year they participated in the festival and the song are activated.


_Source:_ _[https://www.reuters.com/graphics/MUSIC-EUROVISION/dwpkdykkzvm/](https://www.reuters.com/graphics/MUSIC-EUROVISION/dwpkdykkzvm/)_


Lastly, bear in mind that left-aligned text looks more orderly than center-aligned text. Readers will need a fraction of a second more to find the beginning of the next line than when they read left-aligned text.

#### How to use color in visualizations?

Color is a fundamental tool in visualization design. We can use it to guide the reader's gaze and draw attention to some specific aspect of the data we present. Color helps us tell a story and, in that sense, we must always use it consciously as something that serves to reinforce the message of our data.

In color, the 'less is more' maxim also prevails. We must avoid the excessive proliferation of shades in our visualizations. The more colors in a chart, the harder it will be to read. Readers will often need to consult the color key to understand what each one means. Therefore, it is better not to use more than seven colors in the same chart. Moderation is also a basic rule here.

We must also make sure readers understand what each color means. Making this task difficult with too many colors/meanings is not advisable. We can simplify this problem by using the same color for the same variables. With this we will achieve expository clarity and explainability.

Color can be used to differentiate data in the chart in several ways, as we will see in this section:


Identify areas
Distinguish Represent Highlight specific Express
of priority attention
categories quantity data meaning


When considering the use of color in visualizations, we must observe the relationships present in the data. There are two options: for continuous data, color gradients are used and for categorical data (marital status, political affiliation), distinct colors are used.

In the first case, the greatest difficulty will arise when we try to ensure

that the color differences between the data are sufficiently perceptible to the reader. When a problem arises from the similarity of shades in a visualization, we can choose to incorporate different gradients of luminosity. It is much more effective, however, to include dots, stripes and other shapes. This facilitates understanding.


When working with categorical data, it is important to differentiate the colors used to highlight the focal point for the reader. Normally, design applications ease the difficulties of this task, if we are not specialists, as they offer combined palettes with an optimal result.

Other ideas that may prove useful when we work with color in visualizations are the following.


Gray is our great ally. The use of this shade for the less prominent elements of the chart makes the most important data stand out more effectively. This color is also useful for contextual data, minor annotations, and even serves to relax the user if they are facing a chart with some level of complexity.


Source: [https://blog.datawrapper.de/colorguide/](https://blog.datawrapper.de/colorguide/)


Readers associate light colors with low values and dark colors with high values. Our brain identifies this intuitively.


Use culturally relevant colors for the target audience. For example, if we want to create a chart on environmental topics, we can use a predominantly green color scheme.


_Source:_ _[https://www.visualcinnamon.com/portfolio/swissgrid-gencoop/](https://www.visualcinnamon.com/portfolio/swissgrid-gencoop/)_


On the other hand, color contrasts can be used to focus attention on differences or areas of interest within the data. If two conditions are in disagreement with each other, for example an area with severe drought versus another zone suffering heavy floods, a color contrast will help the reader understand the opposing relationship.


_Source:_ _[https://www.miteco.gob.es/es/agua/temas/observatorio-nacional-de-la-sequia/informes-mapas-seguimiento/](https://www.miteco.gob.es/es/agua/temas/observatorio-nacional-de-la-sequia/informes-mapas-seguimiento/)_


You can set an emotional tone using warm or cool colors. Saturated texts (for example, black or red) signal importance and stand out, while light texts (for example, gray or washed out / transparent red) are neutral. Neutral texts are used for additional clarifications or to indicate that the focus of attention is on something else.
