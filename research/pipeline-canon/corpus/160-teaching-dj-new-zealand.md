**Abstract**
This case study details how a postgraduate journalism programme in New
Zealand uses a short, focused module to introduce students to the basic concepts
and practices of data journalism. The students consider and reflect upon the
examples of published data journalism and work through an example of data
visualization. While some areas for improvement are suggested, a survey of the
students reveals they are generally satisfied with the module.


**Keywords**
Data journalism, student survey, statistics


**Introduction**


Encouraging often maths-averse journalism students to study data journalism
is not easy, especially when the topic must be squeezed into an already packed
curriculum. This case study looks at the teaching of data journalism on the postgraduate journalism programme taught at Massey University in Wellington, New
Zealand. The data journalism module was introduced in 2016, and this case study
reports the author’s experience teaching the module over the past two years.
The data journalism module is three hours long. Although it would be desirable
to have more time to teach data journalism, the one-year postgraduate programme
contains many other important topics, including newsgathering, news writing,
media law, multimedia journalism and investigative reporting. The time constraint
means the data journalism module can only be an introduction to the topic.
Data journalism is taught within the programme’s larger business and statistics
module, which lasts 18 hours. I, therefore, teach the students data journalism after

**Teaching Data Journalism**


_Introducing the Concept_


I begin the module by asking the students what they believe data journalism to be.
Often, they do not know. They suggest it might be investigative journalism or
long-form journalism. It is remarkable that so few of the students conceive data to
be sets of numbers, especially given the data journalism module is taught within
a larger statistics module. It is likely this reflects the fact that many of the students
have little affinity with mathematics, a point I return to ahead.
After some prodding from me, the students eventually start to suggest that data
journalism involves numbers. The students often have little, if any, experience of
data journalism and rarely suggest that data journalism articles might exist online
and include interactive elements.
To help the students grasp what data journalism is, I ask them to visit an
excellent New Zealand data journalism site: insights.nzherald.co.nz. This is run
by Auckland-based news organization _The New Zealand Herald_ . I use a New
Zealand site because it has direct applicability to the students, covering topics
they identify with and showing them that New Zealand journalists do data
journalism. Typically, this piques the students’ interest and they read some of the
site’s articles and interact with the data. At the time of writing, the site included
articles and interactive datasets on such topics as New Zealand house prices, the
government’s expenditure on consultants and the batting statistics of the captain
of the New Zealand cricket team.
By now, the students are forming a fairly clear idea about what data journalism
is. I then ask them to suggest some possible data journalism topics, in particular,
thinking of how topics they have written articles about themselves could be
developed as pieces of data journalism. I also ask them to suggest sources of data
for such articles. This fires the students’ imaginations, generating a fertile, lively
discussion.


_Finding Stories in the Data_


I now give the students a large table of figures, in the form of an Excel sheet
downloaded from the website of the government’s statistical agency, Statistics

New Zealand. The table lists about 30 countries, detailing for each the net number
of immigrants who have come to New Zealand over the past year.
I ask the students to suggest stories that might arise from the data. At first, the
students struggle with this and often do not suggest ideas directly related to the
data. Instead, they suggest more conventional types of journalism, like writing a
series of profiles of recent immigrants or researching an investigative piece on
New Zealand’s immigration laws.
I encourage the students to think directly about the data: what stories can be
found there? As some ideas emerge, we eventually settle on identifying which
countries account for the highest net immigration into New Zealand. This allows
me to introduce the notions of cleaning up and manipulating data.
I ask the students whether we need to do anything to make the data more
manageable. I find the students reluctant to do anything—they are somewhat
intimidated by the data’s status as official statistics. Gradually, however, I steer
the conversation around to the need to rationalize some of the data to make it
easier to use (for instance, removing subtotals and summing the data for China
and Hong Kong). I then clean up the data in Excel and sort it from highest to
lowest, explaining to the students what I am doing. This identifies the top five
countries by net immigration into New Zealand.


_Data Visualization_


I then ask the students how we can visually depict the data. Suggestions typically
include producing a simple table or bar chart. I remind the students that, as we saw
on the _Herald_ ’s site, data journalism usually has visually engaging and interactive
graphics. Eventually a student will suggest the data be shown as an interactive
world map.
I now introduce the students to Datawrapper (www.datawrapper.de), a free
online software tool that can produce visual displays of data with simple
interactivity. I take the students through the process of inputting the data into a
world map, using a Datawrapper template. The result is a map that allows users to
hover over each country, producing a pop-up box detailing its population’s net
immigration into New Zealand.
Drawing on an earlier discussion on graphing data in the business and statistics
module, I remind the students of the need for good statistical practice here. The
world map’s title should clearly say what the chart is displaying and should state
the source of the data.


_Students Demonstrate Their Understanding_


To ensure the students understand how to undertake the data visualization, each
student creates his/her own world map of the data. I inspect each map to make

**Students’ Views**


A total of 30 students have been through the data journalism module: 14 in 2016
and 16 in 2017. In February 2018, using SurveyMonkey I conducted an anonymous
online survey of the students. Nine students completed the survey, a response rate
of 30 per cent. There was a good spread of responses across the two classes, with
a third from 2016 and the remainder from 2017.
The students were asked what they found most and least useful about the
module, how it could be improved, whether they have subsequently used data
journalism in their own journalism jobs, whether the module had encouraged
them to do this and their overall level of satisfaction with the module.
The respondents said the most useful aspects of the module were learning how
to produce interactive graphs and how to find stories from data. Among the
comments:
‘Creating easy-to-read graphs’.
‘Datawrapper was an interesting tool’.
‘Looking at the data to find the story’.
‘It helped give a perspective on a world which can often seem quite boring or
a one-way route’.
As to what the respondents found least useful about the module, the main
theme was time. Some said the module was too fast and intense, or the topic did
not warrant time spent on it at all:
‘Not doing enough of it to have remembered it all’.
‘It was quite heavy, so got boring/tiring quite fast and then suddenly it was
over’.
‘I think the time could have been better spent on other topics’.
But three of the respondents said all the material in the module was of value:
‘It seems everything we covered is pretty vital for a journalist to understand’.
‘It was all useful’.
Looking at how the module could be improved, the main theme was that more
time should be devoted to it:
‘A bit of time spent finding information in different archives’.
‘More time to unpack and grapple with this highly technical module’.

**Observations**


_Students’ Aversion to Maths_


I have taught business and statistics to journalism students for the past 15 years.
Throughout that time, I have frequently encountered journalism students’ aversion
to mathematics. At the start of one class early on, a student complained: ‘But we
became journalists because we hate numbers’.
Indeed, attendance was so poor at my early classes that after a few years I felt
obliged to introduce a compulsory test to encourage the students to attend. The
strategy worked, with most students now turning up. As well as this stick, I have
recently been able to introduce a carrot—there is now a NZ$750 business
journalism prize for the best business journalism student on the course. This prize,
awarded to the student who tops the test, is funded by the professional association
of insurance companies in New Zealand. For this reason, part of the business and
statistics test requires the students to write a story on insurance.
It is gratifying that the survey revealed the students were generally satisfied
with the module. While only a small proportion of the respondents actually use
data journalism in their jobs, that is to be expected. It is, after all, an emerging
field of journalism and one that only mathematically confident (or, at least,
adventurous) journalists are likely to undertake.

_Teacher Needs Technical Skills_


To teach journalism students data journalism (and statistics generally), the lecturer
must have a strong relevant background. I have a PhD in economics and before
coming to Massey worked for many years as a financial journalist. Of course,
I had to learn new skills to design and teach the data journalism module. Partly,
I taught myself, including using the very helpful tutorials on the Datawrapper
website.
I also learn from my colleagues. In particular, my data journalism module is
modelled on that designed by Allan Lee, who taught data journalism to journalism
students at Auckland’s AUT University in 2015 and 2016. AUT’s course was not
run in 2017 due to low student enrolments. Lee said this was likely due to
journalism students’ aversion to maths and the fact the course is competing against
two, highly popular, courses: magazine journalism and photojournalism. As Lee
will probably be on leave later in 2018, his data journalism course is unlikely to
run this year (A. Lee, personal communication, 7 February 2018).
I do not use specialist data journalists as guest speakers or lecturers. Partly, this
is simply due to practical constraints. There is not enough time to have a guest
speaker come in. Besides, in my view, the best data journalism is conducted by
journalists in Auckland, not Wellington, and we cannot afford to fly them to
Wellington for the classes. More broadly, when I have heard data journalism
experts speak at conferences, I often find they can be disparaging of their less
numerate colleagues. The students would find this disheartening.


**The Future**


The data journalism module could be developed further. I would like to take the
students through more of the functionality of Excel, so they become more
conversant in the basic tasks of cleaning up and manipulating data. I would also
like to require the students to each produce their own piece of data journalism, in
the form of an article and an interactive chart, which would be graded. But the
constraint is time—it is difficult to see what could be removed from the course to
make space for more data journalism.


**Conclusions**


While more can always be done, the main objectives of the module are being
achieved. The students, many of whom are mathematical novices, are introduced
