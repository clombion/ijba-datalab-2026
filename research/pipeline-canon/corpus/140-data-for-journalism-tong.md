**Understanding data**

_**Data as material**_


Traditionally, data is understood as a type of material. It may assum
various material forms, which are not limited to numbers but ca
include “text, symbols, images, sound, electromagnetic waves, or eve
a blankness or silence (an empty space is itself data)” (Kitchin, 2014: 4
Data can be collected, produced, reproduced, stored, moved, used
proliferated, changed, analysed and mapped to fulfil human need
to find new knowledge and to inform policy-making, judgements an
decisions in a broad spectrum of domains ranging from politics t
medicine. The material side of data also means data can be sold an
bought as a key resource to create profits. As “material artefacts” an
“a tool for communication”, data facilitates “intellectual and mate
rial exchanges” between individuals, social groups, businesses, cu
tures and governments (Leonelli, 2015: 810–811). Some scholars (se
for example, Freeman et al., 2018) draw an analogy between data a
material and other key materials such as electricity and steam engine
in the industrial revolution. Data as material may have distinctive fea
tures in different disciplines. For example, medical big data’s majo
features are different to those of big data in other domains in terms o
data access, production and interpretation (Lee & Yoon, 2017). Med
cal big data is usually hard to access and there has been a hesitancy t
share data and practise open data due to concerns over the potentia
of data misusing. It is often relatively simple, small structured data
which may be collected from “a non-reproducible situation” and su
fer errors and missing values. Patients’ discomfort may be involve
in data collection. Medical data analysis and interpretation requir
domain knowledge and consider patient characteristics.
Being material, data often exists collectively and grows in separat
silos, which means it is usually created separately to serve certain pu
poses. Data structure and storage may vary from case to case. Dat
may exist in different formats such as unstructured, structured an
semi-structured. Examples of unstructured data are a document i
pdf format and a print copy of _The Guardian_ . Structured data is struc
tured and stored as datasets or databases. Examples of semi-structure

extracted and converted into structured data by using techniques an
tools such as Optical Character Recognition (OCR) and data extractio
and mining skills. Therefore data is often – but not always – a set of dat
structured in a particular way and stored in a dataset or database. See
ing data as “an art material”, Freeman and his collaborators conten


hat “material (data) is examined from a number of ­perspectives –

elivery method, how it emerged, format of existence, which system it
epresents, the source or origin, the license” (2018: 76), while the paramters of datasets may concern accuracy, utility, provenance, context,
elevancy, accessibility, format dimensionality and size (78).
Associated with the materiality of data is also the appreciation of the

ecently, more research has been conducted in relation to data power

## **Conclusion**
### The four roles of data journalists in the circulation of data

The discussions in the previous chapters suggest that data journalis
play four roles in the circulation of data in the context of tightening th
control over data and increasingly concerning privacy and nation
security, and under the influence of the problems of data. They ar
(1) “data intermediaries” between data holders and audience
(2) “translators” of data for audiences, (3) “endorsers” for data an
(4) data holders and publishers themselves. Telling stories in and from
data is a process of bringing data to audiences, bridging data holder
and producers with audiences and translating the data for audience
Meanwhile, verifying the reliability of the data that data journalis
use for reporting is a way of endorsing the data’s credibility. The dat
generated in the reporting process may be circulated publicly if th
news media and journalists choose to publish such data. These diffe
ent ways demonstrate the importance of data reporting to the circu
lation of data.
Only data transparency – which means making both data and meta
data (data that explains the data) publicly accessible – can be achieved
data can be regularly and appropriately used. The governments’ ope
data initiatives originate from the idea of increasing the transparenc
of governance so that the public can have a better understanding o
policy-making and governmental activities. However, open data doe
not necessarily mean open governance or media freedom. Journalist
access to data encounters friction. The passage of the FOIA does no
guarantee the transparency of data. In addition to reinforcing an
institutionalising government surveillance in everyday practices, larg
amounts of (public records) data are concealed and never made publ
in the name of national security and privacy. With data leaks, such a
the Snowden Leak, posing challenges to state secrecy, government
politicians, and officials put shields up and would not easily give dat
away. Being in the private sector, commercial data holders are no


gally obliged to release data under FOIA laws. As data is material
nd has the potential to create economic values, they may not be willng to provide data for free but instead hold on to data to meet their
ommercial interests. Despite the open data movement, free access to
ata cannot be taken for granted. The work of data journalists thus
ontributes to pushing data holders to open up data. Their efforts in
ling FOIA requests, for instance, help increase government and commercial transparency as well as improve the well-being of democracy.
Three other related factors – the general public may not have the
me, skills or initiatives to explore data, data may be messy and conain errors, and properly interpreting data requires contextualised
nowledge – also make these roles necessary. Digging stories from
ata contributes to turning data into knowledge and generating the
meanings of data. Checking and cleaning data and talking to those
ho are close to data definitely can help to correct errors if there are
ny and contribute to contextualising and interpreting the data. In

 - doing, the credibility of news organisations and data journalists
lso contributes to boosting or endorsing the credibility of data. Data
ournalists’ endorsement role is stronger, particularly when it comes to
aked data or hacked data.
The practice of data journalism can also facilitate the circulation of
ata through using, creating, publishing and sharing data. The data
hat data journalists publish may be based on open (public records)
ata or leaked data. It may also be a product of data journalists’ own
ata collection through using multiple journalistic methods, such as
OIA requests, interviews, surveys, monitoring media coverage and
ollecting digital trace data, such as social media interactions. Data
ournalism practices get data into media coverage and make it become
media reality. This action is itself helping the publicity of data and
xtending its life. Data stories may also become new data to get into
ata circulation if they are collected by other data journalists to create
ew datasets.
