**Abstract**
Worldwide, journalism students—under the supervision of lecturers, and
backed by the resources of universities—have begun to distinguish themselves
as important creators of public interest reporting. The industry shift towards
collaborative, multi-directional news-gathering processes places these students,
as networked journalists, in a favoured position within emerging information
spaces. However, despite widespread interest within the industry and academe
in the roles non-traditional participants can play in creating media content, literature outlining the frameworks and processes for networked journalism initiatives is scarce. Using a data-driven political investigation by Bond University
students as a case study, this article contributes to the creation of such frameworks. The ‘Order in the House’ project (published by Crikey in June 2013 _)_
mined publicly available Australian parliamentary records to assess 150 politicians’
engagement and efficiency in the lead-up to a federal election. This article outlines
the scope and limitations of the project, and the research design, teaching
techniques and technology used to facilitate data collection and presentation.

Politics, data journalism, investigative journalism, networked journalism, authentic

Beyond the impacts of these changes on the nature of media education, there has

As the nature and definition of journalism has been challenged by ‘nonmarket

multi-directional work places journalism students in a favoured position within
this information space. Bruns (2008, p. 275) characterized ‘collaborative, iterative

networked journalists. There are indisputably also opportunities for students,
as citizen journalists, to have their voices heard within the digital space; however,
if citizen journalism is to be defined as a ‘nonmarket’ actor performing the same
tasks as a journalist (Beckett & Mansell, 2008), students must still compete with
journalists and their training, resources and experience in this arena. Networked
journalists, in contrast, collaborate (often with professional journalists) to diffuse
the information collection, production, analysis and distribution process (van der
Haak, Parks & Castells, 2012)—the network, more than the sum of its parts, can
generate greater scope, harness collective knowledge and resources and consider
a diverse range of angles and viewpoints in investigations (Heinrich, 2012). If the
current trend is towards such collaborations, increasingly in the field of data
journalism (Gray, Bounegru & Chambers, 2012), the training, support, resources
and mentorship provided by universities can position students as powerful nodes
within emerging digital networks.

investigative reporting projects’ (McChesney & Pickard, 2011, p. 75) and more
broadly the Investigative Reporters and Editors (IRE) model of aligning investigative journalism networks with a university is now common in Sweden, Western
Europe, Eastern Europe and Latin America (Houston, 2010). Bacon (2011, p. 46)
describes the collective journalism student body and their educators as ‘a large
editorial resource which can partly be deployed in producing journalism in
the public interest’. As Birnbauer suggests (2011, p. 42), student public interest
journalism is successful, not just because of the economic benefits for resourceconstrained media organizations but also because university journalism (under
the supervision of academic staff and industry representatives) operates outside
of the limitations of industry: ‘[it] has no need to attract advertisers and is free of
some of the other constraints—deadlines, the ‘objectivity’ goal and a blurring
of the editor/publisher roles—that inhibit the mainstream media, nor is it required
to follow traditional reporting templates’.
However, as Flew and Wilson asserted (2010, p. 132), despite widespread
interest in networked and citizen journalism within the industry and academe—

**The ‘Order in the House’ Project: A Case Study**


The ‘Order in the House’ project was a data-driven political investigation
published by independent Australian online news outlet Crikey in June 2013.
Over a 12-week semester, the cohort of 24 journalism students (many of them
first-year undergraduates) mined publicly available parliamentary records to
assess politicians’ engagement and efficiency in the lead-up to a federal election.
The class collected information about the actions of 150 members of parliament
(MPs) on approximately 150 sitting days (a full parliamentary term), including
more than 3,000 questions and 10,000 speeches. Through data analysis, students
identified the most and least active MPs, and raised broader questions about the
efficiency and fairness of aspects of the parliamentary system. The students’
completed stories, which included contextual comment from federal MPs,
academics and industry experts, were published alongside an interactive search
function and students created infographics to succinctly convey key findings.
In working with a publisher, students were given an experience that closely
aligned with the processes and expectations of the workforce, albeit within the more
supported framework of the classroom, and under the mentorship of the lecturer.
The student, industry and audience response to the investigation was positive;
however, the project presented a number of core challenges, some of which were
overcome by harnessing technological opportunities within the research process.


**Pedagogical Advantages and Practical Limitations**


As examples of authentic assessment, practical reporting challenges that mimic
industry conditions and offer students publication opportunities have many
benefits. Collaborations with media outlets offer authentic learning experiences
that encourage students to play a more active role in learning (Fook & Sidhu,
2010), increase intrinsic motivation through task utility (Cumming & Maxwell,
1999), cater to multiple learning styles (Wright, 2000), embed theory and context
within a practical framework (Wilkins, 1998) and increase graduate employability.
In the journalism curriculum, such tasks also encourage more accurate and ethical
reporting and facilitate interviews with high-calibre sources whose involvement
is dependent on the publication of the work.
Clark (2013) asserts that best-practice teaching in the field of journalism
requires students’ comfort zones to be stretched. However, designing authentic
assessments for inexperienced students can be challenging (Steel, Carmichael,
Holmes, Kinse & Sanders, 2007) and care must be taken not to stretch the comfort
zone to its breaking point.

In developing the frameworks for the ‘Order in the House’ project in 2013, the
experience of a previous political and environmental student journalism project
was considered. The research framework for the previous project had been
an interview-driven one and, reflecting on the strengths and weaknesses of that
project (Graham, 2012), many students self-identified interviewing as their
key challenge. Other scholars (Price, 2013; Steel et al., 2007) have written about
the fear and anxiety students feel in securing and conducting interviews, and
reflexive feedback from the past students indicated their lack of confidence in
interviewing, coupled with low levels of environmental and political knowledge,
which meant they missed key story angles or opportunities.
The 2013 project methodology was thus developed around a data-driven
research framework, which could transform the information gathering process
for students so there would be: ‘Less guessing, less looking for quotes; instead,
a journalist can build a strong position supported by the data’ (Gray et al., 2012,
p. 4). The first half of the semester would be dedicated to data collection; students
would spend the remaining six weeks finding story angles, contextualizing the data
findings with research and interviews and compiling their reports and visualizations for publication. In delaying the interviews until the latter part of the semester,
it was hoped that students would be more knowledgeable and confident in
approaching sources. Moreover, in structuring the research so that the story angles
were generated by the data (as primary research), students could be assured strong
news leads independent of their interviewing skills, while still being exposed
to opportunities to speak with high-calibre sources. There were also obvious
educational benefits to a data-driven research project; participation would expose
students to advances in investigative research methodology (data journalism) and
presentation (infographics, visualizations and interactive web applications).


**Project Methodology**


The ‘Order in the House’ investigation was conducted by 23 undergraduate
students and one postgraduate student as the major assessment item in the
12-week ‘Journalism Research Methods’ subject. Students ranged from first-year
undergraduates to final-year students with paid and unpaid industry work
experience. Tutorial classes were conducted in groups of 12 or fewer in a
computer laboratory, which facilitated some of the data-collection process.
Student feedback on the investigation and the course was collected via student
evaluations of teaching and reflective essays. In explaining the project methodology (from concept to presentation), this article will also evaluate it in terms of
both the journalistic output/impact and students’ educational experience.


_Stage 1: Planning the Project_


Networked journalism projects present specific challenges to information collection
and verification, and ensuring accuracy across a broad range of (often untrained)


_Graham_ 251


researchers is problematic. As van der Haak et al. (2012) suggest, although information-gathering and fact-checking tasks can be distributed across a network of
contributors, practices of sense-making invariably require a single analytical voice.
The ‘Order in the House’ project needed a focused, newsworthy direction, yet it
was also important for the students to have a sense of ownership of the project, a
means of contributing their ideas and exposure to the decision-making processes
behind the project design.
Before the semester began, Crikey was approached to secure its interest in
publishing a political accountability investigation that would attempt to quantify
the level of political involvement of Australia’s politicians. Crikey, a national
independent online publication, had a strong commitment to political news
and had previously collaborated with a number of universities on investigative
projects, including once with Bond University journalism students. The work
would be published in the lead-up to a federal election and the nature of the
data (collected rather than open source) guaranteed its exclusivity. It was then
necessary, in collaboration with Crikey’s editorial team, to establish the scope
of the investigation: It would be limited to Australia’s House of Representatives
(150 federal MPs) and political involvement would be quantified across three
fields: committee work, speeches and questions (both questions without notice
and questions in writing).
This ‘brief’ was then taken to the student cohort in a class run as a news conference. Students were encouraged to share their views on politics and political
reporting, and to imagine what a data-driven approach might offer. They brainstormed ideas for the project and questions they had about the federal political
process in Australia. These questions framed the research design, collection
and analysis, and the process of asking them gave the students a greater sense of
ownership of the project and an understanding of its direction and implications.


_Stage 2: Data Collection and Analysis_


Almost all data were collected from the Hansard, Australia’s official online record
of parliamentary debates. Information was also taken from MPs’ biographies on
the same website. From a research perspective, this ensured the information’s
credibility and consistency; from a teaching perspective, it also meant students
became adept at searching the complicated site over the course of the semester.
When discussed with students, the project was couched in journalistic terms.
However, the nature of the data collection was informed by traditional research
methodologies—in this case, content analysis. As part of the research design, it
was important to ensure the validity and reliability of the results. As coders,
students were provided with a clear coding manual and coding response sheets,
and reliability checks (10 per cent of coding cross-coded by another person) to
ensure accuracy were conducted (Weerakkody, 2009).
Each student was assigned six MPs to investigate. Where possible, students
were allowed to choose their local MP or another politician of interest as part
of their quota. In addition, students were also assigned a seventh MP, selected

subject grade), and students had access to additional support and assistance

Before coding began, students were made aware that their results would be

When students were coding speeches and questions, each line in Excel represented one speech or question, which eliminated the risk of fabricated data.
This also made analysis of the data in excel possible. While the time it took
to code each section varied somewhat depending on the MP’s level of political
activity, most students had an even spread of active and inactive politicians so
the workload was reasonably fairly distributed. Five hours of class time was
devoted to the coding process, and each student contributed 3–5 hours outside of
class. Over 4 weeks, the students analyzed 3,000 questions and more than 10,000
speeches, all occurring across approximately 150 parliamentary sitting days—a
task that would not have been possible were it not shared over the network of
willing, competent contributors. The data that were submitted required some
cleaning in terms of format and consistency of presentation; however, there were
very few errors found in the coding during this clean-up process and only one
minor error found and corrected after publication.
Although many students expressed concern about working with data, the coding was not difficult. However, it was monotonous, and one of the key challenges
was trying to link it to the outcomes and implications throughout the process.
Many students identified the tedium of the data collection within their reflective
essays, although they qualified this by acknowledging the utility of the exercise
and findings. One said that: ‘although coding the data was an important steppingstone in writing the article, I found the process stressful and repetitive. I was
however very interested in the results and patterns the data revealed.’ Another
said: ‘Despite the work being time consuming and challenging…knowing the
end result would be the basis of our stories was a rewarding and motivating
factor.’ Continually pointing to the potential findings and placing the coding in
the context of the overall investigative project was very important in maintaining
enthusiasm for the project during the collection phase.
While coding, students were encouraged to develop and share potential story
angles. Where possible, coding questions could then be integrated to collect information for particular stories. Once the coding was complete, the data were analysed by a research assistant who was proficient in statistics. The findings were
presented to students in the form of facts, lists, correlations and tables, including
most and least active politicians; the average numbers of speeches and questions
(by category); average length of committee service (against time served in parliament); the relationships between gender, age, experience, social media and
performance; performance of backbenchers relative to those with a ministerial
portfolio; party differences in performance; the extent to which country MPs are
represented in parliament; the performance difference between marginal MPs and
those in ‘safe seats’; and the breakdown of speeches and questions by topic. From
these areas, the process of creating stories from the data began.

_Stage 3: Finding News Angles and Adding Context_


Students nominated an angle or area they were interested in investigating from
the data and were allowed to work in pairs or individually on their stories. Those
who chose to work alone were still allocated a ‘hub’ of students researching in a
similar area so that they would have some support and feedback from other students and could share some research and resources. There were more than enough
strong news angles to go around, and giving students the opportunity to work in
an area of interest helped develop enthusiasm and confidence. For example, one
student said:


To be honest, when I first heard that this assessment piece would be based around
politics, I wasn’t sure that I would be able to find a specific area of interest to research….
To my surprise, the data research did allow me to develop a story that intrigued me:
gender performance in parliament.


Once students had developed story ideas, they created research plans and began
soliciting and conducting interviews. Having a publication outcome attached
to the project was a substantial advantage in this area; politicians, academics,
industry experts and other high-calibre sources who might not have agreed to be
interviewed for an unpublished assessment all spoke to students. The structure of
the research process (conducting the data collection first) also seemed to have
increased the knowledge base of the cohort who, by their own admission, initially
had limited political knowledge. Working with the data, even at a micro level, had
provided a framework for a broader understanding of the two houses of parliament, the importance of committees, the major political parties, key political
players, the process of lawmaking and the key political issues discussed over the
course of a full electoral term. The timing of the interviews (at the tail end of
the research process) framed them as context or comment seeking (rather than
idea or angle seeking) and meant that students approached their sources armed
with a lead and the key pieces of information already in place.


_Stage 4: Writing and Visualizing the Stories_


For most students, the process of writing the story was the simplest part of
the project. All students or pairs had several interviews and a large quantity
of exclusive data to draw on, and in some cases, the research assistant had
been able to drill deeper into the collected data to answer questions raised
during research or interviews. As part of their completed package for Crikey, students were also required to create an infographic to visualize their findings.
As time within the course was limited and students’ graphic design skills
were varied, the class made use of free, web-based software. Students initially
sketched out their ideas on article, then recreated them electronically using
‘Tableau’, ‘Piktochart’, ‘Infogr.am’ and ‘Easel.ly’, which all provided intuitive
drag-and-drop options, attractive templates and easy embedding within Crikey’s
web platform (see Figures 2 and 3).

**Benefits, Challenges and Conclusions**


Using a data-driven research framework was a successful model for journalism
students within this case study in large part because it changed the nature of
the interviewing process and capitalized on students’ skills, strengths and
opportunities. It guaranteed a string of strong news angles, and integrating it into
an authentic learning experience with an attached publication outcome inspired
students to produce industry-standard reporting. The project culminated in the
publication of a series of 10 student-authored articles and five infographics, which
launched as the lead story and editorial on Crikey on 3 June 2013. As evidence
that the work the students produced was industry standard, their findings were
also reported on by other national media in Australia and the collaboration won
the 2013 Sally A. White prize for student Investigative Reporting.
The students’ initial response to the task as an exercise in data, statistics and
research frameworks provided qualitative support for Dunwoody and Griffin’s
(2013) survey findings; one of the constraints of teaching journalism students
within this area is perceptual (rather than actual) concerns about their ability and
willingness to learn statistics or work with data. However, despite reservations
about the process of data mining and the political subject matter, feedback from
students at the project’s completion was almost unanimously positive. In particular, the publication outcome, working with the collected data and learning visualization skills were all identified as highlights. Responses to the data collection
process were mixed; in order to address the perceived monotony of this phase, a
combination of manual and automatic data collection would be considered in
future. While manual collection appeared to help students understand the process
and guaranteed the exclusivity of the results, combining some collection with
existing open-source data or using technology-based collection techniques would
likely increase student engagement.
While the project had many journalistic and educational benefits, there is
an important caveat. As WIRED contributing editor Jeff Howe (2007, in Flew &
Wilson, 2010, p. 139) states, the ‘dirty little secret’ of crowdsourced or networked
news gathering is that ‘most of the heavy lifting is done, not by the crowd per se,
but by a few select individuals’. In this case, the students did indeed do a great
deal of the ‘heavy lifting’, but the process of designing the research framework
(creating and testing the coding frameworks) and cleaning and analysing the data
was enormously time consuming and a somewhat specialized process.
Herein lies one of the central challenges in the application of networked journalism across the industry more broadly. For such initiatives to work, a small
group of professionals need to invest time into content, coordination and training

(Flew & Wilson, 2010), and developing the underpinning frameworks for contributors. Universities may be in a better position than less organized or experienced networks of ‘nonmarket actors’ to provide the structures and conditions in
which networked journalism may thrive; however, the resources required still
makes this model a difficult one to integrate into many classrooms, particularly
where large cohorts are concerned. Nevertheless, here too the digital landscape
offers opportunities for innovation. The same technologies that the open, collaborative news-gathering processes characteristic of networked journalism is built
on could equally facilitate the sharing of resources and frameworks to underpin
such projects. As networked journalism projects become more common, there is
scope for databases of supporting infrastructure—from coding sheets, manuals
and guidelines for contributors to data-checking and cleaning—to be shared,
crowdsourced and even automated.
