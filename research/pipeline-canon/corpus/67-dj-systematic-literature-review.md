Throughout the history of modern journalism, data has been used to supplement reporting and provide a deeper understanding of the issues at hand. However, the use of computer-assisted reporting (CAR) techniques in the 1960s marked a significant milestone,
allowing reporters to analyze large amounts of data more efficiently. Accordingly, the
beginning of data journalism is often dated to 1967, when Philip Meyer of The Detroit
Free Press, also the writer of Precision Journalism: A Reporter’s Introduction to Social
Science Methods (2002), used a mainframe computer to analyze a survey of Detroit residents to investigate and explain social issues in the city. Meyer’s book, first published
in 1973, was the first attempt to systematize the use of social science research and
data analysis techniques in different stages of news making, such as collection, storage,
retrieval, analysis, reduction, and communication (Stalph and Borges-Rey 2018). Meyer
sought “a higher standard of truthtelling (Meyer 1999, 4)” introducing scientific data gathering and analyzing methods into reporting, whether computer-assisted or not. The emergence of the Internet in the late 1980s and developments in computer and data sciences
in the 2000s accelerated the development of this type of data-driven reporting.
Since CAR, computational journalism, and data journalism have similar -and overlapping- professional and epistemological roots, some scholars and professionals have
emphasized their continuity as forms of quantitative journalism. However, many others,
such as Coddington (2015), argue essential differences between them, which scholars

should consider for detailed investigations. For instance, while CAR and computational
journalism are broader terms encompassing technology, algorithms, and automation to
enhance the journalistic process, data journalism focuses on using data as a primary
source for storytelling and reporting. Additionally, it embraces participatory practices
by involving the public in data collection, analysis, visualization, and storytelling. Furthermore, it advocates for open data policies and promotes the release of government or
institutional datasets to the public (Baack 2017; Hannaford 2022). Openness and transparency are prioritized as factors empowering citizens to explore and analyze the information
themselves, enabling them to become “co-constructors of truth” (Coddington 2015;
Hammond 2017; Parasie and Dagiral 2013). Data journalism’s emphasis on collaboration,
engagement, and collective intelligence in producing impactful and inclusive journalism
represents a departure from these two. Building on Coddington’s typology of quantitative
journalism (2015, 332–337), this study considers data journalism distinct from CAR and
computational journalism and aims to analyze the academic debate around the
concept in this direction.
Data journalism, as a term, emerged in the late 2000s, with Simon Rogers being credited as the first person to use it in a post on the Guardian Insider Blog in December 2008
(Rogers 2008). However, the first recognized “manifesto of data journalism” (Kayser-Brill,
Valeeva, and Radchenko 2016, 414) emerged in 2006 as a blog post by Adrian Holovaty. In
it, Holovaty advocated for newspapers to shift away from a “story-centric worldview” that
aims to deliver structured information to the public fastest possible and instead prioritized disseminating “important, focused information that is useful to people’s lives and
helps them understand the world” (Holovaty 2006). Data journalism pioneers emphasized
trust over attention and employed raw data to enhance the perceived validity of truth
claims using techniques borrowed from scientific investigation and computational
analysis.
Data journalism popularity grew in 2009 with the launch of the Guardian’s Data
Blog, the European Journalism Centre’s (EJC) data-driven journalism program, and DataJournalism.com. In addition, the newly born practice was extensively discussed in the
EJC’s “Data-driven journalism: What is there to learn?” (2010) and the “Data Journalism
Handbook,” edited by Gray, Bounegru, and Chambers (2012). Finally, in 2012, the
Global Editors Network launched the Data Journalism Awards competition, which
helped to establish standards for the field. Many other institutional and non-institutional efforts, such as Paul Bradshaw’s onlinejournalismblog.com, Code for Africa,
and the Arab Data Journalists’ Network, along with graduate programs and massive
open online courses, have played a vital role in advancing the field of data journalism
on a global scale. [1] Today, data journalism is an emerging practice in news organizations worldwide, with many using data analysis and visualization techniques to
report on various topics. The Sigma Awards 2020-2022 projects database [(https://](https://github.com/Sigma-Awards)
[github.com/Sigma-Awards)](https://github.com/Sigma-Awards) showcases that it is being practiced in at least 95 countries
across six continents.
The academic study of data journalism has grown in tandem with the practice
itself. Several works and compilations (Baack 2011; Howard 2014; Lesage and
Hackett 2014; Lewis 2015; Mair and Keeble 2013; Parasie and Dagiral 2013; Rogers
2013) have laid the foundation for data journalism studies and formal education.
As data journalism originated in Anglo-American settings, early research focused


on its emergence in Western media systems. However, recent research on projects
nominated for or winning global data journalism awards -such as the Data Journalism
Awards, Online Journalism Awards, and Sigma Awards (Chaparro-Domínguez and
Díaz-Campo 2021; Loosen, Reimer, and De Silva-Schmidt 2020) reveals that data journalism is spreading worldwide. Therefore, expanding the body of research on data
journalism in non-Western contexts (see, for example, Mutsvairo, Bebawi, and
Borges-Rey 2019; Salaverría and de-Lima-Santos 2021) is essential to expand knowledge and perspectives on the subject.
A systematic literature review (SLR) on data journalism scholarship can help identify
such gaps in knowledge and further research. Such a review plays a crucial role in
delving into data journalism’s theoretical, conceptual, and empirical aspects and shedding light on the most effective approaches, obstacles, and prospects for its advancement. By presenting a thorough overview of the existing literature, a SLR offers
valuable insights for scholars, journalists and educators interested in data journalism,
which might ultimately contribute to the expansion and long-term viability of the field
as a whole.
The study involves a systematic review of literature that outlines the evolution and
current state of data journalism scholarship in communication studies. Unlike previous
SLRs (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; de Souza et al. 2018), this review not only concentrates
on empirical studies but also encompasses conceptual studies. By including both types
of research, readers can gain a broader perspective and deeper understanding of data
journalism, allowing them to explore both the epistemological and socio-political meanings of the term and its practical repercussions. Ultimately, this study has the potential to
provide valuable guidance for scholars and practitioners alike, paving the way for more
innovative and impactful research in data journalism. Therefore, the research aims to
answer the following questions:


RQ1. What kind of research is being conducted on data journalism, and which theories and
methods are being employed?


RQ2. How is the data journalism scholarship evolving in terms of publication activity across
different periods, regions, and subjects?


RQ3. How is data journalism defined in academic literature?


RQ4. How can data journalism scholarship be improved by identifying and addressing
research gaps, and how can future research be guided by relevant suggestions?


Method


Systematic literature reviews (SLRs) involve identifying, assessing, and interpreting
primary research on a particular topic. In addition, SLRs offer advantages over traditional
literature reviews, which tend to be limited to literature already known to the authors or
found through cursory searches. By contrast, SLRs require researchers to use broader
search strategies, predefined search strings, and uniform inclusion and exclusion criteria,
which can help to uncover studies outside of their typical areas of research and networks.
This, in turn, can lead to more precise and objective answers to research questions (Fink
2014).


This study aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the development and current
state of scholarship on data journalism. In this respect, the study thoroughly analyzed
English-language articles published in communication journals that specifically address
data journalism. In addition, a rigorous and reproducible research protocol was developed
to identify relevant studies and specific inclusion and exclusion criteria were applied
throughout the process. The study’s steps and procedures for applying these criteria
are outlined below (See Figure 1).


Keywords and Time Interval


Web of Science (WOS), the oldest international bibliometric database, is characterized by
its selective, structured, balanced, and up-to-date coverage (Birkle et al. 2020, 364–367). It
is a major tool for journal selection, research evaluation, and bibliometric analyses. In
January 2023, an initial search was conducted in the WOS Core Collection (WOS CC)
using “data journalism” and “data-driven journalism” as keywords to identify relevant
studies published on the topic. Keywords related to earlier forms of quantitative journalism were omitted since data journalism is not solely considered an evolution of those
fields. The search spanned from December 1, 2008, to December 31, 2022, marking
2008 as the starting point because that is when the term “data journalism” was first
used. This search yielded 383 publications containing these keywords in their title,
abstract, and author keywords. To refine the list, duplicate publications appearing in
results for both keywords were removed.

Language, Publication Type, and Indexing


Subsequently, the focus was narrowed to English-language journal articles published in
communication journals indexed in the Social Sciences Citation Index, resulting in 151
manuscripts.
The choice of English was made to ensure consistency and manageability in the review
process. First, English is the “lingua franca” for academics, and regardless of their native
languages, researchers often publish their work in English to reach a broader international
audience. Second, due to the language proficiency of the author, limiting the review to
sources written in English helped assess the quality and relevance of the manuscripts.
However, future research could consider non-English sources across various indexes for
a more comprehensive perspective.
The first scan in WOS CC yielded no results from the Book Citation Index, and the
language filter eliminated many sources listed in the Arts & Humanities Citation Index,
Conference Proceedings Citation Index, and Emerging Sources Citation Index. The
author excluded the remaining non-article sources from the list due to their relatively
small numbers.


Title and Abstract Review


Reviewing titles and abstracts, helped the author identify relevant texts directly related
to the topic of analysis, resulting in 87 manuscripts. Articles excluded at this stage
primarily covered general concepts related to data journalism, including open
data, big data, investigative journalism, or the broader digital and quantitative shifts
in journalism.
The initial selection phase revealed that 84% of the corpus came from four journals,
Digital Journalism, Journalism Practice, Journalism, and Journalism Studies, each with
over ten published studies. To ensure comprehensive coverage, in February 2023, the
author conducted a follow-up search on these journal websites using the same keywords
and included 15 more studies in the corpus.


Full Text Review: Coding and Analysis


The final sample comprised 102 manuscripts, and their full texts were qualitatively and
quantitatively analyzed. Since the corpus is relatively small, the author performed the
coding alone. A coding guideline based on prior familiarity with data journalism scholarship was developed, covering (1) article and author details, (2) theoretical approaches,
and (3) methodological approaches. A pilot test involving a random sample of ten manuscripts validated the coding guidelines and predetermined categories for clarity and effectiveness. A coding log documented the rationale of categorization, encountered
challenges, and their resolutions. The author reviewed the same sample twice, a week
apart, to ensure consistency. This resulted in high intra-coder reliability with minor guideline revisions (Fink 2014, 166–180).
The coded content was analyzed and presented using Excel Pivot Tables to identify
thematic, theoretical and methodological trends in the literature and how the relationships between them vary depending on variables such as time and region.


Findings


The Discourse on Data Journalism Continues Under Five Main Topics


Despite the search encompassing the timeframe of 2008 to present day, the earliest
manuscript that met the inclusion criteria was published in 2013. The number of manuscripts published each year have steadily raised ever since, with an average of 10 publications per year. 2015 and 2019 were notable for significant increases in data
journalism research, which Fink and Anderson (2015, 476) reported as “explosion.”
The manuscripts have been categorized into five topics, as shown in Figure 2. First,
journalists’ perceptions and practices of data journalism have been a recurring focus,
with many studies examining the practice in one or multiple media organizations.
These studies have been descriptive, examining organizational culture, newsroom structures, skills and abilities sought in data journalists, professional challenges faced by data
journalists, and expectations for the future of the practice (Appelgren and Nygren 2014;
Borges-Rey 2016; De Maeyer et al. 2015; Fink and Anderson 2015).
The findings show that data journalists face common technical and infrastructural challenges, irrespective of their country in most cases. Nevertheless, the unique economic and
political contexts in which journalists operate create problems specific to institutions,
countries, or regions. These include data accessibility and security issues related to
official open data policies (Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga 2022a).
On the other hand, it is worth noting that gender is an underexplored variable in data
journalism research despite its significance, particularly given the historical exclusion of
female journalists from so-called complex and demanding roles in journalism. Usher
(2019, 18–19) characterizes the data journalism industry as “white and male,” reflecting
a long-standing culture that marginalized women. Studies in the corpus confirm this
gender imbalance, with women often underrepresented (Boyles and Meyer 2016;
Hermida and Young 2017; Lowrey, Sherrill, and Broussard 2019; Porlezza and Splendore
2019; Stalph, Hahn, and Liewehr 2022). Many merely convey the gender distribution of

Some exceptional cases, like Spanish (Palomo, Teruel, and Blanco-Castilla 2019) and
Argentine (de-Lima-Santos 2022) newspapers, show a more equitable gender distribution
attributed to female leadership. Notably, Gondwe and White’s research in Sub-Saharan

female journalists exhibiting more pessimism, partly linked to “mathematical phobia.”
A series of analyses of data journalism artifacts (Appelgren 2017; Lim 2019; Munoriyarwa 2020; Pereira and Mastrella 2022; Stalph 2018; Tabary, Provost, and Trottier
2015; Wu 2022), and explorations of the epistemology and history of data journalism
(Lewis and Westlund 2015; Lewis 2015; Hammond 2017) were almost simultaneously published. The focus of these studies is primarily on the format and content of data-driven
news stories and projects. Some studies aim to establish patterns and standards for
data-driven news by analyzing award-winning projects. For instance, Loosen, Reimer,
and De Silva-Schmidt’s (2020) analysis of 225 award-nominated projects between 2013
and 2016 found that data journalism primarily dealt with serious topics, such as political,
societal, and economic issues. In addition, Ojo and Heravi’s (2018) analysis of 44 winning
projects from the same period revealed that many stories were explaining phenomena for
deeper understanding or revealing system anomalies. Similarly, Young, Hermida, and
Fulda’s (2018) analysis of 26 Canadian data journalism projects as finalists/winners of prominent data journalism awards highlighted the investigative and explanatory side of data
journalism. Web development and publishing, data analysis and visualization (Ojo and
Heravi 2018), textual analysis, and dynamic map visualization (Young, Hermida, and
Fulda 2018) are the essential skills and tools to create successful data stories. Verification,
cross-checking from various sources, contextualizing the data, including the investigation
methodology, and protecting sources are, in turn, the ethical standards of the practice
(Chaparro-Domínguez and Díaz-Campo 2021). Apart from mentioning gold standards
regarding topics, skills and ethical considerations, these studies also point to some patterns worth improving. For example, data journalism’s dependence on readily accessible,
pre-processed public data (Tabary, Provost, and Trottier 2015, 16) from official institutions
and non-commercial organizations (Loosen, Reimer, and De Silva-Schmidt 2020) and
easy-to-use or freely available software solutions, is considered an obstacle to its
development.
Studies in this category not only examine award-winning data-driven projects but also
analyze cases of data journalism practice in different contexts. The United States, the
United Kingdom, and Germany are the most frequently examined single cases, with 13,
8, and 6 studies respectively. This supports previous research (Ausserhofer et al. 2020),
which indicates that data journalism research has mainly been centered on the USA
and the UK. However, the distribution of countries that the literature focuses on contradicts the same study’s assertion that there is only a few English-language research on
practices outside Europe and North America. Although the first wave of English-language
data journalism research concentrated mainly on Western cases, interest in the Global
South has increased since 2019. In the final sample, one-fifth of the cases were from
countries outside North America and Europe.
However, the studies differ in their approach and topics. Cases related to the Global
North countries tend to discuss data journalism’s relationship with technology (Anderson
and Borges-Rey 2019; Baack 2017; Broussard and Boss 2018; Morini, Dörk, and Appelgren


2022; Parasie and Dagiral 2013). Meanwhile, cases related to the Global South highlight its
potential to enhance journalist autonomy and objectivity, as seen in Jamil (2021), Wu
(2021), and Guan and Wang (2022). In countries where democracy and the separation
of powers are relatively weak, data journalism is believed to support the capacity of journalism to hold those in power accountable. In this respect, China has become a new area
of interest in data journalism research since 2019, with scholars from both Chinese and
non-Chinese institutions studying the development of this practice in the country.
Studies with broader scopes, including 19 international and 13 European-scale studies,
have become more popular, particularly since 2018, as the practice of data journalism has
gained global momentum. These studies examine how different journalistic environments affect data journalists’ role conceptions (Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga 2022b; Felle
2015; Lewis and Al Nashmi 2019), training opportunities (Splendore et al. 2015),
working conditions (Appelgren and Salaverría 2018; Gondwe and White 2022; Martin,
Camaj, and Lanosga 2022; Stalph 2020), and products (Lim 2019; Loosen, Reimer, and
De Silva-Schmidt 2020; Young, Hermida, and Fulda 2018).
A significant study in this category by Tom Felle (2015) referred to data journalists as
“digital watchdogs,” which supports the notion that data journalism is used as a tool for
investigative journalism and democratic accountability. This view is reinforced by other
studies that emphasize data journalism’s civic and advocacy roles (Camaj, Martin, and
Lanosga 2022b; Ojo and Heravi 2018; Zhang 2018). However, a survey conducted by
Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga (2022b) with journalists from 63 countries suggested that
economic development was positively associated with the perception of data journalism
as a watchdog, suggesting that developed countries in the Global North may utilize data
journalism more for the fourth-estate function.
On the other hand, Stalph and Borges-Rey’s (2018) interviews with academics, nonlegacy experts, and data journalists from North America and Europe contradict these
favorable views, indicating that data journalism is primarily practiced in large urban
areas and legacy media organizations, which limits its potential for fulfilling the
fourth-estate function as hoped. Similarly, several studies have shown how data journalism failed to help journalists improve their investigative accountability works. These
studies attribute the incompetence of data journalism to individual, organizational,
technological, and structural factors, including political and economic systems. For
instance, incomplete, outdated or error-filled data, inconvenient file formats, such as
scanned copies of texts or PDF files containing tables (Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga
2022a; Martin, Camaj, and Lanosga 2022; Tabary, Provost, and Trottier 2015), and the
lack of digital/data literacy (Bhaskaran, Kashyap, and Mishra 2022; Borges-Rey 2016;
Boyles and Meyer 2016; De Maeyer et al. 2015) are significant challenges faced by
data journalists worldwide. However, these challenges are more severe in the Global
South where the lack of open data infrastructure and right-to-information legislation
hampers data journalists’ work (Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga 2022a). Additionally, high
dependence on official data (Loosen, Reimer, and De Silva-Schmidt 2020; Zamith
2019) and distrust and dissatisfaction with this data are other structural obstacles to
journalists, especially in countries with no-to-low transparency infrastructures (Camaj,
Martin, and Lanosga 2022b; Jamil 2021). The lack of better tools to operate in nonLatin languages/scripts (Martin, Camaj, and Lanosga 2022) is another issue mainly concerning the Global South. Unequal data access and process caused by political,


economic, and technical inequalities widen the gap in data journalism practices
between countries.
Many articles have been published since 2015 on the history and epistemology of data
journalism (Borges-Rey 2020; Coddington 2015; Lewis and Westlund 2015; Parasie 2015;
Parasie and Dagiral 2013). This category of articles contributed significantly to the
explosion in 2015, as half of the articles published that year were related to this topic.
Although these studies give differing definitions of data journalism, they often use
similar concepts for discussion. For example, the logic of letting the data speak rather
than personal opinions or testimonies of people creates a “more accurate, objective,
transparent and reliable” image of data journalism when compared to traditional forms
of storytelling (Borges-Rey 2016; Boyles and Meyer 2016; Guan and Wang 2022; Parasie
2015; Parasie and Dagiral 2013). Indeed, the analysis of the “author keywords” reveals
that discussion about data journalism is frequently related to transparency, objectivity,
interactivity, and democracy (Anderson and Borges-Rey 2019; Appelgren and Salaverría
2018; Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga 2022a; 2022b; Coddington 2015; Hammond 2017; Porlezza and Splendore 2019; Splendore et al. 2015; Wu 2021; Zamith 2019). [2] These concepts,
also associated with open government culture, can be evaluated in relation to participatory democracy. The belief is that open government data can help improve democracy by
giving citizens the information they need to participate effectively. Data journalism, in this
context, is often framed to empower citizens by providing them with access to information and tools for analysis. Nonetheless, there are doubts about whether this data
empowers citizens or primarily serves the interests of non-citizen groups. However,
despite its relevance to these discussions, current research has not adequately addressed
the ethics of data journalism and the doubt that this discourse may overlook power
relations and material injustices related to race, gender, and class (Hannaford 2022).
Even though almost all studies in this category speak of the claims of data journalism
regarding knowledge and truth, they differ in how such knowledge claims are produced
and expressed. The difference in epistemological approaches also affects whether data
journalism is defined as an independent specialty or a kind of skillset integral to mainstream practice. Data journalism was characterized more as a promising new type of journalism practice in many studies. For example, Fink and Anderson (2015, 468) defined it as
a new “field in development,” while Broussard and Boss (2018, 1207) classified it as a
“specialized sub-field situated within the larger field of journalism.” Similarly, more
recent research from Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga (2022b, 1463) offers a comprehensive
definition: a particular form of news reporting that combines traditional investigative
reporting with innovative tools and opportunities for audience engagement caused by
the digitalization of journalism. On the other hand, some scholars described it as a core
skill or knowledge base that all journalists should possess to do their job (Stalph and
Borges-Rey 2018). For example, Stalph, in his 2018 study, suggests that “data journalism
is not a new holistic genre of journalism but a journalistic practice that, when employed,
can enhance stories with visualizations while also enabling journalists to incorporate data
sources as primary sources that previously may have widely been regarded as
inoperative.”
Only a small percentage (3%) of studies have focused on data journalism education
and training, with the first ones appearing in 2015. These studies show that the perceived
division between vocational and academic journalism education hinders industry


progress. For example, Hewett (2015) proposes collaboration with industry professionals,
including data journalists and practitioners, could promote innovative approaches to data
journalism education within universities.
Chaparro-Domínguez and Díaz-Campo’s (2021) review of the “world’s best data journalism 2012-2020” and Zhang and Ho’s (2020) network analysis of publicly available
tweets about data journalism indicate a gap between academia and the industry in the
emerging practice of data journalism. The network analysis shows that accounts
serving as brokerage among different groups and as information hubs are mainly professional organizations on data-driven investigative reporting and news professionals
(except for Alberto Cairo). Unsurprisingly, a significant proportion of these accounts are
located in the USA or the UK. When those findings are evaluated together, one can say
that the suggestions put forward by academic research and reports do not find much
echo in the journalism industry and the emerging practice of data journalism. This situation is, in fact, not unique to data journalism. It can also be considered as a continuation
of the discussion about the challenges of academia in defining and studying journalism
due to its changing nature and lack of consensus (Zelizer 2009).
Splendore et al. (2015) provide a more comprehensive view of data journalism education that includes various formal and informal training opportunities. However, they
also point out that data journalism education has a limitation in that it focuses more
on data-driven news making and often neglects important journalistic topics such as
ethics, transparency, accountability, and responsiveness. A holistic data journalism education should combine practical and theoretical content, encompassing both statistical
and editorial skills, and encourage journalists to think in terms of data. Unfortunately,
the lack of in-house professional development programs makes it challenging for data
journalists working in the industry to acquire these skills.
However, except for one article in 2019, the matter has not been further discussed.
Therefore, it is unclear if data journalism education has considered these perspectives
and if collaboration between academia and industry has improved to provide a more
comprehensive education, at least in the scholarly discussion in analyzed journal articles.
Heravi’s study (2019), on the other hand, shows that the imbalance between technical and
editorial skills in data journalists still exists. Drawing on the 2017 Global Data Journalism
survey results, she emphasizes the lack of technical, data analytics and statistical skills
among journalists. While many journalists express interest in enhancing their knowledge
of data analysis, coding, and data visualization, Heravi’s analysis of 219 data journalism
modules worldwide shows that existing curricula (and instructors) are inadequate in providing the content journalists want to learn.
Apart from formal education by universities and journalism schools, studies also
mention online courses and materials by non-governmental and non-academic institutions. Splendore et al. (2015), for example, attribute a significant role to online selfstudy. However, the fact that even these courses are mostly offered by US, and European-based organizations and in English presents a new barrier that creates inequality
of opportunity for journalists. More studies addressing such barriers to personal training
opportunities from the perspective of journalists can contribute to developing a body of
target-oriented educational material.
Finally, it was not until 2019 that scholars began to conduct literature reviews on data
journalism, comprising only 6% of the total literature. For instance, by analyzing empirical


work on data journalism from the social sciences and neighboring disciplines, Ausserhofer
et al. (2020) found a lack of references to theory or methodology and limited methodological diversity. Other systematic literature reviews on data journalism scholarship,
such as Bhaskaran, Kashyap, and Mishra’s (2022) review on data journalism education
and Heravi et al.’s (2022) review on data journalistic work preservation, also identified
similar shortcomings. de Souza et al. (2018) conducted a review of literature in the communications or computer science field to focus on techniques and tools used in data journalism and found a need for more discussion and guidance on visualization techniques
and tools. They also called for greater diversity in the cases investigated, as they tend
to be western-oriented.


Authorship, Funding, and Publishing Opportunities Concentrated in the Hands
of a Few Key Players


The study identified 148 distinct authors, with 14 of them contributing to three or more
articles. Among those, six authors were considered the top authors (de Souza et al. 2018)
because they were the first authors of three or more manuscripts: Ester Appelgren from
Sweden, who published seven articles; Florian Stalph from Germany with six; MathiasFelipe de-Lima-Santos from Spain with five; Shangyuan Wu from Singapore with four;
Xinzhi Zhang from Hong Kong with three; and Christian Pentzold from Germany with
three manuscripts. Appelgren was the only author who had been publishing regularly
since 2014, while De-Lima-Santos, Wu, and Pentzold had a high level of productivity,
despite only publishing on data journalism since the 2020s.
Data journalism research, like data journalism practice itself, involves collaboration.
According to the review, only 35% of the manuscripts had a single author, while 41%
had two, and 24% had three or more authors.
The manuscripts were also classified based on the institutions where the first authors
were affiliated (de Souza et al. 2018), and the countries with the highest number of studies
were the USA (19%), Germany (14%), the UK (13%), Sweden (10%), and Spain (10%). This
finding confirms previous studies that stated data journalism research mainly originated
from Western countries (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; de Souza et al. 2018), despite Singapore
(5%) and Hong Kong (3%) having an increasing number of publications since 2017, as
seen in Figure 3. However, it is worth mentioning that the number of studies conducted
does not accurately mirror the extent of advancement in data journalism within the
respective countries. Nonetheless, scholars from Western institutions still dominate
data journalism literature.
However, diversity in data journalism research is increasing, with 77% of manuscripts
from the Global South published in the last three years. Research on data journalism’s
epistemology, education, and literature is conducted mainly by scholars from the
Global North, with rates ranging from 83-100%. On the other hand, authors from the
Global South are more visible in works related to data journalism practices and products
but only account for 17% of the research.
Out of the 102 manuscripts examined, 87 involved a case study, and 64 of these 87 cases
were about countries associated with the first authors’ addresses. It is also noteworthy that
authors from Western institutions wrote half of the studies related to the Global South.
However, these studies did not reflect a Western-centric media perspective. Instead, they

literature reviews (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; de Souza et al. 2018), is also reflected in this
study, with only 47% of the corpus referring to a theoretical framework. The most commonly used theoretical frameworks are actor-network theory and field theory. Even
though only four studies directly refer to each of these paradigms, many other studies
imply their presence in varying degrees, even if they do not mention them explicitly.
Latour’s actor-network theory (ANT) can help understand how the production and dissemination of data stories rely on complex networks of actors and actants. For instance, a
data story might involve journalists, developers, algorithms, designers, and audiences. By
using ANT, data journalism scholars can analyze how these actors and actants interact and
influence one another and how these interactions shape the production and distribution
of data stories. This approach can assist them identify sources of bias, explore the role of
technology in shaping news content, and investigate how news is received and interpreted by different audiences. It thus fits well with the epistemological claims for big
data and datafication of journalism. Bourdieu’s field theory, on the other hand, provides
a solid conceptual understanding of how data journalism interacts with external structures such as economic and political pressures, which might also deepen the study of
data journalism practice, be it single case studies or multi-nation comparative studies
(Lowrey, Sherrill, and Broussard 2019; Martin, Camaj, and Lanosga 2022; Tandoc and
Oh 2015).
Boundary work, diffusion of innovation, assemblage, framing, meta-journalistic discourse, and practice theory are also mentioned as theoretical frameworks, but explicit
references to them are infrequent. However, some studies show an interest in practice
theory’s focus on the routines and standards of data-intensive news work and journalists’
situation in many interlocking practices (Baack 2017; Craig, Ketterer, and Yousuf 2017) and
meta-journalistic discourse’s questioning of how journalists evaluate the profession itself
(Lewis and Al Nashmi 2019; Stalph 2018). Practice theory provides a valuable framework
for understanding the complex social practices and interactions that shape the production and dissemination of news in the digital age. Data journalism scholars can use


this approach to identify the underlying cultural and social norms that shape data-driven
storytelling, leading to the development of more nuanced and critical viewpoints on the
role of data in journalism. By employing this framework, researchers can understand how
different data sources and visualization techniques are employed to communicate and
reinforce specific narratives and how broader cultural and ideological factors shape
these practices. Furthermore, it can offer valuable insights to scholars and data journalists
on how diverse audiences perceive and receive their work, facilitating the formulation of
effective strategies to engage and communicate with various communities.
On the other hand, meta-journalistic discourse involves discussions of journalism’s
societal role, the ethics and norms of journalistic practices, and the relationship
between journalists and their audiences. Within the framework of data journalism, it
can help identify the ethical and methodological considerations that emerge when journalists utilize data to inform news stories, including concerns about data quality, privacy,
and bias. Overall, it offers a valuable perspective for studying data journalism, enabling
researchers and practitioners to critically examine the challenges and opportunities
that data-driven approaches present for journalism.
Additionally, the potential of postcolonial theory (Wright, Zamith, and Bebawi 2019) as
a framework for understanding data journalism in its variety, as situationally defined
through discourse and contextually shaped by culture (Lewis and Al Nashmi 2019) still
needs to be explored. Data journalists need to be aware of the broader social and historical contexts in which data is produced and used and to critically interrogate the assumptions and biases that may be embedded in data and data-driven narratives. Moreover,
postcolonial theory highlights the importance of engaging with diverse voices and perspectives and recognizing how power and privilege shape who has access to data and
who can shape data-driven narratives. Furthermore, it can help critically examine how
data is used to reinforce existing power structures and inequalities and identify strategies
for using data to challenge and disrupt these structures. This can involve developing more
nuanced and critical approaches to data analysis and visualization and engaging in collaborative and participatory approaches to data journalism that involve communities and
stakeholders in the production and interpretation of data.
The review reveals that the methodological approaches of the studies, like their theoretical frameworks, needed more diversity and clarity. First, there is a predominance of
empirical studies in the corpus. Only 17 of the 103 manuscripts reviewed are conceptual
research, while the majority (%83) is empirical. Conceptual articles in the corpus speak
globally to related issues such as big data, algorithms, and computer-assisted reporting
and offer valuable insights into the emergence and epistemology of data journalism. Conceptual research can provide researchers with theoretical frameworks and analytical tools
to organize ideas required for further data journalism research and theory building. This is
a significant gap in the scholarship that needs to be addressed.
Second, the majority of the examined empirical studies (61%) adopt a quantitative
approach, while only 27% are qualitative and 12% are mixed-method studies. This
finding contradicts Ausserhofer et al.’s (2020) emphasis on the lack of quantitative
research in the literature between 1996 and 2016. The present study suggests that
there is now more diversity in research designs, at least at the qualitative-quantitative
dichotomy level. However, the consistent use of small samples from a single outlet or
country poses a challenge to generalizing findings and building theories through


comparative work. Moreover, the repeated focus on cases from North America and
Europe risks reinforcing the problematic notion of a more “advanced” West (Wright,
Zamith, and Bebawi 2019) and its implicit norms and assumptions that should serve as
a standard for data journalism. Thus, more research is still needed to explore countries
in Asia, Africa, and Latin America that have received less attention.
Despite the prevalence of quantitative methods, there is diversity in data collection
techniques used by data journalism scholars. The most common methods are in-depth
interviews (48 studies) and content analysis (31 studies). Other techniques such as
surveys (13 studies), observations/newsroom ethnographies (9 studies), and document
reviews (6 studies) are also frequently used. However, less common methods such as
experiments (2 studies), participatory mapping (1 study), and scenario network
mapping (1 study) are used at lower rates. Additionally, a quarter of studies use multiple
data collection techniques that complement each other, which is beneficial for hypothesis-testing and measurement.
One example of a helpful triangulation is the combination of qualitative interviews
with other techniques. Qualitative interviews can provide valuable insights into the processes and dynamics of creating data-driven news (Ojo and Heravi 2018). Data reporters,
technical and editorial staff, academics, and experts from professional organizations can
share their thoughts and experiences in these interviews. However, more than these testimonies alone is needed to reveal whether there are discrepancies between interviewees’
perceived roles and their actual performance. Therefore, researchers often combine interviews with other techniques, such as content or document analysis, to examine the observable attributes of the news. To clarify, content analyses in the corpus primarily focused
on analyzing data journalism artifacts, such as news stories or award-winning projects.
Despite many studies highlighting the importance of visual and interactive elements in
data journalism, only a few content analyses focused on these aspects. Additionally,
more observations and newsroom ethnographies can provide an outsider’s perspective
on the relationships and interactions within data journalism.
Third, data journalism scholarship lacks explicit references to the procedures and applications of the methodology. For example, sampling techniques are mentioned only in a
few studies. Besides, it is remarkable that the sampling techniques mentioned are mostly
non-probabilistic, despite the abundance of quantitative studies in the corpus. Snowball
and purposive sampling are the most common techniques, as they help find relevant
examples in a specialized area like data journalism.
Finally, only 36% of the studies refer to a data analysis method, with the two most frequently used being thematic analysis (used in 12 studies) and grounded theory (used in
11 studies).
In summary, the repeated use of similar data collection, sampling, and data analysis
techniques has led to a lack of diversity in data journalism scholarship. While conducting
similar studies in different contexts is helpful for comparison purposes, using different
methods can offer a more comprehensive perspective on the field. For instance, Link,
Henke, and Möhring’s (2021) experiment challenged the long-held belief in the
influence of statistical information and visualizations in news articles on message credibility and reading experience. Instead, they barely pointed to some topic- and userdependent potentials of the influence ascribed to these elements. Although the results
did not confirm the positive impact of such elements, they still provide valuable insights


into how data should be used to achieve the potential benefits of data journalism, which
can contribute to the overall knowledge of the field. Further research using experiments
can allow researchers to isolate the effects of specific variables and draw causal inferences
about the impact of different data journalistic practices on audience perception, which is
a clear gap in the literature.


Discussion and Conclusion


According to the research, data journalism has been highly regarded by scholars for a considerable time. Early journalism, citizenship, and technology-driven discourses of data
journalism (see, for example, Hannaford 2022) have defined it as “better”, value-laden
journalism done for the public good. Truth, accuracy, justice, transparency, liberation,
and democratization are among the positive values that are linked to data journalism.
Digital technology, datafication, and innovation are viewed as indicators of progress.
These viewpoints challenged the conventional approach of the journalism industry by
prioritizing openness and collaboration over ownership and control. The belief that
open data would lead to an open culture, better decision-making, and innovation was primarily relied upon. Freedom of Information requests, which are an important component
of data journalism, were given significant recognition in this context.
However, these discourses surrounding the dominant Western idea of liberalism, which
emphasizes individual liberties, autonomy, and accessibility, overlook the disempowering
power dynamics that cause journalists to rely on official data sources and big tech companies. The unequal power structures within this network have been ignored. It took
some time to see that the practice did not monolithically coincide with the credits
given to data journalism. Although the field was powerfully shaped by “the outward
flow of ideas, practices, and structures from [North] America and Europe” (Wright,
Zamith, and Bebawi 2019), data journalism practice varies across different regions, even
in countries within these regions. Contextual factors such as journalistic cultures, media
markets, and the political environment (Appelgren, Lindén, and van Dalen 2019; Porlezza
and Splendore 2019), as well as the interplay between journalists and local, national,
regional, and international structures (Wright, Zamith, and Bebawi 2019), are what ultimately determine the adoption, perception, and interpretation of data journalism in
each country.
To illustrate, the availability of digital infrastructure, funding, and trained personnel
have typically supported data journalism in western countries, whereas in the Global
South, political and legal obstacles and a scarcity of these resources have hindered its
development. For instance, Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga’s (2022a) analysis of transparency
infrastructure in 71 countries indicates that technical and economic inequalities may
create unequal access to data, exacerbating the gap between data journalism practices
in information-rich and information-poor countries. More large-scale, comparative
research is required to challenge dominant Western-centric approaches to data journalism and assess local practices in their distinct settings.
However, most empirical studies on data journalism in the current corpus are singlenation cases from North America and Europe, which limits scholars’ ability to draw generalized conclusions and build theories. While multi-nation studies have increased since
2021, they are only 14 in number, with five focusing on North America and Europe,


two on the Arab region, one on Latin America, and one on the Sub-Saharan African
region. These studies report regional characteristics without providing theory-driven
interpretations or comprehensive comparisons between countries or regions. Moreover,
only seven studies focus on less-explored countries in Asia, Africa, and South America,
along with cases from North America and Europe, for comparative purposes. The existence of three large-scale studies in the corpus by a US-based research collective, published in 2022 (Camaj, Martin, and Lanosga 2022a; 2022b; Martin, Camaj, and Lanosga
2022), is an important indication that the gap in this matter (Wright, Zamith, and
Bebawi 2019) is finally being addressed by academia. However, collaborative efforts
must be expanded to include scholars from diverse regions of the world to conduct
longer-term comparative studies.
The shortcomings in methodology are evident not only in the repetitive nature of the
topics investigated but also in the uniformity in data collection, sampling, and analysis
methods, often failing to provide explicit details about these procedures. The lack of
methodological diversity and explicit theoretical references and frameworks persists, as
noted in previous studies (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; Bhaskaran, Kashyap, and Mishra
2022; Heravi et al. 2022). Furthermore, the use of non-probabilistic sampling techniques
and short study periods pose challenges to generalizing the findings. Scholars should
consider utilizing triangulation and long-term explorations to understand data journalism
in diverse contexts comprehensively. For instance, newsroom ethnographies can provide
an in-depth understanding of the roles of peripheral actors, such as non-profits and civic
technologists. Focus groups can also yield comprehensive and in-depth insights into attitudes, beliefs, experiences, and behaviors, thereby providing a multi-dimensional understanding of the subject. Comparing and contrasting various data sources and conducting
long-term explorations could also aid in achieving a more nuanced understanding of data
journalism.
So far, the literature has primarily focused on data journalism’s production processes
and artifacts, neglecting their impact on target audiences. Moreover, many studies in
the corpus rely solely on expert or practitioner perspectives, overlooking the other side
of data-driven news flow. However, Link et al.’s research highlights the apparent need
for further research on data journalism’s audience, both in terms of perception and participation. Fortunately, recent calls for research on audience engagement in data journalism (see, for example, Tong 2022) reveal that the academy recognizes this need. This body
of research can potentially provide valuable insights into how data journalism will position itself in civic life and the journalistic field, ultimately improving the quality of the
artifacts.
Artificial intelligence (AI) also needs more attention in data journalism research. As AI
technology advances, it has the potential to significantly impact data collection, analysis, and presentation within journalism. However, using AI also presents ethical dilemmas, including the risk of biased algorithms, trained on biased data. Consequently,
further research is necessary to understand the interplay between data journalism
and AI.
On the other hand, the literature reveals a disconnection of the data journalism industry from academia. Consequently, the artifacts produced by the industry over the years do
not necessarily reflect concrete responses to the suggestions made in the academic literature. Likewise, Chaparro-Domínguez and Díaz-Campo (2021) further note that even


award-winning projects, which set the quality bar for the industry, do not always follow
academic recommendations.
In one of the seminal works within the field of data journalism, Lewis (2015) proposed
three fundamental components for understanding journalism’s evolution in the era of big
data: empirical cases that demonstrate the transformation brought about by big data,
conceptual frameworks for organizing and interpreting the developments, and critical
perspectives that challenge conventional norms and assumptions. This systematic literature review indicates that there has been a response to Lewis’s call for empirical research;
however, the corpus does not provide a comprehensive and comparative overview of the
data journalism field. As for the second and third components, further research may still
be needed to elaborate on conceptual frameworks and critical perspectives.
Certainly, these findings are limited to the results from 102 journal articles published in
English. Systematic reviews covering different types of academic production, including
books, book chapters, conference proceedings, editorial material, and industry reports;
including those published in languages other than English might provide a more comprehensive perspective on the mentioned limitations.
