#### Introduction

This book is born with a clear idea: to show that journalism is a viable future option in the network society, beyond the crisis of traditional business models. Journalism, as a profession, has an indispensable objective in every advanced society: to seek the truth and make it public. More than a counterpower, a journalist is a social, political, and economic "window cleaner": they clean the dirty panes of corruption so that citizens can see what lies behind the curtains of power and make informed decisions. This book aims precisely to equip the twenty-first-century journalist with ideas and tools to do their job better in a digital environment where the spirit of journalism's golden age must still be upheld, but adapted to new technologies.

Journalism will always be the artillery of freedom, though without losing sight of the fact that the profession must be guided by a passion for writing — whether in a newspaper report, a radio script, a documentary, a television news feature, or a website. Technology is useless without good content, and that is where journalistic talent will always be needed. As the journalist and Nobel Prize winner for literature Mario Vargas Llosa maintains, there are principles that must always guide the profession: self-demand, ideas, extraordinary care in crafting the text, fair play, literary wit, aesthetics, ethics, civic


conscience, and action. And all of this, which was valid in the twentieth century, remains so in the digital environment of the twenty-first.

It is true that the network society — and the philosophy of the Internet in which information should be free and open — has changed the traditional business model. This has led to the loss of many jobs, but new ones have also emerged. Journalism may be in crisis as a business model, but never as an ethical and professional concept. This book wants to give students ideas so they feel that being "social window cleaners" — seeking the truth and making it public — is more important today than ever. In this sense, access to large quantities of open data represents a very interesting opportunity to improve the profession. But the aim is not to be naive either. The final chapter provides an X-ray of what journalism and its professional practice look like in this network society. There is a growing tendency to replace journalists with experts — as in _The Conversation_ — in emerging content farms, and those models are achieving relative success. It is therefore essential to study them and reflect on them in journalism schools. It is also necessary to address why traditional outlets like _The Economist_ are so successful at this moment while others are not. And, of course, one must enter the debate that many of the creators of major emerging media outlets are having about the future of the profession.

The book also addresses the phenomenon of "digital feudalism," in which media workers are practically stripped of their rights. The aim is not to discourage students, but quite the opposite: to ensure they know the reality, are conscious of it, and can fight against those practices.

Journalism needs three conditions to develop optimally: a) personal curiosity and curiosity in the rest of society; b) freedom of action, thought, and expression; and c) access to a great deal of information. The network meets those conditions and, therefore, technology — and the digital environment — should not be seen as something hostile, but as a niche of opportunities. But, obviously, what is being done badly must also be criticized, especially when it degrades journalistic quality. Technology is unstoppable. Blacksmiths did not want there to be motor cars. Some used their experience as horseshoe forgers to make screws. And they succeeded.


Others chose to mourn a past that never returned: they consumed their lives extolling the virtues of the horse-drawn carriage.

A technological change does not necessarily mean the elimination of a profession like journalism, which is so necessary for democracy and — it should not be forgotten — is not based on technology itself, but uses technology to better achieve its goal: to seek the truth and make it public. Although few remember it, a situation similar to the crisis that journalism experienced due to the emergence of digital technology was lived in 1845, with the invention of the telegraph. The possibility of accessing information more quickly led newspapers to value immediacy extraordinarily over the calm explanation of events, which was what they had sold until then. In the years following the adoption of the telegraph, it became clear that immediacy is not incompatible with publishing analysis in subsequent days. In 1845 James Gordon Bennett, editor of _The New York Herald_, concluded that the telegraph would put many newspapers out of business. Yet the best history of journalism has unfolded after 1845.

This book is written for a generation of students who no longer need to be told what hypertext is or what a social network is, but who are eager to use their background as digital natives to improve journalism — an old profession with a great future. There are very competent manuals on how to write for the Internet. This book seeks a different approach: it wants readers to think about how the network society influences journalism. What lies beyond simply writing text for the web? Answering complex questions: how have sources changed with the Internet? Do emerging information models harm or benefit journalism? How can we use the vast amount of data we now have access to through the network to improve a news report? What is the communicative flow of information in the network society? The final result should be to prepare students to be fully aware of the potential, but also the dangers, of the digital environment in the journalistic profession.

Through the content of the six chapters, the aim is to link journalism with the digital environment but, above all, with an emerging technology


that appears very powerful: _big data_. As will be seen in chapter 2, _big data_ will transform not only the way we arrive at knowledge, but knowledge itself. Many believe that the processing of massive quantities of data will be the next industrial revolution. The combination of _big data_ and _open data_ offers journalism a unique opportunity to obtain what best defines the profession: the fact, the data. But it also opens up new unknowns and professional dangers that must be attended to.

The idea for this configuration of the text arose from the author's experience teaching and coordinating the doctoral course "Emerging Journalistic Profiles in the Network Society." Over time it became clear that the subject matter was extremely useful and, as network use spread among students, it was observed that part of the curriculum could be taught at the undergraduate level, in subjects such as "Journalism on the Network," "Citizen Participation on the Network"

- and "Structure and Information Effects of the Media System." It also updated some "Specialized Journalism" programs, such as "Science Journalism," "Economic Journalism," or "International Journalism," among others.

The Journalism Department of the Universidad Carlos III de Madrid, founded and directed by Professor María Pilar Diezhandino, has always fostered a philosophy based on excellence in journalistic storytelling, adapted to any medium, but with special attention to emerging digital environments and how to leverage them to improve professional practice. But criticism of new formats when they deteriorate journalistic quality has never been avoided either. The Journalism Department and its research group, PASSET (Journalism and Social Analysis: Evolution, Effects and Trends), have been pioneers in Spain in connecting the best of the old profession with the most promising aspects of new technology. As Diezhandino notes, with or without technology, "the need to develop journalistic judgment remains intact. Without that judgment, firmly established, one may produce a suitable and multimedia container, but perhaps empty of content" (Diezhandino, 2009). Journalistic judgment is a value that acquires enormous importance in the age of _big data_.

On the other hand, this text has been supplemented by the American perspective that the author was able to observe during his year as a visiting professor


at Harvard University (academic year 2013/2014). Much of what appears here is continually analyzed throughout the entire infrastructure Harvard has designed to improve American journalism: from the _Shorenstein Center on Media, Politics and Public Policy_ (part of the _Kennedy School of Government_) to the _Berkman Center for Internet and Society_ and the prestigious and influential _Nieman Foundation for Journalism_. The great universities of the world, from Harvard to Oxford — and its _Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism_ — have understood that the Internet has diminished academic and social interest in traditional _media studies_, from _film studies_ to _cultural studies_, which are somewhat outdated in the current context; yet it has, however, boosted interest in journalism, above all as a producer of content that reveals the truth. The Anglo-Saxon notion of _journalism_ triumphs in prestigious universities over _media_

- and _communication studies_.

The Anglo-Saxon experience — from Harvard or Columbia (where there is a dual degree in Journalism and Computer Science), to Oxford or the _London School of Economics_ (where the author of this book also spent a year on a fellowship as a _visiting fellow_) — is not trivial. One of the problems that Spanish-speaking countries have compared to Anglo-Saxon ones — and which is reflected in their journalism — is that in Spanish-speaking cultures we confuse terms such as journalist, communicator, or public relations professional, which in Anglo-Saxon culture are very clear, defined, and distinct. For example, a presenter who hosts a reality television contest like _Gran Hermano_ or a music or entertainment program is never a journalist. They are a communicator and can be described as a social communicator or — in the case of television — an audiovisual communicator, but they will never be a journalist. Anglo-Saxons perceive an enormous difference between the two professions, while in countries with recent dictatorships — or influenced by the Inquisition — it is accepted to use spectacle to confuse and narcotize citizens and, on top of that, to call it, wrongly, journalism.

A journalist confronts power to seek the truth; a communicator — or an audiovisual communicator — entertains the masses and is more concerned with audience ratings than with the truth. They prefer the fascinating over the important. A journalist risks their life in armed conflicts to report


with rigor on reality. An audiovisual communicator displays intellectual poverty on a reality television program. On the other hand, communicators working in public relations have destroyed the former trust between sources and journalists (Elías, 2007). And as a result, journalists have undertaken new ways of finding the truth, because it will never be found in a communications office.


In the Anglo-Saxon world, those who work in such propaganda-oriented entities are public relations professionals — they are never considered or defined as journalists. As stated, the journalist's mission is to seek the truth and publish it, not to conceal or polish it, as public relations professionals in political, economic, or corporate power offices typically do. The new techniques of data journalism can bypass the aggressive public relations professionals who each day control more spaces of public information. Public relations professionals will only be able to call themselves journalists when they publish all the relevant information they have access to about the company or ministry — including news that is damaging to their employers but relevant to the public. Journalism has always had — and will have — ethics and epic. In public relations or audiovisual communication, these are not so easy to find.


Journalism also counts among its ranks the finest intellectuals of the Western world: politicians, writers, and philosophers who, above all, were journalists first. In the United States, for example, the profession has been practiced by everyone from Benjamin Franklin to Hemingway; from John Dos Passos to Truman Capote. In England, from Dickens to Churchill. In Spain, from Pérez Galdós to Ortega y Gasset or Delibes, and in Latin America, from Vargas Llosa to García Márquez. All of them always defined — or define — themselves as journalists and, additionally, as writers, politicians, or philosophers, but never with a word as bland as "communicators." Some journalists have crossed from literature to television fiction, but always using narrative to tell the truth and engage in social criticism. What experts consider the best television series of all time, _The Wire_ (HBO: 2002–2008), was created by a veteran journalist, David Simon, who worked as a crime reporter for twenty years at _The Baltimore Sun_.


In countries like Spain, where tabloid television has emerged forcefully due to the low cultural and democratic level of the population (Elías, 2004), those who host such degrading and socially harmful programs call themselves communicators or audiovisual communicators, further muddying both of those terms.

This text wants to instill in readers Kant's maxim: "Dare to think." Examples are presented that have succeeded — some with stronger journalistic ethics and others with less. The aim is for the reader to reflect. One cannot live with nostalgia for the reporter who aspired to a model in which they joined a media outlet's staff and retired there. Now models are fluid, like the network society, and can obviously be used to create companies with permanent staffs, but also to practice the profession as a freelance journalist. The aim is to provide ideas and reflect on how the digital environment affects a profession like journalism, which is always indispensable in a democracy. As Professor of Journalism Xosé López of the University of Santiago de Compostela argues, we are in the era of "total journalism" — a renewed and current journalism that expresses itself through multimediality, hypertextuality, and interactivity; that is, journalism as it has always been — "pure and hard" — but made today for today's society (López García, 2012).

In an environment of political freedom and technological development that gives access to large quantities of data and information, the current era of _big data_ can become a golden age for journalism — and indeed, the world's best universities think so.

#### The network society and journalism

_Juan José Tamayo_


In the journalistic profession — and in academia — very few foresaw the great sociological transformation that the use of computing and its virtual realities would imply. Perhaps one of the few exceptions was Alvin Toffler, in his book _The Third Wave_. For the first time, "many-to-many" communication was discussed, along with the idea that media production would become "de-massified" and that personalized media communication would therefore emerge (Toffler, 1980). The concept of "mass communication," which journalists had so enjoyed (because of the power that control gave them), would no longer exist, and — most revolutionary in the book — the spectator would be able to participate in the newspapers they read and the television they watched. This raised a problem that Toffler was unable to foresee: if the spectator took part in newspapers and television, and since these have the function of informing about reality, that reality would be modified according to the tastes — or pressures — of the audience. For journalists, reality is the media representation that comes from mass media outlets.

When Toffler's book was published in the early 1980s, at least in communication schools it was seen as science fiction, similar to how physicists talk about time travel. Still in the mid and late 1990s, more credibility was given to the sociological aspects of the book (the disappearance of the traditional family, the increase in loneli


ness, "the culture without children," or the new child labor) than to its speculations about how mass media and their representation of reality would evolve and how, from there, society would change.

It was, among others, Manuel Castells's book _The Information Age_, published in the United States in 1996, that made the concept of the "network society" perceptible and envisioned that the world could be different within a few years.

At the beginning of the twenty-first century, when what is now called "web 1.0" was fully operational, the talk was of "Journalism and the Internet: new medium, old profession," and it was stressed that "it should be journalists who introduce journalistic information content into the network, the only way to guarantee quality, objectivity, truthfulness, and credibility in information" (Canga, 2001). At the same time, emphasis was placed on labels from the previous era, such as "brand prestige," and it was noted that "brand image is decisive at this moment for ensuring the reliability of information which, if good in print, will also be good online" (Edo, 2001).

Nevertheless, some in 2001 were already anticipating the radical change ahead and spoke of an "intellectual and technological metamorphosis" (De Pablos, 2001). However, by 2005 the treatment had changed: in an analysis of the web portals of free Spanish newspapers, the portal of the newspaper _Qué_ was described and highlighted as presenting itself as a space for personal communication, since its front page featured the best articles written by readers (Franco Álvarez, 2005). Selection was still controlled by the newspaper's editorial team, which decided which article was most interesting and featured the blog of the day at the top of the front page.

The selection of blogs was still in the hands of the newspaper's editors. That is, journalists still held the power to rank news (one of the most important in the profession), but the change was becoming visible: it was citizens who were making the news. "Citizen journalism" began to be discussed. Spanish media (such as meneame.net) were already producing their front pages exclusively based on readers' selections.


**1.1.** **Web 2.0 and journalism**


What happened between the vision of 2001 and that of 2005? Basically, the widespread emergence of what has been called the "web 2.0 philosophy," a term proposed in 2004 by Tim O'Reilly to describe a second phase in the history of the network based on user communities, where they interact through social networks, blogs, etc. What is most important about web 2.0, beyond easy access, is the possibility of modifying both the content and the structure of texts. Material can also be published online and participation can be both individual and collective. That is, web 2.0 allows citizens to become relevant protagonists of the information process, something previously reserved only for journalists. It is not the same to passively consult the _Encyclopaedia Britannica_ online (web 1.0) as it is to use Wikipedia (web 2.0), in which everyone can collaborate.

It is true that perhaps the pop singer Madonna might have a larger entry than Aristotle, but in the new era the hierarchies of knowledge dissolve. Appearing in the _Encyclopaedia Britannica_, which has 65,000 articles, is quite a distinction. But having an entry in Wikipedia — whose English-language edition had surpassed four million articles by March 2013 — is within anyone's reach (it is common, for example, for many Anglo-Saxon university professors to write their own entries). In Spanish alone it had 975,000 entries and, in total, across all languages, more than 22 million.

Knowledge no longer comes — as it has until now — from what a group of scholars judges to be "what must be known," but from what the virtual public opinion considers must be known. The shift is from the "dictatorship of experts" to the "wisdom of the crowd." In particular, Wikipedia has nearly 19 million registered users, of whom 136,000 contributed in the last 30 days, and this carries the drawback of an excess of non-hierarchical information. Universities in the West were created with the purpose of distilling, from all knowledge, that which is necessary to understand — hence the great difference between someone who has followed a regulated course of study at a university and someone who is self-taught: the former can classify


in order of importance; the latter almost always mixes everything up and does not rank by importance.

When the editors of the _Encyclopaedia Britannica_ commission the entry on Aristotle (almost certainly from a prestigious philosophy professor), the person who writes it has spent their entire life discarding and ranking what has been written about the Greek philosopher. But when it is done on Wikipedia, there are two possibilities: either the entry is literally copied — or inspired by — the _Encyclopaedia Britannica_ or a similar work, or each person contributes what interested them most. This is what happens with the entry on Madonna or Michael Jackson. And another piece of data to bear in mind (though we will return to it): the _Encyclopaedia Britannica_ pays the experts who write the entries; on Wikipedia, the work is done altruistically and for free. This is the new philosophy of the network: collaborative and disinterested work.

Just as in web 2.0 anyone can write the entry on Aristotle or Madonna in Wikipedia, journalism 2.0 can be produced directly by the citizen (although, obviously, journalists are also citizens) independently of large media corporations. New terms emerge: no longer "journalistic genres" but "transmedia storytelling," and we refer to the old networks of journalism companies as "corporate hybridization"; the increasingly outdated concept of "audiences" or even "public opinion" is replaced in web 2.0 by "collective intelligence" (Lévy, 1997) or "smart mobs" (Rheingold, 2003). No longer is talk of "editors" of journalistic texts (the old section heads or editors-in-chief), but of "beta reading," a concept whereby the reader can rework (edit) online the text written by journalists and publish their edited version.


**1.2.** **Journalism in virtual realities**


Traditional journalism experienced a qualitative leap when one of the world's most prestigious news agencies, Reuters, opened its own


avatar — "office and journalist" — in Second Life (a massively multiplayer online game, that is, a virtual reality space) with more than 16 million "residents" (players) in 2006. Can a journalist report on alternative realities? Is there an informational transfer from one reality to another? Should a journalist do it? Is that journalism?

and his avatar in Second Life
and his avatar in Second Life


What happened in Second Life with Reuters transformed the journalist's role forever. First, the agency did not "send" an intern, but an experienced journalist who worked at its London headquarters. But he was confronted with some of the most complex questions the profession has faced since its origins — and it has faced many — questions he was also unable to answer. Should he report to the Second Life community on what was happening inside it, or on what was happening outside it? Should he inform citizens of the actual reality of what was happening in the virtual one?

The answer was that everything was reported, which meant pragmatism both from a journalistic and a social perspective. These evolutions of what reality is were absorbed quite naturally. Reports ranged from "a poll in Second Life favors Obama over McCain" (a story that crossed over into real-world media) to an "exclusive Second Life interview" with Martin Sorrell, the well-known British businessman, following his participation in a very real setting: the Davos Economic Forum.


One of the images that most shocked the traditional profession was watching Second Life avatars sitting and listening to a television newscast reporting not on Second Life but on actual reality. Why don't they watch the news in actual reality and pass it through the filter of virtual reality?


Figure 1.2. Image of a news anchor avatar reporting to the virtual inhabitants of Second Life on what is happening in the real world. One of the iconic images of the twenty-first century


In that not-so-distant past — 2006, 2007, and 2008 — it seemed as if all of society was going to change and would live in the virtual world of Second Life: there were banks, supermarkets, people got married, there were lawyers... When Gaspar Llamazares, then leader of the Spanish political party Izquierda Unida, decided in 2007 to hold a campaign rally in Second Life, what mattered was not what he said, but that the rally received far more media coverage in real-world media than any rally he had held in actual reality. Llamazares was emulating French politicians Nicolas Sarkozy and Ségolène Royal. Second Life did not radically change society, as some


proposed, but it must be kept in mind when explaining journalism in the network society. In 2008 Reuters closed its office, but it proved that the world was not what it had been.

**1.3.** **Media and convergent culture**


One of the most attractive approaches to have emerged in recent times in attempting to explain what is happening in communication following the rise of the Internet (above all, of web 2.0) is the so-called "convergent culture paradigm," a term coined in 2006 by Henry Jenkins, Professor of Media Studies at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). This convergent culture would be the sum of three sub-processes related at different levels to the web 2.0 philosophy: media convergence, participatory culture, and collective intelligence (Jenkins, 2006).

The first approach to the phenomenon will be described through a "case study" that has been widely analyzed (Rosenzweig, 2003; Jenkins, 2006). In the autumn of 2001, following the September 11 attacks in New York,


Dino Ignacio, a Filipino-American high school student, designed a digital collage with Photoshop. With all the innocence of a kid, he combined the image of Bin Laden (considered the mastermind of the attacks in the media) with that of the grumpy Bert, a character from the globally distributed children's series _Sesame Street_ (launched in 1970 and still running today). He posted a series of images titled "Bert Is Evil" on his personal website. In reality, it was just a joke, but the blog spread from one place to another, increasing its ranking in search engines. Bert and his friend Ernie are global icons for all Westerners born since the mid-1960s — that is, the majority of Internet users.


Figure 1.4. Bert and Bin Laden in Dino Ignacio's collage


This small success was possibly the reason why an editor in Bangladesh found and selected the collage when he searched the web for images of Bin Laden to print on posters and T-shirts for anti-American protesters, who would carry them at demonstrations in Pakistan against U.S. policy after 9/11. A localized version of _Sesame Street_ can be seen in Pakistan, but the characters Ernie and Bert do not appear in it. The demonstrations were covered by CNN (one of the world's most important traditional television networks) and broadcast globally. Representatives of _Children's Television Workshop_, creators of the _Sesame Street_ series, watched in outrage as furious protesters carried posters bearing the photograph of Bin Laden alongside the Bert puppet and threatened legal action: "We are outraged by the deplorable and disgusting use of our characters. Those responsible should be ashamed. We are exploring all legal options to stop this abuse," their lawyers said in the media. CNN defended itself by arguing that they were only broadcasting newsworthy images of reality. And those demonstrations, with the images of Bert alongside Bin Laden, were reality.


Figure 1.5. Anti-U.S. demonstration in Pakistan, with Bert in the lower right corner


Immediately, numerous fan forums emerged jokingly linking terrorists to _Sesame Street_ characters. Furthermore, Dino


Ignacio became a cult figure on the Internet, which was also news in traditional media. The publicity grew so much that so too did Ignacio's concern, leading him to dismantle his website and post the following message: "I think this has come too close to reality (...). Bert Is Evil." His personal fantasy had become reality. What started as a joke on his website became worldwide news; then an international crisis between the United States and the Middle East, but also a corporate war between the two giants of global communication. All of this was reflected both in traditional media and, above all, in virtual ones. Subsequently, the unwitting protagonist of the joke was transformed into a cult figure, and since 2003, when the case was published as a "scientific study" in the prestigious journal _American Historical Review_ (Rosenzweig, 2003), it became a first-rate scientific subject, published again from this new angle in the media. This is the world of convergent culture, in which traditional communication schemas, such as Jakobson's sender-message-receiver model, are radically distorted.


_1.3.1._ _Components of convergent culture_

The circuits that Bert has traveled are a consequence of globalization, but also of the convergence and synergies of the media in the network society. What enables this paradigm shift? The components of convergent culture, which are three:


_A) Media convergence_


The new meaning of convergence, according to Jenkins, refers not so much to the convergence of media platforms — as it was defined at the beginning of the twenty-first century — but above all to a cultural change, led by the Network, in which media consumers seek out other information, establish new connections between dispersed media content, and the spectators-users-end-receivers themselves become senders.


That is, convergence does not take place in media devices, however sophisticated their technology may be, but in the minds of senders and receivers. Convergence occurred in the mind of the teenage Dino Ignacio. His reality is a blend of realities: virtual, cultural, and actual.


_B) Participatory culture_


The other new concept necessary for understanding the convergent culture paradigm is "participatory culture," opposed to the old schema of the "passive media spectator." Now we can no longer speak of media producers and consumers as separate entities performing distinct functions. We are all now potential active participants.


_C) Collective intelligence_


The final term of interest here is "collective intelligence" (Lévy, 1997). This concept starts from the premise that none of us can know everything — especially in an interconnected world in which there is more information than the human brain can encompass. However, each of us knows something about something, so it is best to leverage cyber social networks (now more advanced with web 2.0 and 3.0) to build knowledge in which we all share our experiences, join the pieces together, and shed light on new and more accurate information. These conversations on the network create a "murmur" that is increasingly valued in the media industry and will define its future in the coming years. This will be discussed in the next chapter.

Obviously, all of these aspects of the new paradigm can be applied across the entire arc of communication — both mass communication and elite communication (the kind experts have with each other) — but studies are scarce. Some (still very few) interesting works in this field come from the world of cinema


and television, where, for example, the increasingly frequent correspondences between video games and films are analyzed — how one becomes the other and vice versa. Other approaches address the interconnections between film scripts (or television series) and fan clubs grouped in social networks on the Internet, who now pressure writers or develop complementary plots that they spread across the Network. The famous inscription at the entrance to the University of Southern California in Los Angeles (the best school for training filmmakers), which reads: "Reality ends here" — referring to the fact that fiction production is taught there — may become obsolete in a few years because the power of the cyber-murmur will be increasingly important in the world of pure fiction.


**1.4.** **The future of journalism in the network society**


In 2014 the BBC, one of the most prestigious media outlets in the world, published the _Future of News_ report (Harding, 2014) on what the future of the journalistic profession will look like. Some of its conclusions are as follows:


1. There will be more technology, it will be cheaper, and it will be embedded in all our possessions: this is the Internet of Things. This will lead to access to media from any place and in any form. The report believes this will imply greater consumption of video. It believes people will consume more images.
2. By the 2020s the report holds that television programs will be participatory, the viewer will be the protagonist of the story, and that by 2030 everyone will watch television through the Internet.
3. The journalism of the future will be local. The BBC believes that nearby news coverage must be improved — one of the areas that has suffered most from the Internet. On the other hand, regarding international journalism, it bets on journalists going to the scene of events. According to the report "being there counts, and counts a lot."


4. Collaborative journalism will emerge: stories will be found when one interacts with readers. Not only photographs will be requested, but points of view and topics that readers consider should be published.
5. _Big data_ will be an essential part of journalism in the network society. Governments will increasingly publish data, and in those data, as we will see, there are stories. On the other hand, encryption technologies will become increasingly important for protecting information. Users will increasingly give more data to companies but, according to the report, these will improve their customers' lives and make decisions on their behalf.
6. One of the most significant findings is that "the middle will die": the 800-word article does not necessarily work in the digital world; articles that are either very short or in-depth are more likely to succeed.
7. The network society fosters the era of "transparent journalism": reporters will be able to explain to audiences what they know (and what they do not know) by publishing the transcripts of their interviews and citing, with increasing frequency, other journalists.
8. Media outlets must be prepared to offer optimized alerts for text messages, apps, and small screens: mobile video, audio, personalized newsletters, in-depth analysis, and press summaries.


The most notable thing the report says is that Journalism with a capital J will remain important: reporting on what happens, uncovering original stories, keeping watch on power, and, most importantly, analyzing, explaining, and contextualizing the information society generates every day. On the other hand, the report provides interesting reflections — and data — on the current state of journalism and the media. This data must be taken into account when managing what kind of journalism should be produced for the network society:


1. First, people read (mainly) headlines. According to a survey conducted for the BBC among British citizens, 59%


of the country's Internet users said they had "glanced at" headlines in the online press the previous week, while only 43% said they had read long-form stories. The world is divided between those who skim the news and those who seek it out. The gap between the two groups is widening.
2. The journalist's most direct competitor is no longer another journalist: it is the subject of the story. Now politicians, celebrities, and companies communicate directly with the public, bypassing the intermediary.
3. The Internet does not keep everyone informed. In fact, the problems of information inequality, disinformation, and polarization are growing.
4. The future is not mobile: the present already is. In 2014, 30% of those under 45 said they received news via mobile phone, and that figure will continue to grow.

#### _Big data_ and virtual public opinion

_Juan José Tamayo_


By 2020 the network society will have generated 40 zettabytes of information, which — according to scientists at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology — is equivalent to 57 times the number of grains of sand on all the world's beaches. Every citizen generates hundreds of data points per day: Facebook "likes," their own comments on social networks, time spent on news and media, the destinations of plane, train, and hotel tickets they select, or purchases made by credit card. These billions of data points generated daily offer a vast amount of information.

On the other hand, every day governments and companies have a greater interest in publishing data — ranging from pollution levels or causes of death by neighborhood to educational, research, or business-type results by area. When these data are public, we speak of _open data_. As will be seen later, political and economic power publishes data not because of a sudden burst of transparency, but because the analysis of these data can generate very important business results


and public policy management outcomes.

The term _big data_ does not refer only to raw data, but — above all — to the techniques by which interesting conclusions can be drawn from those data, which are in some cases openly available (_open data_). And it is in this context that journalists too must know these techniques (or work with computer scientists who use them) in order to obtain information that is relevant to society from that data.

Computer scientists know how to handle data, but journalists are the ones who know how to ask questions so that the data tell a story. It is journalists who, in contact with political and economic power, use their instinct to organize data and uncover information that the powerful perhaps prefer to keep hidden among trillions of numbers and that should not surface as a journalistic headline. And it is also journalists who know how to turn cold numbers into a compelling story that captures readers.

This data processing is an emerging industry, and many believe this sector could be the origin of the next industrial revolution. As will be seen later, the victory of U.S. President Barack Obama was largely due to the analysis of the profiles and preferences of volunteers and potential voters. But journalistically, the possibilities of _big data_ are immense: we can determine whether there are neighborhoods where residents die more frequently from a particular condition and cross this with specific pollution levels, or examine the types of crimes by area

and region.

One of the first applications of mass data processing, or _big data_, to the social sciences was evidenced in a 2009 article in the journal _Nature_: it demonstrated that, based on Google searches, flu outbreaks in the U.S. could be determined with greater accuracy than through the usual surveys and statistics from medical centers and the national health system (Ginsburg _et al._, 2009). It had previously been used in astronomy and genetics, but the combination of large quantities of data and computing tools to monitor them and draw conclusions is still very new in the field of journalism.

In recent years this state of affairs has changed considerably. _Big data_ has also found its place in journalism (Mayer-Schönberger and Cukier, 2013), especially in leading media outlets, with results that have so far been genuinely spectacular. The major American and European media outlets have opened


_open data_ sections, with the express desire to integrate data journalism and work with large volumes of information into their standard professional practices. This and other changes are contributing to the emergence of an audience interested in interactive access to quality information and, in some cases, also to reviving the battered information business.


**2.1.** **What is** _**big data?**_


_Big data_ is not so much a matter of the quantity of data as, above all, the capacity to search, aggregate, and cross-reference data to obtain new data. Large quantities of data have existed since the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, when many countries began producing censuses and land registries. The U.S., for example, used a precursor to computing — a kind of punched card — as early as the nineteenth century (from 1890 onward) to improve the collection of population census data. And in the 1960s enormous computers were already in use. What is novel in the twenty-first century is the personal computer or mobile device, used on a mass scale by the population, globally and continuously connected. This generates even more data which, with advances in computing, make it possible to write a different kind of story.

_Big data_ can be defined from three perspectives (Boyd and Crawford, 2012):


1. Technological: computational power and algorithmic efficiency are maximized to obtain, analyze, and compare large quantities of data.
2. Analytical: seeking patterns on a large scale with which trends can even be established and future behavior predicted.
3. Mythological: there is a widespread belief that large datasets offer a superior form of knowledge. It is even believed that ideas are generated that were impossible to obtain before the era of _big data_ — ideas that, moreover, backed by numbers, carry an aura of truth, objectivity, and precision.


It must not be forgotten that _big data_ is a technological advance and, like any technology, can be understood from a utopian or dystopian standpoint. Its proponents argue that it offers potential to solve social problems and address areas as complex and diverse as cancer research, terrorism, or climate change. But a significant part of society fears that these data will be used to monitor every daily act of the individual and that we will live in a kind of "Big Brother": allowing the invasion of privacy, the reduction of civil liberties, or the increase of state or corporate control. That is, like any emerging technology, it combines currents of hope and fear.

_Big data_ is a very broad term. It has been used successfully in the natural sciences and in recent times has also begun to be introduced in the social sciences, particularly in areas of economics, advertising, and politics. In fact, the political scientists specializing in _big data_ analysis were responsible for Barack Obama's 2012 electoral campaign.

However, the application of _big data_ to journalism is genuinely recent from a research perspective. In 2013 the first international conference on _big data_ and journalism was held. It took place at the University of Oxford, under the auspices of the prestigious Reuters Institute for Journalism. The conference was called "_Big Data, Big Ideas for Media_." The title says it all. There it was stated that _big data_ "is the new oil, but like oil, it is worthless when crude. It takes a series of technicians to work with the smelly crude to turn it into gasoline, plastic bags, dyes, or a necklace." That work of turning the crude — millions of data points — into a product with added value that can serve society is the journalist's role. The Deputy Editor of _The Economist_, Neelay Patel, argued at that meeting that creativity must always prevail over mere data. This is where big data meets journalism and where enormous possibilities for research and business arise.

Another important aspect is the difference between _big data_ and statistics. There is a tendency to think that statistical sampling is a kind of immutable foundation like the principles of geometry or the laws of gravity, but this is not true. Statistical sampling is less than a century old and arose because, being unable to study all the data, it was necessary to develop mathe


matical theories to determine how many data points were needed to obtain a picture of reality without having to measure all the data. However, as Mayer-Schönberger and Cukier argue, "resorting to a random sample in the age of big data is like clinging to a horse whip in the era of the combustion engine. We can still use sampling in some contexts, but it need not be — and in fact will not be — the predominant way we analyze large datasets. Increasingly, we will be able to go for all of it" (Mayer-Schönberger and Cukier, 2013: 47).


**2.2.** **Ethical and critical questions about** _**big data**_


As noted, journalism is not only a profession with epic dimensions — from war correspondents to Watergate — but, above all, with ethics. In this sense, journalists, both in their professional practice and when engaged in social research, must consider a series of questions worth reflecting on before using this technology (Boyd and Crawford, 2012). They are as follows:


_2.2.1._ _Big data changes the definition of knowledge_


When Henry Ford "invented" the system of mass production, standardizing machinery and ultra-specializing labor with the notion of the "assembly line," he changed not only the way of producing cars, but also the society and politics of his era. In a sense, the twentieth century is defined by that type of factory, which meant a new way of understanding labor, human relations at work, and even the proliferation of dormitory towns near those factories. And, as the philosopher of science Bruno Latour argues, if the instruments — that is, the technology — change, then the entire social theory that explains the era in which that technology is used is also modified. And in this sense, _big data_ can change the way we understand society and human relations, when everything human can easily be quantified.


Chris Anderson, editor-in-chief of _Wired_ magazine ("the bible of computing"), argues that "this is a world [the world of _big data_] where massive amounts of data and applied mathematics replace any other methodology that might previously have been used. There is no longer any need for theories of human behavior, from linguistics to sociology. Forget taxonomy, ontology, or psychology. Who cares why people do what they do? The key is that they do it, and now we can track and measure it with a fidelity never seen before. With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves" (Anderson, 2008).

However, this would mean that the models by which _big data_ arrives at knowledge would first need to be clarified. Its conclusions cannot become orthodoxy or new paradigms without a very deep philosophical study of what _big data_ means.


_2.2.2._ _Are numbers objective?_


_Big data_ offers the humanities — among which Journalism also belongs — a new avenue for claiming the status of quantitative science and objective methodology, as the social sciences have already been claiming. However, as also occurs in the social sciences, working with numerical data, whether in large or small quantities, remains subjective and can in no way aspire to become the truth. It is true that, as Boyd and Crawford (2012) recall, many academics still make the error of thinking that qualitative researchers interpret narratives while quantitative researchers produce facts. This, however, is not true. The way of obtaining data, of quantifying what is human, is always subjective and depends on what we measure and how we measure it. _Big data_ only reinforces the idea that we must know very well the measure of that datum and its source. Moreover, the algorithms for cross-referencing or searching data are also subjective and depend on what the algorithm's creator thinks.

On the other hand, _big data_ is prone to apophenia, which is the pernicious tendency to see patterns, connections, or both in random events


and meaningless data. For example, Leinweber (2007) demonstrated that by using data mining techniques we can prove a spurious correlation — a term we will return to — between butter production in Bangladesh and changes in the Standard & Poor's 500 Index, one of the most important stock market indices in the United States and considered the most representative indicator of the real state of the market.

Apophenia is a professional and deontological corruption consisting of deliberately distorting reality by linking aspects that have nothing to do with each other in order to support far-fetched hypotheses. This professional degeneration is widely used in what is misleadingly called "mystery journalism." It is not journalism, but entertainment fiction presented as non-fiction to hook unsuspecting viewers or readers. One of its rhetorical tools of persuasion is the use of biased data to support paranormal or religious explanations that make no sense, but that attract enormous audiences of citizens without scientific training who believe any nonsense.


_2.2.3._ _Quantity is not necessarily better than quality_


One of the most frequent errors in the emerging field of _big data_ is confusing the quantity of data with their quality. For example, measurement methods favor research into messages and connections among users of the social network Twitter. But their conclusions have serious methodological flaws and should only be extended to that sphere. Not all of society uses Twitter, nor is there a known individual behind every Twitter account (in many cases these are fake profiles, companies, and even algorithms that produce content automatically). Moreover, not all journalists and researchers have access to the inner workings of that data, so companies have more information about the sample than researchers do. And it must not be forgotten that in the humanities and social sciences — and, of course, in journalism — _small data_ often matter more: interviews or opinions from relevant individuals who explain reality to us better, or, for example, a report


or investigation — about a single anonymous person who represents
a particular problem better than any dataset.

One of the differences between the humanities and the social sciences is that the latter developed an intellectual justification for passing off random sampling as a better description of reality than what a philosopher might produce. By introducing a statistic or a survey they turned the old humanities into social sciences. But it is not necessarily true that the latter describe society better than the former. We know an enormous amount about the history of Rome without needing to resort to surveys or statistics. Narrative has always been important for understanding what is human.


_2.2.4._ _Out of context, big data loses its meaning_


When one analyzes, for example, a Facebook friends list or a Twitter follower list, one finds an agglomeration of entities ranging from friends, work colleagues, and family members to politicians, celebrities, and companies. The results of such analyses of large datasets must be handled with great care, because one might conclude that a famous person interests us more than a family member, when reality contradicts that assertion. Human relationships are not generic or abstract and have different levels of concreteness that _big data_ systems may fail to understand. If we analyzed telephone bills and the duration of calls, we might conclude that we spend more time talking with professional colleagues than with our partner and children. Yet one cannot write the headline that citizens are less interested in their families than in their colleagues.

_2.2.5._ _Easy access does not imply that it is ethical or legal_


Article 18.4 of the Spanish Constitution, approved in the now distant year of 1978, nevertheless establishes that: "The law shall limit the use of computing


to guarantee the honor and personal and family privacy of citizens and the full exercise of their rights." In 1978 the Internet as we now know it did not yet exist, and still less the capacity of _big data_, but the legislator already foresaw that possibility, and the use of computing to access the private sphere — at least in Spain — is unconstitutional.

The practice of the journalistic profession — and also ethics in the social sciences — has long debated what is public and what is private. There is a difference between sitting in a public park reading a book or kissing one's partner with the intention of going unnoticed, and using the park deliberately to draw attention — with, for example, a musical performance. A journalist or social researcher must understand that there is a difference between what is legal and what is moral. For example, it is not ethical to spy on a citizen's public behavior: who they meet, where they walk, what rubbish they put out, what they eat, etc. It is true that the law only protects the secrecy of communications, but journalism and the social researcher should not spy on citizens' behavior without their consent. If this is obvious in the real world, it does not seem quite so clear in the virtual one, where all our actions leave a trace. All of these aspects must be analyzed in depth before undertaking an investigation or a report using mass data techniques obtained from the Internet.


_2.2.6._ _Big data generates new social divides_


As noted, not all data are free. Companies such as Google, Twitter, or Facebook, among many others, own their users' data, and it is these companies that decide which data they share freely, which they sell, and which they keep for themselves. As Manovich (2011) argues, "an anthropologist working for Facebook or a sociologist at Google has access to data that the rest of the world's academics will not have." Therefore, the social research coming from those companies is more complete than that from universities.

On the other hand, another emerging trend is the agreement of those companies with elite universities. Only their professors and students will have


access to that data and will know how to handle it, acquiring key competencies for the twenty-first century that the majority of the world's universities will not be able to teach.

A gender bias may also exist: working with _big data_ requires extensive knowledge of computer engineering, mathematics, and statistics — disciplines in which women are still a minority. In fact, in these fields the number of women, rather than increasing, has declined in recent years. In fields such as the humanities and social sciences, whoever designs the research, formulates the questions, and analyzes the answers holds power over discourse and establishes the paradigms. Women's access to the humanities and social sciences may be interrupted if these areas suddenly conclude that only what has substantial mathematical and computing treatment counts. The same applies if the journalistic profession — practiced at the writing level predominantly by women — suddenly demands high levels of mathematical and computing knowledge for producing content. That is, it could provoke a regression in areas where gender equality had been established.

Nonetheless, a technology — and _big data_ is one — with so many features and opportunities will not be stopped because few women have access to it. It is the responsibility of governments to foster an educational system that incentivizes greater access for women to the natural sciences — physics, chemistry, and mathematics — and engineering.


**2.3.** **The cyber-murmur and its quantification**


The previous chapter addressed how the murmur of social networks is important for creators of fiction. But obviously, each day it will also be increasingly decisive in the realm of mass communication, including journalism. In fact, it constitutes the new "public sphere" — a crucial concept in modern political philosophy. Defined, among others, by the great communication theorist Jürgen Habermas, the "public sphere" refers to those spaces in which the established political process gains or loses its legitimacy. Those spaces can be both empirical


and utopian (Habermas, 1962). In this new era of the network society, it is clear that a digital or virtual public sphere is taking shape every day.

Can this cyber-murmur be quantified? In 2011 the network contained more than 5 billion websites. If one considers websites, blogs, social networks, and all the comments produced, it is estimated that each month information grows at a rate of 30 terabytes. This is an astonishing quantity: for example, the Library of Congress of the United States, considered the largest in the world — which collects a large part of the books published in any era, language, and location on the planet — stores information that would amount to about 50 terabytes. That is, every two months the web produces more information than that generated — in books — by human beings since the beginning of time.

Obviously, it is very difficult to determine what is being said on the web, but it is necessary to know. In this sense, companies that analyze the cyber-murmur are becoming more common every day. Knowing what Internet users say about a brand, product, or service on blogs or social networks is the objective of tools such as _BuzzMetrics_ (_buzz_ in English means 'hum' or 'buzz'). A study carried out in 2008 by Nielsen Online (one of the auditors of web traffic) to assess the Spanish market in its objective of launching _BuzzMetrics_ in that country found that 44% of Spain's 20 million Internet users expressed opinions through the Network.

In total, they generate 26% of online content, compared with 18% from corporations and 12% from traditional media. In 2008 _BuzzMetrics_ analyzed 70 million blogs worldwide and 100,000 communities. More than 3 billion comments circulate on the Network. Tools like _BuzzMetrics_ are based on a set of mathematical algorithms that continuously regenerate information combining variables such as comment volume, the audience accessing them, virality, topics, perception (whether favorable or not), the emotion or sentiment of consumers, sources (blogs, forums, or videos), impact on sales, and the author.

Nielsen's executives — the owner of _BuzzMetrics_ — note that the companies most likely to request their services are those in automotive, pharmaceuticals, technology product manufacturers, banks, airlines, and television


channels. All of them value cyber-murmur monitoring and, depending on what is being discussed — that is, based on _BuzzMetrics_ reports (or similar tools, such as _BuzzAudit_, _Media Predict_, etc.) — they modify everything from the shape of a new car's trunk to — as mentioned — the script of a series in which the villain will not die if the Network likes them.

In the United States, politicians increasingly use these reports instead of opinion polls, which are far more expensive and — according to some — less effective, especially among those under 45. Over time, the average age of Internet users will rise until it encompasses the entire population. Obviously, these systems can be used wherever the majority of the population connects and actively participates on the Internet, so it is anticipated that "cyber-murmurometry" tools will have growing influence in the developed world: they will shape the digital public sphere.

From the standpoint of journalism in the network society, _BuzzMetrics_ — or similar tools — and their reports are being consulted daily by politicians, journalists, policy-makers, etc. Parties — especially during campaigns — increasingly tailor their political message based on the comments that virtual public opinion hosts on the Network. Among other reasons, because cyber-murmur reports can be produced with far more immediacy than an opinion poll, allowing the discourse to be adjusted almost daily.

Once politicians are elected, their decisions may also be affected — not so much by what traditional media publish, but rather by what "cyber-murmurometric" reports tell them about the measures they intend to apply or have already applied. Until now they relied on opinion polls, but — as some advocates of these computing techniques argue — these reports are based on traffic statistics from millions of users. In any case, what matters is that virtual comments in the network society do not fall on deaf ears: they are taken into account, and each day they will carry more weight in real-world decision-making. The creators of _BuzzMetrics_ state that the tool has the capacity to identify biased messages from orchestrated campaigns. They add that these are very easy to detect and tend to backfire against the sender of the content.


However, the truth is that this virtual sociology is changing many professional profiles — among them, that of communicators who frantically add comments on blogs, social networks, and virtual forums about hotels, films, cars, political parties, etc. They create the virtual word-of-mouth murmur, indispensable for a product to succeed in the real market. If a brand fails to attract enough online forum writers/commentators, or if despite those efforts the detected murmur is dangerous for the product (the tool _Buzz Threat Tracker_ detects such threats), companies or individuals — politicians, actors, etc. — fight that negative message with still-effective tools ranging from television advertisements to press conferences for traditional media.

Digital or computational communication (still lacking a clear name) is an emerging discipline. Everything done on the Network is recorded and all it takes is algorithms — and communication experts who make sense of the results — to find out not only what society is like as a whole, but what is even more sensitive: defining the individual's privacy down to the most intimate level.

The network society, in this sense, is a gold mine: statistics on visited web pages allow prediction of age, sex, profession, educational level, and even numerous personality traits. The playlists one saves on Spotify or iTunes reveal musical preferences, and the language used in tweets or the list of Facebook friends (in a digital re-edition of the old saying "tell me who you walk with...") allow one to know an individual better than they know themselves, for they predict not only conscious decisions, but even unconscious ones.

In 2013 a study concluded that from the "likes" individuals enter on Facebook, one could deduce ethnicity, sexual orientation, or political ideology (Kosinski _et al._, 2013). The model correctly predicts whether a person is homosexual or heterosexual in 88% of cases, whether they are of African or Caucasian origin in 95% of cases, and whether they lean left or right politically in 85% of cases. Other attributes can also be deduced, though with less precision, such as personality traits, intelligence, degree of happiness, parents' marital status, or drug use. Without doubt, this type of research


will be one of the emerging issues of the future — not only in academia, but above all in the business and professional sectors.


**2.4.** **Politics and the virtual public sphere**


One of the most relevant issues in political communication is how to motivate citizens to: a) participate in elections, b) choose a candidate, and c) support them financially; that is, how to increase public commitment to active participation in political life (_American Political Science Association_, 2004; Macedo _et al._, 2005; Brady, Schlozman, and Verba, 1999). Some researchers have demonstrated a direct relationship between a citizen becoming a sympathizer (and contributing both financially and socially to promote a candidate) and an increase in electoral participation (Boatright, 2011; Boatright and Malbin, 2005; Malbin, 2008; Wilcox, 2001). The relationship between financial contributions to parties and participation as volunteers on electoral committees in the United States has also been widely studied (Biersack and Vray, 2005; Burbank, Hrebenar, and Benedict, 2008; Currinder, 2003; Wright, 2003).

On the other hand, for a long time it has been argued that one of the most important variables for explaining why citizens orient their vote one way or another is partisan identity (Campbell _et al._, 1960; Brody and Rothenberg, 1988; Weisberg and Smith, 1991). However, talk of partisan identities is increasingly diminishing and identity is described as a much broader, more inclusive, and more powerful construct:


Identity as a motivating process of voting orientation is much more comprehensive and diverse than partisan identity, encompassing ideological identity, class identity (classist), identity of geographic origin (geographic), racial identity, gender identity, religious identity, programmatic identity (of project or cause), identity of interests, generational identity, and even identity of a curricular and even affective character. (Valdés Zepeda, Huerta Franco, and Vergara Ochoa, 2012).


Although the image of politicians on television and recognition by the general public remain very important (Canel, 1998), it is clear that the digital age has changed the relationship between candidates and traditional media that existed in the twentieth century.


_2.4.1._ _Big data and the "persuadability index"_

The potential of _big data_ technology applied to political communication and electoral strategies is based on the following premises, developed above all during the 2012 campaign for the election of U.S. President Barack Obama:


1. The technological capacity to collect every imaginable piece of data on large population groups and then distribute them in a highly segmented manner: the unemployed, employment by sector, educational level, schools attended, sex, neighborhoods where people were born and where they live, age, and film, music, or literary preferences.
2. All of this must be entered into large databases: much of it automatically and some filled in by the thousands of volunteers working for each candidate. From there, _focus groups_ are organized — as was done in both Obama's and Romney's 2012 campaigns — on how and whom to persuade; that is, each segmented sector of the population is assigned a "persuadability level" (a ranking of who is easiest to persuade) and "sensitive topics for that person." They are then called and pressed on those issues — in other words, they are promised that the candidate in question will address their concerns.
3. _Data mining_ was also used to maximize advertising investment in traditional media (especially in Romney's campaign, because it had a smaller budget), so that less investment yielded more efficiency. Costly blanket advertisements for the whole population no longer work. In 2012 Romney's strategists defined _microtargets_ in traditional media, and data mining made it possible to determine how many people,


for example, were watching television at any given moment and to produce a "sentiment analysis" of what specific groups of viewers think when they see a candidate or hear their speech.
4. The response of social networks to the candidate's appearance in specific media outlets is also measured — that is, what is the reaction of Twitter or Facebook (and the profile of their users) to each part of the candidates' speeches.
5. If broken down by specific programs and time slots, it can be concluded that at a given time on a specific channel there is a significant percentage of viewers or listeners with a high persuadability index (easily persuadable) to vote for a specific candidate. What the campaign director does is place an advertisement with the right message on that outlet at that time.
6. In statistical sentiment analysis, it is necessary to assess whether the volume of discussion — via "cyber-murmurometry" — is high but negative, or lower but positive. A "high volume" alone is not sufficient: if there is a great deal of discussion — a candidate is well known — but it generates rejection, that is worse than another candidate who is more unknown and indifferent to the audience.
7. Another interesting effect that was analyzed was whether the same advertisement provoked different reactions in different cities and among various segmented groups — but more on the basis of occupation or educational level — or specific degree studied — than on the traditional criteria of gender, race, or religion. Consequently, campaign advertisements were highly localized and carried different messages depending on the geographic area, the outlet, or the time at which they were broadcast.


_2.4.2._ _Steps in organizing electoral campaigns_
_in the era of big data_


The 2012 electoral campaign in the United States also involved the development of campaign organization models that take into account the potential of _big data_. The patterns can be summarized in a series of steps.


1. First, electoral headquarters recruited, in addition to journalists, mathematicians, statisticians, and engineers. Even other profiles that had until then been somewhat removed from political matters: atmospheric physicists, astrophysicists, and biochemists specializing in DNA sequencing. The campaigns were not suddenly interested in science, but were seeking the capabilities of those professionals to handle immense quantities of data. Together with the journalists they drew conclusions.
2. The system for capturing data on potential voters began on the campaign website — created specifically for Obama's reelection — where volunteers were sought. Those wishing to participate logged into the website using their Facebook credentials, via _Facebook Connect_. In this way, the volunteer was sharing all the information they publicly stored on Facebook with Obama's database.
3. Joe Rospars, chief digital strategist for the 2012 Obama campaign (and the 2008 campaign), revealed at the _Social Media Week_ 2012 forum in New York that his objective was to design technology that would encourage potential voters to engage with the candidate. This was achieved by knowing everything from age, sex, and prior voting history to the preferences, dreams, thoughts, friends, decisions, and employment or personal situation of each of the potential voters (190 million), information obtained from the data they had shared on Facebook and other social networks.
4. In addition, data mining made it possible to identify who serves as an opinion leader among their virtual friends, so that greater effort would be made to reach them. This is what Teddy Goff, digital director of Obama's reelection campaign, calls "persuasion through interaction."
5. These data can be used not only to send personalized messages through social networks, but also to identify neighborhoods based on their voting orientation and produce nearly personalized cable television advertisements; also to attract potential financial supporters. It must not be forgotten that the 2012 campaign


anticipated 1.3 million donors, 98% of whom would contribute less than $250.

#### _Open data_ and journalism

_Juan José Tamayo_


_Big data_ is useless, at least in the journalistic environment, if that data is not open — that is, if it is not free and publicly accessible. This characteristic is called _open data_. This is also an entire work philosophy — and even a life philosophy — that advocates that absolutely all data should be freely consultable and reorganizable in order to obtain new information. One of the foremost representatives of the _open data_ philosophy is the _Open Knowledge Foundation_, a foundation based in numerous countries, including Spain.

The objective of the _Open Knowledge Foundation_ is to change the world through data. The definition of _open_ refers to the fact that content must be accessible to all citizens, not just professionals such as journalists or researchers. But they go further: they consider data to be the building blocks of knowledge and that if these are freely accessible, collective intelligence and participatory culture can improve those datasets and generate new ones of greater added value.

All of this freely accessible _big data_ information is stored in a virtual (not physical) cloud called _cloud computing_. According to Cisco System's _Global Cloud Report (2010–2015)_, data traffic in 2015 doubles that of 2010, amounting to 4.8 million zettabytes.


An iPhone has more computing power than NASA had in 1969 when it sent a man to the Moon.


Figure 3.1. Websites of the _Open Knowledge Foundation_
in English and Spanish


The characteristics of this type of database are:


1. _Availability and access:_ the data must not only be available as a whole, but must be easily downloadable from the Internet and must be in a format that allows modification and restructuring.
2. _Reuse and redistribution:_ the data must be provided under conditions allowing reuse and redistribution, permitting mixing with other datasets. They must also be machine-readable.
3. _Universal participation:_ every citizen must be able to use, reuse, and redistribute the data. Furthermore, although the data must be freely accessible, they may be used for both commercial and non-commercial purposes.


- _Types of open data_


There are countless types of open data with potential uses and applications. The most common are:


1. Cultural: data from cultural works (catalogues of galleries, libraries, archives, or museums).
2. Scientific: data coming from scientific research, from astronomy to zoology.
3. Financial: government accounts (expenditures and revenues) and information about financial markets (stocks, bonds, etc.).
4. Statistical: data produced by statistical offices, such as the census and key socioeconomic indicators.
5. Climatic: meteorological information used to understand and predict weather and climate, as well as historical records.
6. Environmental: information related to the natural environment, the presence and level of pollutants, air quality, rivers, and seas.
7. Transport: timetables, routes, delay statistics.


This chapter will describe some portals. Obviously, there are many more than those listed here. They have been selected because they represent interesting models of the types of data and sources a journalist might use.


**3.1.** **The Datahub (http://datahub.io/es)**


The Datahub portal is one of the most powerful open data platforms. It does not belong to a government organization, but to the _Open Knowledge Foundation_. The most innovative aspect is the CKAN tool — free, open-source software developed by the _Open Knowledge Foundation_ that enables the management and publication of data collections. This free software is also used by local and national governments,


research centers, and organizations that need to collect large quantities of data. The first version was launched in 2006 and the latest in 2013.


Figure 3.2. Datahub website


Its search engine is one of the most powerful — not only for browsing and finding specific information, but the software also enables preview visualization through maps, charts, or tables. This makes it widely used by researchers and journalists, but also by citizens who, in the era of collective intelligence and participatory culture, have much to contribute to journalism in the network society.

What is interesting about Datahub is that it not only allows searching for already registered or published data, but that the CKAN tool enables the creation and management of new groups of datasets. It also allows updating the databases or groups of greatest interest to the user. Registration is required and there is a complete user guide (at demo.ckan.org) for learning how to use CKAN. The most interesting applications for journalism concern the possibility of creating and managing specific datasets based on the topic of a news report. This can be for a collective project involving several journalists or for an individual reporter.


_**3.2.**_ _**Open Government Data**_ **(http://datacatalogs.org/)**

This is possibly the most important _open data_ site at the moment. It is a global repository that is continually updated. _3.2.1._ _Why should governments offer their data openly?_


1. _Transparency._ Citizens have the right to know everything from how public resources are spent to, for example, what the pollution levels are in their neighborhood and, above all, to be able to compare them with other areas. That is, governments have a duty to share with citizens all the data they hold about


them or that may affect them in various ways. The _Open Government Data_ portal notes that transparency consists not only of free access to data, but of the capacity to analyze and visualize them.
2. _Social and commercial value data_. Many companies can be created from the analysis of data. That is, _open data_ is a first-rate tool for generating business wealth and quality employment.
3. _Participatory governance_. A genuine democracy implies consulting citizens on multiple matters — and not only every four years when elections are held. The aim is not merely to know how one is governed (a role until now assigned to traditional media), but to participate in governance.


**3.3.** **U.S. data: _Data.gov_ (http://www.data.gov)**


As noted, every day more governments and institutions will offer open data — not because they have a sudden attack of transparency, but because, if they do not, they turn their backs on the enormous business potential and, above all, the improvement of public policies that comes from combining _big data_ with _open data_. One of the first to be established, and which has served as a model, is the open data site of the United States Government. It contains millions of data points on education, health, science and technology, financial data, climate, energy consumption, industrial data, ocean data, etc. What is noteworthy about this site is that the U.S. Government was the pioneer in publishing millions of data points, as it was also among the first to recognize the economic value of making that information public. This contrasts with the philosophy of the twentieth century and earlier centuries, when it was believed that "information is power." Now, power lies in knowing how to analyze information, not in the information itself.

In 2015 it had more than 124,000 different databases, ranging from consumer complaints to political surveys and historical climate data.

#### Journalistic sources and network society

_Juan José Tamayo_


This chapter will not address what sources can be accessed with the Internet, because the answer is all of them. Here we will analyze how the network society has modified the traditional
concepts of sources and media. Journalism has always been what its sources are. Some years ago, the great
opportunity that the Internet represented for accessing sources was glimpsed: from distant ones,
such as NASA, or the media of other countries, to others
closer at hand that posted interesting information. Likewise, the
advance of email was made evident, which allowed two important innovations
compared to the previous era: a) it was now possible to contact any
source in any country and from anywhere, and b) press offices
could include any media outlet, or even citizen, on their _mailing_ list.

In the mid-1990s, when press releases or information were still sent
by fax, the cost in time and money to sources was significant. One person had to send faxes one by one, and there were
workers in newsrooms whose job was to walk around with a tray distributing faxes among reporters. Beyond the time involved, sending a fax
cost money. This encouraged sources to send them only to the most relevant media.

Already in the late 1990s and, above all, in the 21st century, email made it possible, for example, that there was no obstacle to a humble journalist
from a remote territory working for a small outlet to register and receive
information on equal footing with the major media. It cost NASA, the White House, or La Moncloa nothing to include any request to
receive their newsletter.

It was with this trend that sources realized the power they
held. If they could reach humble journalists, why not all citizens
who wanted it. They thus became media outlets in their own right, and this contributed to a situation where, while traditional media
were losing journalists, press offices needed more and more every day. On the
other hand, many citizens who were sources—the usual experts—
also discovered that their blogs could be sources and that they could bypass even the press offices of their institutions. When social networks became widespread, they also used these new possibilities.
The singer Lady Gaga (USA, 1986) in 2011 had 32 million followers
on Facebook and a record 10 million fans on Twitter. When she
wrote there, what was she: a source or a mass media outlet?


**4.1.** **The source as a medium of communication in its own right**


To illustrate this concept of a source as a mass media outlet in its own right,
we will use the case of NASA, the American Space Agency, one of the most relevant and universal sources of the 20th century, in which for the first time a human being reached the Moon (in 1969). In
2004, many scientists interested in Martian geology, geography, or climate had cause for celebration: the NASA probes _Spirit_ and
_Opportunity_ landed on Mars to collect extremely valuable scientific data.
Nothing unusual was expected, nor found. Communication scholars,
however, did find something surprising: in the 24 hours of
the _Spirit_'s landing, the NASA website received 225 million
hits (a hit is counted each time an internet user accesses
a page on NASA, whether text, photo, etc.).

In the ninety days the mission lasted, the number of visits rose
to 6.53 billion, a figure that NASA quickly clarified represented
"more than the total population of the Earth," which that year stood at
6.3 billion. A fifth of all traffic came from outside the USA,
and a survey determined that a quarter of visitors
were primary or secondary school teachers or students.

A study conducted in 2007 showed that in May of that year (a
month without any spectacular mission) unique visits to the NASA portal
were 3,952,000 people (Hedman, 2007). However, unique visits to the science and technology portals of traditional media were far lower. Only CNN reached 502,000 visits—
that is, almost eight times fewer. Those of the FOX or
CBS science portals could not be quantified because they did not reach the minimum threshold for this, which
was set at 360,000 visits. Only the space.com website (which has
nothing to do with traditional media) came close to NASA, with
1,178,000 unique visits. Another very interesting data point was that while
the average time spent on the NASA portal was 12:33 minutes, at
CNN it was only 2:38 minutes (five times less).

This led to the closure in 2008 of the science sections of such important media as the _Boston Globe_ and CNN. What is the role of traditional media if society prefers to go directly to the source? That is,
when journalists at a traditional outlet copy in full
what arrives from the source, they are digging their own grave: for that
purpose the public goes directly to the source, because the Internet allows it.
So, what is NASA: a source for the media or a medium of
communication in its own right? Possibly both, but it is clear that
it is also a media outlet with an audience larger than CNN
(science section), CBS, or FOX. NASA is aware of this and
maintains two channels of communication: one with journalists and another directly with society. It strengthens its brand image in both directions.

The communication channel with journalists has been enriched, in the sense that now any journalist anywhere in the world, regardless of the outlet they work for, can subscribe to the NASA
news service. The latest news on space missions, press releases about scientific findings, and videos with statements and press conferences from the agency's scientists
will arrive punctually by email. That is, it does not depend on major media (news agencies, international television networks, etc.),
because everyone receives the same information.

But the great novelty is that all of this also reaches society at large directly, without the need for journalistic intermediaries. Anonymous interested citizens can access the NASA website, where
they can find all those contents that journalists practically copy. Moreover, any citizen can subscribe to the NASA
news service, because the technology allows it, on equal terms with any journalist. Previously it was physically impossible,
but now it is not. The NASA press release will arrive at the same time to a curious teacher in the most remote corner of the world and to the science journalism section chief at _The New York Times_. Citizens with a communicative spirit—or a vengeful one, or whatever—can comment on NASA
news on their blog, copy the images, upload the video of the
press conferences or the astronauts on the International Space Station
(ISS).

Another interesting aspect occurs, as will be seen in the following section,
when the source has a blog and bypasses the communications office:
in 2006 the Bush Administration banned a NASA scientist, James
E. Hansen, from continuing to give lectures in which he linked
climate change to the polluting emissions of human activity.
He was ultimately removed from his post as head of the Atmospheric Department
of the NASA Goddard Institute. Hansen mentioned it on his blog
and a cyber-murmur flooded the Web. From the cyber-murmur it moved to _The
New York Times_ (January 29, 2006) and to global television networks, such as CNN or
CBS, and from there, back to the Web, with NASA reconsidering its decision
under pressure from the virtual public sphere.


_4.1.1._ _Blogs as sources and as media outlets_


The phenomenon of experts with blogs, and those blogs becoming
both sources and media outlets at the same time, is very interesting and worth
addressing in greater depth. Although they exist in all areas of
specialized journalism, perhaps the area where they are having the greatest impact
is in economic journalism. Will the current economic crisis take long to end?
Should I buy a house now or wait a few months?
When will employment recover in Southern Europe? Should I invest in a
new business or buy gold with my savings? Is my money safe in
the banks?... All these questions are constantly searched on Google
and citizens, with increasing frequency, make their real decisions based on what they find on the Web. Many experts argue that
economic blogs are essential for understanding what is happening in the
world. Dozens of rankings exist, from the one _The Wall Street
Journal_ produces each year to those by internet users themselves. They are ranked according to
their visitors, their influence (number of websites linking to them), or the intellectual leadership of their authors. It should be noted, however, that blogs
can appear and disappear overnight. Their volatility is enormous,
not only in terms of their creation, but also—simply—in the number of posts
they publish. A newspaper is printed every day (or updates its website
constantly); the same goes for a television news program, and that
is important for building audience loyalty. But blogs depend on the
free time their authors have available. Therefore, there may be a
period when daily posts are published and then only monthly ones, causing the audience to drop considerably.

Taking all these caveats into account, this book has selected
six blogs—five foreign and one Spanish—to summarize the possible tendencies of this type of communication. Blogs may disappear, but
others similar to them will emerge. What matters to highlight are the patterns on which they are based.


_A) Naked Capitalism_


Created in 2007 by Susan Webber, a business consultant, it had surpassed
thirty million visits by 2011. It had more than 36,000 daily readers,
who visited 57,000 pages. Its success lies in having more than
twenty contributors—from economists and lawyers to Republican and Democratic advisors and journalists—who write about legal topics, investment opportunities, and insurance and mortgages. Its number of
contributors allows for constant updates, and the fact that they come from different countries—Ireland, New Zealand, the USA—gives a
global perspective to a discipline as globalized as economics.


_B) The Greg Mankiw Blog_


Another very interesting blog—at least heavily cited by traditional media that use it as a source—is that of Greg Mankiw, Professor of Economics
at Harvard University and a Republican advisor. It combines the
author's talent for popular explanation with what in communication theory is called
"brand prestige." The blog was created with the intention of explaining current economic affairs to the first-year Economics students Mankiw was teaching. This means the blog not only addresses economic issues, but has also become a fascinating way to learn
economics, which is the author's aim. The Internet allows us to receive
masterclasses from a Harvard professor without paying the thousands of dollars
that enrollment costs. Mankiw posts videos of the lectures he gives around the world, which is another formidable way to acquire
knowledge that was previously closed off to only a few. His audience in
2011 was around 11,000 daily visits, demonstrating that not only his
students follow his blog.


_C) Marginal Revolution_


From a communications standpoint, the blog of Tyler Cowen
and Alex Tabarrok, both Professors of Economics at George Mason University, and their successful _Marginal Revolution_ stands out. The originality of this blog lies
in the fact that it does not only discuss economics, but also books, history, restaurants, and records, which makes it a fully-fledged traditional media outlet, except with a selective audience of high purchasing power and,
above all, of influence—the type of audience that takes an interest in
economics. It is no surprise, therefore, that when Cowen used his blog to
promote his small pamphlet—barely 15,000 words—_The Great Stagnation_, published as an _e-book_ at the beginning of
2011, the impact was enormous: it reached the audience truly
interested.

In its _e-book_ version it exploded and quickly jumped to the bestseller list of _The New York Times_. With a substantial review in another traditional outlet,
_The Economist_, it appeared in print format in June 2011 (what matters still comes out in print) and sold the rights in several languages (including
Spanish). The book—which addresses why it will be very difficult to emerge from the economic crisis without major technological advances that generate business—,
according to _The New York Times_, was the one that provoked the most debate during 2011.
It all started—and was driven—from an innocent blog that, used skillfully,
turned an anonymous academic into one of the world's leading opinion leaders in 2011 in just a few months.


_D) Conscience of a Liberal_


This is another essential economic blog, in this case written by Nobel economics laureate Paul Krugman. It is hosted on the website of a
traditional outlet: _The New York Times_. Here the author's reputation—a Nobel laureate
and Princeton professor—and the prestige of the hosting outlet are combined. However,
curiously, it fails to reach the top spots among the most visited. One possible explanation is that Krugman is not seen by the
online public as a technical expert on economics, but as a politician.
He has leaned heavily from an ideological standpoint—he is a defender
of center-left policies—and his blog is hosted by an outlet that
also has that orientation. This causes the public to withdraw from him
the stamp of "scientific objectivity" he might have had if he were not
so media-savvy or so committed to certain political ideological causes. In other words, the new environment prefers the expert over the
intellectual.


_E) Freakonomics_


This is a paradigmatic blog regarding what the production of
knowledge in the network society means. It was created by Steven Levitt, Professor of
Economics at the University of Chicago, and journalist Stephen Dubner.
Here the route taken was the opposite of _Marginal Revolution_: _Freakonomics_
was born as a traditional book, becoming a worldwide _best seller_, with more than
five million copies sold.

With informal language, it addresses the economics behind
many everyday acts. The success of the book (and Google searches) was
the origin for creating the successful blog (and even a documentary). The blog has served as a platform to sell more copies of the original book and all
the sequel that has followed. It continues to fuel debate and position
the authors as opinion leaders.


_F) Nada es Gratis_


This is the most interesting economic blog published from Spain. NeG is a blog where the best economists writing in Spanish participate and is edited by Javier Andrés (University of Valencia), Samuel
Bentolila (CEMFI), Antonio Cabrales (Universidad Carlos III de Madrid),
Luis Garicano _(London School of Economics)_, and Tano Santos _(Columbia
Business School)._ With this roster of highly prestigious, politically independent economists, there is no doubt that the blog has become
a reference. Its website makes the objective clear: "To promote the analysis and commentary of prestigious economic researchers on the main
problems facing current society, with special attention to
Spain and other Spanish-speaking countries (...). _Nada es Gratis_ seeks to bring
economic research to the general public, fostering debate on
economic policy." That is, economists in the traditional role of journalists. The Internet enables anyone to create a blog—a medium of communication—and reach global audiences. On the other hand, these blogs also serve
as sources for traditional journalists.


**4.2.** **Leaks journalism or investigative journalism?**


Investigative journalism is possibly one of the most elevated specialties
of the profession. Montse Quesada, Full Professor of Journalism at Pompeu Fabra University,
argues that investigative journalism
focuses on realities that are not reported through official channels
and that affect the public directly or indirectly, going beyond the anecdotal data
of traditional journalism (Quesada, 1997). Already in the 19th century,
US President Roosevelt labeled them _muckrakers_, in response to journalists having uncovered
his extramarital affairs. But from _Watergate_ in the United States
to the thalidomide scandal in the United Kingdom, what has always mattered is
revealing what those in power want to hide from society.

José María Caminos Marcet, Full Professor of Journalism at the University of the Basque Country, describes the characteristics of investigative journalism (Caminos Marcet, 1997: 24–25):


1. It concerns the discovery of hidden truth: everything that
people or institutions wish to keep hidden but that
citizens have the right to know.
2. It is a journalism that does not rely on official sources. "Quite
the contrary, it distrusts them" (Caminos Marcet, 1997: 24).
3. Seeking the truth requires investigating beyond the mere surface appearance
of things. "It demands that journalistic sixth sense that practitioners
highlight as an indispensable aspect of their
profession, combined with unlimited tenacity and capacity for work" (Caminos Marcet, 1997: 25).
4. It uses methods from other social sciences, such as
psychology and sociology. A subsequent chapter will analyze how
this will lead to precision journalism and data journalism.
5. Investigative journalism is eminently informative and is
grounded in a faithful account of thoroughly verified data.
It is characterized by a rigor built on constant verification
of the data.


6. There is no room for error. The data must always be verifiable.


On the other hand, leaks journalism (where the source is the one
interested in something coming to light) is not the same as investigative journalism, where it is the journalist who identifies a possible story—what in the scientific method would be
a research question—and applies journalistic techniques to answer it. Ramón Reig, Full Professor of Journalism at the University of
Seville, offers five criteria for determining whether a report is investigative journalism or not (Reig, 2000: 104). A report is not investigative journalism if:


1. It works with already known data.
2. It covers several unrelated topics at once.
3. It does not focus on any of them deeply enough to provide
new insights.
4. It opens questions instead of closing them.
5. It presents as extraordinary something that is not, through
sensationalist audiovisual devices.


The Full Professor of Journalism at the University of La Laguna, José
Manuel de Pablos Coello, speaks of the five phases of investigative journalism,
which he calls the "5Ps" (P being the initial of each of the
five phases indicated).

The five phases are (De Pablos Coello, 2001: 104–113):


1. _The lead:_ the starting point of an investigative piece. Ben Bradlee, the journalist who participated in the investigation of _Watergate_ and the
_Pentagon Papers_, notes that his first commandment of investigative journalism was "follow the money" (Bradlee, 2000: 102).
Economic matters are often what lies behind many
journalistic investigations.
2. _The inquiry:_ consulting sources and documents to support or refute the lead.


3. _The publication:_ loose ends must be avoided, as they can undermine
the entire investigative work. It is better to hold back a story if
there are doubts. If something turns out to be inaccurate, those in power will use that fact to discredit the rest.

4. _The pressure:_ from the very first moment a piece of investigative journalism is published, the journalist and the outlet where they publish will face
pressure from those affected. They will always try to discredit
the journalist. "That must always be anticipated, and one must be prepared
for the attack" (De Pablos Coello, 2001: 110).

5. _The prison:_ this does not always happen, but in many cases those in power go after the investigative journalist, who "ends up charged, imprisoned, subjected to administrative proceedings, and/or facing serious financial losses or significant reputational damage" (Rodríguez, 1994: 190–191).


In any case, being a journalist implies risk: it is not like hosting a reality television contest. According to De Pablos Coello, "it is undoubtedly worthwhile
to know this 5P process. The professional will be able to know at any moment
what stage they are at. With this knowledge they will be prepared for the next
phase, in case the journey begun has not been concluded. Several of them can be
open at the same time" (De Pablos Coello, 2001: 104).

On the other hand, journalist David Randall, who has been deputy editor of
the prestigious British weekly _The Observer_, points out that:


[...] this type of journalism [investigative] does not consist of summarizing or
piecing together the discoveries or data of others, but of carrying out
original research, often using raw materials.
It can be conducted through a series of interviews, or by cross-checking
data and figures. On many occasions, the fruits and originality of the investigation are due to the discovery of patterns or connections that
no one had previously observed. (Randall, 1999: 100)


And Spanish investigative journalist Pepe Rodríguez, who has infiltrated cults to report on how they operate, argues that:


[...] nowadays the attitude of the journalist who sits
at the desk waiting for someone to call is common. It is a totally
irrational and damaging situation for the profession, but a habitual practice among
the majority of journalists. In this way, the profession has become
a bureaucratic job. There are exceptions, as in everything, but the majority of practitioners wait for someone to call them on the phone and
tell them something. This practice, it must be said, is not journalism.
(Rodríguez, 1994)


None of this implies that leaks should be discarded. They have always
existed and the best journalism has often been generated thanks to them.
However, in addition to the "deep throats" of traditional journalism,
in the network society special attention must be paid to websites that offer
data—frequently _hacked_, in the positive sense of the term—that
the sources normally want to hide from public opinion. In this sense,
it is very instructive to treat the WikiLeaks website with some depth, a
mixture of leaks from which investigation can begin.


**4.3.** **WikiLeaks**


Founded in 2006 by Julian Assange with the declared objective of leaking
information to combat corruption, WikiLeaks earned its informational reputation
when in July 2009 it published the damning footage
of a massacre perpetrated by a US military unit aboard
a helicopter in Iraq. Immediately afterward it posted online 76,000
secret documents about the war in Afghanistan. WikiLeaks—which combines the idea of collective participation _(wiki)_ with leaking _(leak)_—follows the maxim of hacker ethics
that all information should be free and of unlimited access.

In 2009 the WikiLeaks website published the _Trafigura_ case, with which it demonstrated
that this energy company paid another company in Côte d'Ivoire to dispose
of 400 tonnes of gasoline, destroying the environment. In July
2009 Amnesty International awarded WikiLeaks a prize for exposing extrajudicial executions in Kenya.


However, it was in October 2010 when its consolidation
as a paradigm of the best journalism of all time occurred: it leaked 400,000
US military documents that evidenced systematic torture of prisoners by Iraqi forces. Torture that was known,
according to the published documents, but reported neither by US officials nor by traditional media outlets. Another of
its hard-hitting revelations was the confirmation that civilian deaths were
far greater than those recorded in official statistics. Specifically,
traditional media and Western politicians had "forgotten" 15,000
victims, many of them women and children who had nothing to do with Saddam
Hussein.

What WikiLeaks revealed was of such magnitude that Amnesty
International considered filing charges against US authorities, and the UN Special Rapporteur on torture asked President Obama for an urgent investigation.

The two glorious episodes of Western journalism are _Watergate_
(1972) and, above all, the _Pentagon Papers_ case: the publication in 1971
of the 7,000 secret documents about the Vietnam War. That information precipitated the US withdrawal from that country. At the time it sparked
a passionate controversy around freedom of expression and national security. However, subsequent history has glorified the journalists against the demands for opacity defended by governments
at the time. Western journalism had been living off that glory for nearly forty years until the appearance of WikiLeaks.

"All the attacks now directed at Assange and WikiLeaks were directed at me when I published the Pentagon Papers," stated in
Twitter Daniel Ellsberg, an American then aged 79 who leaked the information
for the celebrated case of the 1970s. US President Henry
Kissinger called him "the most dangerous man in America." In an interview for _El País_, Ellsberg notes: "Today they would have called me a terrorist" (_El
País_, December 12, 2010. _Domingo_ supplement: 5), just as they are doing with
Assange or Bradley Manning, the Intelligence soldier who, according to the State Department, leaked to Assange the documents about
American diplomacy on a Lady Gaga disc. "If it was Manning—which is still unproven—I identify with him. I defend WikiLeaks because I believe in
the service it is rendering to citizens," Ellsberg adds, now transformed
into a hero studied in Western Journalism schools.

In December 2010 WikiLeaks leaked to five international media outlets
the 250,000 cables from the US State Department, in a case known as _Cablegate_. They were, in effect, the instructions for how the world works. Traditional newspapers had to use data journalism techniques to find relevant information (normally that concerning each country) among the millions of
sentences in the 250,000 diplomatic cables.


_4.3.1._ _Is WikiLeaks journalism?_


The emergence of WikiLeaks has opened an interesting debate in traditional media and in communication schools around the world: is it journalism or is it a source from which journalists draw? Should the public have access to all the raw information, or should it be selected by journalists? Joyce Barnathan, president in 2010 of the International Center
for Journalism (ICJ), a US organization that promotes quality journalism, considers that WikiLeaks is not journalism because
for Assange "what matters is publishing the information without taking into account
the consequences. This is dangerous" (_El País_, August 29, 2010: 27).

Julian Assange, for example, resists being considered a journalist and
claims to be categorized as a computer scientist. At most, what he does he has
called "scientific journalism" (_El País_, December 12, 2010: 31). He adds: "We work with other media to bring news to the people, but also to
prove that it is true. Scientific journalism lets you read a story,
and then click online to see the original document it is based on.
That way you can judge for yourself: is the story authentic? Did the journalist
report it correctly?"

Ernesto Hernández Busto, essayist (Premio Casa de América 2004)
and editor of the Cuban affairs blog penultimosdias.com, considers that
WikiLeaks does not have the vocation or resources to produce a traditional journalistic narrative, "but as a new type of information outlet or intermediary it undoubtedly contributes to the trust pact on which
modern journalism is founded: that secrets, even the most inconvenient ones,
can be revealed in the name of the public interest, and that the press will demand
that the Government meet the democratic imperative of transparency or
pay the price for concealing it" (_El País_, December 12, 2010: 31).

However, John Pilger, a veteran of committed investigative reporting,
war correspondent in Vietnam and Cambodia, defines WikiLeaks "as
one of the most important and exciting developments in journalism" (_El
País_, August 29, 2010: 27). For Pilger, the way the portal operates is not only
correct, but demonstrates that "Western corporate journalism
is in crisis after having collaborated in some cases with governments in
illegal wars."

Wally Dean, journalist and member of the Committee of Concerned Journalists—a group of journalists, editors, and academics concerned about
the future of journalism, based in Washington—considers that "the product WikiLeaks produces is essentially journalistic" (_El País_, August 29, 2010: 27). Others disagree with this view and argue that journalism is what traditional media do when they report on what
WikiLeaks publishes, but not the portal itself. That is, they grant
journalists the role of hierarchizing and selecting, but not that of finding
information, since they do not have sufficient technical knowledge
to find it in a world where, without that knowledge, access to
important information is blocked.

The latest major global media events—from the Iraq war to
Obama's campaign, which the journalistic elite never backed—show that traditional media "have behaved—in the words
of Gary Kamiya, co-founder of the influential salon.com and guru of online journalism—in a sclerotic manner and have been marked by hidden corporate agendas" (Kamiya, 2009).

However, specialized and explanatory journalism, capable of contextualizing the large amount of information on the Web, will gain ever greater
importance. Jeff Jarvis, one of the gurus of Internet journalism, argues:
"Specialization will take over a large part of journalism. We will no longer all
be doing the same thing (adding value to the news), but we will go out and make a difference covering in depth a specific segment" (Jarvis, 2008).

Bree Nordinson wrote in December 2008 this alternative perspective in one of the most important academic journalism journals, the _Columbia Journalism Review_: "As a result, explanatory journalism may have
a promising future in the information market. On May 9, in
collaboration with _NPR News_, the program _This American Life_ devoted an
hour to explaining the housing crisis. _The Giant Pool of Money_ quickly became
the most famous episode in the thirteen-year history of the program. _Columbia Journalism Review_ selected the piece as the most comprehensive and perceptive account of the system that has produced the financial crisis" (Nordinson, 2008). She adds: "Rather than simply contributing to the noise of the endless torrent of headlines, short sentences, and excerpts, _NPR_
and _This American Life_ take the time to step back and report the story
in depth and then explain it in a way that clarifies one of
the biggest and most complex issues of the year."

In this sense, WikiLeaks has spurred on the rusty traditional journalism
that had grown comfortable with the system. Australian journalist
Gerad Ryle heads the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists, and in March 2013 they published a joint piece by 86 journalists
from 46 countries: 130,000 secret accounts, selected from a total
of 2.5 million, that clients and financial institutions had kept hidden. These were
tax evaders, some as well known as Jerome Cahuzac,
the finance minister of French socialist president François Hollande.
The information triggered a political earthquake in France—meaning it was once again real journalism. Journalism was still alive.

#### From big data to data journalism

_Juan José Tamayo_


Previous chapters have analyzed the concepts of _big data_ and _open
data_. This chapter will show how those concepts can be applied specifically to journalism.
The emergence of the Internet has enormously conditioned the journalistic profession. Many voices argue that the Internet has strangled traditional journalism,
above all in the sense that the business model is still unclear: print is read less every day (especially by newer generations)
and the web has a pattern of free journalism that is incompatible with the
traditional model, in which journalists were professionals (that is,
they were paid a salary for their work). The new model favors a series of
initiatives—such as citizen journalism, or sources themselves becoming mass media outlets (Elías, 2010) through blogs and social networks—that turn the work of informing into an activity for
dilettantes: it is practiced in one's free time, without pay, and is rooted in the
digital philosophy of collective intelligence in which we must all contribute what we know (that is, the Wikipedia model).

The following chapter will address different types of business models. In
this one we will explore the new opportunities that digital technology offers
to journalism as a profession that informs about what those in power want to hide,
but which society should know in a democratic system. It is in this context that computer technology provides access to immense quantities of numerical data which, properly handled, can give rise to
interesting news stories. The journalist's role is fundamental
and generally requires four things:


1. Without being a mathematician, it is useful to have an interest in numbers,
statistics, large quantities of numerical data (data mining), and in general
in everything that can be expressed through numerical variables; as well as the ability to extract relevant information from numbers.
2. Without having to be a computer engineer, it is highly advisable—and
several concrete cases will be examined here—to have a special interest
in computer programming and to be capable, if not of writing
code, at least of knowing what to ask of an engineer. This is not trivial. The norm is to work in a team, but if both types of training are held the processes are more efficient. That is why the
School of Journalism at Columbia University in New York
—the pioneer and most prestigious in the world in this field—has
embraced since 2010 the joint degree in Journalism and Computer Engineering.
3. A passion for journalistic narrative: mathematics and literature have traditionally been separated into watertight compartments; but data are useless if one does not know how to interpret them,
find within them the story that interests society (sniffing out where the story is has always been the most complex part of the journalistic profession), and write a compelling narrative that can capture the
reader and make them understand what those numbers mean.
4. An enthusiasm for artistic expression: sometimes it is simply not enough to build a story from numbers. Digital technology has tools that facilitate data visualization.
This is what infographic artists did in traditional print media who, like photographers, possessed great artistic talent alongside journalistic talent. Technological development has led to dynamic infographics, genuine works of art that, like cartography
    and scientific illustration, have as their ultimate objective a better
comprehension of information. As we will see later, information visualization goes beyond the infographic in which the journalist directed the reader; now, with data journalism,
the reader must find new meanings, because audiences
are no longer passive but active.


It is typical for the major media that are investing in
data journalism—from the British _The Guardian_ to the Argentine _La
Nación_—to have multidisciplinary teams of computer scientists and statisticians
to find the data, journalists to write the story, and experts in
Fine Arts or design for the visualizations. However, it is obvious that,
since the final product is journalistic, the dynamic is leading to
journalists entering the statistical field and that of
visualization—in addition to the narrative field, which is their own—so that
professionals with all these new competencies are increasingly in demand.

The main difference between data journalism and traditional journalism is that if the journalist is skilled, they can surface information that
not even the sources knew existed. That is, while traditional journalism
sought to dig into the sewers of power to obtain information that
power wanted to hide, in the case of data journalism, if governments—or
industries or any source—have not carried out those analyses, they do not have that information either. This means their press offices barely have time to hide it or to prepare canned statements to get off the hook. In other words, data journalism is an effective tool for
fighting against the enormous power press offices have gained
in recent years, turning them into major concealers of interesting information and sellers of publicity (in Anglo-Saxon countries, those who work in them are called not "journalists" but
"public relations").

The downside is that data journalism needs something essential
without which nothing can be done: data. And in countries with a dictatorial past
like Spain, there is a deeply entrenched tradition of hiding them. In 2013,
the conservative government—of the Partido Popular—passed the Transparency Law alone in Congress. The model to follow sought to emulate the mechanism used in countries such as the United States, Australia, New Zealand, and
Sweden called _open data_—open data—consisting of a catalogue
of data to reuse the public information of the various branches of government.
In theory, it is concentrated in a single platform, making information
available to citizens.

However, already in the draft bill it was clear that it would be impossible to access sensitive data from, say, 30 years ago; that is, there was no provision to declassify—or access—reserved information. On the other
hand, there is also no tradition of quantification: for example, in
democratic countries such as the USA or the UK it is common to access data on
mortality by region and also atmospheric pollution data.
By cross-referencing data we can look for correlations, analyze causalities
with experts, and produce a solid report. In Spain the dictatorial tradition
of information suppression has persisted after Francoism: it is very
difficult not only to access data, but also for the administration itself to facilitate their existence. For example, in countries like the USA it is very easy to find data on causes of death by place of residence,
race, religion, sex, etc.

In Spain some of that data may exist, but it is classified. The way research is organized in Spain—bodies such as the INE, the CSIC, and the CIS have directors appointed by
those in political power—favors manipulation and the political suppression of scientific information (Elías, 2007). The exception, perhaps, is environmental data, but not because of any concern on the part of the Spanish government about
this issue, but because it is obliged to publish it by a European Union directive. This European imposition to disclose some environmental data
(such as, for example, where and how fires occur) is the origin of an interesting Spanish data journalism project
entitled _España en llamas_ (http://www.espanaenllamas.es/). However, the project's authors have recounted on numerous occasions how Spanish civil servants in the government threatened them to prevent them from publishing that data.


**5.1.** **Antecedents of data journalism:**
**precision journalism and investigative journalism**


What distinguishes data journalism from traditional journalism—which
obviously also used data—is the enormous advance driven by
digital technology that, on one hand, records large amounts of data—from
"likes" on social networks to comments on blogs or digital media—and, on the other, has developed systems to archive and analyze this
large amount of data automatically. That is, emerging data journalism combines the traditional instinct for finding the story and the ability to narrate an attractive and convincing account with access to programming knowledge to automate the processes of
collecting and analyzing large amounts of data.

Nevertheless, we must always keep in mind that data journalism
is, above all and first and foremost, journalism: that is, telling stories—or, in
the words of Italian journalist Eugenio Scalfari, "telling people what is happening to people"; in this case, it would be finding data that tell stories.
Data can be numerical, alphanumerical, textual content, databases,
tables, etc. in files of different formats. A modern and clear antecedent
of data journalism is precision journalism developed by American journalist Philip Meyer in the 1960s
of the 20th century. Meyer published in 1973 a journalism classic, _Precision
Journalism: A Reporter's Introduction to Social Science Methods_, where
he defines precision journalism as that which uses mathematics—
above all statistics—and the research methods used in social sciences—such as sociology or economics—to interpret data that
explain a newsworthy event (Meyer, 1973).

Meyer was a journalist in 1967 when riots broke out in
Detroit (USA) that produced 40 deaths, 467 injuries, 7,200 arrests,
and 2,000 homes destroyed. It has been described as the deadliest riot
in American history. The newspapers of the time published the
statements of police press offices, which abounded in
preconceived ideas supported by two theories: the _riff-raff_ theory, which held
that rioting is the only possible means of social advancement for people
who are socially and economically depressed, and the assimilation theory (since the African American population of Detroit came from rural areas of the South, they had
difficulty integrating into the urban industrial North, and as a result of frustration, rioting emerged). However, journalist
Philip Meyer did not believe the official version. Inspired by a study that
sociologists at the University of California had developed on the
Watts riots in Los Angeles, Meyer designed a survey to identify who was participating in the riots and ask them why
they were protesting.

The California sociologists took two years to reach conclusions
and Meyer, as a journalist, did not have that luxury of time. He learned to
program an IBM 7090 computer and developed what from that moment onward
was called CAR (_Computer Assisted Reporting_). Contrary to the police and politicians' theory, Meyer found that both those who participated and those who did not in the riots shared income levels and educational attainment. On the other hand, among the
participants, those born in the North were three times as many as those who
had been born in the South. Therefore, neither were they people without income nor rural peasants from the South who had migrated to the industrial North. Meyer proposed his hypothesis supported by theories on the origins of frustration and
anger by psychologist Nathan Caplan of the University of Michigan, or the studies of sociologist Samuel Stouffer on frustration and army troop morale
in the US military. Both narratives held that the closer an individual
is to achieving a goal, the more frustration they will experience
if they ultimately fail to reach it. Above all, this frustration increases if
the individual observes others progressing while they remain stagnant. This
narrative—or sociological theory, for social scientists—did fit with
the data obtained by Philip Meyer. Obviously, Meyer won the Pulitzer Prize for this report, wrote his book, and became a university professor. Precision journalism was born.

The Full Professor of Journalism at the Universidad Complutense José Luis
Dader subtitles his book _Periodismo de Precisión_ (1997) as the "socio-informatic route to discovering news." He points out that precision journalism
is the evolution and complement of investigative journalism,
though he notes that precision journalism and investigative journalism respond
to two different axiological axes that, for that very reason, allow them to maintain
their own autonomy and produce clearly distinct journalistic outputs. Dader
explains that investigative journalism uses "conventional methods," such as interviews or reading documents; while precision journalism
is based on "unconventional methods" in journalism, such as
statistical analyses or computer programming that "allow the practice
of another type of expository or clarifying journalistic precision of any other
relevant information provided by voluntarily identified sources
that involve the handling of figures or alphanumerical quantitative accumulations." Dader anticipates data journalism in 1997 when he notes
that the narrative account of an event is equated with numerical results of data collection expressed in evolutionary graphs and statistical significance indices.


_5.1.1._ _From precision journalism to data journalism:_
_the "Reading the Riots" case in_ The Guardian


Possibly the most interesting aspect of Philip Meyer's story is
that he arrives at a theory starting from data collection. The usual direction in the scientific method is the reverse: a hypothesis is proposed and measurements are taken to check whether it is confirmed or not. Meyer proposes the opposite: he measures a great deal of data and seeks an explanation for it. Taking into account a very important principle of philosophical logic that we will describe later—which states that correlation does not imply causation—it becomes necessary to develop a theory capable of finding the real cause from a data correlation. This theory is called _Grounded Theory_, which in Spanish can be
translated as "teoría fundamentada o del muestreo."

In 2011 significant riots broke out in Great Britain following
the killing by police of a 29-year-old man. The newspaper
_The Guardian_ led a pioneering project—inspired by Philip Meyer's,
but adapted to the new era—that combined modern data journalism with grounded theory. In the experiment, which was called
_Reading the Riots_, a new style of journalism was also initiated: collaboration between traditional media—_The Guardian_—and academia, which had traditionally been kept very separate, was seen as necessary. In this case, collaboration was with the prestigious _London School of Economics_.

The _Reading the Riots_ project will be described in some detail as
an example of what this type of data journalism means—one in which collaboration between academics and journalists (as well as computer scientists) is necessary. The project, as mentioned, was inspired by Meyer's not
only in philosophy, but even in some technical details, such as the fact
that multiple-choice questionnaires were also used to compare
the population that participated in the riots with those that did not, and obtain
some type of pattern (racial, educational, social, economic) that would establish
why some citizens rebel and others do not. The idea of this type of
journalism is to avoid "opinion punditry," an anti-journalistic practice where people
opine on everything but with data about nothing.

The first phase of the project already implied an update of what
Meyer did, since modern computer monitoring techniques were used to analyze the 2.5 million _tweets_
related to the riots. Moreover, the capability offered by
current computer programs was also used to code in depth all the confidential interviews conducted with hundreds
of people directly involved. In a second phase, the other side was also
interviewed (police officers, judges, and court officials).
Debates and discussion groups on this topic were also taken into account.

Not only did social researchers from the _London School of
Economics_ participate, but the much-admired online principle of collective intelligence and collaborative work was applied: _The Guardian_ opened a website
for active, open participation by social scientists who registered and
wanted to participate. They were seeking researchers with good connections
to the affected communities, so that they could locate and interview participants in the riots. The response was astonishing: 450 social researchers volunteered to collaborate without remuneration, though
only thirty were selected. The interviews and surveys were conducted in
the six cities where the riots were most significant: London,
Liverpool, Birmingham, Nottingham, Salford, and Manchester.

The journalistic project was designed as a social science research
project: each researcher received a guide of topics they were to
ask the respondents. The questions were neutral and leading questions were not permitted. They lasted 45 minutes and space was left for respondents
to provide first-person accounts of their experiences. Demographic data were collected from the interviewees, including where they lived, their age, ethnic origin, educational qualifications, and even their criminal history. They were also asked
about personal beliefs: from their reflections on the riots to their attitudes toward the police.

The journalists requested—and obtained—the database of those detained—more than 4,000—and wrote to some 1,000 offering them the opportunity
to participate in the study-report. The journalists/researchers visited
their homes to learn about the vital environment of those individuals. Likewise, the journalists/researchers used their local contacts to find people who participated in the riots but had not been arrested.
In this way they could separate the responses of participants depending on
whether or not they had been arrested. They promised anonymity and were surprised by the large number of people who wanted to participate and have "their story
heard." They ultimately managed to interview 270 of those people
who had not been arrested.

The journalists "got off their backsides"—that is, the interviews were not
simply by phone, but above all in the homes of the protesters, in youth clubs, cafes, and fast-food restaurants. The
British Ministry of Justice even allowed access to prisons
to interview thirteen people convicted and arrested for their participation in the riots. All those interviews were transcribed so that computer programs for processing large amounts of
data based on semantic tags could be used.

The result was complex because it was difficult to draw conclusions that
would support what social scientists grandly call a theory
and what journalists call a narrative. Therefore, there was no clear
headline. For example, despite claims in the media that it was a
racial revolt, the data did not confirm this: with a broad brush one observes
that 33% of those detained on charges related to the riots
were white, 43% were Black, and 7% were Asian. However, when
the variable of home address was applied, these figures varied considerably
from one area to another. In other words, in reality the ethnic composition
of those involved was that of the local population at each riot site: in London, 32% of the accused were white; in Merseyside, the figure was
79%.


The Anglo-Caribbean journalist and activist Darcus Howe disagreed with the conclusions. He maintained that the riots were clearly "an
insurrection" against the classist and racist policies suffered by a part
of the immigrant population. The data did not support that hypothesis, but they did not provide an incontestable explanation either.


For example, no clear educational component was observed: there were
detainees without qualifications, but also some with university degrees. Among
detainees from disadvantaged backgrounds, the percentage of those who had
received free school meals was higher than the average for that social class. The clearest finding was that the accused were predominantly young men (according to official figures, 90% were men
and boys) and that just under half were aged 18 to 24 (26%
were between 10 and 17 years old, children under the law). In West Yorkshire, 44%
of those detained were children. Men were more likely to be arrested for disorder and violent offenses, and women for theft and burglary.


One of the most interesting aspects of using these large-scale data technologies was that journalism was able not only to
refute, but even to explain how various rumors are born, grow,
spread, and die. In interactive graphics obtained through
analysis of tweets and the geographic location of those posting them,
it was possible to observe how a rumor spreads—such as claims that protesters
attacked the London Zoo or a children's hospital in Birmingham—until
it is confirmed or denied.

(source: _The Guardian_)


**5.2.** **Applying grounded theory to data journalism**
At this point, it is necessary to clarify a problem that data journalism frequently
faces: how to construct a theory or a narrative when there are only large amounts
of numerical data with no explanation. Grounded theory is a tool commonly used by
social scientists, but one that can sometimes also help data journalists.
In fact, the social researchers from the _London School of Economics_
who collaborated with _The Guardian_ journalists on the _Reading
the Riots_ project used it. Grounded theory is useful for generating a hypothesis
from the data obtained in an investigation. That is, it can
help us arrive at an explanation—a headline—when we have no predetermined
hypothesis.

Grounded theory appeared in 1967 at the hands of two experienced American sociologists, Barney Glaser and Anselm Strauss, in their now classic _The Discovery of Grounded Theory_. The proposal was quite radical:
they focused exclusively on the collected data, rejecting a
theoretical review. In an update nearly forty years later—in 2004—one of
the authors notes: "I wish to remind people, once again, that classic
grounded theory is simply a set of systematically integrated conceptual hypotheses
generated to produce an inductive theory
about a substantive area" (Glaser and Holdon, 2004: 3).

The use of grounded theory is only one proposal among many, but it must be noted that it has been heavily criticized, among other problems, for its "epistemological candor," its "sloppy sampling," its "questionable justification of small samples," and its "production of hackneyed categories" (Bryant and Charmaz, 2007).

That is, it has detractors and it is very easy for it to lead to error. It must be
applied with care; however, it is one way to obtain a narrative
—or a headline—when we have only millions of data points but no explanation for them. It is typically used in sociology more than in communication; however, it is increasingly being applied in digital journalism research
(Siapera and Veglis, 2012: 10–11).

According to its proponents, the researcher (or journalist) who uses
grounded theory to study a phenomenon is not bound by the constraints
derived from a quantitative methodology, because this procedure stimulates "imagination and creativity" channeled in a rigorous and
systematic way (Soler and Fernández, 2010: 208). Other authors affirm that in
analyses based on grounded theory "the researcher is a key figure in the process of interpreting data through a procedure
that combines artistic skill and scientific systematization" (Abela, García-Nieto, and Pérez, 2007: 55).

The steps for applying this theory to a phenomenon from which data can be obtained are the following:


_1._ _Theoretical sample_: individuals, situations, and events idealized for
the process of analysis. Their representativeness should be seen more in
terms of relevance ("breadth of the studied process") than in probabilistic terms.
2. _Data collection or familiarization_: compiling data and designing
databases. They must be analyzed to discover and label variables
and their interrelationships. In this phase,
"memos" begin to be constructed (theoretical ideas noted separately from
field notes). The memos will provide the guidelines for finding
categories, concepts, and even working hypotheses.
3. _Coding or categorization_: this is the process of naming or labeling
things, categories, and properties. It begins with _open coding_,
which is the analysis involved in identifying,
naming, categorizing, and describing the phenomena found. Next comes _axial coding_, which is the process of relating codes (categories and properties) through inductive
and deductive combinations. And finally, _selective coding_, where the initial categories are reduced and the core variable or _central
category_ is sought—the one that establishes the _paradigm_, the explanation of what that data means.


_5.2.1._ _Effects of applying grounded theory to journalism_


The pioneer of precision journalism, Philip Meyer, wrote a column
in _The Guardian_ titled "_Riot Theory is Relative_" in which he analyzed the application of grounded theory to data journalism reports
about the 2011 English riots (_Reading the Riots_). Meyer argues that
journalists covering important stories need more and better
tools and recalls that if his success in covering the Detroit riots of 1967 lay in the use of surveys and emerging computer programs, the success of _The Guardian_'s _Reading the Riots_ report lies
not so much in what they obtained, but in exploring whether grounded theory—applied by the social scientists from the _London School of Economics_—can be considered a tool for constructing a narrative
—a _storytelling_—that explains why the English were protesting in 2011.
In this sense, he notes that _The Guardian_'s project was "bolder
and more difficult" than his own was.

The main difference between Detroit and the English riots—which
is abysmal—is that in the Detroit of 1967 they had several sociological theories
that explained the riots. The journalist's task was to determine which
was the real one. The urban unrest of the 1960s in the United States
was the logical consequence of the shameful history of American slavery and had very clear reference points: the proclamation of the emancipation of slaves in 1863, the passage in 1896 of
racial segregation—from schools to buses and public restrooms—, the
reversal of that decision in 1954, and the push by Presidents Kennedy and
Johnson for the Civil Rights Act of 1964. All of this had produced
by 1967 a great deal of theoretical sociology on the social changes in African Americans. It is true that no sociological theory predicted specific riots, but it did offer concrete clues for finding a narrative that
explained the events.

However, in England things are different. A great deal of
time will be needed to determine whether what happened in 2011 was an isolated episode or whether it
forms part of a pattern of long-term unrest. As Meyer recalls, when
information was scarce—in the 1960s—journalism was content to
work in the manner of hunters-gatherers: events were sought out to observe and the journalist "told" them to the public. Sometimes, they were even contextualized. But now things are very different: we have an endless and immense flow of data of such magnitude that the biology of the human brain
cannot assimilate them, let alone evaluate them. And in this context, the
need for explanations is fundamental, which is why we turn to
the only tool that, for now, can offer something: grounded theory. Above all, it is useful for its capacity to design or construct stories that explain data. Social scientists call these constructions theories and journalists call them narratives. But both groups
recognize the need for some type of storytelling— _storytelling_—to make sense of data surpluses.

As mentioned, in the Detroit of 1967 there were three prior narratives. According to the "underclass theory," the riots were caused
by the desperation of lower socioeconomic classes who saw that the
system would not allow them to advance. According to the "assimilation theory,"
the Black population of Detroit—in the North—included a large proportion of
African Americans who had been born and raised in the rural South and did not
adapt to the urban industrial North; their frustration could explain the
riot. The great achievement of a journalist like Meyer was that he did not ask
sociologists to lay out a narrative for him, but rather designed
a survey himself and refuted both theories: first, protesters and non-protesters did not differ in educational level or income, so the first narrative did not hold; secondly, of every four participants in the riots, three were people born and raised in the urban industrial North,
which ruled out the second theory.

Therefore, only the third theory/narrative remained standing—that of psychologist
Nathan Caplan of the University of Michigan: frustration is stronger
the closer you are to reaching the desired goal, but ultimately you fail. And above all, the perception of watching others advance while you stagnate produces anger, an emotional state highly conducive to moving people to action. This hypothesis was supported by prior research conducted by sociologist Samuel Stouffer, who had studied morale—in terms
of self-esteem or frustration—in US Army troops during World War II. He observed that in units where promotion was more frequent, those who were not promoted were devastated and very frustrated: each promotion of a colleague was a reminder of the personal failure of those who had not been promoted. However, troop morale was much higher in units where
almost no one was promoted.

Meyer considers that his success as a journalist in discarding the first two sociological theories did not lie in the use of early computers—in 1967 the mainframe computers at Harvard had less capacity than a present-day mobile phone—but in applying the scientific method to the explanation and presentation of the facts. That is, the current change does not lie in the use of
computers—Meyer used them and, obviously, _The Guardian_ also used them—but in the application that newspaper made of grounded theory to construct a journalistic narrative. The case of _The Guardian_'s _Reading The
Riots_ was the first example in the history of journalism of a journalistic application of grounded theory. As
Meyer argues, just as happened with his approach in Detroit,
_The Guardian_'s historical contribution lies in the method, not in the machinery. Social scientists tend to divide into two camps:
qualitative and quantitative. Some explore, others confirm. But journalists must emulate both. _The Guardian_ and the _London School of Economics_
have laid the foundations for something that can be truly potent for
the future of the journalistic profession: the use of grounded theory in
the design of narratives. Above all, it can be a powerful tool for
consolidating data journalism.


**5.3.** **Typology of data journalism**


Data journalism is relatively emergent and therefore not yet completely analyzed or categorized. It is open to many different proposals, because the combination of traditional journalistic tools with modern digital development offers enormous possibilities.
Let us look at some of the proposals that have set trends.


_5.3.1._ _Automated journalism: ChicagoCrime_


The idea behind this type of journalism is to automate the entire process. The journalist must be a programmer (because they must decide how to frame the information) and, at the same time, write code and algorithms that give shape to what they want to produce. An interesting case is that of young American journalist Adrian Holovaty with _ChicagoCrime_, an innovative project in which, through Google Maps, police information, and neighborhood data,
he was able to describe how crimes were occurring in the city of Chicago. The project was awarded in 2005 with the Batten Award for journalistic innovation and _The New York Times_ considered it one of the best ideas
of 2005.

Holovaty is a champion of a new philosophy in the journalistic trade:
"doing journalism through computer programming," since the
idea underlying this journalistic initiative is to automate the process
as much as possible, so that the journalistic product remains alive and updated even long after the journalist has moved on.


In the case of www.chicagocrime.org, Holovaty created a computer program that automatically feeds off the updates of the website
of the Chicago Police Department. The computer program compiles
the updates from the police website and stores all the reports daily in a database. In this way, temporal trend graphs about crimes can be created.


Figure 5.3. Location of crimes in Chicago,
according to chicagocrime.org


A journalistic criterion exists when it comes to prioritizing what information is emphasized: type of crime, zip code, etc. That is, we must decide,
for example, whether we want crimes to also be groupable or not by
the ethnicity of the offender, because that carries significant ethical and moral weight. The data that police can compile are numerous: whether the offender's parents are divorced, level of education, schools attended, etc. The potential is seen to be enormous, but so is the danger
of sensationalism. However, once the journalistic criteria
that interest us have been decided, only the filtering process needs to be automated. Likewise,
an algorithm can be developed to interpret which facts in a story are
most important, so that crimes are hierarchized when there are several. Similarly, how navigation through the website is designed must be determined: the type of crimes with Google
Maps, or whether citizens can interact by contributing information.

In the case of www.chicagocrime.org, its founder, Holovaty, explains
that it is necessary to consider everything from how to display crimes to what
should happen on the screen when longitude/latitude coordinates for a crime are unavailable. Although chicagocrime.org works as an example of a fully automated website, Holovaty warns that
it is often not possible to automate an entire project. However,
he advises using automation to the maximum level possible.


Figure 5.4. Location and quantification of crimes by area
(source: _ChicagoCrime_)


Holovaty, who embodies the combination of journalist and computer programmer,
also founded _EveryBlock_, an information platform that brought together everything from incidents that had occurred in the neighborhood to discussions by its residents about how to improve it or photos of important events. The project was purchased in 2009 by media giant NBC and closed in 2013.


_5.3.2._ _Data journalism in investigative journalism_


One of the greatest applications of data journalism is as a tool enabling traditional journalism to have additional avenues for conducting investigative journalism; that is, to search data for corrupt behavior in certain social sectors. In Europe we have
the case of _The Guardian_ and _The Telegraph_. Both carried out a search
of all parliamentary expenses and were able to publish how much each member of parliament spent of public funds. The story caused a great controversy, because
many of those who preached austerity or progressive ideas about sharing
the wealth among everyone were the ones spending the most on personal matters at the expense of the British government.


Figure 5.5. Expenses of British MPs
(source: _The Guardian_)


Another very interesting way to use data journalism is explanatory
and informative. For example, this case involving the State budget in
Great Britain. We can do it the Spanish way, where newspapers only
report on the empty speeches, insults, and pre-packaged statements
of political leaders; or we can try to practice Anglo-Saxon journalism, in which
the spending of taxpayer money is visualized. In this way,
it is the citizen who will decide whether it has been done well or not based on the
data, but never based on statements from political leaders.


Figure 5.6. How tax money is distributed
(source: _The Guardian_)


Data journalism can be aided by data visualization, not
only to tell a convincing story, but above all to make
certain social injustices or problematic situations visible. Due to their
complexity—because they are usually based on comparative numerical data—
they are often not understood. Once they are visualized, mass public opinion
becomes aware of the problem.

Possibly one of the most interesting examples of how traditional journalism throws itself into data journalism is the _Datablog_
of the British newspaper _The Guardian_. Founded in 2009, it is not only a first-rate journalistic information space but even a site for analysis and
theoretical reflection on this type of journalism. For _The Guardian_, data is
sometimes the source for telling a story and at other times a tool.


_5.3.3._ _The statistical journalism of Gapminder:_
for a fact-based world view


A very interesting case is the use that philanthropist and statistician Hans Rosling proposes for understanding the gap (social, economic, educational, ecological, etc.) in the world, thanks to his platform Gapminder ('mind the gap'). This data, published at gapminder.org, may be provided by governments or institutions in isolated form, but Gapminder's merit is "cleaning" it, selecting it, and above all comparing and visualizing it so that it can be better understood and, therefore, mass public opinion
becomes aware of the problem. Gapminder is a good source for data journalism because it does the hard work, but above all it is
an inspiration for journalists (most of the time that data is
accessible but published in raw form, so that it is not easy to draw conclusions from it).

Gapminder is a non-profit foundation based in Stockholm,
whose objective, as stated on its website, is "to give a fact-based world view" _(for a fact-based world view),_ placing at our disposal
a method that makes it easier to understand the data. It provides us, among other things, with a powerful graphical tool, highly visual, for analyzing different
types of numerical data from different countries in the form of a time sequence.

The data that can be found within Gapminder ranges from
the number of HIV cases, life expectancy, child malnutrition,
the number of children per woman per country, CO2 emissions, the age of first
marriage, per capita income, gross domestic product, energy expenditure, energy consumption... In total, there are 503 indicators that can be analyzed.

The tool allows us to easily correlate any of the indicators and, through a time animation, to have the ability
to analyze the evolution of that indicator or even to combine indicators.
Another option allows selecting a specific country and observing its evolution.


This is a typical Gapminder chart: in this case, the age of first
marriage versus children per woman. We observe how in Europe the
age of marriage is higher and the number of children, lower. In Africa it is the
opposite. All of this can be seen with interactive graphics across
years and countries.

Data journalism is based on a clear idea: knowing more statistics
in order to better understand the world and be better citizens. The journalist
must be the one who helps the public understand the data, since it is the public that must make decisions, but finds it difficult to comprehend social, economic, political, or cultural reality when it is written only in mathematical language.


_5.3.4._ _The International Consortium of Investigative Journalists: icij.org_


One of the most interesting journalistic phenomena of recent years
is the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists _(The International Consortium of Investigative Journalists—ICIJ—),_ a global network that
in 2014 had 185 investigative journalists from more than 65 countries collaborating to produce stories of accountability journalism that
monitors governments and organizations.

Founded in 1997 by respected American journalist Chuck
Lewis, the ICIJ started as a project of the Center for Public Integrity with the aim of extending the "watchdog" journalism style of that center. The ICIJ focuses on
issues that cross national borders: cross-border crime, corruption, and accountability of power.


Figure 5.8.a ICIJ website, featuring
Swiss accounts on its front page


The Consortium is an amalgam of what is needed in the journalism
of the future: working together are specialists in data journalism and computational journalism, experts in public records, _fact-checkers_, lawyers, traditional reporters, and editors. Together they provide real-time tools and state-of-the-art technology to journalists anywhere in the world who want to investigate a matter that is "uncomfortable" for those in power.


Its foundation is based on the need to practice a different
and independent journalism in a globalized world in which the pressures of polluting industries, transnational criminal networks, and
very powerful business or government figures have increased—figures against whom traditional journalism has great difficulty fighting to obtain the
truth. They are funded by philanthropists or through _crowdfunding_. On many
occasions, funding is sought for a specific report and, once obtained,
the investigation and writing are carried out.

According to the information on its website—icij.org—they undertake many types of
projects: from small investigations that can involve
two or three journalists, to very complex proposals in which up to a hundred journalists from different countries around the world can participate. The headquarters are in Washington DC, where editors collect all the
information and produce everything from traditional reports to multimedia visualizations. This information is often picked up by traditional media.

of journalistic investigation


They have published very high-profile cases ranging from exposing the smuggling of tobacco multinationals, to how organized crime mafias operate in different countries, the Swiss accounts of politicians and businesspeople from around the world, private military contractors and their
murky dealings with governments, how lobby groups work
against climate change, and ignored details about the war contracts
in the invasions of Iraq and Afghanistan.


_5.3.5._ _Civio Fundación Ciudadana: the Spanish data journalism platform_


In December 2011 Civio Fundación Ciudadana
(civio.es) was founded in Spain, a platform founded by engineers such as Jacobo Elosua and
David Cabo, to which various journalists have since joined. Their idea was
to emulate the work of organizations such as mySociety in the United Kingdom,
Ciudadano Inteligente in Chile, or the Sunlight Foundation in the United States.
The aim was to bring to Spain a tradition well established in other
democratic countries of "transparency, accountability, and participatory democracy of the good kind." The objective, according to its website, would be "to launch an organization at the service of a more active citizenry, that would help improve transparency, demand accountability from administrations, and promote free access to public data." Its first project
was _¿Dónde van mis impuestos?_ [_Where do my taxes go?_] (http://www.civio.es/project/donde-van-mis-impuestos/).

In 2012 Civio launched another platform, tuderechoasaber.es, a
website through which 250 information requests were submitted to government administrations in just one week. Other projects attempt to criticize the opacity of the Spanish administration: _el indultómetro_ [_the pardon meter_] (http://www.elindultometro.es/index.html) examines who the government pardons; and
_Españaenllamas_ is a major data journalism report that was a finalist
for the Premio de Periodismo García Márquez 2014 and that presents an extraordinary profile of where fires occur, when, the resources used, the costs of extinguishing them, etc.

which uses data journalism analysis tools to detect
the information hidden behind the only source that, in theory, cannot

**5.4.** **What data journalism is and how it works**


Simon Rogers, responsible for _The Guardian_'s _Datablog_, a pioneering site
in finding stories through data, argues that data journalism
is carving out a space in journalistic organizations and highlights that in
2009, when his newspaper began with the _Datablog_, everything was new
and people were asking whether generating stories from data was really
journalism (Rogers, 2011). He notes that in recent years this type of
journalism attracts the most innovative journalists and, with them, new
techniques for approaching the profession. For Rogers, data journalism is
not only evolving but is changing journalism as a profession.

Rogers, drawing on his experience, considers that although this type of journalism does not yet have much history, interesting conclusions can already be drawn about what it is and how it works. Some of these characteristics are
the following.


_5.4.1._ _Data journalism may be an emerging trend,_
_but it is not new_


Data journalism has always existed and there have always been data and even data visualization. _The Guardian_, in its edition of May 5,
1821, reached a series of conclusions about education in Great Britain from the collection of numerical data on the schools
of Manchester, their costs, and their pupils. The difference from modern
data journalism is that _The Guardian_ published that data in cold,
very unappealing tables for public opinion, and now it can insert
that data into a spreadsheet and obtain surprising graphs and relationships.

On the other hand, tables, charts, and data in general were previously
published in expensive books accessible to very few, and now that data
is public and free, can be inserted into computer files, and made to work with them. Technology allows us to ask new questions of computers and to connect
new issues.


_5.4.2._ Open data _also means the obligation of an open_
_data journalism_


Previously data and statistics belonged to a few institutions that
guarded them jealously. Now they are in the public domain—though only
in truly democratic countries—and anyone with a spreadsheet program on their laptop or mobile phone can make use of that
data and obtain information from it. Of course we can make mistakes, but the philosophy of collective intelligence of the Web gives us the
opportunity to seek collaborators to help us. Previously the work was
solitary, now it is collective. Of course, there are examples of poor journalistic use of statistical data, but also magnificent reporting in outlets that take data journalism as something
truly serious. Some prestigious traditional media are investing
in this type of journalism: sometimes they join forces to address a complex topic, such as capital flight affecting several countries. But data journalism is also very relevant in emerging, independent media based on collaborative journalism. Among these, ProPublica, Where Does My Money Go?, and the Sunlight Foundation stand out.

In traditional media, data journalists are integrated into newsroom desks and attend editorial meetings armed with story ideas, to debate which news items need to be reported. From there, data are sought to give different angles to those topics.


_5.4.3._ _Data journalism must seek rigor and precision_


Finding the right data is just as difficult as locating the best source to
interview for a report. Professional standards do not change.
The traditional journalist must always be wary of statements and interested sources
offered to them by communications offices. The same applies to data: one must check who compiled it, under what
methodology, and above all whether the information extracted from it is valid.
For Latin countries, where governments often distort a great deal of information—"cooking the data for political purposes"—it is very useful
to consult the data on those countries published by international
organizations, which today is openly accessible.


_5.4.4._ _Data journalism must find the needle in the haystack_
_of data_


One of the keys to understanding current communication is that an excess
of information produces disinformation. The journalist's task is to
select, contextualize, and hierarchize the information that is valid
and interesting, sparing citizens that work. The same applies to data. The quantity of data grows larger every day. _WikiLeaks_ involved
391,000 data files. Governments, moreover, when obliged to make data public, use the strategy of flooding us with
data so that we cannot find those that are truly valuable. As
Simon Rogers of _The Guardian_ notes, ever-greater quantities of data are being published on ever more specific and specialized topics. There are 32,482 records for measuring
poverty indices in Great Britain. The journalist's skill lies in
finding what is valuable amid the flood of irrelevant data generated to hide the interesting information.


_5.4.5._ _Data journalism: 80% routine work, 10% great idea,_
_and 10% production_


Data journalism does not have the appeal—almost novelistic—of war journalism or even of an international correspondent posting, where
the journalist experiences very stimulating events if they are adventurous. It also lacks
the _glamour_ of traditional political journalism, in which journalists constantly inhabit the levers of power—parliaments,
governments, etc.—waiting for a source to leak them something. No. Data journalism is laborious and highly routine: 80% of the time is spent reformatting PDF files or converting datasets into spreadsheets. The data journalist is the bridge between those who generate millions
of data points and the public, which has no time to filter them but has
the right to know what those data say—data whose collection the public has often
funded.


_5.4.6._ _Different ways of working with data: searching or guiding_


In traditional data journalism the norm is to spend weeks in
a large dataset, ultimately discovering a major story
within it. However, a new format is gaining increasing relevance: one that involves quickly locating the key data in a story that is current news
and guiding public opinion through it using an increasingly advanced interactive technology. Logically, these suggestions
must be carried out in accordance with journalistic ethics: the journey through data can lead us to very well-documented lies.


_5.4.7._ _Visualization turns out better when done by human journalists_


The phrase "human journalists" is not redundant, because there are increasingly more algorithms that even write news articles. It may seem like a corporate premise, but the reality — in the few years that data journalism has existed — is that there is an important difference between visualizations produced by journalists and those created by citizens _motu proprio_ through free tools such as _Google Fusion Tables_, _Many Eyes_, _Google Charts_ - _Timetric_ — many of which are incorporated into traditional newspapers (in _The Guardian_ they are uploaded to its _Flickr_ group) —. But as Simon Rogers, head of _The Guardian_'s Datablog, maintains, "good design still matters." And these turn out much better — more informative — if the visualization design is developed _ad hoc_ for a specific story. That is, there is no comparison if the visualization is produced by human journalists who understand the specific problems they want to visualize, because they have in mind the story to tell, versus when the data are fed into a machine with a standard algorithm.


_5.4.8._ _You don't need to be a computer engineer — above all, you need to be a journalist_


An interesting debate in data journalism concerns who it belongs to: computer scientists and statisticians, or journalists? It is obvious that a solid background in computing and mathematics is useful, which is why universities such as the prestigious Columbia in New York have committed to a dual degree in Computer Engineering and Journalism. However, journalistic instinct remains the most important thing. Asking the same old questions: what's new?, do I see something interesting?, what happens if I combine this data with that? The ideal is collaborative work between journalists and computer scientists, but not computer scientists alone.


_5.4.9._ _The mission of data journalism is still to tell a story_


Data journalism is not just charts and visualizations. That is an encyclopedia or a treatise on econometrics or geography. Journalism — with or without data — continues to have as its objective the telling of a story that reveals something interesting. The vast amount of data we now have at our disposal is only another avenue for finding compelling stories that describe reality, demonstrate corruption or solidarity, and ultimately reveal how human beings live. Sometimes a single number is enough to tell a good story; at other times, we need many data points and comparisons, and that is where visualizations come in handy. Data journalism is very flexible in its methodology: finding a piece of data — or several — that tells a story. It is, above all and before all else, journalism.


**5.5.** **Steps to follow in the practice of data journalism**


Data journalism is something very modern; consequently, there is barely any theorization on how to practice it. Having analyzed several representative cases, we can outline some general guidelines on how it should be practiced. Giannina Segnini, a journalist at _La Nación_ in Costa Rica who exposed corruption in public procurement of medical equipment implicating three former presidents of Costa Rica, maintains that "data journalism does not replace the traditional forms of the craft; it is only a tool for doing the work better" (Flores Vivar and Salinas Aguilar, 2013). There are a number of steps that are essential in data journalism.


_A) Data acquisition_


It may seem like easy work, but it is not in precarious democracies such as Spain's. Access to data becomes complex for several reasons. First, because there is no tradition of compiling it: there is no data on what people die from, which schools are most successful and in which subjects, where university professors earned their doctorates, etc. All of this data is public in the majority of democratic countries. Second, even where it exists, there is no tradition of making it available: for example, in Spain the news about the approval of the national budget always follows the same format — an image of whatever minister of finance is currently in office holding up a _pendrive_. But journalists rarely have access to what matters, which is not the image of the _pendrive_ but its contents. Therefore, it is impossible to practice data journalism on, for example, where each euro is invested, as happens in other countries. The most one gets are canned statements from the government and the opposition about the budget. Finally, in Spain, laws such as the data protection law are designed to allow the government to access much data about citizens, while neither the citizens nor journalists can access it. That is, in countries with a weak democratic tradition such as Spain, the right to privacy is invoked to shield the traditional opacity of the public administration. Political and economic elites also pass laws to keep banking data secret. These are ways of allowing corruption to go unpunished. In the United States they have had decades of data transparency: the _Freedom of Information Act_ was signed into law in 1966 by President Lyndon Johnson: that law requires all offices of the US administration to make all existing databases public (which in the US are extremely numerous: from taxpayers to the Treasury to the average grades in different school subjects). In the US it is assumed that no private interest can outweigh the need to oversee and uncover information of public interest. Since the 1980s, with the advent of computers, it was very common for US newspapers to have complete data archives ranging from, for example, the list of driver's licenses in each state to lists of taxpayers and their income. It is very common for the press to publish each year the list of the highest taxpayers in each town (if it is a local paper) or state (if regional), something unthinkable in Spain and something that fuels corruption in that country.


_B) Data compilation_


Journalist Paul Bradshaw (2011) considers that data journalism can only begin in two ways: either you have a question that requires data, or you have a dataset that needs to be questioned. Compilation is the true heart of data journalism. This data can be found through methods ranging from advanced computing techniques — even hacking — government websites to _scraping_ ('scraping, scratching') hidden online databases using tools such as _OutWit Hub_ - _Scraperwiki_. Sometimes data is provided by an organization, but many other times surveys or online forms are used: this is called _crowdsourcing_. Another very important compilation technique is the use of computing methods that convert documents into files that can in turn be automatically analyzed by tools such as _DocumentCloud._


_C) Data cleaning_


Raw data normally arrive duplicated, in incorrect codes, and in formats that those in power — political, economic, scientific, etc. — introduce to make them difficult to read. On the other hand, there is a lot of information irrelevant for journalistic purposes. Detecting and extracting the important data is the hardest part for the traditional journalist. In this regard, it is recommended to receive training in spreadsheet design or statistics and data mining programs.


_D) Analysis_


This is the most important step of all. The point is not to publish data, but to draw a conclusion, which becomes the headline. That is, the analysis of data is the work that journalists have always done. What has changed is that one must now have training in handling large amounts of data. What matters is knowing how to cross-reference data from different sources to obtain new information: for example, we can cross-reference data on illnesses in Madrid's neighborhoods with data on air pollution. Illness data are already newsworthy, as is pollution data; but combining the two has very important investigative power in journalism. Within numerical data series, it is very important to detect patterns and trends and, above all, atypical behaviors. Anomaly has always been a news value since the birth of journalism.


_E) Information verification_


It is not just about verifying the source that compiles the data, but above all the data themselves. One of the best-selling books on statistics was not written by a statistician, but by a journalist, Darrel Huff, who pointed out that if you want to prove something absurd, take a lot of data, torture them until they say what you want to prove and call the resulting confession "statistics." His book _How to Lie with Statistics_ was published in 1954 and is almost required reading for journalists, because it demonstrates that it is very easy to use numbers to induce sensationalism. It is true that many of his examples are now dated, but his warnings are not only timeless — they regain relevance with the flood of data that digital technology provides us.


_F)_ Cum hoc ergo propter hoc _(Latin for 'therefore, because of this')_


One of the most common fallacies that can occur — not only in journalism, but in all aspects of life — is inferring that two events are related because they occur together. From the beginnings of Western thought, great philosophers such as Aristotle, Hume, and Kant have warned us that "correlation does not imply causation." The temptation to link data and, _motu proprio_, draw causal conclusions — that is, produce a good headline — can be very dangerous if what we seek is the truth, journalism's ultimate objective. For example, in the Middle Ages it was observed that very sick people — with high fevers — did not have lice. And it was inferred that lice were good for health. In reality, the opposite is true: lice transmit many diseases, but when the organism is so weakened that a fever appears, the lice jump to another body because they cannot tolerate high temperatures. There are many cases of this "perverse logic," not only in journalism, but in science (or rather, in pseudoscience, widely disseminated by the media): from studies that "claimed" that children with large feet were better at mathematics, to those that indicated that smokers were more attractive to potential partners.


_G) Narrative visualization_


In journalism, data are useless if they do not tell a story. Moreover, the story cannot be fiction — it has to be true. Modern digital technologies provide us with a large number of tools with which to make data visually appealing. Infographics expert Alberto Cairo considers that information visualization is not a synonym for infographics and situates it as an important part of information design, which deals with "the diagrammatic presentation of data, their visual transformation into information, to facilitate understanding, and draws from the arts and techniques of graphic communication" (Cairo, 2008: 27, 28–29). José Manuel de Pablos, professor of Journalism, introduces the concept of "infojournalism" (he refers to the press) and notes that "its basis is the infographic, but it does not stop there, as it implies a way of working that strengthens this revived visual print genre, with the help of journalists equipped with sufficient visual culture to know at any given moment what is most infographically appropriate" (De Pablos, 1999: 43). De Pablos underscores the journalistic or informational sense of the genre and compares it to other visual arts such as television or photography: "not all television, nor all photography, is journalistic" (De Pablos, 1999: 43). A study of the Spanish press during 2008 and 2009 concludes, regarding infographics, that the vast majority of content revolves around statistics and geographical locations — where photography is not possible — so the visualization of messages is also confined to diagrams and maps, as structures that present fewer production difficulties and can fit into any available space (Otero López, López García and González Veira, 2012). The study adds that graphic narration, as a consistent informational element, is stronger when current events involve catastrophes or incidents whose consequences are extreme for human beings. In any case, in the digital realm, information visualization grows more relevant every day. It is a true art, and normally it is graduates in Fine Arts or Graphic Design who handle these visualizations. However, what is visually attractive need not be true, and here, once again, lies journalistic craft. This is why it is so important that visualizations of journalistic content be produced by journalists. Since Parmenides, and especially since Plato, we have known that human senses are fallible; the sense of sight in particular. The Enlightenment demonstrated that the senses deceive us: painting — one of the pinnacles of human art — uses perspective to crudely trick the eye into believing it sees three dimensions, when it is looking at a canvas that has only two.


Figure 5.11. (a) Copernicus's representation of circular orbits
(b) Kepler's representation of the elliptical orbit of Mars around the Sun


Modern science began with the discovery that our eyes deceive us when we see the Sun rise and set every day — even literary language still uses expressions like sunset or sunrise. In reality, it is the Earth that moves around the Sun, and this conclusion could not be reached through the senses; in fact, it was reached against the senses and through mathematical logic. However, once that truth was obtained, nothing works better than a visualization to understand it, such as the one Copernicus used in 1543 in his _De revolutionibus orbium coelestium_ (_'On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres'_), where, for the first time, the heliocentric motion of the planets is demonstrated. The visualization that Copernicus produced is one of the most powerful images in the history of thought, which accounts for the rapid acceptance of the theory. Most importantly, this visualization is not the product of the senses or of artistic sensibility, but of mathematical data and a subsequent more visual "representation." However, Copernicus's visualization is not entirely accurate: the planets do not trace circular orbits around the Sun, but elliptical ones, as Kepler demonstrated after ten years of observations and collection of numerical data on the trajectory of Mars. The circle is more artistic and conceptually purer than an ellipse (perhaps that is why Kepler's representation is less well known than Copernicus's). But science and journalism share the same objective: to obtain the truth and make it public. That is, the journalist's obligation is not that of the artist. The journalist must first obtain — above all else — the truth, and only then produce an appropriate visualization. Not the reverse: attracting audiences with appealing visualizations that do not describe the truth, or that mask it.

# 6
#### Emerging journalistic models in the network society

_Juan José Tamayo_


Western media companies have been shaken by the arrival of digital culture. On top of the economic crisis, which has affected all businesses in the West, a crisis of the business model has been added: paper has been replaced by the screen, which implies redefining all the concepts that sustained the journalistic product, from the immateriality of the Network to the loss of advertising. This effect has been studied from two points of view. The first approach examines how these changes have affected traditional media: the first studies from the early twentieth century (Picard, 2000; Gual and Ricart, 2001) already sensed the dynamics — and the decline of the traditional model — that works from the mid-decade would go on to demonstrate (Chan-Olmsted, 2004; Chyi, 2005; Guallarte, Granger and Rodríguez, 2005). The second approach is the opposite: it sought to detect what new prospects the emerging digital environment offers (Brody, 2000; Pavlik, 2000; Doyle, 2003, 2004). On the other hand, when discussing media companies, it must be borne in mind that the obvious objective of any company — and media companies are no different — is economic profitability. Nevertheless, unlike a company that makes screws, the media company produces a consumer good — information — that is also a right of the population, and at the same time that product — the journalistic story — has become the axis around which political, economic, and social power revolves in democratic capitalist societies (Bergés, Sabater and De Mateo, 2009). That is, the media company is an industry that must function as a public service safeguarding a right: access to truthful information. This specificity shapes the industry both in terms of company management (Lavine and Wackman, 1992; Albarran, 1996; Nieto and Iglesia, 2000; Aguado, Galán, Fernández-Beaumont and García, 2008) and media economics (Alexander, Owners, Carveth, Hollifield and Grego, 2004; Doyle, 2003; Doyle and Frith, 2004). That is, the social value of the product must coexist with its ability to be monetized. If this does not happen — that is, if the company makes no profit — journalists will not be able to earn a salary. This does not mean that journalism (the publication of truthful information relevant to society) will disappear, but it does mean the end of the journalism profession (someone who could make a living from seeking and publishing that type of information). This final chapter will address the current state of affairs. This is a snapshot at the time of this book's publication. Obviously, in a few years it will be different. But the contrast between the situation in 2015 and that of a few years later will also be very illustrative for students. On the other hand, someone might argue that the business situation or business models are not journalism. Nothing could be further from the truth. Schools of Journalism should aspire not only to train reporters, but also the managers and editors of media outlets; likewise, from their classrooms should emerge the ideologists of the media of the future — hence the relevance of this chapter, which is not strictly professional — as the preceding ones are — but intellectual: on the state of the profession in the network society. With its lights and shadows, which necessarily must be analyzed in a subject like journalism and the network society.


**6.1.** **The magnitude of the business problem**


Jeffrey Alexander, professor of Cultural Sociology at Yale University, considers that problems become crises only when they transcend their own sphere and seem to put society as a whole at risk (Alexander, 2006). On the other hand, the "crisis of journalism" has only been argued from an economic or business perspective, or even a labor one, but no such crisis exists from the standpoint of the importance of the media, both politically and culturally (Luengo, 2014). Journalistic discourse is a fundamental pillar in the articulation of democratic discourse in the broad sense and is necessary to build a civil sphere independent of power (Alexander, 2006). The narrative that journalists offer remains essential for understanding the contemporary world and for ensuring that it functions properly in terms of freedom and social and civil rights. If a powerful journalistic discourse does not exist, power will strangle society. And a powerful media company does not always go hand in hand with influential journalistic discourse. This section will analyze the "crisis" from a business or labor perspective, but obviously this does not imply that journalism's narrative is not growing more powerful by the day, nor that it is not very necessary for understanding complex reality. In Western countries, journalism is hemorrhaging as a traditional profession. This is not the case in Asian countries, where the print press, for example, gains more readers and hires more journalists every day. But in the West — and above all in Europe, with the Bologna Process — free interns supplied by universities are replacing workers, in a business attempt to reduce losses. Despite the fact that in the 2011/2012 academic year there were 19,000 journalism students in Spain, in just one year — 2012 — the Observatory of the Federation of Associations of Journalists of Spain (FAPE) recorded 3,923 journalist layoffs and 71 media closures (not counting the 925 at _Telemadrid_ and those at _Abc Punto Radio_, whose layoffs were still being processed in the courts and who ultimately lost their claims). In 2012, among others, there were more than 600 layoffs at Spain's flagship media group, _Prisa_ (including radio and television), and 256 at the second most important group, _Unidad Editorial_. The newspaper _Público_, which shut down its print edition, laid off 134 journalists. The free papers were also affected: 34 layoffs at _20 Minutos_, 60 at _ADN_ (which closed) and 32 at _Qué_ (which disappeared). And regional groups: 75 at _Grupo Joly_, 134 at the magazine group _GyJ_, 18 at _El Periódico de Extremadura_, 49 at _El Punt-Avui_, 55 at _La Voz de Asturias_ (which closed); 40 at the _El Día_ group (which closed four mastheads) and 30 at _Diario de Jerez_ (which closed) and _Diario de Cádiz_. From 2008 to 2012, nearly 10,000 media workers in Spain had lost their jobs and 197 media outlets had closed. In public media, such as _Agencia EFE_, there were hundreds of incentivized redundancies and a 50% salary cut. The same happened in public television: Radio Televisión Valenciana expelled more than 1,000 workers — and in November 2013 dismissed those who remained (about 500) when the channel closed permanently —; Televisión de Murcia cut 250; that of the Balearic Islands — IB3 — 64; Televisión de Mallorca closed, firing 117 people, and Televisión de Castilla y León laid off 17 workers. But this is neither a Spanish nor a new phenomenon. Since 2009 we have seen how an iconic media outlet such as _The New York Times_ was losing the battle for readers and advertising, resulting in losses of 16.9% of revenue. In the third quarter of 2009 it lost 35.6 million dollars compared to the previous period. This economic hemorrhage led to one of the worst pieces of news for traditional journalism: an incentivized redundancy policy for _Times_ journalists (about a hundred in 2009) and a 5% pay cut for those who remained on staff. Eliminating journalists does not seem like a good strategy for a newspaper like _The New York Times_, because, obviously, it will diminish its quality, which is the way it can differentiate itself from free papers or the internet. The same thing that happened with _The New York Times_ happened with other iconic newspapers such as _USA Today_ (a 17% decline), _Los Angeles Times_, and _The Washington Post_. On December 31, 2012, the last print issue of _Newsweek_ magazine was published, after more than eighty years of existence. Many saw it as yet another example of print press slowly dying. However, in March 2014, with the scoop of having — in its own opinion — identified the person responsible for creating _bitcoins_ (the virtual currency), _Newsweek_ returned to newsstands with a print run of 70,000 copies, far from its glory days when it sold 3.3 million copies. But what mattered was that it returned to print. That is, this format is not only far from dead — it offers an interesting synergy with the digital world, and this phenomenon must not be lost sight of.


**6.2.** **The traditional journalism model**


There are, as will be seen in this chapter, several models that are not only navigating the digital crisis, but are turning it to their advantage to consolidate themselves. We will begin this tour with traditional journalism models. That is, those media outlets that, rather than losing readers — and money — have gained them. But above all, in addition to gaining readers and revenue, they are consolidating their position as leaders in cultural influence and thought.


_A)_ The Economist _and_ The Wall Street Journal


One sector that is indeed being saved is that of specialized newspapers. _The Wall Street Journal_ grew by 12,000 copies in 2009, reaching 2.02 million, and its financial results increased by 10%. In 2013, according to _Alliance for Audited Media_, it had already reached a circulation of 2.4 million copies, including the remarkable figure of 900,000 digital subscriptions. Its managers attribute this economic success to new applications for _i-Pods_ and _blackberries_. But prestigious weeklies such as _The Economist_ have also grown, written by very well-prepared journalists who conceal their names to demonstrate that what they publish are facts and not opinions. _The Economist_, founded in 1843, is a clear example that high-quality traditional journalism is more alive today than ever: in 1877 it sold 3,700 copies; in 1920, 6,000; in 1970, 100,000; in 2001 it sold around 600,000 copies; in 2009, 1,200,000; and in 2014 it surpassed one and a half million copies. That is, while most outlets lose ground, _The Economist_ extraordinarily increases its circulation. What is the secret? Very rigorous specialized information and a certain openness to other topics previously covered by generalist outlets. The policy of _The Wall Street Journal_ and _The Economist_ has been to take some market share from generalists while remaining specialized. They tackle topics that truly matter: the creation of artificial life, climate change, virtual currencies, or how new technologies change the brain. Statements from politicians or content produced by press offices never appear.


Figure 6.1. Various covers of _The Economist_


Another secret is the excellent preparation of their journalists. For example, the editor-in-chief of _The Economist_ since 2014, Zanny Minton Beddoes (the first woman in the weekly's history to reach that position) graduated in Politics, Philosophy and Economics at the University of Oxford, specialized with a master's degree at Harvard, and worked in the research group of the prestigious American economist and Harvard professor Jeffrey Sachs. She then joined the International Monetary Fund as a macroeconomics expert for two years, and from there was hired in 1994 as a journalist specializing in emerging markets for _The Economist_. After 20 years as a journalist, she became editor of the weekly.


_B)_ Living Stories _by Google_


The response of traditional outlets has been to try to specialize not by area, but by topic. Although it has not yet produced the expected results, these are experiences worth taking note of. For example, the agreement reached in 2009 between _The New York Times_ and _The Washington Post_ and Google's laboratory. They created the _Living Stories_ service, where stories were grouped by topic: climate change, US healthcare reform, and the war in Afghanistan. The experiment can be consulted at http://livingstories.googlelabs.com/. It was cancelled after a few months, but it remains an interesting model of collaboration between traditional media and search engines that deserves further exploration.


_C)_ The Atlantic Monthly


An interesting observation is that the journalism that survives is the kind that discusses another technical discipline (economics, politics, science, etc.) but written using traditional journalistic genres. It is still too early to see what happens with very well-written human-interest journalism that lingers on the writing itself and becomes literature. This is what has been called "the new journalism." An interesting case is that of _The Atlantic Monthly_, the celebrated magazine founded in Boston in 1857 where writers of the caliber of Faulkner, Twain, and Hemingway published their work, among others. In 2007 the magazine eliminated its literary fiction stories and literary essays on education, science, or literature. Now it is, above all, a magazine of political and economic analysis. In 2010 it turned a profit for the first time after a decade of losses. Reports circulated that they wanted to close their print edition and move entirely to the internet, ending a glorious 150-year history of legendary journalistic literature. However, there was concern that not having a print edition would harm its digital reach.


Figure 6.2. Various covers of _The Atlantic_


In 2015 it was still being published in print. Its website is achieving relative success, especially when measured by the fact that it has more visits than that of its main competitor, _The New Yorker_. But the "success" is based on replacing traditional journalists and journalistic reports with cheaper content, such as blogs and opinion articles by, for example, Nobel Prize-winning economist Joseph Stiglitz; or from controversial professional politicians — who generate debate and, therefore, web visits — such as Christopher Hitchens (a fierce defender of the invasion of Iraq, but a declared atheist) or Andrew Sullivan (a Republican, economically liberal, HIV-positive, homosexual, and Catholic). All of this was far removed from those masterfully written reports that, for example, helped bring about the abolition of slavery in the United States. However, little
by little it is returning to them, curiously enough, to attract younger readers. It deals in great depth with the changes in our current society.


In an interesting face-off in Washington between the old-press magnate Rupert Murdoch — owner of _The Times_ and _The Wall Street Journal_ — and the emerging Arianna Huffington — founder of _The Huffington Post_ — they only agreed on one thing: journalism on the internet can only survive if the public is willing to pay for that information. "The advertising model as the support for journalism is dead," Murdoch declared. But they detect that the public is only willing to pay for very specialized and very well explained and contextualized information (Killian, 2009).


**6.3.** **The academic journalism model**


Philip Meyer calculated in his book _The Vanishing Newspaper: Saving Journalism in the Information Age_ that the last print newspaper in the United States would appear in 2043. The result was obtained by extrapolating the decline in readership that the American press had been suffering for decades until reaching the "last reader." But, obviously, newspapers will disappear much sooner, because they are not going to wait until they have just one reader.


Nevertheless, Meyer is optimistic: he thinks that, certainly, the newspapers of the future will not generate the profits and audiences they have enjoyed up to now, since both (especially audiences) are migrating to the internet. Their future would lie in selecting information and giving it an analytical and explanatory content, so that they can inform intellectual and economic elites. And those elites are very important. Therefore, Meyer argues, there is a business there.


_A)_ The Conversation


This is the model of the Australian web portal _The Conversation_, as reflected in its masthead motto, "Academic rigor, journalistic flair." Founded in March 2011 in Australia, by 2013 it had also established itself in the United Kingdom. By publishing in English, its audience is practically global. Its rationale is as follows: traditional journalism was based until now on a set of professionals — journalists — who wrote or spoke about things they did not know, asking, in the best of cases, those who did know — the experts. What _The Conversation_ proposes is to give the experts a direct voice without it being filtered through the journalist. For journalists it reserves the role of selecting contributors' information and editing it so that it can be read by mass audiences. But in this model, the number of salaried journalists is minimal and the number of contributors is maximal: "_The Conversation_ is an independent source of news and analysis provided by the academic and research community and distributed directly to the public," its website states (http://theconversation.com/uk/who_we_are). And it adds: "Our goal is to rebuild trust in journalism. [...] We only allow our authors to write exclusively about topics in which they have demonstrated professional expertise, which they must disclose alongside their article." The sponsors — £500,000 for the UK launch — are prestigious Anglo-Saxon universities — though they want to expand to other countries — which the _The Conversation_ website frames as a positive, since, in their view, they are not subject to traditional advertising rules or commercial market interests. The founder and owner of _The Conversation_ is Andrew Jaspan, with a proven track record as editor of several traditional newspapers (including _The Observer_). Stephen Kahn was the editor in 2014 and also came from traditional print journalism: he had been deputy news editor of _The Guardian_.


The headline of an article published in _Press Gazette: Journalism Today_ (16-5-2013) perfectly describes the business model: "Eight editors, 10,000 specialist reporters" (Ponsford, 2013). The model, therefore, is as follows: a very small leadership team, drawn from traditional journalism, who work full time and earn a living from that product. And alongside these few journalists, a huge legion of academic contributors who are not paid — they make this very clear on the website, describing themselves as a "non-profit product" — but who have the opportunity to express their ideas. These contributors carry the informational weight. In February 2014 _The Conversation_ had to hire six more editors, having reached an audience of 1.56 million unique users in January. The success was evident, especially because many of its articles are sold to media such as _The Guardian_, _New Statesman_, _The Week_, and _Huffington Post_. One of the editors, Stephen Khan, told _Press Gazette_ that "there is room to keep growing." And added: "You can feel the takeoff. Many academics are happy to have reached a number of readers like never before."


_B)_ Le Monde Diplomatique


_The Conversation_ is an exclusively web-based portal. In contrast, in the realm of print journalism, there is a very interesting example that has also increased its circulation: _Le Monde Diplomatique_, a monthly publication situated in the orbit of the anti-capitalist left (a defender, for example, of Hugo Chávez's policies in Venezuela). In November 2010 its Spanish-language edition turned fifteen years old and its editorial stated: "In a very depressed economic context in which the print press is experiencing perhaps its deepest crisis, our monthly [newspaper] continues to see its global circulation grow (newsstand sales and subscriptions). Every month, more citizens join our readership, in a show of support and solidarity with our way of informing." That is, while Spanish newspapers were losing readers, they were gaining them. The editorial revealed the keys: "Some are surprised that a newspaper of such austere appearance, which continues to publish long, serious, documented articles enriched with a solid apparatus of footnotes, manages not only to retain its readers but to expand them. Those who are thus surprised can only conceive of two ways of doing journalism in the media landscape: by betting on the infantilization of the audience or by proceeding to an abhorrent simplification of reality. Today the media reproduce the media, like a mirror reflecting another mirror, in an abyssal configuration. The internet and social networks accelerate the phenomenon. And they sketch a reality in which the important dissolves into the trivial, the true and the false are confused, Manichean logic triumphs and sensationalism replaces explanation" (Montesa and Ramonet, 2010). From a professional standpoint, _Le Monde Diplomatique_ is not a strictly journalistic product, as it is barely written by journalists. The majority of its contributors are political, economic, or scientific analysts belonging, above all, to intellectual elites or are university professors. That is, it is a kind of _The Conversation_, but in print and with a clear ideological line. But most of its articles are not written by professionals who live exclusively off a salary from _Le Monde Diplomatique_ — as is the case with _The Economist_ and other traditional media — but rather occasional contributions.


_C)_ Die Zeit


Giovanni Di Lorenzo, editor of the German weekly _Die Zeit_, founded in 1946, also subscribes to this idea of academic journalism. His magazine, which celebrated its 60th anniversary in 2006, is at the antipodes of the visual and narrative culture that has contaminated the press since the advent of television. _Die Zeit_ barely incorporates images (although its covers are very carefully designed and spectacular) and its articles contain a great deal of text, like _The Economist_. It would seem doomed to certain death in the current era; yet it has generated its best results — both in readers and in economic resources — since 2009.


What is the formula? Not trumpeting the current news that the internet had already covered in the preceding days. Moving away from the deafening torrent of headlines. _Die Zeit_ abstracts and delves into issues as only philosophers, lawyers, writers, or scientists know how to do. According to its editor, the most successful issue of 2010 was _Das Rätsel Autorität_ (_El País_, 31-10-2010. Sunday supplement: 8), an academic debate — or, in Di Lorenzo's words, "a political-philosophical inquiry" — on the meaning of authority. The fact that this issue was the best-selling one gives us interesting clues about where the future is headed. As a curiosity, the publisher of _Die Zeit_ since 1983 is the German politician and economist Helmut Schmidt, who served as Chancellor of his country — as leader of the Social Democratic Party (SPD) in West Germany — from 1974 to 1982.


_6.3.1._ _Freedom of choice and the tyranny of choice_


The traditional generalist journalist will focus on immediate news, though this no longer holds the value it had in the twentieth century, because in the twenty-first anyone is capable of filming and uploading to the internet what is happening. The internet is the territory of free _amateurism_ — in a certain sense, a young intern is an _amateur_ — and to make money, one must differentiate. But above all, the current reader rejects the news as constantly reshaped on web portals. The digital era has killed the news story as it was conceived in twentieth-century journalism. As Barry Schwartz aptly notes in his controversial book _The Paradox of Choice: Why More is Less_, in psychology, passivity as a result of a lack of control is known as "learned helplessness." Although logic would suggest that an increase in available news would give consumers more control, the opposite happens. Having too many options can become burdensome. Instead of feeling in control, we feel overwhelmed. "Freedom of choice becomes the tyranny of choice," Schwartz writes. The public ceases to be interested and instead becomes voluntarily uninformed about the kind of short, immediate news stories that have always been prioritized in journalism.


Television will also have to radically change its model. It had great power in the West from the 1960s to the 1990s, but now, with the internet, the evening or nightly news broadcast makes no sense. The public constantly consults its laptops or, more importantly, its mobile phones. It no longer accepts a closed programming schedule. Television journalism has evolved into "infotainment" as a lever to attract audiences, especially those with a lower cultural level (Elías, 2004). But that will be the end of its influence. There are also heavily visited websites with very shallow news content, particularly of a sexual nature. They have audiences, but no influence. An influential elite does need and value contextualized information whose producers have a very significant university-level command of the discipline (science, economics, politics, law, etc.) they are covering, which is what grants them the stamp of "quality" and the "brand" guarantee so important for being selected on the internet from among hundreds of millions of websites.


**6.4.** **The** _**The Guardian**_ **model: the business of influence**


As mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, the journalistic narrative of reality grows more important every day, because, thanks to the digital model, it reaches more people every day. The destiny of the press will not be to sell so much information as influence. And here, traditional journalists also know how to do it very well — much better than the experts from models like _The Conversation_. One of the clearest examples is the British newspaper _The Guardian_, founded in Manchester in 1821. Gary Kamiya, co-founder of the influential blog on media trends _Salon.com_, believes that journalism will survive; what will disappear is the news item (Kamiya, 2009). According to Kamiya, the internet provides readers with what they want; and newspapers, with what they need. The physical layout of a newspaper encourages one to read information, even without actively searching for it. For example, if someone buys the newspaper to read the science news, they find on the front page that there may be an international story or a political analysis that the reader was not looking for, but ultimately reads.


This does not happen on the internet, where one goes only to specific portals. Even generalist websites do not rank news by importance, but in reverse chronological order: the most recent is the most important. Audiences fragment on the internet and on digital television, and this also implies specialization. But also in the opposite direction: only an intellectual elite is capable of making the effort (economic and mental) to read what it needs but which, in principle, does not interest it _a priori_. All the same, it is not yet known whether this new information (which seems likely to be in print and aimed at influential elites) will cease to be in the hands of journalism professionals and return to technicians, philosophers, writers, scientists, and academics in general — especially those who know how to write their thoughts and results clearly. The most obvious conclusion is that it will be a mixture of both.


Figure 6.5. Various covers of _The Guardian_


One possible salvation for traditional journalism is literature: writing very well and using the journalistic genres that have been refined over decades to attract the reader. A good chronicle or a good feature article is unbeatable compared to a ponderous opinion column with no varied sources or descriptions of times and places. And only in this way is the great success of traditional newspapers on the internet explained. The great paradox of the internet, with respect to the traditional press, can be observed in the exquisite and progressive British newspaper _The Guardian_. From 2005 to 2012 its print circulation fell by 46% (barely 217,000 copies in March 2012). But in 2012 its internet audience reached the staggering figure of 67 million unique visitors (in the US alone it had 20 million visitors, and in 2013 it was already considered the reference for progressive journalism). However, it cannot convert that potential into money. It is dying of success: every day it loses 126,000 euros (Bahón, 2012). This drives traditional media companies crazy. If they hide information and allow access only through payment — as _The New York Times_ has done since 2012 and _The Wall Street Journal_ did a year earlier — the number of readers drops enormously (and the revenue obtained still does not compensate for the decline in print advertising). The _porous paywall_ experiment, which leaves some content free but tries to convert readers into paying subscribers for other content, is still in the experimental phase. The first indications show that it does not work for all media. Economic journalism of global scope or media with audiences speaking languages with high cultural and economic levels — English or German — are not the same as, for example, Spanish. Nevertheless, with its 500 million speakers, Spanish-language media can see the glass as half full. The problem will be faced by less globally used languages — Italian, Danish, Catalan, Polish, etc. — and local information.


Figure 6.6. Digital version of _The Guardian_


On the other hand, the digitally literate public believes that information on the internet should be free and openly accessible. It is one of its founding principles. In fact, the editor of _The Guardian_, Alan Rusbridger, is a hero on the internet for his defense of _Open Journalism_ and his refusal to allow _The Guardian_ to restrict content from those who cannot pay for it. In his view, the newspaper on the internet should be free, as that was the principle with which the internet was born. The dilemma is that Rusbridger lives in Great Britain, where a capitalist market system exists; therefore, the company's shareholders, who lose thousands of euros daily with _The Guardian_, may not share the _Open_ philosophy and may close off its information so that one must pay for it. But care must be taken with this initiative. Asking readers to pay is not the same thing for a progressive and generalist newspaper like _The Guardian_ as it is for a conservative and economics-specialized one like _The Wall Street Journal_. If readers turned their backs on the new payment model, _The Guardian_ would lose all its audience and with it its great treasure: massive influence. That is, shareholders tolerate the losses because they are paying to influence 67 million unique readers. This idea — selling influence rather than information and/or advertised products — will be very important in the future and is one of the keys to the survival of traditional media with salaried journalists (although the majority will be expert contributors who may even pay to write in an influential media outlet).

This trend at _The Guardian_ is widespread. In Spain the same process is taking place. According to the Daily Press Report prepared in March 2014 by Deloitte for the Association of Spanish Newspaper Publishers (AEDE), the two newspapers with the largest print audiences — _El País_ and _El Mundo_ — experienced major losses in advertising revenue — _El País_ billed in February 2014 14% less than the previous year and _El Mundo_, 6% less —. They also experienced a significant decline in print readership: _El País_, a 16% drop (from 201,075 in February 2013 to 167,373 in February 2014) and _El Mundo_, 32% in the same period (from 166,100 to 112,047), according to data from the OJD (Oficina de Justificación de la Difusión) published in March 2014. However, they have a large online audience, unimaginable in the era before the internet. In February 2014 _El Mundo_ had 7.2 million unique readers and _El País_ was in second place with 6.6 million (data published in March 2014 by the digital measurement firm ComScore).

It should be noted that _El Mundo_ had lost 6.5% of its digital audience compared to the previous year as a consequence of its _paywall_ policy. This led _El País_ in 2014 to abandon plans to introduce this strategy. It wanted at all costs to overtake _El Mundo_ and be first both in print and online. This implied significant losses. But, as with _The Guardian_, in this case the investors are seeking influence.

Buying influence is not new. That is why Spanish regional governments fund their public television channels: politicians pay, at the expense of public finances, to influence voters in order to hold on to power. What is happening is that they do so with public budgets and, in times of crisis, it will be more cost-effective to purchase that influence in sympathetic media groups.


**6.5.** **Content farms: low-cost information**


Another model for information-based businesses is content farms. The clearest example is the US portal _Demand Media_ (www.demandmedia.com), created in 2006. According to its founding manifesto, which can be consulted on its website, it has set itself the mission of "satisfying global demand for commercially valuable content." Its objective is to "create content that solves problems, answers questions, saves money, saves time, and makes people happy."


To determine what topics should be covered, the _Demand Media_ algorithm takes into account the most searched terms on the internet, the keywords most in demand from advertisers, and whether or not articles on those topics already exist online. It weighs what internet users want to know against the extent to which advertisers are willing to pay to appear alongside those topics. Once demand has been identified by the algorithm, the subjects to be covered are placed online on Demand Studio, the platform through which 10,000 _freelance_ writers and "videomakers" supply articles and videos to society. The latter simply need to register on the Studio website and wait for requests on the topics on display there (sometimes 62,000 suggestions in a single day). They are paid per article — $10 — or per video — $20. (Boudet, 2009)


According to Ignacio Ramonet, "_Demand Media_ has thus invented, through the mass industrialization of the production of online content, low-cost information" (Ramonet, 2011a). For example, during the second quarter of 2010, its 10,000 collaborators produced, on average, around 6,000 written articles or videos per day. The site has become the most important contributor to _Youtube_: it provides between 10,000 and 20,000 videos per month, generating around 1.5 million page views per day (Ramonet, 2011b). Its motto is "Real life. Real experts," on the basis that journalists neither know about real life nor are experts in anything. Self-help experts or plumbers with DIY tips thrive on this type of information platform.


Figure 6.7. _Demand Media_ website


The most interesting — and disturbing for the future of the media — aspect is that while traditional media lose money with their digital editions — produced by salaried journalists, some on permanent contracts — _Demand Media_ is a functioning business: it only pays if the product is bought and has thousands of contributors — from homemakers to students, scientists, gardeners, doctors... — who earn little or nothing from their contributions, since their reward is having someone read what they write. They do not live off it. Those who do enrich themselves are the computer engineers who invented the algorithm: in 2009 _Demand Media_'s revenue was estimated at between 200 and 300 million dollars. In Europe there is a similar content farm: the _Populis_ portal, whose slogan proclaims: "When content creation meets web science." Created by the Italian twentysomethings Luca Ascani and Salvatore Esposito, _Populis_ is the leading content farm in Europe: in 2011 it had 26 million unique monthly users and provided information not only in Italian but also in English, French, German, and Spanish. In April 2011 Luca Ascani participated as a star guest at the _Youth Communication in Social Media Age_ congress, held at the European University of Rome, where he stated that "the content of _Populis_ is of the highest quality" and backed this up by pointing out that its writers "are not simply cynical and jaded journalists, but economics graduates passionate about financial topics, seasoned travelers who recount their experiences firsthand, scientists fascinated by science communication, or political scientists or doctors." To attract writers, its website asks: "Do you have a passion? Are you an expert in something? Are you interested in sharing your ideas? The article is paid once published and rates range from 5 to 150 euros." Ascani, who turned 30 in 2011, is something of a celebrity in Europe. In a continent where unemployment is battering young people, his idea of creating a media outlet fed by non-journalist contributors was born from nothing (like a _start-up_) and in 2011 he purchased for six million euros Blogos, a blog aggregator, through which _Populis_ contributions will be distributed via blogs, generating synergies. The topics covered are highly varied: from "the cheapest trips to London," to "ten ways to cut your insurance costs," or "how to drive your boss crazy." At the _Journalism and Information_ congress, held in 2010 in Strasbourg and funded by the European Commission, Ascani delivered a controversial talk, _Noise or News?_, in which he asked who produces more communicative noise: content farms, with their enthusiastic and passionate contributors — but also experts in certain areas (who only write about what they know) —; or journalists from traditional media, who frequently write about what they do not know. One more degree of perversion in this retreat of traditional journalism that the web represents can be seen in the Canadian portal _Suite 101_. It does not even pay its thousands of _freelance_ writers, even if their contributions are published. They only get paid if the advertising placed alongside the content produced by the contributor, which is sold to a digital outlet, is clicked on by the user. A US survey found that 79% of online news readers never — or very rarely — click on an advertisement (Ramonet, 2011a). For the media outlet, therefore, the deal is perfect, and for the writer, economically disastrous. But economic motives are not what drives the contributors, who come from all professional backgrounds and do this work as a _hobby_ in their free time or as a way to promote their message. Now it is not the media owners who are the persuaders, but the experts who feed the blogs.


**6.6.** **The** _**The Huffington Post**_ **model**


This "participatory information" has one advantage: it expands, almost infinitely, the number of information providers. Information will no longer be in the exclusive hands of journalists. Costs fall so much that the profits are spectacular, which is why traditional media — which have faced major financial losses in recent years — are eyeing this new content-farm business model. For now, the German press group _Hubert Burda Media_, owner of 186 magazines in about ten countries, has acquired between 35 and 40% of the capital of the _Suite 101_ site. What is relevant is that the internet is something new and the alliance with traditional media does not always work. This is demonstrated by, for example, the case of the US group _American Online_ (AOL), a pioneer on the internet, which merged in 2003 with a traditional medium in the audiovisual fiction field, _Time Warner_, to — so they thought — profit from the synergies of both systems. However, the two came close to sinking each other. In 2011 AOL announced its separation "to reposition itself as an information company." And most importantly: it purchased for 231 million euros the online publication _The Huffington Post_, founded in 2005 by the veteran traditional media columnist Arianna Huffington. A graduate in Economics from the University of Cambridge, Huffington created practically from scratch a media outlet that is, in reality, a content farm: it aggregated blogs by political scientists and economists who interpreted, sometimes better than some veteran journalists, what was happening in Washington. This army of thousands of bloggers (15,000 registered and 6,000 active in 2013) joined the _Huffington_ solely in exchange for greater visibility and impact for their opinions, but they neither received payment nor would share in the profits when the _Huffington_ was sold for 231 million euros. This provoked the fury of the bloggers: they were part of the product and, therefore, had a right to the profits, they complained. However, in the new digital economy what counts is having an idea — a new, differentiated product — and, above all, being the first to have it. The rest of participatory information is produced out of devotion. This changes all the traditional laws of the market. The _Huffington Post_'s algorithm compiled interesting opinion (supplied free of charge by Arianna Huffington's powerful and influential friends), along with gossip (also from politicians) and entertainment or culture news sent by press offices. Arianna Huffington came from the right: for ten years she had ardently engaged with the Republicans and in fact founded the website _resignation.com_ with the aim of achieving the resignation of President Bill Clinton over the Monica Lewinsky affair. In 2004 she switched sides: she chose to support Democrat John Kerry against Republican George W. Bush and, to promote Democratic ideas, launched in 2005 her digital newspaper _The Huffington Post_, which would become the ideological beacon of American progressivism. Hence among its featured bloggers were Dominique Strauss-Kahn, Michael Moore, Bernard-Henri Lévy, and the wealthy — though progressive — Hollywood stars Alec Baldwin and Charlize Theron. Headlines were designed not to persuade the reader, but rather the Google algorithm so as to position them favorably when a particular word was searched. On the other hand, it turned reality television into journalism: it published political or entertainment news lived out like a _reality show_, to take advantage of the virality of social networks. Bill Keller, editor of _The New York Times_, described the _Huffington_ as the "_American Idol_-ization of information," playing on words (because _American Idol_ is the US equivalent of the Spanish _reality show_ _Operación Triunfo_). The _Huffington_'s formula has achieved an undeniable media reach (in the sense of reaching the masses): in 2008 it had one million unique monthly visitors and at the beginning of 2011 it already had more than 25 million and ranked among the ten most important websites of current American news. It has versions in several languages: from Spanish and German to Japanese. It has received criticism because it is less interested in the truth than in topics that generate engagement, even if they are not true. One of the false topics to which it tends to give attention is homeopathy. There is no consensus on whether the _Huffington_ does journalism or whether, simply, it is a content farm that, instead of housing blogs about gardening, houses blogs about politics, exercising a form of counter-power. What is clear is that its cocktail of analysis, large amounts of opinion, and rumor made it the most influential — and one of the most profitable — media outlets on the internet. And another revealing detail: that model is the one that journalism considered "quality" traditional journalism wants to copy. One month after Arianna Huffington sold her political content farm to the _AOL_ group, the Spanish newspaper _El País_, considered a reference publication, recruited her for its board of directors. The founder and first editor of the newspaper, Juan Luis Cebrián — now corporate president of _El País_ — justified the hire by arguing that Huffington "will help to boost the digital transformation and increase the international profile." The epithets that _El País_ devoted to her were "the commander of the blog" and "queen of the blogosphere." It is clear that many of those in charge of influential media — and in the cultural world, since Cebrián is a member of the Language Academy — believe that the Huffington model, of expert bloggers who write virtually for free, will be the journalism of the future. In the Spanish version, launched in May 2012 and directed by veteran journalist Angels Barceló, the same model was followed: among the bloggers were the Minister of Justice, Alberto Ruiz Gallardón; the scientist and director of the National Cancer Research Centre, María Blasco; or the former Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs, Bernardino León. Each wrote about their area of specialization and interpreted reality from their position and privileged sources. The question is: are they doing journalism or self-promotion?


**6.7.** **"Digital feudalism"**


Anthony de Rosa, product manager at the Reuters news agency, describes this way of working as "digital feudalism": "The technologies implemented by these sites have an enormous power of seduction; they induce us to contribute for free without us even realizing it. We docilely provide content that enables others to make enormous profits" (Carr, 2011). Curiously, he was making this complaint from the free blogging site _Tumblr_. In feudalism, serfs received no salary for their work: they performed it for free in exchange for the lord's protection against possible risks (attacks by other armies, bandits, etc.). Now it is the same: one works for free in exchange for the visibility and audience that these content farms allow. Just like the feudal lord, the owners of the cyber-castles (the content farms) take all the profits and do not share them with their serfs. At the worldwide _PaidContent_ conference, organized in March 2011 in New York, Huffington argued that bloggers should be glad to work for free, stating: "Personal expression has become an incredible source of fulfillment for people" (_La Correspondence de la Presse_, 7-3-2011). Obviously, this is not happening only in journalism, but is a characteristic of the new online environment. When we add our profile to Facebook or Linkedin, we are working for free, offering our most intimate data so that these companies can do business with it without remunerating us. We do it for the visibility. Although, obviously, it is much more work to feed a blog daily than to update Linkedin. Contrary to what one might think, these writers/bloggers who work for free need not be bad; in fact, on many occasions they are very brilliant. Mayhill Fowler was one of them: she published in _The Huffington Post_ her excellent reports on "those poor Americans who cling to their guns and their religion," referring to comments by Obama, whom she helped in his campaign with these articles. After the elections, during 2009 and 2010, she wrote numerous posts on political relations, featuring many sources she had spoken with personally. She was twice nominated for the Pulitzer Prize, wrote more than a hundred reports, and never managed to be remunerated. In September 2010 she wrote on her website _(http://www.mayhillfowler.com)_ an article titled "Why I Left _The Huffington Post?_", in which she basically complained that she sent emails to the editor and her subordinates asking to be paid something, and they never replied. The case of her resignation appeared in the media and, then, one of those responsible at the _Huffington_ declared: "How can you quit a position you never held?"
As sociologist Rodney Benson, professor at New York University, notes, "this journalism model certainly opens up an unprecedented space for progressive ideas, but it promotes an organization of labor that participates in a broader feudalization of the social and economic order of the United States" (Benson, 2011). Chris Hedges, a former correspondent for _The New York Times_, wrote on the web, in an article titled "The Plunder of Huffington," "whatever ideology they profess, people who use workers in this way, extracting such enormous profits, are obviously part of the class of exploiters. Reread Marx. They are the enemies of workers" (Hedges, 2011). _The Huffington Post_, however, won a Pulitzer Prize in 2012.


**6.8.** _**ProPublica**_**. Public interest journalism**


As a conclusion to this book, it is pertinent to describe a very interesting example that demonstrates the philosophy underlying these pages. Journalism does not change its objective because technology evolves; rather, the opposite: technology is an opportunity that journalism exploits to practice its craft better. Journalism — as has been said — seeks stories of social interest and makes them public, and this is the purpose of a small emerging outlet: the reporting agency _ProPublica_, with its motto "Journalism in the Public Interest." Founded in 2007, it is proof that journalistic ideas can work, not only economically but journalistically. _ProPublica_ is a synthesis model between Kapuscinski and data journalism (that is, the traditional sustained by the emerging). The headquarters of _ProPublica_, based in Manhattan (New York), is a small independent newsroom which in 2015 had barely 50 journalists and which — according to its website — is non-profit, its mission being to invest the money given by its benefactors in "producing investigative journalism in the public interest." It is significant that they commit to investigative journalism rather than to entertaining information, which has little or nothing to do with the profession. "Our work," they state on the _ProPublica_ website, "focuses on truly important stories with moral force, that uses journalism to expose the exploitation of the weak by the powerful." Technology does not alter by a hair's breadth the importance of that statement of intent. In fact, it provides tools to improve that mission. An important part of the investigative journalism published by this reporting agency uses data journalism, but not all of it. That is, not all twenty-first-century journalism must be data journalism, but neither can journalism be practiced in the twenty-first century without data journalism (which remains, above and beyond the data, journalism). _ProPublica_ was founded by Paul Steiger, former editor-in-chief of the traditional and powerful _The Wall Street Journal_. In 2015 the director was Stephen Engelberg, former director of the Investigative Journalism section of _The New York Times_. Its founders consider, as they state on the agency's website, that, as a consequence of the economic crisis in the West, "investigative journalism is at risk and, indeed, many consider it a luxury." This leads current media to devote no time, budget, or journalists to investigation. And, in this regard, _ProPublica_ attempts to address this gap by producing those reports itself and offering them to the media: it does not aim to be a news agency, but an agency of good investigative journalistic stories that offers its reports to traditional media.


Figure 6.8. _ProPublica_ website (home page)


The work of these journalists can be published in several ways. The most common is to send it to important traditional media (_CNN_, _Newsweek_, _USA Today_, _The New York Times_) for them to publish it exclusively (and almost always free of charge). _ProPublica_ also publishes them on its website (often in expanded form). This achieves a symbiosis of reader traffic from traditional media to the emerging website. At other times it collaborates with journalists from a traditional media outlet (and they publish the report jointly), and in other cases it has agreements to expand a topic that has been published with another outlet but which that outlet decides not to continue pursuing.


Figure 6.9. _ProPublica_'s data journalism site


_ProPublica_ is financed primarily by contributions from social interest, non-profit foundations (common in the United States) that, just as they invest in student scholarships or in social dining halls, also believe that independent investigative journalism is important for democracy. Among the foundations are: the Sandler Foundation, _Pew Charitable Trusts_, the Knight Foundation, the MacArthur Foundation, the Ford Foundation, the _Carnegie Corporation_, and the _Open Society Foundations_ of George Soros, among others. They also accept donations from anonymous citizens (which are growing as they witness the agency's prestige).

Its success was demonstrated in 2010, when it won the Pulitzer Prize in the most prestigious category — investigative journalism — for a report by journalist and physician Sheri Fink, published in _The New York Times_ on August 25, 2009. The report addressed the decisions of a group of doctors who treated terminally ill patients in the days after Hurricane Katrina devastated New Orleans. It is titled _The Deadly Choices at Memorial_. It is an extremely long report (12,800 words), which contradicts the idea that the internet is only suited to brief information. Written in excellent literary style (writing well never goes out of fashion), it is written in the first person and incorporates interviews, dialogues, and descriptions. It is a classic investigative report that addresses whether the doctors injected lethal doses of painkillers into patients. Fink holds a medical degree and had written books and reports on healthcare before publishing in _ProPublica_. The report is journalism — pure and of the highest quality. The internet has not killed it; rather, it allows us to read it anywhere and at any time, without having to visit a cold newspaper archive.


**6.9.** **The future of journalism in the network society**


Information will always be important. We will need people who tell stories and contextualize data and events. The key lies in knowing whether the professionalization that began in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — in 1912 the Columbia University School of Journalism was founded — is the only option, or whether others exist. Everything points to the fact that this future can exist, because journalistic genres — especially the chronicle and the feature report — are far more attractive for achieving large audiences or goals of persuasion of public opinion than simple analyses or opinion columns by experts. _The Guardian_, _The New York Times_, _El País_, and _El Mundo_ will always have far greater audience and influence than an aggregator of academic opinions. And, furthermore, it is becoming ever more necessary to distance oneself from trashy television and infotainment (Elías, 2004). Many studies indicate that journalism on the internet is losing quality. According to María Teresa Sandoval, senior lecturer in Journalism at the Universidad Carlos III, digital journalism establishes "the primacy of immediacy over reflection and interpretation and, very especially, to the detriment of quality" (Sandoval Martín, 2005). Sandoval also draws attention to plagiarism and the pirating of content. On the other hand, young people — the readers of the present and the future — prefer the internet to the print press, which they never buy and barely read. According to a study designed by Miguel Túñez, senior lecturer in Journalism at the University of Santiago de Compostela, for young people "the press is the most credible medium, the internet the most independent. Young people see the newspaper as the medium closest to power and the internet as the one closest to individuals" (Túñez, 2009: 522). The best thing about twentieth-century journalism was that there was a sector of the population that could make a living from it without having to work in another profession. However, the internet has changed the environment. To start a blog one does not need the same effort — economic or personal — as to found a print newspaper or a radio or television station. If a blog offers public information, this means that, in a certain sense, it allows anyone to practice journalism. It could be an advantage, although one would need to analyze why someone, _motu proprio_ and without pay, begins to publish information. On the other hand, it is clear that it will no longer be as easy to earn a living solely from the journalism profession, as in the twentieth century. Perhaps the editors-in-chief of media outlets will be able to do so, but the bulk of information will be written by interns from the ever-growing number of Communication schools and by experts — the blog of Nobel Prize-winning economist Paul Krugman has more audience and is more respected than many traditional media outlets — or by science communicators — the blogs of NASA scientists are replacing traditional journalism — (Elías, 2010). These bloggers — journalists? — inform the public for free because they have other means of survival (the university, research centers, companies, banking, cinema...). That is, they will be dilettantes with respect to journalism. This phenomenon will need to be analyzed in depth: do these journalism dilettantes write as a social service or to promote their personal brand? Traditional journalism has made mistakes, particularly in countries with a weak democratic tradition. José Zepeda, director of the Latin American Department of _Radio Nederland_, considers that, for example, traditional media have done a great deal of harm to Latin American politics and society: "Pressure on the public agenda, improper use of facts to politically attack undesirable rulers, undisguised support for coup attempts, silence about successful activities or information intended to strip all relevance from positive government actions. Manipulation to exaggerate flaws, personal and collective smear campaigns" (Zepeda, 2011). And he adds: "The media feel themselves to be holders of an authority that greatly exceeds their functions. Their main defense is freedom of the press, a right transformed into the panacea that cures various ills." Something similar happens in Mexico. Traditional media, above all the duopoly of Televisa and Televisión Azteca, hold so much power that it is the media (and not society) who negotiate which candidate they select and support for the presidency of the Mexican Republic. In fact, workers from these traditional media are put forward to be elected as members of parliament. Mexico is the clearest case of a country where traditional media do not merely exercise counter-power: they are themselves the only power, above all Televisa and Televisión Azteca (Orta Vélez, 2012). The Mexican parliament has members of parliament who are chosen by the media (what they call the "telebancada"). This provokes a very strong reaction on social networks against traditional media or the journalists who work in them. It remains to be seen how the new cybernetic environment evolves. A significant part of society would welcome traditional media not having as much power as in the twentieth century. They demand that civil society, and above all experts, be the ones who analyze what is happening in the world and why. Everything points to economists, scientists, filmmakers, writers, historians, philosophers, sociologists, and political scientists sharpening their teeth to regain control of mass media — both in print (the few prestigious ones that survive) and on the internet. Experts are cheap (their salary is paid elsewhere) and highly specialized. Some have adopted the forms of communication that journalists have developed over decades (and many unemployed journalists have retrained as communication technique trainers for those experts). However, an economist — or a physicist or a sociologist — may be able to write a good economic — or scientific — analysis that is comprehensible to the general public, but it would be altogether ridiculous for them to publish a report in which they interview colleagues in the hallway or question themselves. It would be very difficult for them to write a vibrant chronicle of how a budget is debated in a parliament or how a new exoplanet is discovered. And not because they lack intellectual capacity, but because journalism — defined in the words of Italian journalist Eugenio Scalfari as "telling people what is happening to people" — is, above all, a craft: and writing a good report requires having written many not-so-good ones over a long career. Years of exclusive dedication are needed — accumulated experience, as in all crafts — to achieve mastery. If journalism wants to survive, it will have to take in young people and offer them good working conditions so that they can train in peace. But also, if it wants to survive, it cannot do without veteran journalists, who are the only ones capable of transmitting to the young the idiosyncrasies of the craft. Nor should one forget that journalistic genres (above all, the chronicle and the feature report — that is, journalism as a great literary genre —) have proven to be one of the most effective elements, not only for informing adequately, but for attracting mass audiences. The same is true of photography and infographics. The best weapon of mass persuasion is beauty; and journalists have always tried to use art — whether literary or visual art through the image — not only to recount and publish the truth, but to bring it closer to the segment of the public most reluctant to become aware of real problems. There is hope. It is true that _The New York Times_ and _The Guardian_ — or, in Spain, _El País_ and _El Mundo_ — have financial losses, but the digital environment has exponentially multiplied their influence and prestige. They have never been as influential as they are now (certainly far more so than when they were profitable). And, both before and now, their dominance in all respects — except the economic one — is infinitely greater than any that _The Conversation_ or any content farm could possess. On the other hand, a physicist or an economist working at a university does not contemplate denouncing corruption, uncovering a Watergate or a GAL scandal; rather, they will use the media to proselytize their point of view and sell more books, have more cited articles, or attract more funding for their research. It is not necessarily a social service, any more than the magazine published by an energy multinational or a tobacco company need be. Communication and journalism must not be confused. A society that does not want to know the truth — and that prefers reality television or fiction — does not deserve a future, and will not have one. If a society wants to survive with dignity, it will have to support and bet on quality journalism and on journalists committed to the truth.
