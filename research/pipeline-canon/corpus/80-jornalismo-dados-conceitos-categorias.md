# Data Journalism: Concept and Categories

## Data journalism: Concept and categories

### Leonardo Mancini [1] Fabio Vasconcellos [2]


**ABSTRACT**
In this article, we discuss possibilities for classifying Data Journalism as practiced in Brazil. After observing how quantifiable data are present in journalistic narratives on news websites throughout the country, this article proposes a classification that includes what would constitute Data Journalism — that is, reports in which data guide the construction of the narrative and which can be found in outlets such as Folha de São Paulo, O Globo and Estado de S. Paulo — and what we call Journalism With Data, namely, publications that use data without these being the focus of the narrative. Methodologically, this study monitored the online version of 48 journalistic outlets over five months and analyzed 2,296 reports, with the aim of identifying competencies involved in data-driven reports that we consider fundamental, among them: the investigative competency (search and independent treatment of data), the interpretive competency (ability to tell the story and the relationships between the data) and the communicative competency (data visualization as a way of broadening understanding of the story itself).

**Keywords:** Data Journalism, CAR, information visualization.


**ABSTRACT**
In this article, the authors propose new ways to classify how Data Journalism is being used in Brazilian newspapers. After having observed how data is presented on news pieces in online newspapers from all of the country, this study shows there are many different approaches, ranging from what is considered the typical data journalism pieces, which can be found on Folha de São Paulo, O Globo and Estado de S. Paulo, to what is considered here as "journalism with data", were the data is not on the center of the story. Methodologically, the research monitored 48 online newspapers during 5 months and analyzed 2.296 news pieces, in which we were able to observe the main skills needed to produce data journalism: investigative competences (data search and treatment), interpretative competences (how to tell the story and show the relations between the data) and communicative competences (data visualizations as a way to facilitate the understanding of the story).

**Keywords:** Data Journalism, CAR, Data Visualization.

## Introduction

In 2012, the _European Journalism Centre_ and the _Open_
_Knowledge Foundation_ concluded the first guide that
systematizes a minimum understanding of Data
Journalism (DJ), a practice that has been spreading
in newsrooms, _blogs_ and _sites_ throughout the world. _The Data_
_Journalism Handbook_ (Gray _et al._, 2014) is a
collaborative document that brings together examples and analyses from specialists
from various countries. The manual was quickly translated
from English into Spanish and Chinese and, in 2014, was
launched in Portuguese, thanks to the efforts of the
Brazilian Association of Investigative Journalism (Abraji).
The growing interest in DJ can be explained
by several reasons. The first is that the business model
of current media companies, called industrial
by C.W. Anderson, Emily Bell and Clay Shirky, from the Columbia
School of Journalism, in the study "Post-Industrial
Journalism: adapting to the present" (Anderson _et al.,_ 2013),
is in transformation, heading, probably, toward
organizations with smaller, more dynamic structures
using the internet as an ally, not an enemy, of their practices.
In parallel to this movement, DJ
is seen to be inserted in a context of dissemination of open data culture on the _web_, a trend that governments of
many countries have been following to meet the
democratic principle of _Open Government_. With this, the work
of the journalist changes in nature. In the words of (Flew
_et al._, 2012), "when information was scarce, most
of our efforts were directed toward hunting and gathering
data. Now that information is abundant, processing it
has become more important."
Seen beyond the journalistic field itself, we can say that the practice of DJ, by demanding to some extent a
greater professional qualification, mobilizes discussion about
the quality of the role of the press in the democratic dynamic. In other words, if journalism can be viewed
as an important actor in the process of political _accountability_,
understood here as actions that generate flows and
counter-flows of public life themes and still demand from
the political field justifications for its decisions, its
institutionalized performance ends up reflecting on the
_performance_ of democracies, as Hallin and Mancini (2004) discuss.
In this sense, changes in the _modus operandi_ of journalistic
production, that is, the qualification that leads, for example,
to Data Journalism, implies to some degree changes
in the type and quality of content offered to actors
in the democratic public space.


Faced with this, how then can we understand
the way in which DJ has been used in Brazil?
To discuss this, we analyzed the still scarce bibliography
available on DJ — much of what has been written was produced by professionals from the journalistic field itself —
and we carried out an analysis of the _online_ version of 48
journalistic outlets, present in all 27 of the country's states,
over five months (from September 2014 to March
2015, considering an interruption of 30 days between 15
December 2014 and 15 January 2015). This sample,
which represents 67% of the verified print circulation
for the period, and the information collection, gave rise to a
database with 2,296 reports that use data
in some way in their composition.
At this initial stage, therefore, our problematization of how DJ has been practiced in Brazil
sought to answer not exactly what DJ is —
a question that obviously accompanies our entire text —
but specifically what characteristics or necessary competencies
we can expect when dealing with this
journalistic practice in the Brazilian context.

## Data Journalism: some principles


In the section of the _Handbook_ dedicated to defining what
DJ is, Bradshaw (2014) argues that "journalism" and
"data" are problematic terms. In the digital environment,
according to the author, data would not be any group of
numbers gathered in a spreadsheet, as journalists had been
accustomed to until then. Confidential documents,
photos, videos and audio can now also be described
in numbers, more specifically in binary reading of 0
and 1. Bradshaw's description obviously does not settle

- the debate about in what sense journalism using
these data would differ from that accustomed only
to numerical tables. He acknowledges this limitation and
argues that DJ differs not by the use of data
itself, but "perhaps by the new possibilities that open up
when the traditional 'journalistic nose' and the ability to tell an engaging story are combined with the scale and

- the absolute reach of digital information now available"
(Bradshaw, 2014).
We have here an interesting point. If the use
of data itself, given its conceptual imprecision, is not
a novelty in the productive process of news, it becomes

in some sense dispensable for characterizing what
DJ is. On the other hand, journalistic investigation, viewed
in isolation, leads us to the same problem. Bradshaw,
in a way, understands that DJ would be defined more by
the association of these two terms with a third: new
technologies, or, more precisely, the tools that allow
the journalist today to automate processes,
make complex associations between thousands of documents
or even produce "engaging infographics."
Anderson (2015) has a critical assessment of this
argument. For him, not only the use of data, but
also the simple appropriation of technology becomes
inadequate for defining Data Journalism. This is because,
if we understand journalism from the perspective that it
involves the search and gathering of information from human
and social materials, such as documents, interviews and data,
then DJ has a history that would not follow a chronological
order of improvement, as the technological view suggests, but rather one of irregular and discontinuous passages,
with changes in the understanding of what it is and of the techniques
used to construct content with data. In other
words, Anderson opposes the idea that DJ is born
from technological evolution and proposes debating
the different ways in which reports used
data long before the process of improvement of the
technological apparatus [3].
Although with some variants, Brazilian authors,
among them researchers and journalists who use DJ,
adopt a view that associates investigative capacity
and the new possibilities of technology to conceptualize
DJ. Basically, the techniques would consist of production,
treatment and cross-referencing of large quantities of
data, allowing greater efficiency in the retrieval
of information, in the reporting from data sets, in circulation across different platforms
and in the generation of visualizations and infographics (Träsel, 2013).
Again, the automation of the processes of
reporting and data measurement would allow the practice
of this type of journalism (Barbosa, 2006, 2007). Lima Júnior
(2011) adds that the use of Information and
Communication Technologies (ICT) would be a fundamental element of
what he calls "Computational Journalism," which gains
importance in the context of Big Data and which would be the main
reason for a change in the type of skills required of future
journalists, classified by the author as "_hacking journalism_,"


that is, the capacity to explore technologies filtering information and presenting it visually.

## The dimension of new competencies


Perhaps the central point in the debate about what
DJ is, is the incorporation of new competencies by the journalistic field. This topic is present
in the numerous attempts to define DJ; however, in our
view, still in a tangential way. Examining these competencies,
whether through ethnographic research or
through the examination of the content and form of DJ practiced in
Brazil, can help to move beyond some theoretical difficulties
that still persist.
Defining DJ solely by the technological dimension,
for example, would imply accepting a deterministic
argument: once the technical apparatus is adopted, we would be in the presence of a necessary change
in journalistic practice, in this case, an inexorable change
toward DJ. In this sense, since the internet and _software_
have become apparatuses accessible to newsrooms, then all
journalism _with_ data would necessarily be Data
_Journalism_, which does not seem correct to us. On the other hand, if
only the investigative attitude of journalism (_watchdog_)
defines the relationship that journalism establishes with the
data it has access to, then DJ would be nothing more than
a semantic variation of investigative journalism,
incorporated into newsrooms long before the advent
of the internet.
This could be an answer that, however, remains
still little explored. Perhaps what is truly new
in this scenario is the adoption of the Freedom of Information Law
and the culture of _Open Government_ that, once associated with
_online_ search and new analysis and visualization _software_,
amplifies the field of journalistic investigation. The limitation
of this argument is that, if true, DJ would not be the result of improvement of the journalistic field, but the fruit
of changes in the political-institutional field. Consequently, governments that do not adopt open data
would not create — nor promote — DJ in their countries.

To some extent, technology encourages and can create
conditions for new practices in the journalistic reporting process. The telephone allows conducting an interview
at a distance of kilometers, altering the face-to-face contact
between a journalist and an interviewee to a non-face-to-face one. However, and this is our point, the telephone
does not modify the journalist's place as a professional who
needs to develop some competency to conduct
interviews. Beyond the physical object that simulates a
face-to-face interview relationship, there is a journalist endowed
with skills and the interest in extracting information through
the method of interview. The point here is not the technical
object itself, but how that object can be exploited.
For our purpose, therefore, it is not relevant to know whether new journalistic competencies are the result of
the adoption, before or after, of a technology, or even whether
there is or is not a legal apparatus that subsidizes its application,
but rather that these competencies, the knowing-how-to-do, have
been incorporated by the journalistic field into its routine of
news production. In sum, in our view, technology,
the Freedom of Information Law or the investigative attitude
are means that facilitate and promote the practice of DJ, and
not what defines it.
Saying that journalism has always depended on
some competency in order to be carried out is not exactly
new. Knowing how to collect information, organize it and
produce a text or images, according to the criteria of the
journalistic field, are classic competencies required
of journalism after the process of growing professionalization
at the turn of the 20th century. Variations on these
competencies were also appropriated by the so-called
"investigative journalists" who, according to Hunter _et al._
(2013), are characterized as those who have an
active attitude in the search for information, as opposed
to the passive attitude of a mere mediating agent between
institutions and society [4].
Having said this, what needs to be problematized in the definition of DJ, for us, especially in the way it exists
in Brazil, is how the incorporation or deepening of
some of these competencies, until then little or almost
never used by journalism, alters the way of making
news or, if preferred, enables the management of new techniques in the construction of content. In short, it is no use
possessing statistical analysis _software_ if there is no


professional who knows that tool, knows how it
works and how it can serve the purpose of journalism of producing relevant information and knowledge.
On the other hand, if we are pointing toward the "how"
as a methodological procedure for thinking about

- DJ, that is, how information is produced from these
new data management techniques, we are confronted
with another question: journalists endowed with these new
competencies, capable of mining and analyzing data on the
internet, but who do not use "engaging infographics," do they cease to be data journalists? How does a
binary cut between "uses/does not use engaging infographics"
relate to the numerous other variations in the type of
content that uses data and other forms of visualization of that data?

## The use of data by journalism


Although relatively recent, DJ can be
considered part of a tradition begun in the 1960s
by journalist Philip Meyer (1991). He was one of
the first to adopt the concept of "Precision Journalism,"
which consisted of using the computer associated with methods
from social science to produce reports with fewer
chances of error. Meyer's study ended up encouraging

- the emergence, in the 1990s, of the term Computer-Assisted
Reporting (CAR), still used today in
specialized forums, such as _Investigative Reporters and_
_Editors_ (IRE) and Abraji.
These new procedures helped journalists to improve their own knowledge about
social and political reality, reducing dependence
on sources external to the process of production and analysis
of information. All of this gains new importance
when the context changes from a scarcity scenario, in
which journalists had enormous difficulties in obtaining
information, to a moment of abundance, in which the
problem is no longer finding information, but knowing
which should be sought, analyzed and used to support
the journalistic story (Flew _et al.,_ 2012).

In a certain sense, DJ is a variant of CAR
because it would involve not only the use of computers,
but a minimum knowledge of statistics, computer
systems and social science methods (Flew _et_
_al.,_ 2012; Hamilton and Turner, 2009; Gray _et al.,_ 2014;
Hackett, 2013; Howard, 2014). Coddington (2014)
agrees with this assertion. For him, CAR, DJ
and Computational Journalism, the latter more focused
on machine programming, are categories that
overlap. Despite this, the author sees some important
differences regarding the orientation of these three groups
of professionals.
Coddington proposes a typology based on
four dimensions for characterizing journalistic practices.
Regarding professional nature, he compares
the differences in attitude between journalists with extensive
experience and those who seek training in a networked mode,
that is, who seek to make knowledge exchanges,
including with non-journalists. While, in the first
case, reporters are more conditioned by the norms and
routines established by their group, in the second, professionals would seek forms of interaction with a greater
diversity of groups.
The second dimension analyzed by Coddington
refers to the transparency of the techniques and reports produced,
that is, how visible are the methods used by
journalists to readers or interested professionals.
The third dimension analyzes whether the reports are made
from oriented quantitative samples or with the use of more robust structures, such as those arising
from Big Data. The fourth — and last — dimension observes
how journalists understand the role of their audience.
In this case, whether as a passive audience or as an
active audience, that can and should participate in the process of
producing and understanding the news.
As a conclusion, Coddington states that there is
a gap between the professional and epistemological orientations of CAR, on one hand, and DJ and Computational
Journalism, on the other. These divisions would have a
cultural basis from which each seeks to practice journalism.
CAR emerged from an effort to unite modern
social science techniques with professional journalism and investigative journalism. DJ and Computational Journalism,
in turn, are practices that relate not only to
some social science techniques, but to the culture
of open data.
In this way, DJ would be closer to a
fusion with the principles of open data culture and
with computational work. The latter, in turn,


resembles DJ, as it incorporates the principle of open
data, although more focused on programming. It would be
a practice with more emphasis on networked work and exchanges
with other professionals, although its technique and its
work material are oriented, in the end, toward
DJ (Coddington, 2014).
The debate about the culture of the three journalistic practices offers some clues for attempting to conceptualize

- DJ, although, in our view, it does not deepen its
practical competencies. On this point, Stray (2014)
suggests a quite simple and interesting theoretical model. Starting from the example of a time-series chart
of unemployment in the United States, Stray problematizes
the procedures for gathering and presenting the
information, arriving thus at three stages that, according to him,
would help to configure the difference between a typical
traditional journalism report and one classified
as DJ.
For Stray, DJ begins with quantification
and, evidently, questions about this quantification
with the aim of clarifying what is counted and how
it is counted. "Data is created, it is a record, a
document, an artifact that presents a meaning."
In this sense, for the author, an unemployment chart,
for example, is only a representation of a phenomenon,
and this representation was only possible because there
was a process of quantification of that phenomenon. The key
question, says Stray, is that there are various procedures
that can be adopted in the process of identifying
the unemployed, as well as in organizing that
data. "Data is not something that exists in nature.
The unemployed are a very different thing from unemployment
data."
Starting from the premise that DJ begins with
a quantification process, Stray then argues
that it is complemented by analysis, that is, if
quantification transforms the world into data, analysis
transforms data into knowledge. The author explains,
in this way, that this is the stage at which journalism most resembles science, with a strong inclination
toward mathematics, statistics and logic. "In DJ, there is
deep technical and specific knowledge" that allows, for example, the journalist to compare unemployment
rates with tax rates applied in
various countries, in search of some explanation for the
phenomenon of unemployment.
Next, for Stray, it is necessary for the quantification that transforms the world into data and into analysis
to be communicated. "The journalist does not publish their story

Known for his DJ publications, Silver (2014)
also presents his definition of procedures that

for this journalistic practice. Silver worked at _The New_

_FiveThirtyEight_, a _site_ that sets out to practice DJ. The _site_
went live at the beginning of March 2014, and, on the occasion of
its launch, Silver presented his recipe for the

strong proximity with the field of statistics is present

In the site's launch presentation in 2014, Silver says that
doing DJ does not mean only using numbers instead of words. According to him, "the use of numbers is neither necessary,

(Silver, 2014). Despite this proposition, he argues
that DJ involves some procedures, among them: collecting,
organizing and exploring data to obtain relationships
that may be significant among the data.
Silver thus argues that the first stage
of DJ is data collection or an evidence that,

of interviews and documents or personal observation.
In the case of the Data Journalist, there is also the collection
of information, but as a combination of research, experiments or data extraction from the internet. The
second phase is that of organization. In traditional journalism, organization translates into telling a story
chronologically, in order of importance, from the inverted
pyramid. Data journalists, however,
organize information with the statistical description of
data, that is, through the relationships between them or through
the construction of visualizations.
The third phase corresponds to what Silver calls
explanation. In traditional journalism, this stage
would consist of identifying who, what, where, when, why and how. The data journalist, in turn,
presents their explanation using statistical techniques
to demonstrate or to verify relationships between the data.
The fourth — and last — phase proposed by Silver is called
generalization. This stage would consist of using data
and analyses of past events to infer how those
events will behave in the future. This technique involves the use of scientific tools as a way of
hypothesis verification or presentation of predictions,
quite different from the inference model practiced by
traditional journalism, which starts from non-statistical,
but intuitive knowledge.

## From Journalism With Data to Data Journalism

If the competency of journalists to handle data, organize it and analyze it is a convergence point
among authors and if we can state that this practice
does not emerge spontaneously, but is facilitated by the uses
of internet technology and statistical and
visualization _software_, it remains for us to understand the way in which

- journalism has been appropriating data to produce
content. In other words, we agree with Anderson
(2014) when he argues that the understanding of what
DJ is changes more as a function of the way we define
data (numbers, _bits_, etc.) and the manner in which we work with them (techniques) than because of the application
or not of a technology.
In this sense, the need seems clear to
produce a matrix that contemplates the step-by-step
defended by Stray (2014) and Silver (2014), but that also seeks to aggregate the active dimension of journalism interested in revealing something beyond the simple dissemination of
quantitative reports from public agents. The dimension
of data communication seems equally
important in the construction of this matrix. But it would not be,
in our view, a dimension about the aesthetic quality or
degree of complexity of graphics and infographics. Although
desirable, because of its greater communicability,
it seems more interesting initially to know whether the graphic communication suggests or induces analyses and understandings
about the content or phenomenon that the journalist presents
in their report.
This does not mean that bold and creative infographics
that accompany reports lose
their analytical dimension. It is not a debate between
aesthetics and analytical knowledge. It is simply
a matter of incorporating into the analysis of what DJ is, infographics
that are less creative from an aesthetic point of view or that do not
require a great deal of production time and programming,
but that maintain their analytical character. This observation
is important because we run the risk of considering as
DJ only content that necessarily
incorporates bolder graphic presentations.
The cases presented in the DJ Handbook (Gray _et_
_al.,_ 2014), for example, limit the identification of DJ,
at least in the case of Brazil or of countries whose journalistic
companies have not acquired more
advanced technologies. There are two implicit issues on this point.


The first of them is that, by exaggerating the visualization model of what DJ is, the Handbook sets the
parameters through which this practice should be identified. This point is directly linked to the second.
By establishing a visualization standard that
depends greatly on the use of sophisticated _software_
or programmers, the Handbook, to some extent,
associates this practice with a data aesthetic. In other
words, all production that does not present visualizations
even close to these models would be doomed not to be
considered DJ. Again, we are not saying
with this that the aesthetic improvement of data visualization
is not desirable or important for its communicability, but that this dimension cannot alone
determine the definition of what DJ is. If it did,
the report and the static graphics presented by
_The Guardian_ in the 1821 publication relating the
schools of the city of Manchester to the number of students and
the cost of each would not be considered an
example of DJ.
A matrix with basic dimensions for thinking about what DJ is would also help us to better assess
some questions, such as, for example, whether all
use of data by journalism is necessarily DJ. There is
a difference, in our view, between reports _**WITH**_ and reports _**OF**_ data. While the first would contemplate
reports that appropriate data illustratively, in the second case, data would be the very reason
of the report. In the first case, quantitative data
would play the role of helping to illustrate a report; in
the second, data would be the very foundation of the topic and
the story of the relationships between the data would drive,
in this case, the report.
Another important issue is the role of the internet,
which, if it does not create DJ, as we argue, certainly
encourages the development of publications that allow
various interactive processes between the reader and the content.
In this way, how could we classify a publication
that focuses solely on the resource of data visualization that,
although it does not bring a text that presents its relationships,
allows the reader to have an active participation in
the interpretation of this data? A _site_, for example,
developed by journalists that searches for and organizes data about

- a municipal administration's budget and presents interactive tools that allow the reader to follow the spending
of the municipal executive should, in our eyes, be
categorized as DJ.
Having said this, we understand that our matrix needs
to be constructed on a vertical axis that starts from the classi-

fication of _**Journalism with Data**_ up to the other extreme, namely
_**DJ**_. This matrix somewhat limits the abrupt cut
between what is and what is not DJ, because it allows the
nuances between publications to be contemplated. In
sum, the _**Journalism with Data**_ scale at one extreme and, at
the other, _**DJ**_, seeks to contemplate the various ways in which
the press already produces content with quantitative data and
how it has now begun to produce. For this, it is necessary, on
the horizontal axis, to establish which attributes reports
present. These attributes seek to meet the model
proposed by Stray (2014), Silver (2014) and Bradshaw (2014),
namely: the importance of DJ seeking and presenting relationships
between the data and, from that point, telling their stories.
Therefore, on the horizontal axis, we would have three categories that attend to the dimension of the investigative character of
publications, as well as their interpretive dimension and
the communicative dimension. In this last case, we are not interested in knowing whether publications have or do not have
high technical and aesthetic quality, but whether there is a graphic
communication of the data that is associated with its analytical
content in a way that enriches understanding
of the story told. In other words, they are not mere
charts occupying space on the page, but charts that
tell part of the story presented on the page. Our
four categories are thus defined as:

### _Investigative Dimension:_


_**Extraction and/or Organization:**_ Seeks to identify
whether the report includes data for which the team
of journalists was responsible for extracting and structuring the
raw material from some source and/or was responsible for
the production and organization of the data itself. Reports
of this type tend to carry in their text indications
about the effort of the team itself or even indications


about the novelty of the data presented after the search and/
or structuring carried out by the team [5].

### _Interpretive Dimension_


_**Argumentation:**_ In this dimension, the aim is to identify
whether there is an analytical journalistic text, that is, one that
seeks not only to present the content of the report
and its context, but mainly whether it brings an analysis
about the relationships between the data in a way that indicates causes
and/or consequences of the subject of the publication. The text can
be both a direct analysis by the journalist and by interviewees
who are included throughout the report and who
fulfill the role of analyzing the relationships between the data
brought by the publication.

### _Communicative Dimension_


_**Graphic Visualization**_**:** In this category, the aim is
to identify whether the publications bring any type of
visualization, such as charts or infographics. The way
in which this visualization is used so as to allow that

- the content of the report is improved by visual communication is assessed, not in an aesthetic sense, although desirable, but
in the sense that this communication promotes/encourages the
analytical understanding of the data report [6].
From the investigative, interpretive and communicative dimensions, we arrive at our matrix, which seeks
to establish relationships between these dimensions and the scale
that goes from Journalism With Data to DJ.

## Conclusion


In this text, we sought to problematize the definition
of DJ. To do this, we turned to authors who presented
some general lines about this practice. Our argument is based on the following question: if data is a problematic term in the definition of DJ, due to its conceptual imprecision, the same occurs through an analysis that takes into account only the technological dimension that takes hold of
many newsrooms. In the first case, the imprecision stems
from the fact that quantitative data are not a novelty
in newsroom content production; in the second point,
the question concerns the deterministic view, that is, once
technology is adopted, are we necessarily moving
toward the practice of DJ?
In our view, a way of better understanding
what DJ is and how it is practiced is to construct a matrix that takes
into consideration three dimensions, relating to the
competencies that journalism may or may not adopt: the investigative dimension (proactive pursuit of data
and revelations), the interpretive dimension (capacity or interest in
exposing relationships of causes or consequences between the data)
and the communicative dimension (the centrality of data visualization,
understood here as a component that
helps the reader to understand through images the relationships between the
data). In our matrix, we established on the vertical axis
a scale ranging from the extreme _Journalism_ _**with**_ _data_ to
_Journalism_ _**of**_ _Data_. It is an attempt not only to
conceptualize DJ through differentiations implicit in
the way of producing, but also through the content of publications.
We understand that the scale is a more adequate model for understanding DJ for two central reasons: the
first is that it contemplates in a more harmonious way
the different nuances existing between press publications that use quantitative data. The second is
that the matrix contemplates dimensions that, in our view, are
indispensable for establishing a difference between
the production of content that is born and structured from
quantitative data and the production of content that
uses quantitative data. For, if in both cases there is
the use of data, the same cannot be said about
the competencies that the team employed to produce the


content. In sum, in our matrix, what matters is not exclusively the use of a technique, but how the technique can be
used by the team to produce journalistic content.
