**ABSTRACT**
**This study examines the state of Data Journalism scholarship within the African**
**context. The analysis is based on 11 published academic articles retrieved from,**
**Google Scholar, and Sage, ResearchGate and Taylor&Francis and a content analysis**
**process obtained from the existing literature. The review reveals that most of the**
**data journalism research on the African continent is empirical and qualitative in**
**nature. More so, the majority of the studies focused on the practice of data-driven**
**journalism by journalists in print newsrooms. This systematic review is a**
**significant contribution to data journalism research in literature. It serves as the**
**first attempt to aggregate the corpus of literature on data journalism within the**
**African context. It specifically exposes scholars to the focus of the current studies**
**on the phenomenon, the theories and methods that have been employed as well as**
**the gaps that are yet to be explored by researchers on the phenomenon.**


**Keywords:** Data-driven journalism, data journalism research, appropriation of datadriven journalism, journalism practice, Africa.


**INTRODUCTION**
The phenomenon of data journalism has received research attention as an innovation in the
journalism practice in recent years (Bhaskaran et al, 2022). This is because Coddington (2015)
has argued that journalism is now seeing a quantitative turn where journalists are expected to
integrate and interpret big data to tell a story-thus, the increased incorporation of data-driven
journalism. Data journalism is defined as a journalism practice that employs datasets,
computational tools, and algorithms to compose news stories (Heravi & Lorenz, 2020). Studies
have examined this phenomenon and have concluded that data journalism is a special form of
journalism that is different from the traditional form of journalism practice (eg, Borges-Ray,
2020; Coddington, 2015). These data-driven news stories make use of texts and audiovisuals

as well as data visualizations (Heravi, 2019). The authors add that data visualizations are
mostly interactive in nature and go beyond just a single image. Other studies have also
confirmed its adoption by media organizations (eg, Appelgren & Nygren, 2014; Beiler et al,
2020; Knight, 2015). Although there has been an increase in scholarly attention to this
phenomenon, there are growing concerns about inadequate studies on comparative case
studies. The focus of existing articles on the phenomenon has been on isolated case studies
(Ausserhofer, 2020).

However, even though data journalism has received the attention of scholars, most of the
attention has been given to studies that fall within the western context (Ferrer-Conill&
Cheruiyot, 2017). This can be attributed to the fact that the concept is still new in other parts of
the world and its practice is still in the developing stage. That notwithstanding, it is expedient
for scholars outside the west to provide scholarly works on the state of data journalism in their
various continents, regions, and countries. It is for this reason that this systematic review of
data journalism within the African continent becomes imperative. This will provide an
overview of the state of data journalism research within the continent by providing scholars
with the focus of the identified studies, methods, and theories that have been employed by
scholars and the various research gaps that require researchers’ attention. Bhaskaran (2022)
argues that recent examples of systematic reviews in the field of journalism are scant. This
paper, therefore, seeks to add to the scant systematic review in the field by systematically
reviewing the literature on data journalism as a contribution to the scant literature. More so,
although the phenomenon of data journalism has received some attention in relation to
systematic reviews (Ausserhofer, 2020; Bhaskaran, 2022; de Souza, 2018; Heravi, 2021) the
reviews are all situated outside the African continent. For instance, Heravi et al (2021)
conducted a systematic literature review on preserving data journalism. The paper aimed at
studying the current and past practices of how data journalism and related digital object
outputs are preserved and the recommendations that have been made for their preservation.
The review further provided recommendations for the preservation of dynamic visual objects
in data journalism. Similarly, Ausserhofer et al (2020) conducted a systematic review of the
literature on data journalism between 1996 and 2015. The study relied on forty (40)
publications to answer the various research questions posed in the study. The findings of the
review show an increase in data journalism research since 2010. The study revealed that before
2010, only a small number of isolated research publications focused on the issue of data
journalism. The year 2015 (12 publications) recorded the highest number of publications on
the subject followed by 2015 (11 publications) with 2010, 2011, and 2016 recording 2
publications each. In terms of the context of the studies, the US and the UK dominated with 26
of the studies focusing on case studies from these countries. There was no study from the
African continent reviewed.

Bhaskaran et al’s (2022) systematic review on teaching data journalism focused on data
journalism training to ascertain the present state of research on the subject. The study found
that data journalism educators face challenges such as tight journalism curricula schedules
which make it difficult to accommodate new courses, and the fear of delving into the realm of
mathematics by journalism students hinders the teaching of data journalism. The evidence,
therefore, shows that there appears to be no literature that has attempted to aggregate the
extant literature situated within the African continent to aid researchers to appreciate the

The purpose of this systematic literature review is to describe the development and current

Petticrew and Roberts (2008) assert that an important purpose for conducting a systematic
review is to identify gaps in the literature to assist future researchers to pursue or conduct
further studies in those areas. In line with this, the current study intends to establish the various
research gaps available in the existing corpus of literature on data journalism in Africa for
researchers to initiate attempts to direct their inquiries into the area.


**METHOD AND PROCEDURE**
Fink (2005) postulates that a systematic literature review is meant to provide a comprehensive
and reproducible method for the identification, evaluation, and synthesis of the existing body
of works produced by scholars and practitioners. In consonance with Fink’s assertion, this
review identified, evaluated, and synthesized the available body of literature on data journalism
within the African continent. Okoli (2015) advanced Fink’s postulation by adding that for a
systematic review to be rigorous, it must follow an unequivocal methodological approach that
explains the procedures by which the review was conducted. The author adds that the approach
must be comprehensive in such a way that the scope will include all available and relevant
materials. For Petticrew and Roberts (2006), systematic reviews help researchers to reconcile
existing evidence on a particular issue or subject to inform policy and practice. Consequently,
this review is an attempt to aggregate various perspectives from systematically collected
literature on data journalism in Africa to inform academics and practitioners on the current
developments and state of research in the field of data journalism. To identify the appropriate
methodology for this systematic review, the study reviewed methods that have been employed
by other scholars who have conducted systematic reviews in several fields including
journalism. In previous systematic literature reviews, researchers have employed various
methods. For instance, Ausserhofer et al (2020) adapted Massaro et al’s (2016) literature
review protocol in their systematic review on data journalism. Heravi et al (2021) in their
systematic review on preserving data journalism employed Okoli’s guidelines for conducting a
systematic review. Bhaskaran (2022) employed Gough, Oliver, and Thomas’s (2017) steps to
conduct a systematic review to select the corpus of literature for the study. Additionally, the
study applied Thomas and Harden’s (2008) framework to systematically analyze the collected
literature to answer the formulated research questions for the study.

This paper adopted a framework for a systematic review developed by Heravi et al (2021). The
author in his systematic review adopted ideas from Kitchenham and Charters (2007) and Okoli
(2015).

In line with the above procedure, the current study, first of all, identified the purpose of this
systematic review, which was to provide an aggregation of scholarly work on data journalism
within the African continent. This was necessary because there appears to be a gap in the
literature in this regard. Secondly, the procedure requires that a protocol should be drafted.
Because this review is authored by one person, the sole author developed the protocol for this
review. This included the purpose of the review, the objectives and research questions, and the
methods employed. These have been discussed throughout this review. The next step was to
search for literature. The researcher identified relevant research database that will be useful
for this study. To start with, the researcher used _Google Scholar, Sage, ResearchGate, and_
_Taylor&Francis_ as the research database to search for literature using specific keywords as well
as the Boolean operator OR. The keywords employed were: “Data Journalism in Africa” OR
“Data Journalism in Africa” OR “Data Based Reporting in Africa” OR “Computer-Assisted
Reporting in Africa” OR “Computational Journalism” OR “Computational Journalism and Data
Visualization. In the selection of the studies from the literature search, the researcher screened
the data to identify the scholarly works relevant to this study. The researcher purposively
selected all articles published in a journal and or as book chapters. This is because journal
articles and book chapters go through peer reviews and could be accorded some high level of
credibility. The search was not limited by date. All available journal articles and book chapters
that have been published within the African continent on data journalism were selected for this
study. Another inclusion criterion was on the issue of the language of publication. Article and
book chapter selection was limited to works published using the English language. This is
because the researcher has advanced knowledge in the English language only and can critically
assess such works written in the English language. The next procedure in this framework was
data extraction. In this case, the researcher is expected to read thoroughly the selected articles
to identify the areas needed for the systematic review. For this purpose, the study relied on a
qualitative content analysis procedure. The researcher went through all the selected articles
rigorously. The researcher color-coded the parts relevant for achieving the objectives of the
systematic review. For instance, the focus of the study, theories and methods employed were
code-colored. The close and continuous reading of the selected data was to satisfy the
immersion criterion proposed by Braun and Clarke (2018) to help the researcher familiarize
himself with the data. The researcher developed a table with columns that contained data on
the study focus, theories, methods, and the findings and discussion sections in each literature
(Bhaskaran et al, 2022). The last thing the researcher did was to synthesize the data extracted
from the selected works. This was done through an examination of the final extracts under each
column to translate the issues identified. Translation according to Thomas and Harden (2018)
as cited in Bhaskaran et al (2022) is the process of recognizing and organizing similar concepts

**Research rate**
The table above indicates that scholarly works on data journalism within the African context is
quite recent. In all, eleven articles were available for analysis in this systematic review. The first
article from the available literature was published in 2017. The table shows that only one article
was published in that year. One article was also published in 2018, six articles were published
in 2019 representing the highest for a year. The year 2020 witnessed a decline from six in 2019
to two in 2020. The year 2021 also saw one publication. As of September 2022, only one article
had been published in the year 2022 on the phenomenon of data journalism within the African
continent. In 2019, five of the six articles were book chapters from a Palgrave Macmillan
publication on data journalism in the global south. This dedicated book on data journalism
invited chapters from countries in the global south. This dedicated Volume perhaps justifies the
tremendous increase in publications on data journalism within the African continent. It is worth
noting that the last 4 years accounted for more than 80% of the selected publications This
shows an improvement in the interest of scholars in studying the phenomenon on the
continent. However, as indicated by scholars like Ya’u (2018) and Gondwe and White (2021),
scholarship on data journalism in Africa is on the low as compared to that of the west. With
regards to journal representation, the analysis reveals that five of the articles were published
by Palgrave Macmillan as book chapters in their book on data journalism in the global south.
One of the articles was published in the _Journal of Digital Journalism_, whiles Journals like _African_
_Journalism Studies,_ Journalism ( _SAGE), Journal for Information System, Technology Management_
and _Acta Academica_ published an article each. It is therefore worth noting that half of the
research papers found on the African continent were published by Palgrave Macmillan. Souza
et al (2018) argued in their systematic review paper that journals that publish works on data
journalism are not just from the journalism and mass communication discipline, but also from
other disciplines like Computer Science and Engineering. Within the context of this study, only
one study was published in a journal outside the journalism and mass communication
discipline. What this means is that scholars in data journalism research within the African
continent prefer to publish articles on the phenomenon in journals within the discipline.

**Focus of research**
The paper sought to identify the focus of scholarly articles on data journalism within the African
continent. The findings revealed that all the studies focused on the practice of data journalism
from different contexts. However, under this focus, three themes emerged. These are the
appropriation of data-driven journalism in work routines, the importance of data-driven
journalism in investigative journalism, and the perception of journalists on data-driven
journalism. In relation to the appropriation of the phenomenon by journalists, seven of the
articles focused on that phenomenon based on findings from 6 countries. For instance, Moyo
and Munoriyaran’s (2019) study explored the practice of data journalism in three selected
newspaper organizations in South Africa on the coverage of events leading up to the 2019
elections. The study found that the selected newspapers do not frequently practice data-driven
news reporting, however, they were pushed to do more of that during the period of study. The
study concluded that data journalism is yet to be seen as a newsroom culture in the selected
newspaper organisations. Similarly, Akinfemisoye-Adejare (2019) examined how print
journalists in Nigeria appropriated data-driven journalism practices in their coverage of the
2019 elections. The findings of the study reveal that Journalists did not shy away from
practicing some form of data-driven reporting during the 2019 elections. Munoriyarwa (2020)
also conducted a study on Data journalism updates in South Africa’s mainstream quotidian

basic knowledge on data-driven journalism only 1.6% understand and apply the principles of
data journalism in their news stories. The study concluded that there is very low awareness,
knowledge, understanding and application of data journalism principles among journalists in
Kano.

**Theoretical Review**
One objective of this review is to identify the various theories and frameworks that have been
employed by scholars on data journalism works within the scope of this review. The review of
the literature revealed that the phenomenon of data journalism within the African continent is
under-theorized. This is in line with Gondwe and White (2021). Thus, most of the scholarship
on data journalism on the continent relies on western theories to explain findings within the
continent. For instance, Moyo and Munoriyaran (2019) and Allen Munoriyarwa (2020)
anchored their studies on Schudson’s (1989) Sociology of News Production theory, whiles
Hassan Alhaji Ya’u (2018) draw on Innis and McLuhan’s (1964) Media Determinism theory.
Sewchurran and Hofmeyr (2020) on the other hand relied on the theory of the Public Sphere
and theory of Communicative Action by Habermas to critically reflect on digital tools in
journalism such as how data visualization has affected the journalism practice and journalism
education in South Africa. Okocha and Odeba (2022) also anchored their study on the Field
Theory by Lewin (1947) and the Normalization Theory. The analysis shows that out of the
eleven articles, six of the papers had no theoretical or conceptual frameworks that underpinned
the studies. The dominant theory, the Sociology of News Production explains how news work
is socially constructed and as such could be altered. Whiles the Media Determinism theory
employed by Hassan Alhaji Ya’u (2018) on the other hand explains the relationship between
technology and society. Thus, it argues that technology drives societal behaviors.

**Methodologies**
The analysis revealed that most of the papers used in this systematic review were empirical in
nature. Thus, ten of the papers were empirical while one was a literature review paper. The
dominant nature of empirical studies on the phenomenon within the African continent is in line
with findings from similar reviews from other contexts (Ausserhofer, 2020; Bhaskaran et al.,
2022; de Souza, 2018; Heravi, 2021). Most of the literature corpus employed qualitative
research as the approach for the study. In total, six of the eleven articles used the qualitative
research approach. The quantitative and mixed method had two papers each. Similarly, Heravi
(2021) and Bhaskaran (2022) also found that majority of the empirical studies on data
journalism employed the qualitative research approach. With regards to the research designs
adopted by these studies, the analysis shows that nine of the studies employed the case study
design while two of the studies employed textual analysis as a design. All those that used the
case studies approach presented the experiences of journalists on data journalism practices
within the continent (Gondwe & White, 2021; Moyo & Munoriyaran, 2019; Muneri, 2019; Ya’u,
2018). The textual analysis studies focused on analyzing news content to identify data
journalism elements in the news stories (eg, Akinfemisoye-Adejare, 2019; Munoriyarwa,2020).

**Research gaps**

phenomenon, a systematic review provides a definite representation of the main focus of
scholars on the state of the particular field’s development. For Gough et al (2012) a systematic
review also serves as an effective way of identifying prevailing lacunae in the current literature.
This is expedient because the recommended gaps help to direct the focus of future research.

Consequent to these assertions, one objective of this systematic review was to establish existing
research gaps in the aggregated literature on data journalism in Africa.

The current study has identified several gaps in the existing literature. To start with,
methodologically more than half of the studies relied on the case study design to conduct their
studies to understand the phenomenon in-depth within a particular context (eg, Cheruiyot,
Baack &Conil, 2019; Munoriyarwa, 2020; Ya’u, 2018). This reinforces the argument in the
literature by other scholars who have conducted a systematic review on subjects in the field of
journalism (Bhaskaran 2022) that there is a lack of diversity in methodology when it comes to
research on data journalism in Africa. The current study argues that future scholars interested
in understanding in a more in-depth manner how journalists have appropriated data-driven
journalism techniques in their news reporting should employ the ethnographic design to
perform newsroom ethnography to better appreciate the culture in the newsroom. Again, more
comparative studies should be encouraged since there appears to be no comparative study on
data journalism on the African continent. The focus has been on multiple and single case
studies. The current systematic review also realized that the two studies that sought to identify
the perceptions of journalism on data-driven journalism (ie, Gondwe &White, 2021; Ya’u, 2018)
situated their studies within the quantitative research approach. This study, therefore,
recommends that future studies should adopt the qualitative or mixed method approach to fill
the methodological gap in understanding audience perspectives on the phenomenon. This
approach will afford the researchers and research participants the opportunity to be more indepth and share their experiences.

Furthermore, the analysis shows that there is a contextual gap in the literature on the
phenomenon. Thus, the literature within the African continent is scant and as such researchers
should be encouraged to pay more attention to this growing phenomenon by directing attention
to countries and regional cases, especially from North and West Africa. As evident in the
analysis, only one county (ie, Nigeria) in West Africa was cited in three of the case studies (ie,
Akinfemisoye-Adejare, 2019; Okocha & Odeda, 2022; Ya’u, 2018). across the continent Moreso,
there appears to be no study from North Africa on the phenomenon under investigation.

Another gap identified is a knowledge gap. The focus of these studies within the African context
has been on the practice of data journalism. Some of the studies attributed the low progress
with the appropriation of data-driven journalism in journalistic practice to inadequacy in skills
and organizational commitment which can be attributed to the lack of appreciation for datadriven journalism (Moyo & Munoriyaran, 2019; Munoriyarwa, 2020; Ndubura, 2019; Ya’u,
2018). Studies have not focused on the relevance of data-driven journalism in the practice of
journalism, hence, the need for scholars to fill this gap by interrogating the phenomenon within
that context. Also, the issue of inadequate skills in data-driven reporting calls for studies that
will analyse journalism curriculum within the continent to ascertain whether there is a gap
between the growing needs in the newsroom and the classroom in relation to data journalism.
Another gap in focus has to do with the site or bounded cases employed in the existing studies.
Existing works that narrowed their scope on just one type of media paid attention to only the
print media as a case study for examining the practice of data-driven journalism on the African
continent. For instance, Akinfemisoye-Adejare’s (2019) study in Nigeria focused on newspaper
journalists. The study recommended that further studies should look at the broadcast media.
Similarly, Moyo and Munoriyaran (2019) recommended the same perspective in their study

**CONCLUSION**
This systematic review is a demonstration that scholarly attention has not been given to a
review on data journalism in Africa. This study is an aggregation and synthesis of research
studies that have been conducted on Data Journalism within the African context. The
comprehensive literature search identified 11 academic papers comprising five book chapters
and six journal articles. These articles were critically examined through content analysis to
identify research focus and gaps. The review shows that while research on data journalism in
Africa is still at its infant stage, there appears to be steady but slow progress in the last four
years. Findings also revealed that most of the studies have focused on the appropriation of datadriven journalism in journalistic works. Insights from the review suggest a gap in the literature
relative to journalism education and data journalism in Africa. Again, even though these studies
paid attention to the practice of the phenomenon, the various cases for the study are all from
the print media, leaving that of the case of journalists in the broadcast media unattended.
Furthermore, even though these studies focused mostly on case studies within the continent,
literature from the north and west of Africa is scant. Other areas of interest for further research
directions revealed by this review include the need for more interpretive data journalism
perspectives from journalists. This has the potential to reveal more persuasive evidence on
journalists’ views on the phenomenon on the continent. These are important observations for
future scholars because this paper, plausibly, appears to be the first to provide a review that
shows the state of data journalism research within the African context.


**LIMITATIONS**
It is worth reiterating that the primary purpose of this systematic review was to examine the
existing scholarly research works on data journalism in Africa. Curran (2007, as cited in
Bhaskaran (2022), postulates that a foremost challenge with systematic reviews in journalism
studies is the existence of grey data (thus unpublished and non-peer-reviewed materials such
as research reports, working papers, conference proceedings, theses, etc). This challenge
limited the scope of this systematic review. The scope of this review does not include grey data.
Paez (2017) argues that a systematic review that includes grey literature is able to
comprehensively gather more useful information about the topic and to further reduce the
biased nature of systematic reviews that only involves peer-reviewed research works.
However, the diverse nature of the available grey literature on the subject made it difficult for
them to be added to the corpus of literature used in this study. It is worth noting that a recent
study by Hartling et al (2017) found that the inclusion of grey literature in a systematic review
does not significantly change the results of the review. Lastly, another limitation of this study
was the fact that all articles selected were written in the English language. Thus, any studies
within the study context which was written in other languages were excluded. Future
researchers may want to address these shortcomings.
