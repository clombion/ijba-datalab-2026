## **Data-Driven Journalism: approximations between journalistic identity and hacker culture [1]**
###### **Marcelo Träsel**

**Abstract**
Data-Driven Journalism is a practice in the process of adoption by newsrooms around the world since the mid-2000s. It is a development of the concepts of Precision Journalism and Computer-Assisted Reporting, initially proposed in the 1970s and given impetus by the digitization process of newsrooms and the adoption of information access policies by governments and institutions. This paper hypothesizes that Data-Driven Journalism is configured as an overlap between the professional culture of journalists and hacker culture. The results of the preliminary stage of an ethnographic study conducted among Brazilian journalists in November and December of 2012 suggest that data-driven journalists share some practices and values with members of hacker culture.
Keywords
Data-Driven Journalism, Computer-Assisted Reporting, Ethnography


**Abstract**
Data Driven Journalism is a practice in the process of adoption by newsrooms around the world since the mid 2000s. It is a development of the concepts of Precision Journalism and Computer-Assisted Reporting, initially proposed in 1970, which has gained momentum by the digitization process of newsrooms and the adoption of policies on access to information by governments and institutions. Keywords
Data-driven Journalism, Computer-Assisted Reporting, Ethnography.

DDJ aims, precisely,

allow greater efficiency in the retrieval

in circulation across different platforms

audiovisual and image.
According to Gray _et al._ (2012), the first
reference to _Data Journalism_,
a term derived from
_Data-Driven Journalism_,
occurred in the article
"A fundamental way newspaper
sites need to change," published by
programmer Adrian Holovaty (2006)
on his personal website. In the proposal,
Holovaty recommends the incorporation of
database management techniques into the daily routine of newsrooms, as
a way of facilitating the reuse of
information collected in the daily
work of reporting.


So much of what local journalists
collect day-to-day is structured
information: the type of information
that can be sliced-and-diced, in an
automated fashion, by computers.
Yet the information gets distilled
into a big blob of text – a newspaper
story – that has no chance of being
repurposed. (HOLOVATY, 2006)


The alternative to this problem was
to create productive routines whose goal
was the development of structured databases on all subjects of
interest to a given newsroom.
Once the databases were
available to journalists, they could
be reused in the creation of "different
configurations for the information
and, including, new thematizations
and conceptual elements for the organization
and presentation of content"
(BARBOSA, 2007, p. 130), as required by the
DJDB paradigm.


Beyond the technical and narrative aspects, however, DDJ essentially seeks
to introduce scientific rationality into journalistic production routines. In the work _Precision Journalism_,
written between
1969 and 1970, journalist Philip Meyer
proposed the use of quantitative techniques
from the social sciences so that reporters
would err less frequently in
understanding the facts. Combining the resources
of sociology with the computing speed
of computers, a
return of objectivity to newsrooms was possible, in which
many journalists had drifted
toward interpretive approaches with the
New Journalism.


Instead of starting from a base of
personal conviction, ideology, or
conventional wisdom we can start
with intensive and systematic fact-
finding efforts. Such a suggestion may
seem to be a plea for a reactionary
return to the old ideal of objectivity,
but it has this difference: instead of
reporting competing viewpoints for
what they are worth, we could make
an effort to determine just what they
are worth. It is necessary to reduce
the size of the leap from fact to
interpretation, and to find a more
solid base of fact from which to leap.
(MEYER, 1973, p. 13)


The goal of applying techniques
such as sampling, questionnaires,
statistics and factorial analysis, among
others, was to transcend the concern
for objectivity as a mere "strategic
ritual" (TUCHMAN, 1993) against
internal and external criticisms of journalistic work,
seeking instead
a genuine objectivity. In other
words, the first adherents of
Precision Journalism saw the techniques
of social science as a path


to bring journalism as close as possible
to the truth.
There is a certain proximity between Meyer's proposal and the perspective of Park (1972),
who situates journalism midway
between common sense and scientific knowledge.
The former is based on the direct
experimentation of phenomena through the
senses; the latter, on the systematization and
ordering of phenomena.
"Knowledge of" (common sense)
can be shared only
partially through language, while
"knowledge about" is expected to have universality (science). Knowledge
concretized in the form
of news would be situated at some point
on the _continuum_ between common sense and
science. It would be a type of common sense
transformed by narrative techniques
into a "knowledge of" communicable to
an entire society: "...news fulfills, in a certain sense, for the public the same
functions it fulfills for the individual;
that is, it not only informs but
primarily orients, keeping each
and every person informed of what is happening"
(PARK, 1972, p. 176).
Meditsch (1997) also sees in
journalism a form of knowledge
positioned between science and common sense. It would tend, however, toward
the latter, given that its direction is to
universalize, not to specialize.
As a form of knowledge,
journalism suffers two criticisms from
academia: lack of analytical rigor and
inability to reveal the new.
This is due to the fact that it does not generate
new universal laws through the systematic
analysis of phenomena, but reveals
singularities from common sense,


"Precision Journalism" ended up being
abandoned in favor of "Computer-
Assisted Reporting" (CAR).
These practices received new
impetus in newsrooms in the decade of
the 2000s due, first, to
the development of free and
abundant tools for database analysis and, second, to the adoption
of information access policies by
governments and organizations, which
began permitting access to their databases in recent years (ANGÉLICO,
2012). In the first decade of the 2000s,
the expressions "Data Journalism" and
"Data-Driven Journalism" became
more common to refer to this
set of productive routines.


The premise of this work is that DDJ
constitutes a new formation
in the culture of the journalistic profession,
engendered over recent decades
by the progressive computerization of
productive routines in newsrooms, by
the availability of analytical tools
accessible to the lay public, by the
adherence of institutions and governments to information access policies and by the immersion
of contemporary journalists in
cyberculture.

Taken as a whole, the
observations from this ethnographic
approach to Brazilian data-driven
journalists, summarized here, suggest that
professionals engaged in the practice of
DDJ share characteristic traits
of hacker culture, such as the tendency toward
technology appropriation, the valuing
of freedom of information and the disposition
for collaborative work. On the other hand, they are not only embedded in the productive

a worldview common to journalists
of other specialties, but seem to
place great importance on the traditional values
of the professional culture of
journalism, judging by the statements in
favor of reporting work and the
criticisms of technological fetishism.
