## Introduction

This book began to take shape over coffees and conversations among several journalists and other media professionals in 2012. The initial idea was to create a Latin American counterpart to the "Data Journalism Handbook," the data journalism pocket book aimed almost entirely at English-speaking audiences. But our continent is different, and with the passage of time and observation of the reality of journalism in general and of data journalism in the region, the original objective shifted: how to bring together our diversity in a single text, without losing it, to create a tool that would be useful for new journalists and for those who are entering the world of telling stories supported by data.

Giving voice to the protagonists themselves was a choice from the very beginning of the project, and the result was encouraging: Spanish- and Portuguese-speaking journalists, designers, and programmers were eager to share their experiences and what was happening in their countries. The network of collaborators activated itself, and the Manual took on a life of its own, driven by the committed work of the members of our community.

The first and greatest difference in the practice of data journalism in our countries compared with the US and Europe is access to data. In countries where transparency principles are more deeply rooted, journalists obtain up-to-date data in appropriate formats as part of everyday practice. We do not. In the region there are states that have not yet imposed on themselves the starting point of discussing and formulating transparency and freedom-of-information laws. Even so, journalists in those countries generate stories and reveal to citizens information gathered from thousands of documents, often scattered or not conceived with the purpose of providing useful data to the public. The work of collecting, processing, and delivering data in adverse circumstances has produced a form of learning — not without ingenuity — that this book aims to rescue.

From the reflection that emerged during the editing of the articles we now present, it seems to us key for the future possibilities of quality data journalism that we are still traveling, with respect to access to public information, through territory that is still being formed. That situation — of building the road as we walk it — should keep us alert so as not to fall into the trap of easy shortcuts when processing data and creating visualization formats. Rigor, an obsession with details, and the determination to tackle issues of social impact are elements that will enable the generation of relevant, well-told, and well-documented stories. Stories that make a real contribution to the societies that inspire them.

## Data Journalism: History and Current Moment

**Ana María Ávila**

### The Origins of Data Journalism

"Don't believe anyone," the newspaper's editor would shout every time he came out of the newsroom and saw me walk by his office. It was my first job and he feared my naivety when listening to statements from public officials. Times have changed. Today, in addition to always carrying questions and hypotheses that lead to investigations, there are tools like databases that leave no room for doubt. They are precise and verifiable.

Clay Shirky, professor at New York University, sees current journalism as an ecosystem of investigative efforts that brings together various disciplines in a non-hierarchical network of collaborators to respond to citizens' accountability needs.

The event that enabled the takeoff of this journalism was the arrival of the internet, which dismantled the monopoly on news production held by print media, radio, and television. However, this history has very old roots.

In the first third of the twentieth century, there lay a latent concern to make journalism a measurable and verifiable trade. Max Weber in Germany and Robert E. Park in the United States raised the idea of a complementary relationship between the work of the journalist and the social scientist.

The great leap toward journalism grounded in scientific methodologies was made by Philip Meyer in 1959 when, tired of newspapers being filled with politician interviews and official press releases, he decided he would create his own source by building a database. He worked at The Miami Herald and chose a citizen complaint about the high cost of school fire and hurricane insurance. Meyer investigated the campaign financing of board members and demonstrated that 65 percent of the campaign contributions of the board president originated from insurance companies.

Meyer decided to specialize and, thanks to a Nieman fellowship, devoted himself to studying social research methods at Harvard University. In the summer of 1967, following the marches and riots in Detroit, he discovered that the demonstrators had university degrees and were middle-class. After winning the Pulitzer Prize for that work, precision journalism was born — along with its sociological research methods — as the precursor to what is known today as database or data journalism.

In 1975 the organization Investigative Reporters and Editors (IRE) was founded, whose contribution to investigative and database journalism has been fundamental in fostering in-depth, scientifically rigorous work.

Latin American countries were immersed in the struggle for freedom of expression, as many were going through dictatorships that prevented any free exercise of the press. Hence, specializations in databases and transparency seemed impossible given the difficulties of access to information.

### Data Journalism Today

With the arrival of the internet, access to information sources became immediate and free, which brought about an exponential leap in the use of technology for journalism. Between 2005 and 2007, digital tools and technologies began to emerge at accessible prices for handling data.

In 2009 the St. Petersburg Times received the Pulitzer Prize for the Politifact project. Thanks to the interest that three journalists had in the convergence between journalism and technology, the Hacks and Hackers movement was born in 2009. Aron Pilhofer of The New York Times, Rich Gordon of Northwestern University, and Burton Herman, former AP correspondent, created an international community to foster encounters between journalists and technology experts.

In 2010, the European Journalism Centre (EJC) organized the first data journalism conference in Amsterdam. The following year, at MozFest held in London, the initiative to write the first Data Journalism Handbook was born. In 2011, the Knight Foundation awarded $4.7 million to 16 projects related to innovation in the field of data journalism.
