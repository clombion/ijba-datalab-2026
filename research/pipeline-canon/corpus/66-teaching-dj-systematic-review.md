As an innovation in journalism practice, data journalism has received substantial academic attention in the recent past. Several scholars have examined data journalism’s
status as a subspecialty in journalism practice, its distinction from allied streams of practice (Borges-Rey 2020; Coddington 2015; 2018) and its adoption by different media industries (Appelgren and Nygren 2014; Beiler, Irmer, and Breda 2020; Knight 2015) causing
almost an explosion (Fink and Anderson 2015, 476) in terms of number of studies published over a short time period. Despite the increasing academic attention on data journalism, concerns about the prevalence of isolated case studies over broader comparative
studies have been raised (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; Wright, Zamith, and Bebawi 2019). In
this scenario, there is growing demand to initiate specific attempts to more theorising
and to create summaries to help chart the research roadmap to make the growing academic research on data journalism more comparative (Mutsvairo 2019; Wright, Zamith,
and Bebawi 2019).

The surge of academic attention in data journalism has effectively caused the growth
of a related subset of academic studies about data journalism education. However, there
has been a general absence of a systematic approach to take stock of the strategies
adopted by the educators, challenges faced by them, different approaches used in training and the nature of academic studies about data journalism education. According to
Splendore et al. (2016), “even the most complete review of data journalism literature
lacks a systematic analysis of educational strategies in the field”. Similar to general data
journalism research, there is a need to summarise the existing research on data journalism
training to point out specific directions it has taken and the areas that need more attention. This study is an attempt to review and summarise academic research on data journalism education to chart out the progress till date and to ascertain the future directions for
research in this regard.


Research Design


There have been similar attempts in journalism studies to take stock of the nature of the
growing academic research about different genres of journalism practices and associated
aspects like science journalism (Nguyen and Tran 2019), narrative journalism (van Krieken
and Sanders 2019), mobile journalism (López-García et al. 2019) and news consumers’ willingness to pay for news (O’Brien, Wellbrock, and Kleer 2020). There have been systematic
reviews to map the development of academic studies specifically on data journalism as
well (Ausserhofer et al. 2020; de Souza et al. 2018).
Academicians focused on journalism education research also have relied on systematic
reviews to bring together insights scattered across different studies in the past. For
instance, Hoag et al. (2002) reviewed available literature on the use of computers in pedagogy with special focus on journalism and mass communication education. King (1994)
reviewed research literature produced over two decades about magazine journalism to
chart out future directions. However, recent examples of systematic reviews about journalism education research are rare.
Inspired by these attempts, this study followed a systematic way of selecting academic
studies about teaching data journalism to chart out the development and the directions
of academic research about data journalism training. While Ausserhofer et al. (2020) did
not document literature on data journalism education as a significant sub-category of
data journalism research, another similar study has acknowledged that research about
data journalism education is also growing as a “meta-category” (de Souza et al. 2018).
There is a growing consensus that data journalism research should explore different
methodologies and comparative perspectives instead of restricting itself only to isolated
case studies (Mutsvairo, Bebawi, and Borges-Rey 2020). In this context, this study puts
forward an attempt to examine the growing academic literature on data journalism education with the help of a systematic review.
According to Petticrew and Roberts (2006, 21), systematic reviews in social sciences are
called for “when a general overall picture of the evidence in a topic area is needed to
direct future research efforts”. Along this line of thought, this review attempts to describe
the present status of academic research on data journalism training. The following
research questions were formulated before reviewing the corpus of works collected.


RQ1: How has the peer-reviewed, academic literature on data journalism education evolved
in terms of publication activity?


RQ2: What are the major publication outlets and influential works in terms of citations?


Hoag et al. (2002) in their review of studies about the use of computers in journalism
and mass communication education examined certain specific aspects of the studies like
student attributes and faculty attitude among other factors. On a similar line, the present
study formulated certain additional research questions to summarise relevant findings
from the studies in the corpus.


RQ3: What are the challenges of teaching data journalism?


RQ4: What are the methods and recommended best practices in teaching data journalism?


Research about journalism education has been marked as being devoid of contextualised
focus, mainly with an obsession to curriculum reviews and producing descriptive
accounts of “doing journalism education” (Deuze 2006). Most often, other stakeholders
like students are ignored by the research agenda. In this context, this review also
attempted to examine whether the research on data journalism education sheds any
light on students’ perception of data journalism and data journalism training. For this
purpose, one more research question was formulated as follows:


RQ5: According to the studies in the corpus, how do students perceive data journalism
training?


One of the purposes of systematic review is to identify research gaps so as to enable the
future researchers to pursue research in those areas (Petticrew and Roberts 2008). As data
journalism training is an emerging field, the researchers intended to find out the research
gaps present in the existing literature and therefore, the following research question was
formulated:


RQ6: What are the gaps that exist in research about data journalism teaching?


Method


Sweet and Moynihan (2007) define systematic review as a transparent means for “gathering, synthesising and appraising the findings of studies on a particular topic”. Kahn et al.
(2003) define systematic reviews as a research article which identifies relevant studies,
appraises and summarises their results. Traditional literature reviews, unlike systematic
ones, lack rigorous, transparent and replicable “explicit methodology” (Gough and
Elbourne 2002). Systematic reviews are quite important in helping researchers to make
sense of the ever-growing research literature on any particular topic. If the researchers
choose to work based on the findings or evidence shared by a single case study, they
run the risk of getting biased. According to Jesson, Matheson, and Lacey (2011), “the
purpose is to combine information from various sources to provide more data to
answer a specified research question without the need to set up a new study.”
A method which was primarily prevalent in evidence-based medical research, systematic review is now being used across disciplines. However, there are certain challenges in
the use of systematic reviews in social sciences especially considering the difficulty to


systematise the process due to inconsistent use of keywords, abstracts and definitions
and the issue of compatibility of indexing databases (Curran et al. 2007). Nonetheless,
the method is potent to bring together findings scattered in studies which took place
over a period of time. According to Petticrew and Roberts (2006, 33), systematic
reviews are helpful to “reconcile the evidence in order to inform policy and practice”.
In line with this argument, this review attempts to bring together insights from systematically collected academic literature about teaching data journalism to answer specific
research questions so as to inform journalism educators and practitioners about known
issues and solutions.
According to Gough, Oliver, and Thomas (2012), “identifying and describing relevant
research” is one of the three crucial steps involved in systematic reviews. Since the intention of our review was to examine studies about teaching data journalism, we followed a
three-phase, systematic procedure during our literature search. Primarily, we searched
Google Scholar, Web of Science, Scopus, Science Direct, JSTOR & ERIC databases using
the specific set of keywords as exact phrases and using the Boolean operator OR. The
search query was: “teaching data journalism” OR “data journalism education” OR “data
journalism training”. These keywords produced a total of 510 results (12 results were
from WoS, 223 from Scopus, 273 from Google Scholar and one each from ERIC and
JSTOR). Science Direct did not return any result for the query, so we searched it with
“data journalism” which fetched 70 results. But on closer inspection, none of those was
related to data journalism education.
Each search result was examined by reading through the associated information
snippet. In this phase, due to limited language proficiency, we eliminated the nonEnglish results. To keep it systematic, we selected only those peer-reviewed journal
papers and conference publications from the search results which were associated with
data journalism education or training. For this purpose, the abstracts of the publications
and the associated keywords were examined. This selection process also eliminated
studies which had only a mention of any of the three keywords, with the major focus
of the study being something else. For example, if a commentary article about global
data journalism practice mentions data journalism training in one paragraph only, it
was excluded from the corpus.
In the second phase, studies citing these selected studies (as per Google Scholar indexing of citations) were examined separately and relevant studies which fell within the
aforementioned criteria were selected. All together there were 277 studies citing the
studies included in the initial corpus, out of which we identified 72 relevant studies
related to data journalism education. Many were duplicates, which were eliminated at
the last stage. During the first and second phase, it was found that certain journals like
Asia Pacific Media Educator (APME) and Journalism & Mass Communication Educator
(JMCE) published multiple studies which were relevant to the selection criteria. So, the
websites of these journals were visited and a separate search was carried out using the
keyword “data journalism”. There were 29 and 27 results for the search in APME and
JMCE, respectively. After excluding book reviews and editors’ introductions from the
results, studies which were about teaching data journalism were selected if they were
not already part of the selected corpus. To make the selection of corpus for the review
comprehensive and systematic, the reference list of the selected studies was examined
to find any relevant study (journal article or conference proceedings) which was not


yet part of the corpus. In the end, the selected corpus was checked again to eliminate
duplicates. Finally, a corpus of 29 studies was selected for review. Figure 1 Illustrates
the protocol followed in this study to select the literature corpus for the review.
After finalising the literature corpus to be reviewed, the researchers broadly followed
the framework suggested by Thomas and Harden (2008) to systematically analyse the literature corpus to answer the research questions formulated. Thomas and Harden (2008)
suggest a three-step approach to carry out the thematic analysis for systematic reviews of
qualitative studies. In the first stage, the literature corpus is coded through a line-by-line
reading. These codes are used for formulating descriptive themes and analytical themes
at later stages. However, this approach is primarily used when the intention is to inductively ascertain the recurring themes in the literature corpus. In the present study, our
intention was not to examine the recurring themes in the academic literature on data
journalism education but to identify the presence of themes which were relevant to
the research questions formulated. Because of this difference, the line-by-line reading
of the corpus was carried out, but it was not freely coded. Instead, excerpts identified
as relevant to the themes associated with the research questions were coded accordingly.
Authors themselves were the coders and carried out a general reading of the whole
corpus individually as part of performing immersion (Braun and Clarke 2006) to familiarise
with the nature of the literature corpus. In the next stage, a content matrix was created in
tabular form where each column was devoted to the broader themes in the RQs (like challenges, methods of teaching, etc.) and also other bibliographic data for each publication
(year, author, author affiliations, type of publication, etc.). Coders placed the excerpts from
the individual studies in the literature corpus in the relevant columns of the matrix, effectively isolating the relevant excerpts according to themes. In this process, more importance was given to the methods, findings and discussion sections of individual studies.


Figure 1. Steps followed in the finalisation of the literature corpus.


Later, to ensure validity, the coding done by different coders was shared among themselves and areas of disagreements were identified and discussed. The finalised excerpts
under each thematic category were examined again to “translate” the concepts identified.
According to Thomas and Harden (2008), translation is “the process of taking concepts
from one study and recognising the same concepts in another study, though they may
not be expressed using identical words”. After this stage, the concepts and findings
from different studies were summarised according to their similarities and differences
to synthesise the findings of the review.


Results


Growth of Academic Literature on Data Journalism Education


In order to answer the RQ1, we examined how the corpus of academic publications about
data journalism education grew over time. The growth of academic literature specifically
related to data journalism education can be observed from 2015 onwards. However, academic literature related to quantitative literacy or computer assisted reporting in journalism education can be traced back from the early 90s onwards (for instance, DeFleur and
Davenport, 1993). Due to the comparative novelty of the term “data journalism” (Gynnild
2014, 719), journal publications carrying this specific keyword associated with journalism
education started appearing in 2014 only, as per the corpus of studies we examined. This
trend is similar to the sudden growth of academic interest in data journalism practice.
According to Ausserhofer et al. (2020), academic literature on data journalism started
growing since 2010, with 2014–2015 marking a steep increase. de Souza et al. (2018)
documented that studies related to data journalism practice peaked in 2016. These
trends show that academic attention on data journalism education could be reflective
of the general trend in data journalism research, as a result of which more studies
started coming out by 2015–2016. However, this may not be the case if grey literature
about the subject is included where self-published books, reports and blog posts pertaining to data journalism education can be spotted before this period as well. Table 1 shows

Challenges


To answer the RQ3, we examined the corpus to identify the challenges in teaching data
journalism reported by different studies. Differing skill levels among the participants is a
major issue (Britt 2019; Davies and Cullen 2016; Feigenbaum et al. 2016; Graham 2015;
Plaue and Cook 2015; Treadwell et al. 2016). This challenge was more prevalent in
project-based or workshop-based approaches where experiential and collaborative learning were the focus. Some relied on flipped classes and online tutorials (Davies and Cullen
2016; Plaue and Cook 2015) or dedicating preliminary classes for bringing all participants
to acquire basic skill levels (Treadwell et al. 2016) to get a common ground in university


classes. Workshops implemented “scaffolded tool training” (where every participant was
briefed on preliminary tools before each session), and creation of shared definitions for
terms among the participants to address this issue (Feigenbaum et al. 2016).
Data journalism is a vast stream which demands an ensemble of skillsets and expertise
to complete the workflow, especially in case of advanced projects. This aspect has raised a
challenge in formulating an exact boundary to ascertain what skillsets to focus on and
which tools to be included in the training (Bradshaw 2018; Treadwell et al. 2016). This confusion intensifies when it comes to the question of whether to teach coding in data journalism training (Lewis, McAdams, and Stalph 2020). This issue also manifests in other
forms especially due to the interdisciplinary nature of data journalism. For instance,
due to the interdisciplinary nature, data journalism trainers lack expertise in some
related disciplines like Computer Science or Data Sciences. This has led to the outsourcing
of data journalism teaching to departments and faculty outside journalism departments
(Broussard 2015; Green 2018; Treadwell et al. 2016). In many cases, the background of the
educators decided the nature of the courses offered. Educators from a Computer Science
background tend to offer courses leaning more towards coding and data analysis, while
educators with journalism background offer courses which are more focused on storytelling—often with tool-based visualisations (Heravi 2019; Lewis, McAdams, and Stalph
2020). According to Heravi (2019, 2018) popular data journalism modules try to focus
on the complete data journalism workflow, often failing to impart advanced skillsets.
Due to the interdisciplinary nature of the practice, educators find it difficult to keep
track of rapid changes and satisfactorily meet the up-skilling demand. The interdisciplinary nature of the stream also raises issues in cases where the existing educational framework is not updated (Kashyap and Bhaskaran 2020) or lacks relevant educational
resources (Radchenko and Sakoyan 2014; Splendore et al. 2016).
Even though data journalism has a predominant focus on the use of technology, it is
still a journalistic practice at the core. So the confusion regarding the focus of the programme is not just about what kind of technical skills to impart or which technological
tools to focus on, it is also about striking a balance between imparting editorial or
story-telling skills along with technical skills besides formulating befitting assessment criteria for the same (Bradshaw 2018; Treadwell et al. 2016; Yang and Du 2016).
Accommodating a new programme in the tight schedule of the existing academic framework is also a challenge (Davies and Cullen 2016; Green 2018; Hewett 2016). Considering the learning curve which many journalism students perceive as a steep one,
instructors have to devote more time for close monitoring of student projects and
solving issues (Treadwell et al. 2016). Students also find it difficult to dedicate time for
a new paper, especially with the already compactly packed schedule of a journalism programme (Weiss and Retis 2018; Zhu and Du 2018).
Ensuring effective and fair student participation in the collaborative project-based
method is another challenge reported by different scholars. Enabling student involvement
right from the initial point of formulating a research framework for the data project and
identifying datasets viable for generating different story-leads are different challenges
documented in the literature. Any flaw in the research design at the initial point will
seriously affect the outcome (Graham 2018; Treadwell et al. 2016) and often student
work is prone to inaccuracies, especially considering their unproven numeracy skills
(Graham 2015; Treadwell et al. 2016). Due to the differing levels of expertise in the


cohort, the advanced portions of the workflow (like data analysis, verification and validation,
etc.) are difficult to be equally assigned to all individuals in that cohort (Graham 2015).
Identifying datasets which hold currency over the period of time by which the student
projects would produce final products is a challenge (Graham 2018). Getting access to such
datasets was also reported as a challenge in certain cases (Treadwell et al. 2016). When
access was not an issue, rectifying the distortions in the dataset was difficult at times (Feigenbaum et al. 2016). Math-fear in students is a formidable challenge mentioned in many
studies (Bradshaw 2018; Davies and Cullen 2016; Davies 2018; Fuller 2018; Graham 2018;
Hannis 2018). In some cases, students were hesitant to clean or to transform datasets
since they perceived such wrangling as tampering with official data (Hannis 2018).


Methods of Teaching


To answer the RQ4, we examined the methods of teaching data journalism as shared by
the publications in the literature corpus. The studies in the corpus examined for this
review differed in their approach and format, but most of them documented different
methods of teaching data journalism. While the case studies (for example, Davies 2018;
Treadwell et al. 2016) invariably included details of the respective approaches adopted
by specific institutions or academicians, other studies put forward normative suggestions
on teaching data journalism either based on experience (Bradshaw 2018) or based on
different kinds of empirical data (Kashyap and Bhaskaran 2020; Splendore et al. 2016).
Many studies point out that university departments are not the only places where data
journalism training is taking place (Feigenbaum et al. 2016; Splendore et al. 2016).
However, most of the case studies in the literature corpus dealt with data journalism training in universities (Davies and Cullen 2016; Graham 2015). Other means of imparting data
journalism education like workshops and short duration courses offered by vocational
institutions and other organisations are prevalent in Europe. Data journalism courses
offered by universities in Europe are generally holistic (include history, etc.), as
opposed to courses offered by other institutions which are more practical/skill oriented.
According to Splendore et al. (2016), “at the higher education level, the focus is on statistical and mathematical topics, whereas shorter courses or workshops try to combine
statistical and editorial skills”.
In the universities, according to the literature corpus, use of project-based approach is
a common method in data journalism teaching, with slighter variations in its implementation. This approach follows a teaching hospital method by engaging students to work
on a data story project, with each of them ideally going through different steps in the production of a data story. The focus on experiential learning in this approach is similar to
what is done in the workshop mode (Feigenbaum et al. 2016). In this approach, multiple
authors suggested hackerspace workshop as an effective method to bring together collaborations from different disciplines (Feigenbaum et al. 2016) and to help students get
cutting-edge knowledge (Bradshaw 2018).


Recommended Practices


We examined the studies in the corpus to see the best practices recommended, especially
to tackle the challenges identified. In order to address the difficulty of accommodating


data journalism into an existing journalism programme, a major recommendation is to
integrate lessons on basic numeracy into existing units of the programme, especially
during the first year (Davies 2018; Green 2018; Lewis 2020; Lewis, McAdams, and Stalph
2020; Zhu and Du 2018).
In the studies examined in this review, scholars reported several techniques they
used for addressing or alleviating the “fear of maths” in students. Several of these
techniques reported across different studies have certain common threads. For
instance, improving student motivation and confidence was a particular strategy
used by different academicians. The confidence building strategy included the “guerrilla method” where data journalism was introduced to the students as investigative
journalism projects (Graham 2018) or as a routine practice with the main focus on
story-telling which is applied across beats (Bradshaw 2018; Hannis 2018). The latter
idea was conveyed by showing examples from mainstream national press across
different beats where data-based story-telling was prominently used. Another
approach involved discussing the issue with the students. Davies (2018) reported
holding a discussion session in the class during the early part of the programme
about maths aversion among students.
Students also have anxieties about dealing with datasets. However, there are differing
opinions on using datasets as a pedagogical tool to impart skills for data analysis and visualisation. Some scholars (like Britt 2019; Burns and Matthews 2018; Hannis 2018) recommend the use of actual datasets instead of summaries to help students develop
data interrogation skills. Many others prefer giving cleaned summary datasets in the
beginning to avoid scaring off the students (Bradshaw 2018). The former approach
focuses more on skill-building required to critically approach and manage large datasets,
while the latter is more focused on inculcating story-telling skills in students. Fuller (2018)
suggests offering hands-on practical lessons with datasets so that the students move from
being scared by the technical details to developing confidence to tackle data using
spreadsheet applications like Microsoft Excel. This confidence may facilitate further
exploration to develop individual expertise.
Confidence building is also carried out by asking students to explore the curriculum, by linking the project or assignment activities to a social or political aspect in
which the students are affectively involved and by rewarding engagement instead
of deducting marks for wrong answers (Davies 2018; Fuller 2018). Collaborative learning and hands-on practical sessions are also recommended as confidence-building
measures by several scholars. Group activities enabling peer-to-peer experiential
learning are recommended (Davies 2018), especially through practical sessions in
data collection and analysis (Fuller 2018; Graham 2015; Treadwell et al. 2016).
Other forms of collaboration like industry collaboration and hackathon workshops
have been recommended since they help the students to make a comparative assessment about their expertise and knowledge, even with working journalists, which
often results in building confidence and knowledge (Bradshaw 2018; Feigenbaum
et al. 2016). Moreover, industry collaboration which would enable publication of
the outcome of the student project is also recommended as a strategy to motivate
students undergoing data journalism training (Treadwell et al. 2016). Correspondingly,
students also prefer hands-on training and interdisciplinary interactions in data journalism training (Zhu and Du 2018).


Student Perception of Data Journalism Training


In order to answer the RQ5, we examined whether the studies in the corpus dealt with the
student perception of data journalism programmes. While not a major repetitive theme,
students’ perception of data journalism training and their response to the courses offered
were reported by certain studies. While Weiss and Retis (2018) and Zhu and Du (2018)
exclusively examined students’ perception of data journalism training, others like
Hewett (2016) examined it along with other aspects of teaching data journalism. A
common aspect of student perception reported by several studies in the literature
corpus is students’ fear of mathematics. Students’ also perceive data journalism as a
trendy, futuristic practice which offers better job prospects.
Even those studies which did not exclusively deal with student perception have invariably mentioned students’ fear of math as a major challenge in teaching data journalism
(Bradshaw 2018; Davies 2018; Fuller 2018). This fear of math has been reported and
explored by past studies about imparting quantitative literacy in journalism schools (for
example, Nguyen and Lugo-Ocando 2016). This is attributed to the perception that journalism is more related to writing than to math (Mencher 1995 as cited in Maier 2002).
While the math-fear seems to be widespread across educators and students, the idea
that journalism students lack numerical abilities is not necessarily based on conclusive
empirical evidence. On the contrary, journalism students, in certain countries like US,
perform on par with other major students in their SAT scores in mathematics (Dunwoody
and Griffin 2013).
The studies in the literature corpus which specifically examined students’ perception of
data journalism training also point to this direction. Although there is math-fear, it is not
the only aspect of students’ perception about data journalism training. Weiss and Retis
(2018) found that majority of the journalism students in the United States at the undergraduate and graduate level (64.3%) who participated in the study expressed interest
in enrolling if a course on data journalism was available. Similarly, they found that
factors like major of study, gender, affinity to certain specialised streams in journalism
might influence students’ interest in learning data journalism. Zhu and Du (2018) found
that science major students performed better in terms of the outcomes of the activities
in the data journalism course. Science majors were able to improve their theoretical
and practical understanding of data journalism more than the journalism major students.
This difference was more pronounced in the case of data analysis and data visualisation
capabilities. According to Yang and Du (2016), language journalism students generally
showed less interest in data journalism and in working with data as opposed to those
in majors like financial journalism.
Multiple studies found that gender had no significant impact on students’ interest in
data journalism training. While Zhu and Du (2018) found that gender had no significant
role in deciding the outcome of individual students in the data journalism course, Yang
and Du (2016) reported that gender had no significant impact on students’ interest,
concept knowledge and career expectations about data journalism. However, male students had comparatively better knowledge about data journalism before enrolment in
the course.
Career expectations associated with learning a cutting-edge practice were a major
motivation for enrolling in data journalism training, especially for journalism students.


Students considered enrolment in the course and learning the techniques as something
which improved their chances of employment (Plaue and Cook 2015; Weiss and Retis
2018) and reputation (Hewett 2016). This trend also reflects in students who are not interested in learning data journalism. In a survey among 121 students enrolled in journalism
courses at Hong Kong Baptist University, Yang and Du (2016) found that even when only
less than half (45.5%) of the respondents were interested in data journalism training, more
than 51.2% respondents knew about the practice, and 84.3% respondents associated data
journalism with high career expectations. However, this career expectation significantly
differed between students depending on their year of study. They found that senior students associated higher career expectations with data journalism than the juniors.
Students prefer small cohorts and more hands-on training in data journalism training
(Hewett 2016; Zhu and Du 2018). Learning computer science and data-related courses
was generally perceived as difficult. However, those with basic training in these fields
were more comfortable with the idea.


Research Gaps


By taking stock of the growth and development of studies in a particular stream, systematic literature reviews lay out a clear picture about the major focus of the developments in
the field. They are also effective in identifying existing gaps in the research attempts
(Gough, Oliver, and Thomas 2012). Such suggestions are helpful to mould future research
attempts in the field. Keeping this in mind, RQ6 was formulated to identify existing gaps in
the research literature about data journalism education.
One of the critiques about the nature of research about journalism education is that it
is very much descriptive and normative (Becker 2003 as cited in Deuze 2006), rather than
empirical and systematic. Deuze (2006) pointed out that most of the academic research
on journalism education tends to be very specific case studies or very generic descriptions
based on personal experiences of performing the role of a journalism educator. The
corpus of the present study shows that the research about data journalism education
also seems to follow a similar pattern with the major chunk of the corpus being case
studies written by instructors themselves or normative suggestions from academicians.
This shortcoming reflects in the general lack of methodological diversity and absence
of references and frameworks from allied areas of theory or research. Approaches which
are of interdisciplinary nature are largely absent in the literature. Similarly, studies which
developed strong theoretical frameworks from education (like critical pedagogy) are also
rare. The reluctance to use diverse methods to generate empirical data about data journalism training also contributes to the research gap. For instance, in general research
about data literacy education, researchers employed diverse methods like digital ethnography of the online community of young learners (Hautea, Dasgupta, and Hill 2017) or
social design experiment examining data visualisations created by adolescents (Stornaiuolo 2020) to study about students developing critical data literacies. Such methodological diversity is absent in data journalism education research so far. Utilising
experimental studies or systematic qualitative approaches in this regards, especially to
understand the perception of stakeholders like students could be useful in developing
usable insights about charting the future course of data journalism training.


Learning an innovative practice is not just about developing technological competencies, but also about understanding the practice within its ethical context. It can be seen
that the corpus had no studies which examined the methods to impart ethical awareness
needed for data journalists and the challenges to it. Splendore et al. (2016) point out that
ethical aspects of data journalism were largely absent in the course materials reviewed by
them and that data journalism educators lacked awareness about ethical data journalism
practices. This trend is visible in case studies as well where the ethical aspect finds only a
fleeting mention or no mention at all. This is understandable considering these case
studies emerged at a point where the practice was getting adapted. This is not to
claim that the studies in the corpus are silent about ethics. For instance, Lewis et al.
(2020) argue that lessons about ethical practices are necessary in data journalism training
as these enable students to understand the importance of accountability, transparency
and privacy issues. They recommend the inclusion of ethical lessons relevant to data journalism practice in the existing courses on media ethics. Bradshaw (2018), Feigenbaum
et al. (2016) and Kashyap and Bhaskaran (2020) also mention about the importance of
including ethical aspects in data journalism training, though this is not the major focus
of these studies.


Discussion and Conclusion


Most of the studies which were examined in the review consider data journalism education primarily as a means of imparting technological and management skills required
to deal with datasets, rather than developing critical data literacy in students. Despite
many studies acknowledging the importance of inculcating data literacy as part of data
journalism programmes (Heravi 2018; Treadwell et al. 2016), most of the studies in the
corpus primarily focus on imparting technological skillsets (Fuller 2018; Treadwell et al.
2016). This is a general trend in data literacy education-related research in other academic
disciplines also. For instance, reviewing studies about data literacy practices of higher
education faculty, Raffaghelli and Stewart (2020) found that data literacy is overly perceived as “technical abilities like extracting data, making statistical analysis, creating visualizations, interpreting and reporting appropriately”. A similar reductionist approach,
along with a kind of naive data optimism which considers datasets as neutral pedagogical
tools is reflected in many studies in the corpus.
This approach is evident in the general indifference towards the role of datasets as
material artefacts in data journalism education. For instance, there is a widespread tendency in many studies to consider data or datasets as passive, value-free elements
which are used by students to develop technological skills. Many studies acknowledge
the status of datasets as a new pedagogical tool in journalism education and express
certain concerns about their use. However, most of these concerns are logistical in
nature like relevance (Graham 2018), access (Treadwell et al. 2016) or cleaning (Feigenbaum et al. 2016), not exactly related to their role as “mediational artefacts” in the teaching learning process (Raffaghelli et al. 2020). This denial of the materiality of datasets as
techno-social assemblages (Dixon-Roman 2016; Stornaiuolo 2020) is problematic, since
such an approach stops short of helping students to explore the role played by the
techno-social data infrastructures which formulated the dataset and their influence on
the meaning-making process. This shortcoming needs to be addressed especially


considering the necessity to take data journalism education from just enabling technical
skills to developing critical data literacies (Gray, Gerlitz, and Bounegru 2018).
However, it does not mean that the critical data literacy aspect is completely missing
from the picture. Due to the inherent nature of journalism as a story-telling practice, educational attempts to help students in interacting with datasets invariably involve a
meaning-making process at the students’ end when they explore potential story angles
(for instance, Hannis 2018). Students’ interaction with datasets enabling such meaningmaking to support an argument is considered a major aspect of developing data literacy
by many scholars (Bhargava 2019; Fontichiaro and Oehrli 2016). However, there is no discernable evidence shown in these studies to believe that present pedagogical approaches
in data journalism education focus on developing deeper critical data literacy. There are
indeed exceptions to this where the pedagogical measures demand more reflexive
approach from students considering the value laden nature of datasets (Burns and Matthews 2018) or when specific pedagogical measures are called for with critical data literacy in focus (Kashyap and Bhaskaran 2020). But these are a minority, not a wider trend.
Findings of the review establish the existence of challenges like adequately addressing
the interdisciplinarity of data journalism, both in the classroom and at the academic management level. For instance, the lack of properly trained educators in journalism departments to deal with data journalism courses is a problem faced by higher education
organisations. This issue is deeper where the educators need to be not just technically
skilled to manage the data workflow, but should be literate to deal with the datasets. Generally, in higher education research, there is a growing concern about the data literacy of
educators, considering the omnipresent datafication of educational activities. However,
most of such studies are about learning analytics and how educators are able to
manage and technically handle data (Raffaghelli and Stewart 2020) and to use their
data literacy to enable student success (Yang and Li 2020). Critiques to this approach
argue that it is based on a reductionist, naive instrumentalism which ignores the
complex context of datafication and data literacy competencies. While this is the case
with higher education research in general, such concerns about data journalism educators’ data literacy are largely absent in the studies reviewed.
The review findings show that academic research on data journalism education is still
over-dependent on certain methodological approaches like case studies and there is a
definite lack of diverse approaches. Notwithstanding the interdisciplinarity of data journalism, there is no observable interdisciplinary approach present in the research
studies, both in terms of author-collaboration or in terms of the constructive use of interdisciplinary theoretical frameworks or methodological approaches. With more interdisciplinary explorations, the insights derived through data journalism education research has
more potential to contribute to an increasing corpus of academic research on the development of pedagogical approaches to impart critical data literacies and to address the
concerns related to datafication, especially in a situation where there is a general lack
of research about pedagogies of data literacy (Wolff, Wermelinger, and Petre 2019). But
presently, academic research on data journalism research is restricting its scope mainly
due to its reluctance to make interdisciplinary theoretical engagement and
collaborations.
Looking at the journalism studies scenario, there is a growing call for bringing in more
research which can balance the present Western-centrism in data journalism research.


However, the present review shows that attempts in this direction are still scarce. For
instance, in their systematic review of academic research about data journalism, Ausserhofer et al. (2020) found that non-Western research published in English language was
absent based on the literature selection protocol followed in their study. However,
things have improved since with specific initiatives in general data journalism research
in the form of anthologies about the status of data journalism practice in the Global
South (Mutsvairo, Bebawi, and Borges-Rey 2020) as well as several country/regionspecific case studies from Pakistan (Jamil 2021), India (Kashyap, Bhaskaran, and Mishra
2020), South Africa (Munoriyarwa 2020) and the Arab region (Fahmy and Attia 2021)
among other similar works coming out recently. However, such a trend is not yet
widely seen in research about data journalism education. There is especially an
absence of such comparative studies from the Global South.


Limitations


The primary intention of this review was to systematically examine existing research on
data journalism education. One major challenge with systematic reviews in social
science disciplines like journalism studies is the existence of considerable amount of
grey literature (Curran et al. 2007, 304). This was a challenge which has limited the
scope of this review. Formulating a search protocol to systematically include relevant
grey literature available in the form of industry reports or non-peer-reviewed research
reports brought out by academic institutions was beyond the scope of this study.
Some scholars argue that inclusion of grey literature is important especially to evaluate
all useful information about the topic being researched and also to reduce publication
bias involved in systematic reviews which includes only peer-reviewed literature (Paez
2017). However, common ways of finding non-peer-reviewed literature (like Google
search) have been flagged to have a filter bubble effect due to the presence of personalisation algorithms, thus leading to selection bias in systematic reviews (Curkovic 2019).
Moreover, the available grey literature about the topic was very vast and diverse (in
the form of blog posts, reports by academic and other institutions, etc.) which made it
challenging to include them in the corpus. Their diversity also created an issue of
mutual-comparison, especially when trying to translate the contexts of the insights
shared by different studies. Generally, there are concerns about the inclusion of grey literature in systematic reviews. Due to the opaque nature of the way in which knowledge
or evidence in those was collated or the role played by commissioning organisations in
deciding the final version of certain types of grey literature, making their findings
biased (Curran et al. 2007, 307; Jesson, Matheson, and Lacey 2011, 55–56). A recent
study found that inclusion of unpublished, non-peer-reviewed literature in systematic
reviews did not bring significant differences in the results of meta-analyses (Hartling
et al. 2017). Due to this limitation, works like Berret and Phillips (2016) were not included
in the literature corpus of this review.
Another major limitation of the literature selection and thus the findings of the study is
the exclusion of studies which were published in other languages. During the literature
search, we have spotted several studies which may have been relevant to the corpus,
but were in other languages (for example, Domínguez 2014; Tejedor et al. 2020).
Reading those studies was not possible because of the limited language proficiency in


our research team. Such an exclusionary criterion has in effect made the final literature
corpus more focused on studies coming from English-speaking countries mainly from
the West. This systematic review has only one study from the Global South. Future
research endeavours may address these shortcomings. Also, studies which came out
after the collection of the literature corpus were not included.
Similarly, studies included in the literature corpus were diverse in their nature (commentaries, case studies, empirical studies) and approach (qualitative, quantitative and
mixed methods). Due to this reason, bringing out a meta-analysis of findings was not
viable. So the synthesis section of the review should be approached only as a thematic
discussion of the studies in the literature corpus.
